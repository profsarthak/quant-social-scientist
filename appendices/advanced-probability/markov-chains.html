Markov Chains

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Systems that hop between states with no memory of how they got there — and the remarkable fact that many of them forget their starting point entirely.

A Markov chain is a system that moves between states where “the future depends on the present, but not the past.” Knowing where you are now is enough; the route that got you here is irrelevant. That one assumption — the Markov property — makes the whole future computable from a single transition matrix, and leads to a beautiful result: run many chains long enough and they converge to the same stationary distribution, no matter where they started. That convergence is the engine under MCMC.

The Markov property

A Markov chain \{X_n\} takes values in a set of states, and obeys: (Ross 2014) \Pr\{X_{n+1}=j \mid X_n=i, X_{n-1}=i_{n-1},\dots,X_0=i_0\} = \Pr\{X_{n+1}=j\mid X_n=i\} = P_{ij}. In words — “the future depends on the present, not the past.” (Ross 2014) The entire history collapses to the current state. That’s a strong assumption, but a stunning number of systems satisfy it (or can be made to by enlarging the state), and it’s what makes the chain tractable.

The transition matrix

All the dynamics live in the transition probability matrix \mathbf P, whose entry P_{ij} is the probability of moving from state i to state j in one step. (Ross 2014) Each row is a probability distribution: P_{ij}\ge 0 and \sum_j P_{ij}=1 (from any state you must go somewhere). (Ross 2014)

Multi-step transitions are just matrix powers. The Chapman–Kolmogorov equations, P^{n+m}_{ij}=\sum_k P^{n}_{ik}P^{m}_{kj}, say that to get from i to j in n+m steps you pass through some intermediate state k — and they imply the clean rule (Ross 2014) \mathbf P^{(n)} = \mathbf P^{\,n}. The n-step transition matrix is the one-step matrix raised to the n-th power. Want the state distribution after n steps? Multiply your starting distribution by \mathbf P^n.

Classifying states

Not all states behave alike: (Ross 2014)

  • Recurrent vs transient. A state is recurrent if, starting there, you return to it with probability 1; transient if there’s positive probability you never come back. In a finite chain, at least one state must be recurrent. (Ross 2014)
  • Periodicity. A state has period d if returns are only possible at multiples of d; period 1 is aperiodic. (Ross 2014)

These distinctions decide whether the chain settles down.

The stationary distribution

The payoff. For well-behaved chains, the probability of being in state j after many steps converges to a limit \pi_j that does not depend on where you started. (Ross 2014) If the chain is irreducible (you can get from any state to any other) and ergodic (aperiodic and positive recurrent), it converges to a unique stationary distribution \pi, the solution of (Ross 2014) \pi_j = \sum_i \pi_i\,P_{ij}, \qquad \sum_j \pi_j = 1. \pi is the distribution that reproduces itself under one more step (\pi\mathbf P=\pi) — an equilibrium the chain forgets its way toward.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# A 3-state transition matrix (e.g. sunny/cloudy/rainy)
P = np.array([[0.7, 0.2, 0.1],
              [0.3, 0.4, 0.3],
              [0.2, 0.3, 0.5]])

# Stationary distribution: left eigenvector with eigenvalue 1
vals, vecs = np.linalg.eig(P.T)
pi = np.real(vecs[:, np.argmin(np.abs(vals - 1))]); pi = pi / pi.sum()

steps = 12
start_a = np.array([1.0, 0.0, 0.0])
start_b = np.array([0.0, 0.0, 1.0])
def evolve(d):
    out = [d]
    for _ in range(steps):
        d = d @ P; out.append(d)
    return np.array(out)
A, B = evolve(start_a), evolve(start_b)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.4, 4.0))
colors = ["#1f3a5f", "#3f8f6f", "#9a6a2f"]
for s in range(3):
    ax.plot(A[:, s], "-o", color=colors[s], ms=3, lw=1.6, label=f"state {s} (start A)")
    ax.plot(B[:, s], "--s", color=colors[s], ms=3, lw=1.2, alpha=0.7)
    ax.axhline(pi[s], color=colors[s], ls=":", lw=1.0)
ax.set_xlabel("step n"); ax.set_ylabel("probability of each state")
ax.set_title("Two different starts → the same stationary distribution")
ax.legend(fontsize=7.5, frameon=False, ncol=3, loc="upper center")
ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Signature examples

Ross’s classics: (Ross 2014)

  • Weather in the “Land of Oz” — a chain over {rain, nice, snow}, predicting tomorrow from today.
  • Random walks — a particle stepping \pm 1 on a line; the symmetric walk (p=0.5) is recurrent (returns to the origin with probability 1), a surprisingly delicate fact.
  • Gambler’s ruin — bet $1 repeatedly until you hit $0 (broke) or $N (goal): a chain with two absorbing states, and the question is the probability of each fate.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Assuming the Markov property holds. If the future genuinely depends on more than the present, enlarge the state (e.g. include yesterday) — or it isn’t a Markov chain. (Ross 2014)

  • Expecting convergence without the conditions. A periodic or reducible chain may never settle to a unique \pi; check irreducibility and aperiodicity. (Ross 2014)

  • Confusing a stationary distribution with an absorbing state. Gambler’s ruin absorbs; it doesn’t mix to an interior equilibrium. (Ross 2014)

  • Reading \pi as “where the chain is.” \pi is the long-run distribution over states, not a single destination — the chain keeps moving, visiting state j a fraction \pi_j of the time.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Ross, Introduction to Probability Models — the Markov property (“future depends on the present, not the past”); the transition matrix P_{ij} (rows sum to 1); Chapman–Kolmogorov and \mathbf P^{(n)}=\mathbf P^n; recurrent vs transient states and periodicity; the stationary / limiting distribution \pi=\pi\mathbf P under irreducibility + ergodicity; and the Land of Oz weather, random walk (symmetric → recurrent), and gambler’s ruin examples.

Connections

  • Builds on: conditional probability (the transition probabilities) and matrix algebra.
  • Powers: MCMC — Markov chain Monte Carlo constructs a chain whose stationary distribution is the Bayesian posterior, then samples by running it.
  • Cousin of: the Poisson process (its continuous-time relative) and the state-space view of time series.

## The Markov property

A Markov chain $\{X_n\}$ takes values in a set of **states**, and obeys: [@ross_prob_models]
$$
\Pr\{X_{n+1}=j \mid X_n=i, X_{n-1}=i_{n-1},\dots,X_0=i_0\} = \Pr\{X_{n+1}=j\mid X_n=i\} = P_{ij}.
$$

collapses to the **current state**. That's a strong assumption, but a stunning number of systems satisfy it
(or can be made to by enlarging the state), and it's what makes the chain tractable.

## The transition matrix

All the dynamics live in the **transition probability matrix** $\mathbf P$, whose entry $P_{ij}$ is the
probability of moving from state $i$ to state $j$ in one step. [@ross_prob_models] Each **row is a
probability distribution**: $P_{ij}\ge 0$ and $\sum_j P_{ij}=1$ (from any state you must go *somewhere*).
[@ross_prob_models]

**Multi-step transitions** are just matrix powers. The **Chapman–Kolmogorov equations**,
$P^{n+m}_{ij}=\sum_k P^{n}_{ik}P^{m}_{kj}$, say that to get from $i$ to $j$ in $n+m$ steps you pass through
*some* intermediate state $k$ — and they imply the clean rule [@ross_prob_models]
$$

$$
The $n$-step transition matrix is the one-step matrix raised to the $n$-th power. Want the state distribution
after $n$ steps? Multiply your starting distribution by $\mathbf P^n$.

## Classifying states

Not all states behave alike: [@ross_prob_models]

- **Recurrent vs transient.** A state is **recurrent** if, starting there, you return to it with probability
  1; **transient** if there's positive probability you *never* come back. In a finite chain, at least one
  state must be recurrent. [@ross_prob_models]
- **Periodicity.** A state has **period $d$** if returns are only possible at multiples of $d$; period 1 is
  **aperiodic.** [@ross_prob_models]

These distinctions decide whether the chain settles down.

## The stationary distribution

The payoff. For well-behaved chains, the probability of being in state $j$ after many steps converges to a
limit $\pi_j$ that **does not depend on where you started.** [@ross_prob_models] If the chain is
**irreducible** (you can get from any state to any other) and **ergodic** (aperiodic and positive
recurrent), it converges to a *unique* **stationary distribution** $\pi$, the solution of [@ross_prob_models]
$$
\pi_j = \sum_i \pi_i\,P_{ij}, \qquad \sum_j \pi_j = 1.
$$
$\pi$ is the distribution that **reproduces itself** under one more step ($\pi\mathbf P=\pi$) — an
equilibrium the chain forgets its way toward.
