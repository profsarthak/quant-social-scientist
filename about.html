How to use this

The learning loop, the house style, and the source library it’s built on.

What this is

A reusable, growing reference for becoming a quantitative social science researcher. It is opinionated about pedagogy: intuition and use-cases lead, mathematics follows only where it clarifies a principle or a deviation, and the full derivations live in appendices. The goal is a reader who can move from undergraduate foundations to the academic rigour of causal research and the realities of field and client work.

It is designed to be self-contained: every concept you need is on the page, with one runnable figure to make it concrete and explicit links up and down the curriculum. You should never have to leave the document to follow an argument.

How to read it

  • Follow the spine, or jump in. The Parts (0 → VI) build in order, but each article stands on its own and links to its prerequisites — so you can start anywhere and follow the Connections back to whatever you’re missing.
  • Read the intuition first; treat the math as optional depth. The argument is carried by the prose and the figure. The tinted Appendix · the mathematics block is there when you want the derivation and skippable when you don’t.
  • Use the tracks for breadth. Beyond the main spine, parallel tracks go deep on the ML Track, Thinking in Dataframes (R), and Complexity Science (systems, networks, emergence).

The source library

This encyclopedia is built from a locked library of canonical texts — it is a structured, intuition-first synthesis of those sources, not a replacement for them. Every article carries a From the sources note giving explicit per-source attribution, so any claim can be traced back and read further. The library spans:

Theme Key sources
Probability Ross A First Course; Grinstead & Snell; Ross Probability Models
Applied statistics Practical Statistics for Data Scientists; Statistics Done Wrong; Albers
Econometrics Wooldridge; Angrist & Pischke Mastering ’Metrics & Mostly Harmless; Panel Data; Moffatt Experimetrics
Bayesian Lambert A Student’s Guide to Bayesian Statistics
Causal inference Cunningham The Mixtape; Gerber & Green Field Experiments; Pearl Book of Why & Primer; the DiD Practitioner’s Guide; Chernozhukov et al.
Machine learning ISLR; ML for Social & Behavioral Research; Murphy Probabilistic Perspective
Complexity science Meadows Thinking in Systems; Menczer Network Science; Barabási Network Science; Mitchell Complexity; Miller & Page; Sayama; Strogatz; Epstein & Axtell
Computing (R) Wickham & Grolemund R for Data Science; Dayal
Methods & critique Bryman Social Research Methods; Muller The Tyranny of Metrics

The house style (for contributors — including future-me)

Articles are written to a fixed template so the encyclopedia stays coherent as it grows:

  • In one sentence — a single italic intuition kernel under the title.
  • Why it matters — the motivating use-case before any notation.
  • Intuition — plain language, concrete examples, analogies that hold up.
  • Worked examples — taken from the source library, with real studies where possible.
  • One runnable figure — a numpy/matplotlib simulation built by hand, never a black box.
  • The math, where it earns its place — inline and minimal; if a derivation is longer than the idea it supports, it goes to the appendix.
  • Common pitfalls — how the concept is misused in practice (the Statistics Done Wrong lens is a recurring guest).
  • From the sources — explicit per-source attribution.
  • Appendix · the full mathematics — wrapped in a tinted block the reader may skip.
  • Connections — links up and down the curriculum.

Math uses common sense first and proof second: notation is introduced because the prose demanded it, never to decorate.

Building locally

quarto render

quarto preview

Executable code blocks (e.g. simulations) run on a Python virtual environment in .venv/ with numpy and matplotlib. R support can be added per-document later — Quarto handles both engines in the same project.
