Communicating Quantitative Findings

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A result no one understands has no value; a result that misleads has negative value. Honest communication is part of the analysis, not an afterthought.

The analysis ends when someone acts on it — which means the last mile is communication, and it is where rigour most often dies. A correct estimate buried in stars and jargon informs no one; a trivial effect dramatised by a truncated axis misinforms everyone. The same discipline you brought to the model must reach the chart, the number, and the sentence: show the data, show the uncertainty, and never let the presentation claim more than the evidence.

Why it matters

A finding that decision-makers can’t understand — or that quietly misleads them — is worse than no finding. Reinhart and Bryman treat communication as a part of statistical integrity, not polish: the choices of number, frame, and chart determine what people actually believe. (Reinhart 2015; Bryman 2016) This is where the open-science transparency reaches the reader, and where a researcher takes responsibility not just for being right but for being correctly understood.

Communicate uncertainty, not stars

P-values and significance “stars” are a famously poor way to report a result: a p-value says nothing about the size of an effect, only how incompatible the data are with a null. (Reinhart 2015) Reinhart’s quip is that p-values are like mosquitoes — impossible to swat out of the literature despite their faults. (Reinhart 2015) Report confidence intervals and effect sizes instead: a range of plausible values shows how big and how sure in one stroke. (Reinhart 2015)

Natural frequencies beat probabilities

For risks and base rates, how you phrase the number changes whether people grasp it. People understand natural frequencies (“10 out of 1,000”) far better than the mathematically identical probabilities or percentages (“1%”). (Reinhart 2015) The classic case is the mammogram:

NoteThe mammogram, in frequencies

Suppose 0.8% of women have breast cancer and the test has a 7% false-positive rate. Told as probabilities, a positive result sounds terrifying. Reframed as natural frequencies, the base-rate truth is plain: of 1,000 women, about 8 have cancer, while roughly 70 healthy women also test positive — so a positive result means cancer only about 1 time in 10. (Reinhart 2015) Same arithmetic, opposite intuition. The frequency framing makes Bayes’ theorem visible.

Significant ≠ important

The most common reporting error in social science: conflating statistical significance with practical importance. (Reinhart 2015) Two failure modes, opposite directions:

  • Trivially significant. With a large enough sample (say 70,000 observations), a minuscule, useless effect becomes “statistically significant.” Significance is partly a statement about n, not importance. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence. A non-significant result (p>0.05) is not proof of no effect; it often means the study was underpowered and couldn’t tell. (Reinhart 2015) And even a non-significant point estimate remains the best available guess of the truth given the data — “no effect” is rarely the right summary. (Reinhart 2015)

Always pair the question “is it real?” with “is it big enough to matter?” — and answer the second with an effect size and its interval, not a star.

Don’t let the chart lie

Graphics should show the data, not the maker’s cleverness. (Bryman 2016) Bryman leans on Tufte’s principles of graphical integrity: (Bryman 2016)

  • Honest scales. Truncated or zoomed y-axes that don’t start at zero exaggerate small differences — the single most common deception in business and media charts.
  • Proportional ink. The size of the effect shown should match the effect in the data (Tufte’s “lie factor”).
  • No chartjunk. Strip content-free decoration that distracts from the substance.
  • Show uncertainty. Include error bars — clearly labelled as SD or CI — so readers can see how stable the values are.

The figure makes the axis trick undeniable: identical numbers, two charts, opposite impressions.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

groups = ["Group A", "Group B"]
values = [99.0, 101.0]
colors = ["#9aa0a6", "#1f8a4c"]

Know your audience

You are responsible for how your results are interpreted, so tailor the message: (Reinhart 2015; Bryman 2016)

  • Numeracy. Less numerate readers often prefer verbal risk labels (“likely,” “improbable”) to numbers. Their exact mental values vary between people, but the relative order is consistent — so verbal scales communicate ranking reliably even when they blur magnitude. (Bryman 2016)
  • Framing effects. “30% will survive” and “70% will die” are identical facts that land very differently; the responsible move is to present both frames so the listener isn’t steered. (Reinhart 2015)

Tailoring the message is not spin — it is meeting the audience where they are so the true picture lands.

Honest write-up

Transparency in reporting is the antidote to the replication crisis: (Reinhart 2015; Bryman 2016)

  • Report everything you measured, not just what reached significance — selective reporting is outcome-reporting bias and poisons meta-analysis.
  • Detail the methodology — sample-size justification, stopping rules, and any deviations from the protocol.
  • Acknowledge the mess. Good research is messy; stating limitations and mistakes makes the work more credible, not less.
  • Crosstabs before regressions. Lead with the simplest transparent comparison (means, cross-tabs) before the complex model, so readers can see the raw pattern the model claims to explain. (Bryman 2016)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reporting stars instead of intervals. A p-value hides the effect size; give the confidence interval. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Probabilities where frequencies would clarify. For risk and base rates, “8 in 1,000” beats “0.8%.” (Reinhart 2015)

  • Truncated axes. A non-zero baseline inflates trivial differences; default to a zero baseline for bar charts and label any break. (Bryman 2016)

  • Equating significance with importance — or non-significance with zero. Report effect sizes; remember absence of evidence isn’t evidence of absence. (Reinhart 2015)

  • One-sided framing. “70% survive” vs “30% die” steers the reader; present both. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Cherry-picking reported outcomes. Report all measures and analyses, including the boring ones. (Reinhart 2015)

Appendix · the numbers behind the framings

The mammogram, by Bayes. With prevalence \Pr(C)=0.008, sensitivity \approx1, and false-positive rate \Pr(+\mid \lnot C)=0.07, \Pr(C\mid +)=\frac{\Pr(+\mid C)\Pr(C)}{\Pr(+\mid C)\Pr(C)+\Pr(+\mid\lnot C)\Pr(\lnot C)} \approx \frac{1\cdot 0.008}{0.008+0.07\cdot0.992}\approx 0.10. Per 1,000 women: \approx 8 true positives vs \approx 70 false positives — the natural-frequency statement is just this arithmetic made countable, which is why people reason with it correctly while the percentage version triggers the base-rate fallacy.

Tufte’s lie factor. \text{lie factor}=\dfrac{\text{size of effect shown in graphic}}{\text{size of effect in data}}. Graphical integrity demands \approx1. A bar chart with a baseline at 98 instead of 0 on values 99 vs 101 shows a 3{:}1 visual ratio for a 1.02{:}1 data ratio — a lie factor near 3.

Why significance tracks n. For a two-group mean difference, the test statistic is t=\dfrac{\hat\delta}{s\sqrt{2/n}}\propto \hat\delta\sqrt{n}. Fixing a real but tiny \hat\delta, t\to\infty as n\to\infty — so any nonzero effect becomes significant with enough data. Significance answers “is it distinguishable from zero?”; the effect size answers “does it matter?”
