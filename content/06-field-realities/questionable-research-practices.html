Questionable Research Practices & Researcher Degrees of Freedom

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

How honest, well-meaning researchers manufacture false results without ever committing fraud — by making defensible choices, after seeing the data.

The replication crisis is not mostly a fraud problem. It is a problem of flexibility: every analysis involves dozens of defensible choices — which outliers to drop, which covariates to include, which outcome to feature, when to stop collecting — and if you make those choices after seeing the data, you can find significance in almost anything. This is Goodhart’s law turned on researchers: when a p-value below 0.05 becomes the target for publication, the analysis bends until it clears the bar. The result is an honest literature full of effects that aren’t there.

Why it matters

Reinhart’s central, unsettling claim: most false findings come not from cheats but from honest researchers with poor statistical instincts. (Reinhart 2015) That makes this everyone’s problem — including yours. The defences (preregistration, replication, correction for multiple testing) only work if you can recognise the failure modes in your own workflow. This entry catalogues how a sincere analysis goes wrong, turning the replication crisis from a headline into a set of habits to guard against.

Researcher degrees of freedom

Reinhart’s name for the root cause: the “enormous amount of freedom” a researcher has in analysing data. (Reinhart 2015) Each choice is individually defensible:

  • Which observations to exclude as outliers (drop the subjects who had diarrhea during the diet trial?).
  • How to define the groups (where exactly does “overweight” begin?).
  • Which covariates to control for.
  • Which outcome to treat as primary, and when to stop collecting data.

None is fraud. But made after seeing the data, they let you — usually unconsciously — pick the path most flattering to your hypothesis. Reinhart’s striking number: handling these choices differently can change the estimated effect size by a factor of two. (Reinhart 2015) The analysis is honest at every step and biased as a whole.

P-hacking and the garden of forking paths

P-hacking is selectively analysing data until the p-value drops below 0.05. (Reinhart 2015) Its subtler cousin is the garden of forking paths: even a researcher who runs one analysis can be biased, because the analysis they chose was contingent on the data — at each fork they took the defensible branch that happened to lead to significance. (Reinhart 2015) No conscious cherry-picking is required; the flexibility alone does the damage.

NoteReinhart’s anti-aging Beatles

The demonstration that made the point unforgettable: researchers “proved” that listening to the Beatles’ When I’m Sixty-Four made undergraduates nearly a year and a half younger — a logical impossibility. They did it honestly, by deciding after seeing the data which covariates to control for (the subject’s father’s age, etc.) and which outcome to report. Simulation shows this kind of flexibility can push the false-positive rate as high as 50%. (Reinhart 2015) If you can “discover” reverse aging, you can discover anything.

The figure shows the mechanism directly: take pure noise, try a handful of defensible analyses, report the best — and “significance” appears far more than the advertised 5% of the time.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statistics import NormalDist

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
studies, n, forks = 4000, 40, 10

def two_sided_p(z):
    return 2 * (1 - NormalDist().cdf(abs(z)))

single_p, hacked_p = [], []
for _ in range(studies):
    # honest single test on a null effect
    g1 = rng.normal(0, 1, n); g2 = rng.normal(0, 1, n)
    z = (g1.mean() - g2.mean()) / np.sqrt(g1.var(ddof=1)/n + g2.var(ddof=1)/n)
    single_p.append(two_sided_p(z))
    # garden of forking paths: try `forks` defensible analyses, keep the smallest p
    ps = []
    for _ in range(forks):
        a = rng.normal(0, 1, n); b = rng.normal(0, 1, n)
        zz = (a.mean() - b.mean()) / np.sqrt(a.var(ddof=1)/n + b.var(ddof=1)/n)
        ps.append(two_sided_p(zz))
    hacked_p.append(min(ps))

single_p, hacked_p = np.array(single_p), np.array(hacked_p)
fp_single = (single_p < 0.05).mean()
fp_hacked = (hacked_p < 0.05).mean()

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.0))
bins = np.linspace(0, 1, 21)
ax.hist(single_p, bins=bins, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.7, density=True,
        label=f"single honest test — {fp_single*100:.0f}% significant")
ax.hist(hacked_p, bins=bins, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.55, density=True,
        label=f"best of {forks} analyses — {fp_hacked*100:.0f}% significant")
ax.axvline(0.05, color="#1f3a5f", ls="--", lw=1.6)
ax.text(0.06, ax.get_ylim()[1]*0.9, "p = 0.05", color="#1f3a5f", fontsize=8)
ax.set_xlabel("reported p-value (data with NO real effect)")
ax.set_ylabel("density")
ax.set_title("Flexibility manufactures significance from noise")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Multiple comparisons

The most quantifiable QRP: run enough tests and false positives are guaranteed. (Reinhart 2015) Test 20 independent true-null hypotheses at \alpha=0.05, and the chance of at least one false positive is 1-(1-0.05)^{20}\approx \mathbf{64\%}. (Reinhart 2015) This is the jelly-bean problem: test 20 colours of jelly bean for a link to acne and you’ll “find” one — a famous comic, and a real research pathology. (Reinhart 2015) The corrections: (Reinhart 2015)

  • Bonferroni — divide the threshold by the number of tests (\alpha/n); controls the chance of any false positive (family-wise error), at the cost of power.
  • Benjamini–Hochberg — controls the false discovery rate (the expected fraction of significant results that are false), less conservative and often more sensible when many tests are genuinely exploratory.

HARKing and circular analysis

HARKing — Hypothesising After Results are Known — is dressing up a pattern you found in the data as a hypothesis you predicted. (Reinhart 2015) It is a form of circular analysis or “double-dipping”: using the same data to both generate and test a hypothesis. The canonical case is neuroimaging — select a brain “region of interest” because it lit up to a stimulus, then run a detailed test on that same region, and you’ve guaranteed an inflated effect: you selected for the strongest random noise, then “confirmed” it. (Reinhart 2015) Discovery and confirmation must use different data.

Optional stopping

Optional stopping is peeking at the data as it accumulates and stopping the moment p<0.05. (Reinhart 2015) Because the p-value random-walks as n grows, it will dip below 0.05 by chance eventually even under the null — and if you stop there, you bank a false positive. Testing after every subject and stopping at significance massively inflates the error rate. The clinical signature: medical trials stopped early for benefit exaggerate their effects by an average of 29% versus trials that ran to completion. (Reinhart 2015)

Publication bias and the file drawer

Even a literature of honest, correct studies can lie in aggregate, because of what gets published. Eye-catching significant results are far more publishable than null findings — the file-drawer problem — so the published record is a biased sample of all results. (Reinhart 2015) Two cases: (Reinhart 2015)

  • TP53 and cancer. A meta-analysis of 18 published studies showed a highly significant correlation; add the unpublished data and the effect vanished.
  • Reboxetine. Pfizer’s antidepressant looked effective in the published trials — while studies comprising three times more data, showing it didn’t work, went unmentioned.

This is why meta-analysis must explicitly probe for publication bias: pooling a biased sample faithfully reproduces the bias.

Truth inflation and the winner’s curse

The final twist, and the link back to statistical power: truth inflation (a Type M, or magnitude, error). (Reinhart 2015) An underpowered study has high sampling variability, so it only reaches significance when it draws a lucky, large-effect sample. The published estimate is therefore systematically too big — the winner’s curse. (Reinhart 2015) Hence the Proteus phenomenon: in fast-moving fields the earliest published effects are the most extreme, and follow-ups shrink them — and higher-impact journals, chasing splashier findings, may overstate effects more. (Reinhart 2015) A significant result from a small study is not just possibly false; if true, it is probably exaggerated.

Reading other people’s research

Every practice above is also a checklist for consuming research — you spend more time reading studies than producing them, and the same flaws hide in others’ work. (Reinhart 2015) Martin & Bridgmon offer a disciplined five-step way to evaluate a paper before trusting its headline: (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

  1. Gain a general overview — what is the study about, and what does it claim?
  2. Ascertain the rationale — why was it done; what gap or question motivates it?
  3. Identify the theoretical framework — what assumptions and prior theory underpin the design?
  4. Identify the setting — who, where, when; the sample and context that bound the result.
  5. Determine the nature of the research — design, methods, and analysis: is the inference licensed by the design? (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

Two habits sharpen the read. First, return to the research question as your anchor — it is easy to get lost in charts and jargon, so keep asking whether each result actually bears on the question posed. (Reinhart 2015) Second, look for the mundane failure modes this Part catalogues: an uncorrected multiple-comparisons fishing expedition, a subgroup that smells of HARKing, a small “significant” study with an implausibly large effect, a non-reproducible analysis with no shared data or code, sloppy code errors like mislabelled columns. (Reinhart 2015) Critical consumption is not cynicism — it is applying to others’ work the same scrutiny you owe your own.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Deciding the analysis after seeing the data. Outlier rules, covariates, subgroups, and the primary outcome should be fixed in advance; otherwise you walk the garden of forking paths. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Running many tests and reporting the winners. Correct for multiplicity (Bonferroni / FDR) and report all the tests you ran, not just the significant ones. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Testing a hypothesis on the data that generated it. HARKing/double-dipping inflates effects; split discovery from confirmation. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Peeking and stopping at significance. Optional stopping inflates false positives and effect sizes; fix the sample size or use a proper sequential design. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Trusting a small significant study’s effect size. Truth inflation means it’s likely overstated; expect regression toward a smaller true effect. (Reinhart 2015)

Appendix · the mathematics

Family-wise error. For m independent true-null tests at level \alpha, the probability of at least one false rejection is \text{FWER}=1-(1-\alpha)^m, \to1 as m grows (64\% at m=20,\alpha=0.05). Bonferroni uses \alpha/m per test, bounding FWER \le\alpha. Benjamini–Hochberg instead orders the p-values p_{(1)}\le\dots\le p_{(m)} and rejects the largest k with p_{(k)}\le \frac{k}{m}\alpha, controlling the expected false discovery proportion at \alpha — more powerful when many alternatives are true.

Optional stopping inflates \alpha. Under the null the test statistic behaves like a random walk; by the law of the iterated logarithm it crosses any fixed boundary infinitely often as n\to\infty, so \Pr(\exists\,n: p_n<0.05)\to1. Stopping at the first crossing therefore rejects with probability far above 0.05.

Truth inflation (Type M). Condition the estimate \hat\theta\sim\mathcal N(\theta,\sigma^2) on significance |\hat\theta|>1.96\,\sigma. When power is low (\theta/\sigma small), the conditional mean \mathbb E[\hat\theta\mid \text{significant}] greatly exceeds \theta — the published effect is inflated, by a factor that grows as power falls. This is the formal winner’s curse, and the reason power protects magnitude, not just detection.

## Reading other people's research

Every practice above is also a **checklist for consuming research** — you spend more time reading
studies than producing them, and the same flaws hide in others' work. [@reinhart_swrong] Martin &
Bridgmon offer a disciplined five-step way to evaluate a paper *before* trusting its headline:
[@martin_bridgmon]

1. **Gain a general overview** — what is the study about, and what does it claim?
2. **Ascertain the rationale** — *why* was it done; what gap or question motivates it?
3. **Identify the theoretical framework** — what assumptions and prior theory underpin the design?
4. **Identify the setting** — *who, where, when*; the sample and context that bound the result.
5. **Determine the nature of the research** — design, methods, and analysis: is the inference licensed
   by the design? [@martin_bridgmon]

Two habits sharpen the read. First, **return to the research question as your anchor** — it is easy to
get lost in charts and jargon, so keep asking whether each result actually bears on the *question
posed.* [@reinhart_swrong] Second, **look for the mundane failure modes** this Part catalogues: an
uncorrected multiple-comparisons fishing expedition, a subgroup that smells of HARKing, a small
"significant" study with an implausibly large effect, a [non-reproducible](open-and-reproducible-science.qmd)
analysis with no shared data or code, [sloppy code errors](open-and-reproducible-science.qmd) like
mislabelled columns. [@reinhart_swrong] Critical consumption is not cynicism — it is applying to others'
work the same scrutiny you owe your own.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Deciding the analysis after seeing the data.** Outlier rules, covariates, subgroups, and the primary
  outcome should be fixed *in advance*; otherwise you walk the garden of forking paths. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Running many tests and reporting the winners.** Correct for multiplicity (Bonferroni / FDR) and
  report *all* the tests you ran, not just the significant ones. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Testing a hypothesis on the data that generated it.** HARKing/double-dipping inflates effects; split
  discovery from confirmation. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Peeking and stopping at significance.** Optional stopping inflates false positives and effect sizes;
  fix the sample size or use a proper sequential design. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Trusting a small significant study's effect size.** Truth inflation means it's likely overstated;
  expect regression toward a smaller true effect. [@reinhart_swrong]

:::

## From the sources {.unnumbered}

::: {.callout-note collapse="true" title="Who says what"}
- **Reinhart, *Statistics Done Wrong*** — false results from honest researchers via **researcher degrees

  forking paths** (the *When I'm Sixty-Four* study; false-positive rates to 50%); **multiple
  comparisons** ($1-0.95^{20}\approx64\%$; jelly beans; **Bonferroni** and **Benjamini–Hochberg FDR**);
  **HARKing** / circular analysis / double-dipping (neuroimaging ROI); **optional stopping** (early-
  stopped trials exaggerate ~29%); **publication bias / file drawer** (TP53; Reboxetine); **truth
  inflation / Type M / winner's curse** and the **Proteus phenomenon**; the consumer's habit of
  returning to the research question and spotting these failure modes in others' work.
- **Martin & Bridgmon** — the **five-step process** for critically evaluating a study (overview,
  rationale, theoretical framework, setting, nature of the research).

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Family-wise error.** For $m$ independent true-null tests at level $\alpha$, the probability of at
least one false rejection is
$$
\text{FWER}=1-(1-\alpha)^m,
$$
$\to1$ as $m$ grows ($64\%$ at $m=20,\alpha=0.05$). **Bonferroni** uses $\alpha/m$ per test, bounding
FWER $\le\alpha$. **Benjamini–Hochberg** instead orders the $p$-values $p_{(1)}\le\dots\le p_{(m)}$ and
rejects the largest $k$ with $p_{(k)}\le \frac{k}{m}\alpha$, controlling the expected false discovery
proportion at $\alpha$ — more powerful when many alternatives are true.

**Optional stopping inflates $\alpha$.** Under the null the test statistic behaves like a
[random walk](../04-predictive/time-series-fundamentals.qmd); by the law of the iterated logarithm it
crosses any fixed boundary infinitely often as $n\to\infty$, so $\Pr(\exists\,n: p_n<0.05)\to1$.
Stopping at the first crossing therefore rejects with probability far above $0.05$.

**Truth inflation (Type M).** Condition the estimate $\hat\theta\sim\mathcal N(\theta,\sigma^2)$ on
significance $|\hat\theta|>1.96\,\sigma$. When power is low ($\theta/\sigma$ small), the conditional mean
$\mathbb E[\hat\theta\mid \text{significant}]$ greatly exceeds $\theta$ — the published effect is
inflated, by a factor that grows as power falls. This is the formal winner's curse, and the reason
[power](../02-inference/statistical-power.qmd) protects *magnitude*, not just *detection*.

:::
