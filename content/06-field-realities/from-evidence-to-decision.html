From Evidence to Decision

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

An estimate is not a decision. Turning a number into a defensible action means weighing effect size, base rates, costs, and who bears the risk — under a deadline.

The academy stops at the estimate; the world begins there. A coefficient with a confidence interval is an input to a decision, never the decision itself — because acting requires what the number leaves out: how big the effect is, how common the case, what each kind of mistake costs, who bears the risk, and what you should do before the evidence is complete. Evidence informs the choice; it cannot make it. The last competence this book teaches is wearing both hats — analyst and decider — at once.

Why it matters

This is the subtitle of the whole module: the client who needs an answer by Friday. Evidence-based practice says decisions should rest on the best available research — a genuine advance over hunch and authority. (Bryman 2016) But evidence is partial, contested, and value-laden, and a number never speaks for itself. The skill of translating an estimate into a defensible action — explicit about costs, risks, and uncertainty — is what separates a technician from someone who can be trusted with a question.

Evidence-based policy, and its limits

The ideal: ground decisions in rigorous research, synthesised through systematic reviews. (Bryman 2016) The limits are just as important to state: (Muller 2018; Bryman 2016)

  • Evidence is partial — and can deceive. What is measurable is not what is important; leaning only on metrics yields “distorted knowledge” that looks solid. (Muller 2018)
  • Context-dependence. “What works once may not work again” — David Kilcullen’s lesson from counterinsurgency, where human actors adapt to whatever you do (as students adapt to a tested curriculum). (Muller 2018) External validity is not guaranteed.
  • The is/ought gap. Evidence describes how the world is; it cannot dictate what a society ought to value. Isaiah Berlin’s point that judgment needs a “talent for synthesis” and a “feel for the whole” names exactly the part no metric supplies. (Muller 2018)

Evidence narrows the argument and disciplines it; it does not end it.

Translating an estimate into a decision

Four lenses turn a “best guess” into an action: (Reinhart 2015; Muller 2018)

  • Effect size, not just significance. Decision-makers need how big, not is it p<0.05. A trivial effect can be significant in a large sample and still not worth the investment. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Base rates. A low base rate flips the calculus: screen for a rare condition and even an accurate test yields more false alarms than true cases, inviting harmful over-intervention. (Reinhart 2015)
  • The asymmetry of errors. A Type I and a Type II error rarely cost the same. An administrator weighing a $900,000 reading curriculum fears a Type I error (paying for something no better than the status quo); a traffic-safety regulator fears a Type II error (missing a real hazard — measured in preventable deaths). (Reinhart 2015) The same p-value warrants different decisions depending on which error you can least afford.
  • Expected value. Weight each outcome by its probability and its payoff — the logic even of Pascal’s Wager, where a large enough stake dominates a small probability. (Reinhart 2015)

The figure puts these together: the right choice depends not just on how likely the intervention works but on the cost of being wrong — and the break-even probability moves with it.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

p = np.linspace(0, 1, 200)            # probability the intervention truly works
benefit = 100                          # value if it works

# Expected net value of adopting = p*benefit - (1-p)*cost_of_wrong_adoption
cost_cheap = 40                        # a cheap mistake
cost_costly = 400                      # a costly/harmful mistake
ev_cheap = p * benefit - (1 - p) * cost_cheap
ev_costly = p * benefit - (1 - p) * cost_costly

be_cheap = cost_cheap / (benefit + cost_cheap)
be_costly = cost_costly / (benefit + cost_costly)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.axhline(0, color="#b7b6a8", lw=1.2)
ax.plot(p, ev_cheap, color="#161613", lw=2.4, label="cheap to be wrong")
ax.plot(p, ev_costly, color="#e8531d", lw=2.4, label="costly/harmful to be wrong")
for be, c in [(be_cheap, "#161613"), (be_costly, "#e8531d")]:
    ax.axvline(be, color=c, ls=":", lw=1.4)
    ax.text(be + 0.01, -120, f"act if p > {be:.2f}", color=c, fontsize=8, rotation=90, va="bottom")
ax.fill_between(p, ev_cheap, 0, where=ev_cheap > 0, color="#161613", alpha=0.06)
ax.set_xlabel("probability the intervention truly works")
ax.set_ylabel("expected net value of adopting")
ax.set_title("The same evidence, different decisions — set by the cost of error")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Deciding under uncertainty and time pressure

Decisions rarely wait for clean evidence. (Reinhart 2015)

NoteRight turn on red

The 1970s oil crisis pushed the U.S. to allow right turns on red to save fuel. Early studies said it was safe — but they were underpowered. Only years later, with more data, did the policy reveal a roughly 60% increase in pedestrian accidents at affected intersections. (Reinhart 2015) Acting on weak evidence was perhaps unavoidable under the deadline — but treating “not significant” as “safe” was the error.

Two principles for the deadline: (Reinhart 2015)

  • The precautionary principle. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence: no proof of harm is not proof of safety. Where the downside is catastrophic or irreversible, the burden shifts toward caution.
  • “More research is needed” — wisdom or dodge? Usually scientific honesty; sometimes a way for organisations to defer a hard, unpopular action. (Reinhart 2015) Knowing which it is, in a given case, is itself a judgment.

Stakeholders and how organisations (mis)use numbers

A decision lands inside an institution with its own incentives — and the tyranny-of-metrics dynamics return in force. (Muller 2018) Organisations use numbers not only to learn but to control, and control invites gaming: surgeons avoid risky patients to protect mortality scores; police downgrade felonies to hit targets; Vietnam body counts were fabricated. (Muller 2018) The practical lesson for anyone handing an estimate to an organisation: anticipate how the number will be used and gamed, and prefer metrics that are internal, diagnostic, and built bottom-up with the people measured. (Muller 2018)

The researcher–decision-maker gap

Researchers and decision-makers inhabit different worlds, and the handoff between them is where good evidence goes to die or to work: (Bryman 2016; Muller 2018)

  • Incommensurate goals. Researchers chase novel, generalisable truths and journal publication; decision-makers need local, actionable solutions to a specific problem, now. (Bryman 2016)
  • Even transparency cuts both ways. Researchers prize openness; but Muller notes that in politics and diplomacy, total transparency can be the enemy of performance — it kills the backstage negotiation real decisions require. (Muller 2018)
  • Judgment over mechanical precision. Muller’s bottom line: measurement should inform judgment, not replace it — there is no silver bullet and no substitute for actually knowing your subject and your organisation. (Muller 2018)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reporting significance and calling it a decision. Decisions need effect size, base rate, and the costs of each error — not a p-value. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Ignoring the asymmetry of errors. The cost of a false positive vs a false negative should set the evidentiary bar; the $900k curriculum and the traffic hazard warrant different thresholds. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Treating non-significant as safe. Absence of evidence isn’t proof of safety — the right-turn-on-red trap. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Forgetting the decision lands in an institution. Anticipate gaming and incentive distortion before your metric becomes someone’s target. (Muller 2018)

  • Letting evidence pose as values. Data informs the is; it can’t settle the ought. Name the value judgments explicitly. (Muller 2018)

Appendix · the decision arithmetic

Expected value and the break-even probability. Let p=\Pr(\text{intervention works}), benefit B if it works, and cost C if you adopt and it doesn’t. Adopt when \mathbb E[\text{adopt}] = pB-(1-p)C > 0 \iff p > p^\ast=\frac{C}{B+C}. The break-even probability p^\ast rises with the cost of a wrong adoption C — formalising the asymmetry of errors. A cheap mistake (C\ll B) justifies acting on weak evidence; a catastrophic one (C\gg B) demands near-certainty. The same estimate p implies opposite decisions across regimes.

Base rates and the cost of screening. Screening a population with prevalence \rho using a test of sensitivity s and false-positive rate f yields, per N people, sN\rho true positives and fN(1-\rho) false positives. The ratio of false alarms to true cases is \dfrac{f(1-\rho)}{s\rho}, which explodes as \rho\to0 — so even an accurate test does net harm when the base rate is low and the follow-up intervention is risky. The decision must weigh that harm, not just the test’s accuracy.

## Appendix · the decision arithmetic {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Expected value and the break-even probability.** Let $p=\Pr(\text{intervention works})$, benefit $B$
if it works, and cost $C$ if you adopt and it doesn't. Adopt when
$$
\mathbb E[\text{adopt}] = pB-(1-p)C > 0 \iff p > p^\ast=\frac{C}{B+C}.
$$
The **break-even probability** $p^\ast$ rises with the cost of a wrong adoption $C$ — formalising the
asymmetry of errors. A cheap mistake ($C\ll B$) justifies acting on weak evidence; a catastrophic one
($C\gg B$) demands near-certainty. The *same* estimate $p$ implies opposite decisions across regimes.

**Base rates and the cost of screening.** Screening a population with prevalence $\rho$ using a test of
sensitivity $s$ and false-positive rate $f$ yields, per $N$ people, $sN\rho$ true positives and
$fN(1-\rho)$ false positives. The ratio of false alarms to true cases is
$\dfrac{f(1-\rho)}{s\rho}$, which **explodes as $\rho\to0$** — so even an accurate test does net harm
when the base rate is low and the [follow-up intervention](../01-foundations/bayes-theorem.qmd) is
risky. The decision must weigh that harm, not just the test's accuracy.

:::
