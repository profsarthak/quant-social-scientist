Open & Reproducible Science

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A result no one can check is a rumour with a p-value. The structural fix for questionable practices is to make research transparent, pre-committed, and repeatable.

Questionable research practices flourish in the dark — in analytic choices no one sees and null results no one publishes. Open science is the architecture that turns the lights on: preregister the plan before the data arrive, share the data and code so anyone can re-run them, and replicate to see if the effect was ever real. None of it requires trusting the researcher’s virtue; it changes the system so honesty is the path of least resistance.

Why it matters

Every fix in the last entry was something an individual must remember to do. Open science makes integrity structural — baked into how studies are registered, reviewed, and published — so it survives ordinary human incentives. (Reinhart 2015) It is the infrastructure of trust, and the reason this very book is built as a reproducible document. It does not make weak studies strong, but it makes all studies checkable — the precondition for science to self-correct.

Two kinds of “it holds up”

The vocabulary matters because the two are routinely confused: (Reinhart 2015)

  • Reproducibility (computational). An independent analyst, given your same data and same code, gets the same numbers. This is a floor, not a ceiling — and shockingly often unmet. If code and data were posted, a colleague could simply read the code instead of spending thousands of hours reconstructing your steps. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Replicability (empirical). A new study, with new data and subjects but the same design, finds the same effect. Replication is called the heart of science — yet is rarely done, because it is laborious and brings little professional reward. (Reinhart 2015)

A finding can be perfectly reproducible (the code runs) and utterly un-replicable (the effect was noise). You need both.

Preregistration: closing the garden of forking paths

The most direct antidote to researcher degrees of freedom: preregistration — committing to the hypothesis and the full analysis plan before seeing the data. (Reinhart 2015) It works by drawing a bright line: (Reinhart 2015)

  • Confirmatory research tests a priori hypotheses with the pre-specified analysis — these p-values mean what they claim.
  • Exploratory research searches the data for patterns — legitimate and valuable, but its findings are hypotheses to test later, not confirmed results.

By fixing the outliers, covariates, and outcome in advance, preregistration closes the garden of forking paths: you can no longer (even unconsciously) pick the analysis most flattering to your hypothesis. (Reinhart 2015) It doesn’t forbid exploration — it forbids disguising exploration as confirmation.

Registered reports: defeating publication bias

Preregistration fixes the analysis; registered reports fix the publication incentive. (Reinhart 2015) Here the journal peer-reviews the study design before any data are collected and, if the design is sound, grants in-principle acceptance — a promise to publish regardless of whether the results are significant. (Reinhart 2015) This strikes directly at publication bias / the file drawer: when null results are guaranteed a home, the literature stops being a biased sample of only the “eye-catching” findings. (Reinhart 2015)

Replication: direct and conceptual

Replication is the system’s error-correction, in two forms: (Reinhart 2015)

  • Direct replication recreates the original conditions as precisely as possible — did this specific finding hold?
  • Conceptual replication tests the same idea with different settings, subjects, or measures — does the underlying effect generalise?

A failure to replicate suggests the original was a fluke (noise — the winner’s curse made visible) or depended on unmeasured context. (Reinhart 2015) The figure shows the pattern the Reproducibility Project found across psychology: replication effects are systematically smaller than the originals, and many vanish — exactly what truth inflation predicts.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(12)
k = 100
# True effects are mostly small; originals are significance-selected (inflated upward).
true = np.abs(rng.normal(0.2, 0.15, k))
sig_thresh = 0.25
orig = true + np.abs(rng.normal(0.25, 0.12, k))   # winner's curse: originals overshoot
repl = true + rng.normal(0, 0.10, k)              # replications scatter around the truth

failed = repl < sig_thresh
frac_fail = failed.mean()

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.8, 4.4))
lim = 0, max(orig.max(), repl.max()) * 1.05
ax.axhspan(lim[0], sig_thresh, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.07)
ax.plot(lim, lim, color="#9aa0a6", ls="--", lw=1.4, label="equal effect (45°)")
ax.scatter(orig[~failed], repl[~failed], s=22, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.7, label="replicated")
ax.scatter(orig[failed], repl[failed], s=22, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.7,
           label=f"failed to replicate ({frac_fail*100:.0f}%)")
ax.axhline(sig_thresh, color="#c0392b", lw=1.0, ls=":")
ax.text(lim[1]*0.6, sig_thresh*0.4, "replication not significant", color="#b03a2e", fontsize=8)
ax.set_xlabel("original published effect size")
ax.set_ylabel("replication effect size")
ax.set_title("Replications shrink toward the truth")
ax.set_xlim(lim); ax.set_ylim(lim)
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Data, code, and reproducible workflows

The practical machinery of openness: (Reinhart 2015; Bryman 2016)

  • Open data, code, and materials — post everything, so verification is reading the code, not archaeology. A study by Wicherts found that, despite a requirement to share, nearly three- quarters of authors refused when asked — and refusers were more likely to have made statistical errors. (Reinhart 2015) Reluctance to share is itself a signal.
  • Literate programming. Tools like Sweave and Quarto embed the analysis code inside the document, so every table and figure is regenerated from raw data — no copy-paste. (This encyclopedia is built that way: each figure is executable code, not a pasted image.) (Reinhart 2015)
  • Version control. Recording every change to the analysis means you can always reconstruct how a specific number was produced. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Permanent repositories — GenBank, Dryad, Figshare — give data and code citable, lasting homes. (Reinhart 2015)
WarningWhen the code is the bug

Openness catches errors that peer review never could. Biostatisticians Baggerly and Coombes spent roughly 2,000 hours trying to reproduce a Duke cancer-genomics study (Potti) and uncovered a mess — mislabelled data, duplicated samples, columns shifted by one — that invalidated research already guiding clinical trials. (Reinhart 2015) And Reinhart’s plain warning: manually copying data into spreadsheets is “tremendously error-prone,” and Excel’s regression is often numerically ill-behaved. A result no one can re-run is a result no one has truly checked. (Reinhart 2015)

The incentives, and the limits

Why isn’t all science already open? Because the incentives fight it: (Reinhart 2015)

  • The novelty premium. Careers reward novel, groundbreaking results in prestige journals; there is little glory in replicating others or cleaning code for public release. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Fear of being scooped. Researchers worry that sharing hard-won data lets competitors beat them to the next finding. (Reinhart 2015)

And the honest caveat: open science is necessary but not sufficient. Transparency cannot rescue a study that is underpowered or built on a flawed premise — it just lets everyone see that it is. (Reinhart 2015) It makes science checkable; it doesn’t make it correct.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Confusing reproducibility with replicability. Code that runs is not an effect that’s real; you need both. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Presenting exploratory findings as confirmatory. Without preregistration the distinction blurs and p-values lose their meaning; label which is which. (Reinhart 2015)

  • “Data available on request.” Wicherts shows requests are routinely refused; deposit data and code in a real repository. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Spreadsheet pipelines. Copy-paste and Excel formulas are error-prone and unauditable; use scripted, version-controlled, literate workflows. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Expecting openness to fix bad design. It reveals problems; it doesn’t cure underpowered or ill-conceived studies. (Reinhart 2015)

Appendix · why preregistration restores the p-value

The forking-paths inflation, formally. Suppose the data are consistent with K analysis paths, each a valid test of level \alpha, and the analyst (consciously or not) reports the most significant. The realised false-positive rate is \alpha_{\text{eff}} = \Pr\Big(\min_{k\le K} p_k < \alpha\Big) \approx 1-(1-\alpha)^{K} \gg \alpha, the same multiplicity math as explicit multiple comparisons — even when only one test is reported — because the choice of path was data-dependent. Preregistration fixes K=1 in advance, restoring \alpha_{\text{eff}}=\alpha. It is multiplicity control applied to the decisions you’d otherwise make implicitly.

Registered reports and selection. Let publication probability be \pi(\text{sig}). Ordinary publishing has \pi(\text{significant})\gg\pi(\text{null}), so the published effect distribution is the truth conditioned on significance — inflated (the winner’s curse). In-principle acceptance sets \pi independent of the result, so the published distribution is unbiased for the truth. Replication then samples that unbiased process and regresses estimates toward the real effect — the shrinkage in the figure.

  Project**, **Wicherts** (¾ refused to share; refusers more error-prone), and the **Duke/Potti** genomics
  debacle (Baggerly & Coombes, ~2,000 hours).
- **Bryman, *Social Research Methods*** — replication norms and open materials/data sharing as part of
  credible research practice.
:::

## Appendix · why preregistration restores the p-value {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The forking-paths inflation, formally.** Suppose the data are consistent with $K$ analysis paths, each
a valid test of level $\alpha$, and the analyst (consciously or not) reports the most significant. The
realised false-positive rate is
$$
\alpha_{\text{eff}} = \Pr\Big(\min_{k\le K} p_k < \alpha\Big) \approx 1-(1-\alpha)^{K} \gg \alpha,
$$
the same [multiplicity](questionable-research-practices.qmd) math as explicit multiple comparisons —
*even when only one test is reported* — because the *choice* of path was data-dependent.
**Preregistration fixes $K=1$ in advance**, restoring $\alpha_{\text{eff}}=\alpha$. It is multiplicity
control applied to the decisions you'd otherwise make implicitly.

**Registered reports and selection.** Let publication probability be $\pi(\text{sig})$. Ordinary
publishing has $\pi(\text{significant})\gg\pi(\text{null})$, so the published effect distribution is the
truth conditioned on significance — inflated (the [winner's curse](questionable-research-practices.qmd)).
In-principle acceptance sets $\pi$ **independent of the result**, so the published distribution is
unbiased for the truth. Replication then samples that unbiased process and regresses estimates toward
the real effect — the shrinkage in the figure.

:::
