The Tyranny of Metrics

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The moment you turn a measure into a target, people optimise the measure — and the thing it was meant to track quietly slips away.

Everything before this taught you to measure well. This entry is the warning that measuring is never neutral. The instant a number carries stakes — pay, promotion, ranking, funding — the people being measured become self-conscious actors who optimise the number, not the goal beneath it. Test scores rise while learning stagnates; survival rates climb because surgeons turn away the sick. The metric improves; the world it represented does not. Knowing when not to trust a number is the final skill of the quantitative researcher.

Why it matters

The whole manual has been a machine for producing trustworthy numbers. Muller’s The Tyranny of Metrics supplies the conscience: a number that drives behaviour changes the behaviour it measures, often perversely. (Muller 2018) This is not a niche caution — it is the central risk of applied quantitative work, where your estimate doesn’t sit in a journal but becomes a target someone is paid to hit. The researcher who can build a metric and foresee how it will be gamed is the one who can be trusted with a real decision.

Metric fixation

Muller names the disease metric fixation: “the seemingly irresistible pressure to measure performance, to publicize it, and to reward it.” (Muller 2018) It rests on three beliefs: (Muller 2018)

  1. Replace judgment with numbers — that standardised, comparable metrics can and should substitute for the judgment of experienced practitioners.
  2. Accountability = transparency — that making the numbers public guarantees institutions serve their purpose.
  3. Motivate by extrinsic reward — that people work best when pay and penalties are tied to measured performance (pay-for-performance, league tables).

Each belief is plausible; together they license replacing professional judgment with a dashboard. The rest of the entry is what goes wrong when you do.

Goodhart’s law and Campbell’s law

Two “laws” name the mechanism: (Muller 2018)

  • Goodhart’s law — “when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.”
  • Campbell’s law — “the more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor.”

The engine under both: people are self-conscious actors. Attach stakes to a measurement and they respond by skewing the data to maximise the score, whether or not the underlying reality improves. (Muller 2018) A measure passively observed and the same measure optimised against are different objects — the act of targeting corrupts the signal. The figure shows the signature: a proxy that faithfully tracks the goal until the day it becomes a target, then peels away.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
t = np.arange(0, 24)
target_start = 12
# Before targeting, proxy and true goal move together; after, proxy is gamed up, goal drifts down.
true_goal = 50 + 1.2 * t + rng.normal(0, 1.0, t.size)
true_goal[target_start:] = true_goal[target_start-1] - 0.8 * (t[target_start:] - t[target_start-1]) \
                           + rng.normal(0, 1.0, t.size - target_start)
proxy = true_goal.copy()
proxy[target_start:] = true_goal[target_start-1] + 3.2 * (t[target_start:] - t[target_start-1]) \
                       + rng.normal(0, 1.0, t.size - target_start)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.plot(t, proxy, "o-", color="#c0392b", lw=2.2, ms=3, label="measured proxy (the metric)")
ax.plot(t, true_goal, "o-", color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.2, ms=3, label="true goal (what we care about)")
ax.axvline(target_start - 0.5, color="#9aa0a6", ls="--", lw=1.4)
ax.text(target_start - 0.3, ax.get_ylim()[1]*0.96, "metric becomes a target", fontsize=8, color="#555")
ax.fill_between(t[target_start:], true_goal[target_start:], proxy[target_start:],
                color="#c0392b", alpha=0.10)
ax.text(18, 70, "the gap =\nGoodhart corruption", fontsize=8.5, color="#b03a2e", ha="center")
ax.set_xlabel("time"); ax.set_ylabel("performance")
ax.set_title("A measure tracks the goal — until it becomes a target")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

How metrics get gamed

When judgement is reduced to a narrow score, practitioners “juke the stats.” Muller catalogues the recurring tactics: (Muller 2018)

  • Teaching to the test. Effort shifts from the institution’s true purpose to whatever lifts the metric — narrowing a curriculum to the tested subjects and test-taking strategy.
  • Creaming / cherry-picking. Avoid the hard cases that might dent your success rate. Cardiac surgeons facing public scorecards refer the sickest patients elsewhere — improving their mortality numbers by not treating the people who most need treatment.
  • Lowering standards. Hit the target by making it easier — inflating graduation rates by making courses easier to pass.
  • Outright gaming and fraud. Misclassify or falsify: police downgrading felonies to misdemeanours (or thefts to “lost property”) so they don’t count against headline crime.
  • Short-termism. Sacrifice long-term investment (R&D, training) for an immediate numerical bump.
  • Goal displacement. The deepest pathology — the metric means replace the organisational ends they were meant to serve. Hitting the number becomes the mission.
NoteMuller’s case files
  • Education (No Child Left Behind). High-stakes testing narrowed curricula to math and English and, in several cities, produced systematic cheating — teachers altering answer sheets. (Muller 2018)
  • Healthcare (surgical scorecards). Public mortality rankings drove case-selection bias: surgeons declined high-risk patients to protect their stats. (Muller 2018)
  • Policing (Compstat / FBI reports). Pressure to “reduce crime” led to reclassifying offences downward and chasing easy street arrests over hard long-term cases. (Muller 2018)
  • The military (Vietnam body counts). McNamara’s reliance on body counts as a success metric produced fabricated numbers — and soldiers risking their lives to find corpses to hit the target. (Muller 2018)
  • Universities (rankings). League tables push schools toward glossy marketing and revenue-maximising programs over their educational mission. (Muller 2018)

Surrogate outcomes: measuring the easy thing

A quieter failure: metric fixation pushes you to measure what is easy to count, not what matters. Organisations track inputs (money spent) or processes (workshops held) because the real outcomes are complex or delayed. (Muller 2018) Muller’s barb is worth memorising: “the ease of measuring may be inversely proportional to the significance of what is measured.” (Muller 2018) This is the field cousin of the surrogate-outcome and proxy-variable problems — only here the substitution is driven by managerial convenience, not statistical necessity.

The costs

Metric fixation is not free, even when it isn’t gamed: (Muller 2018)

  • A data arms race. Effort migrates from frontline work to documenting it — more managers and analysts, producers spending their hours on reports instead of the job.
  • Discouraged innovation. Real innovation needs experiments that might fail; metrics punish failure, so people retreat to safe, established, measurable goals.
  • Demoralisation. Professionals resent the loss of autonomy and a scoreboard that conflicts with their vocational ethos — driving the most creative people out.

The balanced verdict

Muller is not anti-measurement — he is anti-fixation. (Muller 2018) Metrics are a valuable complement to judgment, never a replacement for it. They work best when they are: (Muller 2018)

  • Internal and diagnostic — used by practitioners themselves to spot outliers and refine practice, not as a public cudgel.
  • Low-stakes — not the sole basis of pay or promotion, so the incentive to game is weak.
  • Bottom-up — designed with the people measured, who then trust the metric’s validity.

The mature stance the whole book has built toward: a number is an input to judgment, not a substitute for it. The skill is holding both — rigorous measurement and the wisdom to know its limits.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Attaching high stakes to a single number. Goodhart guarantees it will be gamed; the tighter the incentive, the faster the corruption. (Muller 2018)

  • Measuring the proxy and forgetting the goal. Optimise a surrogate and you can improve it while the real outcome decays — verify the proxy still tracks the thing you care about. (Muller 2018)

  • Counting inputs/processes as if they were outcomes. Workshops held is not learning gained; spend follows ease of measurement, not importance. (Muller 2018)

  • Ignoring gaming when designing a metric. If you can’t say how people will game it, you haven’t finished designing it. (Muller 2018)

  • Replacing professional judgment wholesale. Metrics inform judgment; they don’t substitute for the experience that knows which cases the number misreads. (Muller 2018)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Muller, The Tyranny of Metrics — metric fixation (replace judgment / accountability-as- transparency / extrinsic reward); Goodhart’s and Campbell’s laws and the self-conscious-actor mechanism; the gaming taxonomy (teaching to the test, creaming, lowering standards, fraud, short-termism, goal displacement); surrogate/proxy outcomes and “ease of measuring inversely proportional to significance”; the costs (data arms race, discouraged innovation, demoralisation); the balanced verdict (metrics as complement — internal, low-stakes, bottom-up); and the case files: NCLB, surgical scorecards, Compstat policing, Vietnam body counts, university rankings.

Appendix · the mechanism

Goodhart formalised. Let the true goal be G and a proxy M=G+\eta with measurement slack \eta an agent can influence at cost. Passively, \eta\approx0 and \operatorname{Corr}(M,G)\approx1 — the proxy is informative. Introduce a reward R(M) for high M. A rational agent now invests effort to raise \eta (gaming) up to where marginal reward equals marginal cost, independently of G. The observed M rises, but \operatorname{Corr}(M,G) collapses — the very act of using M for control destroys its correlation with G. The proxy was a good measure because no one was optimising it; the optimisation is what breaks it.

Why surrogates drift. A surrogate S validated to satisfy \mathbb E[G\mid S]=f(S) under observational conditions need not preserve that relation under intervention on S — exactly the \operatorname{do} vs conditioning distinction. Optimising S intervenes on it, and the conditional relation that licensed S as a stand-in can fail — the managerial twin of reward hacking.

  finished designing it. [@muller_tyranny]

- **Replacing professional judgment wholesale.** Metrics inform judgment; they don't substitute for the
  experience that knows which cases the number misreads. [@muller_tyranny]

:::

## Appendix · the mechanism {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Goodhart formalised.** Let the true goal be $G$ and a proxy $M=G+\eta$ with measurement slack $\eta$
an agent can influence at cost. *Passively*, $\eta\approx0$ and $\operatorname{Corr}(M,G)\approx1$ — the
proxy is informative. Introduce a reward $R(M)$ for high $M$. A rational agent now invests effort to
raise $\eta$ (gaming) up to where marginal reward equals marginal cost, *independently of* $G$. The
observed $M$ rises, but $\operatorname{Corr}(M,G)$ **collapses** — the very act of using $M$ for control
destroys its correlation with $G$. The proxy was a good measure *because* no one was optimising it; the
optimisation is what breaks it.

**Why surrogates drift.** A surrogate $S$ validated to satisfy $\mathbb E[G\mid S]=f(S)$ under
*observational* conditions need not preserve that relation under *intervention* on $S$ — exactly the
[$\operatorname{do}$ vs conditioning](../05-causal-inference/directed-acyclic-graphs.qmd) distinction.
Optimising $S$ intervenes on it, and the conditional relation that licensed $S$ as a stand-in can fail —
the managerial twin of [reward hacking](../04-predictive/interpretability-and-ml.qmd).

:::
