Meta-Analysis & Research Synthesis

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

One study is mostly noise. The literature is signal — but only if you combine it honestly. Meta-analysis is the statistics of pooling studies, and the discipline that exposes when the literature itself is biased.

No single study settles a question — each carries its own sampling error and quirks. Meta-analysis statistically combines the effect sizes of many studies into one more precise, higher-powered pooled estimate, and surfaces patterns invisible in any one sample. Done as part of a systematic review, it is the most rigorous form of literature synthesis — but it inherits the sins of its inputs, so it doubles as a diagnostic for publication bias and the field’s collective bad habits.

Why it matters

A lone “significant” finding might be a fluke; a lone null might be underpowered. The truth lives in the accumulation — and turning a scattered literature into a single defensible number (with honest uncertainty) is exactly what policy and theory need. Meta-analysis is also where the replication crisis becomes measurable: a biased literature leaves a fingerprint you can detect.

Systematic review vs meta-analysis

Two stages, often confused (Bryman 2016):

  • Systematic review — a “replicable, scientific, and transparent process” that minimises researcher bias through exhaustive searches of published and unpublished work, with an explicit audit trail of inclusion criteria. The opposite of a narrative review, which can be “random and less organized” (Bryman 2016).
  • Meta-analysis — the quantitative component: statistically combining the effect sizes of multiple independent studies on the same question (Bryman 2016). By pooling, it “corrects the various sampling and non-sampling errors that arise in a single study,” yielding a more precise estimate (Bryman 2016).

The common currency: effect sizes

Studies use different scales, so results are converted to a standardised effect size — the common currency (Bryman 2016):

  • Standardised mean difference (Cohen’s d) — the effect divided by a standard deviation.
  • Odds ratios (binary outcomes) and correlation r (associations).

Standardising lets studies be treated as independent draws from a common population of effects, enabling direct statistical comparison (Bryman 2016).

Fixed-effect vs random-effects

The central modelling choice is what you assume about the true effect (Bryman 2016):

  • Fixed-effect — assumes one true effect underlies every study; the only variation is sampling error. Studies are combined by inverse-variance (precision) weighting, giving larger, more precise studies more influence (Bryman 2016).
  • Random-effects — allows the true effect to vary across studies (different settings, populations, “doses”). It adds a between-study variance component \tau^2 on top of within-study error, producing wider confidence intervals that reflect the diversity of effects (Bryman 2016).
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
K = 8
mu_true = 0.45
SE = np.sort(rng.uniform(0.07, 0.34, K))     # study standard errors (precision varies)
d = mu_true + rng.normal(0, SE)              # observed study effects
w = 1.0 / SE**2                              # inverse-variance weights
pooled = np.sum(w*d) / np.sum(w)
pooled_se = np.sqrt(1.0 / np.sum(w))

y = np.arange(K, 0, -1)
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.4, 4.6))
for i in range(K):
    ax.plot([d[i]-1.96*SE[i], d[i]+1.96*SE[i]], [y[i], y[i]], color="#161613", lw=1.4, zorder=1)
    ax.scatter(d[i], y[i], s=30 + 320*w[i]/w.max(), color="#161613", marker="s", zorder=2)
ax.axvline(0.0, color="#999", ls=":", lw=1.0)
ax.axvline(pooled, color="#e8531d", ls="--", lw=1.0, alpha=0.6)

# pooled diamond at y = 0
cw = 1.96 * pooled_se
ax.fill([pooled-cw, pooled, pooled+cw, pooled], [0, 0.45, 0, -0.45], color="#e8531d", zorder=3)
ax.text(pooled+cw+0.02, 0, f"pooled = {pooled:.2f}", va="center", color="#e8531d",
        fontsize=9, fontweight="bold")

ax.set_yticks(list(y) + [0])
ax.set_yticklabels([f"Study {i+1}  (n∝{w[i]/w.min():.0f})" for i in range(K)] + ["Pooled"], fontsize=8)
ax.set_ylim(-1, K+1); ax.set_xlabel("effect size (Cohen's d)")
ax.set_title("Forest plot: many noisy studies → one precise pooled estimate")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Heterogeneity: are the studies even measuring the same thing?

Before trusting a pooled number, ask how much the studies disagree beyond chance (Bryman 2016):

  • Cochran’s Q — tests the null that all studies share one common effect; a significant Q signals real heterogeneity.
  • I^2 — the proportion of total variation due to real between-study differences rather than sampling error. I^2 > 50\% is high: the effect likely depends on context (region, age, dose), and a single pooled number may mislead (Bryman 2016).

High heterogeneity is a cue to use random-effects, and better, to investigate the moderators driving the differences (meta-regression).

The funnel plot and publication bias

Meta-analysis’s second gift is detecting when the literature is skewed. The funnel plot graphs each study’s effect size against its sample size / precision (Reinhart 2015). In an unbiased field it is a symmetric inverted funnel — small studies scatter widely at the bottom, large ones cluster tightly at the top, all centred on the true effect.

Asymmetry betrays the file-drawer problem: “boring” or null results are never published, while flukes get into print (Reinhart 2015). That buries small studies with null results, gouging a gap in the funnel’s bottom corner — visible asymmetry that Egger’s test can detect statistically (Reinhart 2015). A meta-analysis that ignores its funnel can certify an inflated effect built from a censored literature.

Garbage in, precisely-estimated garbage out

The decisive caution: meta-analysis is no cure for bad inputs (Reinhart 2015; Gerber and Green 2012). If the underlying studies suffer p-hacking, truth inflation, or double-dipping, pooling them aggregates and amplifies those errors (Reinhart 2015). A meta-analysis of flawed studies yields a “precisely estimated” error — the false confidence of a tight interval around the wrong number (Gerber and Green 2012). Synthesis raises precision, not validity.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Skipping the systematic search. Cherry-picked studies make a biased pool; the review must be exhaustive and pre-registered. (Bryman 2016)
  • Pooling apples and oranges. High I^2 means the studies aren’t estimating one effect — don’t report a single number without investigating heterogeneity. (Bryman 2016)
  • Ignoring publication bias. Always inspect the funnel plot / Egger’s test; a tidy pooled estimate from a censored literature is inflated. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Treating precision as validity. A narrow pooled CI around biased studies is precisely wrong. (Gerber and Green 2012)
  • Fixed-effect by default. If effects plausibly vary across contexts, use random-effects and report \tau^2. (Bryman 2016)

  • Reinhart, Statistics Done Wrong — the funnel plot, the file-drawer problem / publication bias, Egger’s test, and how meta-analysis amplifies p-hacking and bias in the source studies.

Appendix · the mathematics

Inverse-variance weighting. With study effects d_i and variances v_i=\text{SE}_i^2, the fixed-effect pooled estimate and its variance are

\hat\mu = \frac{\sum_i w_i d_i}{\sum_i w_i},\quad w_i=\frac1{v_i}, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(\hat\mu)=\frac1{\sum_i w_i}.

Weighting by precision is the minimum-variance linear combination — the meta-analytic echo of GLS. The code computes exactly this.

Heterogeneity and \tau^2. Cochran’s Q=\sum_i w_i(d_i-\hat\mu)^2 is \chi^2_{K-1} under homogeneity. Then I^2 = \max\!\big(0,\ (Q-(K-1))/Q\big) — the share of variance beyond chance. The DerSimonian–Laird random-effects estimator sets \hat\tau^2=\max\!\big(0,\,(Q-(K-1))/C\big) with C=\sum w_i-\sum w_i^2/\sum w_i, and re-weights with w_i^*=1/(v_i+\hat\tau^2) — adding the between-study variance to every study’s, which shrinks the weight gap between large and small studies and widens the pooled interval.

Egger’s test. Regress the standardised effect d_i/\text{SE}_i on precision 1/\text{SE}_i; a nonzero intercept indicates funnel asymmetry — small studies systematically reporting larger effects, the signature of publication bias.

**Inverse-variance weighting.** With study effects $d_i$ and variances $v_i=\text{SE}_i^2$, the fixed-effect

[GLS](../03-regression/heteroskedasticity-and-gls.qmd). The code computes exactly this.

**Heterogeneity and $\tau^2$.** Cochran's $Q=\sum_i w_i(d_i-\hat\mu)^2$ is $\chi^2_{K-1}$ under homogeneity.
Then $I^2 = \max\!\big(0,\ (Q-(K-1))/Q\big)$ — the share of variance beyond chance. The **DerSimonian–Laird**
random-effects estimator sets $\hat\tau^2=\max\!\big(0,\,(Q-(K-1))/C\big)$ with
$C=\sum w_i-\sum w_i^2/\sum w_i$, and re-weights with $w_i^*=1/(v_i+\hat\tau^2)$ — adding the between-study
variance to every study's, which **shrinks the weight gap** between large and small studies and **widens** the
pooled interval.

**Egger's test.** Regress the standardised effect $d_i/\text{SE}_i$ on precision $1/\text{SE}_i$; a nonzero
**intercept** indicates funnel asymmetry — small studies systematically reporting larger effects, the
signature of publication bias.
