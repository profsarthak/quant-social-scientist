Research Integrity & Data Ethics

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Ethics beyond the consent form: not fabricating, not hiding who paid, earning your authorship — and, in the age of harvested data and deployed models, not doing harm at scale.

The Overture’s ethics covered protecting subjects — consent, the IRB, do-no-harm. This entry covers protecting the science and the public: don’t fabricate or falsify, disclose who funded you, claim only the authorship you earned — and, now that data is scraped from billions of people and models are deployed on millions, honour privacy and check for the biases your model will otherwise amplify. Integrity is not a compliance form; it is the precondition for the whole enterprise being worth anything.

Why it matters

Every technical safeguard in this Part — preregistration, honest reporting — assumes a researcher acting in good faith. This entry is about the good faith itself, and its institutional pressures. (Reinhart 2015; Bryman 2016) It matters because the failures here are not subtle statistical slips but betrayals of trust — faked data that kills clinical trials, hidden funding that warps public health, a deployed model that quietly discriminates. The quantitative researcher wields real power; integrity is what makes that power legitimate.

Scientific misconduct: FFP

The gravest breaches are FFP — Fabrication (making up data), Falsification (manipulating data or materials), and Plagiarism (appropriating others’ work). (Reinhart 2015) These are not honest QRPs; they are fraud.

NoteThe Anil Potti case

A Duke researcher whose cancer-genomics work promised personalised treatment — until it emerged that Potti had falsified both his data and his résumé. (Reinhart 2015) The fallout: papers retracted, clinical trials already under way cancelled, and a company built on the technology closed. Uncovering it took biostatisticians Baggerly and Coombes an estimated 2,000 hours of forensic reconstruction — possible only because they fought for the data and code. (Reinhart 2015) The case is why open science is an integrity tool, not just a convenience: fabrication hides where no one can re-run the analysis.

Conflicts of interest and funding bias

Money bends findings — sometimes subtly, sometimes by contract — so disclosure of funding and conflicts is a baseline duty. (Bryman 2016) The corpus’s cases: (Bryman 2016; Reinhart 2015)

  • Food industry. Agreements between researchers and Coca-Cola were found to let the company review and comment on research before publication and included early-termination clauses that could kill a study producing unfavourable results — pre-loading the published record toward favourable findings. (Bryman 2016)
  • Pharma. The Reboxetine case: published trials suggested Pfizer’s antidepressant worked, while three times as much unpublished data showed it didn’t — selective publication as a tool of funding bias. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Tobacco (and a sobering aside that statistics itself grew up entangled with the eugenics movement) — a long history of industry shaping which questions get asked and which answers get out. (Reinhart 2015)

The figure shows the aggregate footprint: when a funder controls design, review, and what gets published, the literature tilts even if no single study is fraudulent.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(4)
n = 600
# True effect is mildly unfavourable to the funder (centered slightly negative).
truth = -0.1
independent = rng.normal(truth, 1.0, n)
# Industry: favourable-design shift + selective publication (drop the most unfavourable).
industry_raw = rng.normal(truth + 0.6, 1.0, n)
industry = industry_raw[industry_raw > np.quantile(industry_raw, 0.35)]  # file-drawer the bad ones

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.0))
bins = np.linspace(-4, 4, 40)
ax.hist(independent, bins=bins, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.7, density=True, label="independently funded")
ax.hist(industry, bins=bins, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.55, density=True, label="industry funded (published)")
ax.axvline(truth, color="#1f8a4c", ls="--", lw=1.8)
ax.text(truth - 0.1, ax.get_ylim()[1]*0.92, "truth", color="#1f8a4c", fontsize=9, ha="right")
ax.axvline(np.mean(industry), color="#c0392b", ls=":", lw=1.4)
ax.set_xlabel("finding:  unfavourable  ←→  favourable to funder")
ax.set_ylabel("density"); ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_title("Who pays can tilt what gets published")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Perverse incentives and authorship

Misconduct doesn’t happen in a vacuum — it grows in an incentive structure. The “frenzied quest” for publications, where tenure, grants, and prestige hang on a long list of top-journal papers, creates relentless pressure to cut corners or torture the data into significance. (Reinhart 2015) The same novelty premium that starves replication rewards the splashy and the fast.

Authorship is where credit and responsibility meet, and it has its own ethics: (Bryman 2016)

  • Authorship should reflect a substantial contribution. A statistician who shapes the analysis is a research partner deserving co-authorship, not unpaid technical support.
  • Honorary authorship (a senior name added for clout, having done little) and ghost authorship (the real authors omitted — common in industry-funded trials, to hide the sponsor’s hand) are both violations. (Bryman 2016)
  • The fear of being scooped pushes researchers to hoard data until they’ve wrung every paper from it — in tension with the openness science needs.

Data ethics in the age of harvested data

Classic research ethics assumed you collected the data from consenting subjects. Big data breaks that assumption: much research now uses harvested data — social media, administrative records — generated with no research in mind. (Bryman 2016) New duties follow: (Bryman 2016)

  • The right to erasure. If someone deletes a post, immortalising it in a paper bypasses their right to withdraw and can expose them to real harm. Harvested ≠ free to use forever.
  • Consent friction. Users technically “agree” via Terms of Service they never read and don’t understand — a thin substitute for informed consent.
  • Anonymisation and re-identification. Truly anonymising data is hard and gets harder as outside datasets grow (re-identification risk rises with time); proper scrubbing causes real delays — up to 20 months for some Facebook data. (Bryman 2016) “It’s public” is not an ethics argument.

Algorithmic fairness before deployment

The book’s causal and predictive ML tools don’t sit in a notebook — they get deployed, and a model is only as fair as the data that trained it. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) Models learn the historical biases in their training data — the documented gender and racial bias in word embeddings is the textbook case — and then amplify them at scale. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) Acting on a biased model in a clinical or policy setting produces systemic injustice, efficiently. The obligation is concrete: fairness checks before deployment — verify the model performs (and estimates effects) accurately across subgroups, not just on average. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) A model that is right on average and wrong for a protected group is not “mostly right”; it is discriminatory. This is the tyranny of metrics with the stakes of real lives — and the reason interpretability and fairness auditing are ethical, not optional.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Treating QRPs and fraud as a spectrum you can drift along. Honest flexibility and FFP differ in kind; never falsify, full stop. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Non-disclosure of funding/conflicts. Even good-faith work needs the funding source on the table; contracts that gate publication are a red flag. (Bryman 2016)

  • Honorary or ghost authorship. Credit must track contribution; hidden authors hide hidden interests. (Bryman 2016)

  • “It’s public data, so consent isn’t needed.” Harvested data carries privacy duties — the right to erasure, re-identification risk, and meaningful (not ToS) consent. (Bryman 2016)

  • Deploying a model without a fairness audit. Average accuracy can mask severe subgroup harm; check performance across protected groups before go-live. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024)

Appendix · two formal notes

Funding bias as selection. Let a study’s finding be \theta=\theta_0+\varepsilon around a truth \theta_0. A sponsor’s influence acts as (i) a design shift \theta_0\to\theta_0+\delta (favourable framing) and (ii) selective publication \Pr(\text{publish})=\pi(\theta) increasing in favourability. The published mean is \mathbb E[\theta\mid\text{published}] = \theta_0+\delta+\operatorname{Cov}(\theta,\pi)/\mathbb E[\pi] > \theta_0 — biased upward even if every individual study is internally valid. The distortion lives in the selection, which is why registered reports (publication independent of result) are the structural fix.

Fairness across subgroups. A model can satisfy small overall error while badly failing a group g: \mathbb E[L]=\sum_g \Pr(g)\,\mathbb E[L\mid g] is dominated by large groups, so a small \Pr(g) lets \mathbb E[L\mid g] be large undetected. Fairness auditing reports the per-group losses (or per-group treatment-effect accuracy) rather than the aggregate — the same lesson as the rare-class accuracy paradox, now an ethical mandate.

## Appendix · two formal notes {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Funding bias as selection.** Let a study's finding be $\theta=\theta_0+\varepsilon$ around a truth
$\theta_0$. A sponsor's influence acts as (i) a **design shift** $\theta_0\to\theta_0+\delta$ (favourable
framing) and (ii) **selective publication** $\Pr(\text{publish})=\pi(\theta)$ increasing in
favourability. The published mean is
$\mathbb E[\theta\mid\text{published}] = \theta_0+\delta+\operatorname{Cov}(\theta,\pi)/\mathbb E[\pi] > \theta_0$
— biased upward even if every individual study is internally valid. The distortion lives in the
*selection*, which is why [registered reports](open-and-reproducible-science.qmd) (publication
independent of result) are the structural fix.

**Fairness across subgroups.** A model can satisfy small *overall* error while badly failing a group
$g$: $\mathbb E[L]=\sum_g \Pr(g)\,\mathbb E[L\mid g]$ is dominated by large groups, so a small
$\Pr(g)$ lets $\mathbb E[L\mid g]$ be large undetected. Fairness auditing reports the **per-group**
losses (or per-group treatment-effect accuracy) rather than the aggregate — the same lesson as the
[rare-class accuracy paradox](../04-predictive/classification-and-knn.qmd), now an ethical mandate.

:::
