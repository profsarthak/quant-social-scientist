Transformations & Sums of Random Variables

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

What happens to chance when you log it, add it up, or feed it to a computer — three moves you make constantly.

You rarely study a random variable raw. You log skewed income, you add up item scores into a total, you ask a computer to simulate a distribution. Each move transforms one random variable into another, and each follows a precise rule. Learn the three — change of variables, convolution, and the probability integral transform — and you can track chance through almost any manipulation you will perform.

Why it matters

Three everyday operations are all secretly the same mathematical question — if I know the distribution of X, what is the distribution of the thing I built from it?

  • You transform a variable: take logs of income, square a deviation, standardise a score.
  • You sum variables: total a survey battery, aggregate daily sales, pool independent trials.
  • You simulate: every Monte Carlo draw a computer makes rests on one transformation theorem.

This entry supplies the rules. It also closes a loop left open in the random-variables entry (how a function of X gets its distribution) and lays the mechanical groundwork beneath the Central Limit Theorem: the CLT is, after all, a statement about what happens when you keep summing.

Transforming a single variable: change of variables

Suppose Y = g(X) for a strictly monotonic g, and you know X’s density f_X. The density of Y follows the change-of-variables formula (Ross 2018):

f_Y(y) = f_X\!\big(g^{-1}(y)\big)\,\left|\frac{d}{dy}\,g^{-1}(y)\right|.

The first factor relocates the probability; the second — the derivative (Jacobian) — rescales it.

TipWhy the derivative factor must be there

A density is probability per unit length, so it must respond to how the transformation stretches or compresses the axis. If g spreads X’s values over a wider range of Y, the same total probability is smeared over more room, so the density must be thinner there; if g compresses, the density piles up thicker. The derivative is exactly that stretch factor, and it is what keeps the total area equal to 1. (Ross 2018)

NoteRoss’s worked example: Y = Xⁿ

Let X be uniform on (0,1) and Y = X^n. Work through the cdf (always the safe route): F_Y(y) = P\{X^n \le y\} = P\{X \le y^{1/n}\} = y^{1/n}, \qquad 0 \le y \le 1, since X is uniform. Differentiate to get the density: f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{n}\,y^{\frac1n - 1}, \qquad 0 \le y \le 1. A flat distribution, cubed, becomes one piled up near zero — the transformation reshaped the chances. (Ross 2018)

The log-normal: taming the tail

The most important transformation in social science is the logarithm, and the distribution it produces has a name. A positive variable Y is log-normal if \log Y is Normal. (Ross 2018) This is the workhorse model for income, wealth, and prices — quantities that are strictly positive and heavily right-skewed, with a long tail of the very rich. (Ross 2018) The log pulls in those extreme values, compressing the tail until the distribution becomes the symmetric bell that so many methods assume. (Ross 2018)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
# log-income ~ Normal(mean 10.5, sd 0.6); income = exp(that) -> log-normal
log_income = rng.normal(10.5, 0.6, 20000)
income = np.exp(log_income)

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 3.5))
axL.hist(income, bins=80, range=(0, 200000), color="#9a6a2f", alpha=0.85)
axL.set_title("Income (log-normal): a long right tail")
axL.set_xlabel("income ($)"); axL.set_yticks([])
axR.hist(log_income, bins=60, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85)
axR.set_title("log(income): a symmetric bell")
axR.set_xlabel("log income"); axR.set_yticks([])
fig.suptitle("Why social scientists log skewed positive quantities", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

This is why “log income,” “log wage,” and “log GDP” are everywhere in empirical work: the transformation buys symmetry, stabilises variance, and lets the Normal-based toolkit apply to data that, raw, would violate its assumptions.

The probability integral transform: the engine of simulation

A small theorem with enormous reach. If X has a continuous cdf F, then the transformed variable

U = F(X) \;\sim\; \text{Uniform}(0,1).

Feeding any continuous variable through its own cdf flattens it to a uniform. (Ross 2018) Run it backward and you get the trick that powers all of computational statistics — inverse-transform sampling: if U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1), then X = F^{-1}(U) has distribution F. (Ross 2018)

TipHow a computer draws from any distribution

This is the foundation of Monte Carlo simulation (and of the MCMC you met in the Bayesian sub-chapter). To sample from a Normal, an Exponential, anything: the machine generates a plain uniform random number between 0 and 1, then passes it through the inverse cdf of the target distribution. Every simulated dataset in this book was born from a uniform draw and a F^{-1}. (Ross 2018)

Summing independent variables: convolution

When you add two independent variables, Z = X + Y, the distribution of the sum is their convolution — sum (or integrate) over every way the parts can combine to the total. (Ross 2018)

P\{Z=z\} = \sum_k P\{X=k\}\,P\{Y=z-k\} \quad\text{(discrete)}, \qquad f_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(z-y)\,f_Y(y)\,dy \quad\text{(continuous)}.

The intuition is direct: to land on a total z, X can take any value k provided Y supplies the remainder z-k; sum the probabilities of all such pairs. (Ross 2018)

Reproductive results: families that stay in the family

Convolution is laborious in general, but several distributions are closed under addition — the sum of independents stays in the same family, with parameters that simply combine. (Ross 2018) These are worth memorising; they save constant work.

Sum of independent… …is distributed as Parameters combine by
Normals \mathcal N(\mu_i,\sigma_i^2) Normal means add, variances add: \mathcal N(\sum\mu_i, \sum\sigma_i^2)
Poissons \text{Pois}(\lambda_i) Poisson rates add: \text{Pois}(\sum\lambda_i)
Exponentials \text{Exp}(\lambda) (n of them) Gamma / Erlang \text{Gamma}(n,\lambda)
Binomials \text{Bin}(n_i,p) (same p) Binomial trials add: \text{Bin}(\sum n_i, p)

The Normal case is the quiet hero behind the standard error: averaging Normal data keeps it Normal, with variance \sigma^2/n. The exponential-to-Gamma result is the bridge to waiting-time and survival models. (Ross 2018)

The punchline: sums become Normal

The closure results above are special — they need a specific starting family. The Central Limit Theorem is the miracle that needs none: add up enough independent pieces from almost any distribution and the sum is approximately Normal, the original shape — skew, gaps, lumps — dissolving into the bell as more terms accumulate. (Ross 2018) Convolution is the machinery; the CLT is what that machinery converges to. This is the deep reason aggregate social quantities — totals, averages, indices built from many small contributions — are so reliably bell-shaped, and why the Normal-based methods of Part II work as well as they do.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Forgetting the Jacobian. Transforming a density without the derivative factor breaks the total-probability-equals-1 rule and gives a non-distribution. The stretch factor is not optional. (Ross 2018)

  • Back-transforming a mean naïvely. Because E[g(X)] \ne g(E[X]) (Jensen), the mean of log-income is not the log of mean income. Exponentiating a log-scale average understates the arithmetic mean — a routine error in reporting.

  • Convolving dependent variables. The convolution formula and the “variances add” rule both require independence. With correlated terms a covariance appears (variance of a sum).

  • Assuming every sum is Normal at small n. The CLT is asymptotic; for few terms or heavily skewed pieces the sum can still be far from Normal. Closure results are exact, the CLT is approximate — don’t confuse them. (Ross 2018)

Appendix · the mathematics

Change of variables, derived. For strictly increasing g, the cdf of Y=g(X) is F_Y(y)=P\{g(X)\le y\}=P\{X\le g^{-1}(y)\}=F_X(g^{-1}(y)). Differentiate with the chain rule: f_Y(y)=f_X(g^{-1}(y))\cdot \frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y). For decreasing g the inequality flips and the derivative is negative, so the absolute value appears, giving the general formula. The Y=X^n case: g^{-1}(y)=y^{1/n}, \frac{d}{dy}y^{1/n}=\frac1n y^{1/n-1}, and with f_X\equiv1 on (0,1) this is f_Y(y)=\tfrac1n y^{1/n-1}.

Probability integral transform. For continuous strictly-increasing F, P\{U\le u\}=P\{F(X)\le u\}=P\{X\le F^{-1}(u)\}=F(F^{-1}(u))=u for u\in(0,1) — the cdf of a Uniform(0,1). Hence U=F(X) is uniform, and inverting, F^{-1}(U)\sim F.

Convolution, derived (continuous). F_Z(z)=P\{X+Y\le z\}=\iint_{x+y\le z} f_X(x)f_Y(y)\,dx\,dy. Fix y and integrate x up to z-y: F_Z(z)=\int f_Y(y)F_X(z-y)\,dy; differentiate in z to get f_Z(z)=\int f_X(z-y)f_Y(y)\,dy.

Sum of independent Normals. Quickest via moment generating functions: M_{X+Y}(t)=M_X(t)M_Y(t) for independents, and \exp(\mu_X t+\tfrac12\sigma_X^2 t^2)\exp(\mu_Y t+\tfrac12\sigma_Y^2 t^2) =\exp((\mu_X+\mu_Y)t+\tfrac12(\sigma_X^2+\sigma_Y^2)t^2) — the MGF of \mathcal N(\mu_X+\mu_Y, \sigma_X^2+\sigma_Y^2). The same product-of-MGFs trick proves the Poisson and binomial closure results.

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Change of variables, derived.** For strictly increasing $g$, the cdf of $Y=g(X)$ is
$F_Y(y)=P\{g(X)\le y\}=P\{X\le g^{-1}(y)\}=F_X(g^{-1}(y))$. Differentiate with the chain rule:
$f_Y(y)=f_X(g^{-1}(y))\cdot \frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)$. For decreasing $g$ the inequality flips and the
derivative is negative, so the absolute value appears, giving the general formula. The $Y=X^n$ case:
$g^{-1}(y)=y^{1/n}$, $\frac{d}{dy}y^{1/n}=\frac1n y^{1/n-1}$, and with $f_X\equiv1$ on $(0,1)$ this is
$f_Y(y)=\tfrac1n y^{1/n-1}$.

**Probability integral transform.** For continuous strictly-increasing $F$,
$P\{U\le u\}=P\{F(X)\le u\}=P\{X\le F^{-1}(u)\}=F(F^{-1}(u))=u$ for $u\in(0,1)$ — the cdf of a
Uniform$(0,1)$. Hence $U=F(X)$ is uniform, and inverting, $F^{-1}(U)\sim F$.

**Convolution, derived (continuous).** $F_Z(z)=P\{X+Y\le z\}=\iint_{x+y\le z} f_X(x)f_Y(y)\,dx\,dy$.
Fix $y$ and integrate $x$ up to $z-y$: $F_Z(z)=\int f_Y(y)F_X(z-y)\,dy$; differentiate in $z$ to get
$f_Z(z)=\int f_X(z-y)f_Y(y)\,dy$.

**Sum of independent Normals.** Quickest via moment generating functions: $M_{X+Y}(t)=M_X(t)M_Y(t)$
for independents, and $\exp(\mu_X t+\tfrac12\sigma_X^2 t^2)\exp(\mu_Y t+\tfrac12\sigma_Y^2 t^2)
=\exp((\mu_X+\mu_Y)t+\tfrac12(\sigma_X^2+\sigma_Y^2)t^2)$ — the MGF of $\mathcal N(\mu_X+\mu_Y,
\sigma_X^2+\sigma_Y^2)$. The same product-of-MGFs trick proves the Poisson and binomial closure
results.

:::
