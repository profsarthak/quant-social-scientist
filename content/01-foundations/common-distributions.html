The Common Distributions and What They Model

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A distribution is a story about how randomness is generated. Learn the dozen stories and most data has a home.

You don’t meet a new distribution for every problem — you meet the same handful, over and over, each encoding a particular story about how chance operates. Two outcomes? Bernoulli. Counting successes? Binomial. Rare events in an interval? Poisson. Waiting for something? Exponential. Many small effects adding up? Normal. Knowing the stories lets you recognise the right model instead of guessing.

Why it matters

A named distribution is a massive shortcut: identify it and you instantly inherit its mean, its variance, its shape, and a century of results. But the deeper skill is reading the story — the generative assumptions each one encodes — because that tells you when the model fits and, more importantly, when it doesn’t. The chapter’s recurring warning (Bruce & Bruce) lives here: the Normal distribution is so iconic that people reach for it reflexively, yet real social and economic data is routinely long-tailed, and forcing it into a bell underestimates exactly the extreme events that matter most.

These seven also wire directly into later chapters: the Binomial underlies proportions and A/B tests, the Normal underlies the CLT and nearly all classical inference, the Poisson and Exponential underlie event and duration models.

The menagerie at a glance

Distribution Models pmf / pdf Mean Variance
Bernoulli(p) one success/failure trial p^x(1-p)^{1-x}, x\in\{0,1\} p p(1-p)
Binomial(n,p) successes in n trials \binom{n}{i}p^i(1-p)^{n-i} np np(1-p)
Poisson(\lambda) rare-event counts per interval e^{-\lambda}\lambda^i/i! \lambda \lambda
Geometric(p) trials until first success (1-p)^{n-1}p 1/p (1-p)/p^2
Uniform(\alpha,\beta) all values equally likely 1/(\beta-\alpha) (\alpha+\beta)/2 (\beta-\alpha)^2/12
Normal(\mu,\sigma^2) sum of many small effects \tfrac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/2\sigma^2} \mu \sigma^2
Exponential(\lambda) waiting time to an event \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, x\ge 0 1/\lambda 1/\lambda^2

The discrete stories

Bernoulli — the atom. A single trial with two outcomes, “success” (1) and “failure” (0) (Ross 2018). It’s the simplest possible random variable and the building block of everything below. Bruce & Bruce note that binary outcomes — buy/don’t buy, fraud/not fraud, click/no click — sit at the heart of business analytics (Bruce et al. 2020).

Binomial — counting successes. Add up n independent Bernoulli trials, each with the same success probability p, and you get the number of successes (Ross 2018). Its mean is np and variance np(1-p) — note variance is largest at p=0.5 (maximum uncertainty) and vanishes at p=0 or 1.

Worked examples. Ross: in 5 fair coin flips, P(\text{exactly 2 heads}) = \binom{5}{2} (\tfrac12)^5 = 10/32. A quality-control case: screws defective with p = 0.01 — the probability a 10-screw package needs replacement (more than 1 defective) is \approx 0.004 (Ross 2018). Bruce: with a 2% conversion rate, the chance of 0 sales in 200 clicks (Bruce et al. 2020).

Poisson — rare events in an interval. Count events that occur at a constant average rate \lambda over a fixed span of time or space, each independent of the last (Ross 2018). Its signature: mean equals variance, both \lambda.

Worked examples. Ross: typographical errors on a page (\lambda = 0.5), or alpha particles emitted by a radioactive source (\lambda = 3.2) (Ross 2018). Bruce: queuing and capacity — how much server capacity to handle web traffic arriving in 5-second windows (Bruce et al. 2020).

Geometric — waiting for the first success. How many independent trials until success first strikes (Ross 2018). It is memoryless (the discrete version): past failures don’t make success any more “due” — a direct echo of the gambler’s fallacy.

Worked examples. Ross: the expected number of fair-die rolls to get a “1” is 1/p = 6; drawing balls from an urn of N white and M black until the first black (Ross 2018).

The continuous stories

Uniform — featureless ignorance. Every value in (\alpha,\beta) equally likely; the density is flat (Ross 2018). The model for “could be anywhere in this range.”

Worked example (Ross). A passenger arrives at a bus stop where buses come every 15 minutes; if their arrival is uniform between 7:00 and 7:30, the waiting-time probabilities are areas of rectangles (Ross 2018).

Normal — the sum of many small effects. The bell curve, symmetric about \mu, with spread \sigma. Its story is the deep one: a quantity shaped by many small, independent, additive influences tends to be Normal (Ross 2018) — which is precisely the Central Limit Theorem, and why the Normal is the most important distribution in statistics. The 68–95–99.7 rule is worth memorising: about 68% of the mass lies within \pm 1\sigma of the mean, 95% within \pm 2\sigma, and 99.7% within \pm 3\sigma (Ross 2018).

Worked examples. Ross: the heights of 25-year-old men, \mu = 71 inches, \sigma = 2.5 (Ross 2018). Bruce: regression errors and body temperatures (Bruce et al. 2020).

Exponential — time until an event. The waiting time to the next event in a Poisson process (Ross 2018). Its defining trait is the memoryless property: the chance the event happens in the next hour is the same no matter how long you’ve already waited — the distribution doesn’t age (Ross 2018).

Worked examples. Ross: time until the next earthquake, or until someone dials a wrong number (Ross 2018). Bruce: time between website visits, or until an electronic component fails (Bruce et al. 2020).

Two approximations that connect them

The distributions aren’t isolated — they bleed into one another in limits, which is both beautiful and practically useful.

Binomial → Poisson (rare events). When n is large and p small with np = \lambda moderate, the Binomial converges to the Poisson (Ross 2018). The engine is the limit (1 - \lambda/n)^n \to e^{-\lambda}. Use it when you know the rate \lambda but can’t pin down n and p separately — exactly the situation for “rare events at a known average rate.”

Binomial → Normal (De Moivre–Laplace). When n is large, the Binomial is approximately Normal with mean np and variance np(1-p) (Ross 2018). Ross’s rule of thumb: trust it once np(1-p) \ge 10. Because the Normal is continuous and the Binomial is discrete, apply a continuity correction — treat P\{X = 20\} as P\{19.5 < X < 20.5\} (Ross 2018; Bruce et al. 2020). This is a special case of the CLT (a Binomial is a sum of Bernoullis), and a preview of why the bell shows up everywhere.

Show the code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from math import comb

def binom_pmf(n, p):
    ks = np.arange(0, n + 1)
    pmf = np.array([comb(n, int(k)) * p**k * (1 - p)**(n - k) for k in ks])
    return ks, pmf

p = 0.5
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(11, 3.2))
for ax, n in zip(axes, [5, 15, 50]):
    ks, pmf = binom_pmf(n, p)
    ax.bar(ks, pmf, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.8, width=0.9)
    mu, sigma = n * p, np.sqrt(n * p * (1 - p))
    xs = np.linspace(0, n, 300)
    normal = np.exp(-0.5 * ((xs - mu) / sigma) ** 2) / (sigma * np.sqrt(2 * np.pi))
    ax.plot(xs, normal, color="#b5446e", lw=2)
    ax.set_title(f"n = {n}", fontsize=10)
    ax.set_yticks([])
fig.suptitle("A Binomial becomes a bell — De Moivre–Laplace in action", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The crucial caveat: real data isn’t Normal

Bruce & Bruce sound a warning that the rest of this book takes seriously: the Normal distribution is iconic, but raw data in the real world is rarely Normal (Bruce et al. 2020). Incomes, city sizes, firm revenues, financial returns, social-media activity — these are long-tailed, with extreme “black swan” values far more likely than a bell predicts (Bruce et al. 2020). Modelling such data as Normal systematically understates the risk of the very outliers that dominate outcomes.

WarningThe Normal is for averages, not raw data

Reconcile this with the previous section: the CLT promises that averages and sums tend to Normal, not that your individual observations are. A histogram of incomes is sharply right-skewed; a histogram of mean incomes across many samples is bell-shaped. Confusing the two is the single most common modelling error in applied work — see the CLT article’s pitfalls.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Assuming Normality for raw data. Especially for money, counts, and durations, which are skewed or long-tailed. Plot the data first.
  • Using the Normal approximation when np(1-p) is small. For rare events (p tiny), reach for the Poisson, not the Normal — the bell approximation is poor in the tail you care about.
  • Forgetting the continuity correction. Approximating a discrete Binomial by a continuous Normal without the \pm 0.5 adjustment biases small-count probabilities.
  • Misreading the Poisson’s independence assumption. Events must occur at a constant rate, independently. Clustered events (contagion, bursts) violate it and need richer models.
  • Trusting the memoryless property where it doesn’t hold. Human waiting times (a bus on a schedule, a machine that wears out) are often not memoryless — the Exponential assumes no aging, which real systems frequently break.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Identify the story, then the distribution. Two outcomes → Bernoulli; counting them → Binomial; rare counts per interval → Poisson; waiting → Geometric/Exponential; many small additive effects → Normal. The story picks the model.
  • Plot before you assume. A five-minute histogram tells you whether “Normal” is a fantasy. For money, counts, and durations, expect skew and long tails.
  • Match the approximation to the regime. Large n, moderate p → Normal; large n, tiny p → Poisson. Don’t Normal-approximate rare events.
  • Remember mean = variance flags a Poisson. If a count’s variance roughly equals its mean, Poisson is a reasonable start; if variance far exceeds the mean (overdispersion), it isn’t.

Appendix · the mathematics

Bernoulli moments. E[X] = 1\cdot p + 0\cdot(1-p) = p; E[X^2] = p, so \operatorname{Var}(X) = p - p^2 = p(1-p).

Binomial via Bernoulli sums. Writing X = \sum_{k=1}^n B_k with independent Bernoulli(p) B_k, linearity gives E[X] = np, and independence (variances add) gives \operatorname{Var}(X) = np(1-p) — no need to sum the pmf directly.

Binomial → Poisson. With \lambda = np fixed as n\to\infty, \binom{n}{i}p^i(1-p)^{n-i} = \frac{n!}{i!(n-i)!}\Big(\frac{\lambda}{n}\Big)^i\Big(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\Big)^{n-i} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{\lambda^i e^{-\lambda}}{i!}, using \big(1-\tfrac{\lambda}{n}\big)^n \to e^{-\lambda} and \tfrac{n!}{(n-i)!\,n^i}\to 1.

Poisson moments. E[X] = \sum_{i\ge 0} i\,\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^i}{i!} = \lambda; a similar sum gives E[X(X-1)] = \lambda^2, so \operatorname{Var}(X) = \lambda^2 + \lambda - \lambda^2 = \lambda — mean and variance coincide.

Exponential memorylessness. P\{X > s+t \mid X > s\} = \dfrac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda s}} = e^{-\lambda t} = P\{X > t\} — the past is forgotten. The geometric satisfies the discrete analogue.

Normal moments and the 68–95–99.7 rule follow from \int \phi = 1 and Gaussian integration; the empirical rule is \Phi(1)-\Phi(-1)\approx 0.6826, \Phi(2)-\Phi(-2)\approx 0.9544, \Phi(3)-\Phi(-3)\approx 0.9974.

  heights $\mu=71,\sigma=2.5$; earthquakes/wrong numbers); the 68–95–99.7 rule; the

## Practitioner's notes — tips & tricks {.unnumbered}

- **Identify the story, then the distribution.** Two outcomes → Bernoulli; counting them →
  Binomial; rare counts per interval → Poisson; waiting → Geometric/Exponential; many small
  additive effects → Normal. The story picks the model.
- **Plot before you assume.** A five-minute histogram tells you whether "Normal" is a fantasy. For
  money, counts, and durations, expect skew and long tails.
- **Match the approximation to the regime.** Large $n$, moderate $p$ → Normal; large $n$, tiny $p$
  → Poisson. Don't Normal-approximate rare events.
- **Remember mean = variance flags a Poisson.** If a count's variance roughly equals its mean,
  Poisson is a reasonable start; if variance far exceeds the mean (*overdispersion*), it isn't.

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Bernoulli moments.** $E[X] = 1\cdot p + 0\cdot(1-p) = p$; $E[X^2] = p$, so
$\operatorname{Var}(X) = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$.

**Binomial via Bernoulli sums.** Writing $X = \sum_{k=1}^n B_k$ with independent Bernoulli$(p)$
$B_k$, linearity gives $E[X] = np$, and independence (variances add) gives
$\operatorname{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$ — no need to sum the pmf directly.

**Binomial → Poisson.** With $\lambda = np$ fixed as $n\to\infty$,
$$
\binom{n}{i}p^i(1-p)^{n-i} = \frac{n!}{i!(n-i)!}\Big(\frac{\lambda}{n}\Big)^i\Big(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\Big)^{n-i}
\;\longrightarrow\; \frac{\lambda^i e^{-\lambda}}{i!},
$$
using $\big(1-\tfrac{\lambda}{n}\big)^n \to e^{-\lambda}$ and $\tfrac{n!}{(n-i)!\,n^i}\to 1$.

**Poisson moments.** $E[X] = \sum_{i\ge 0} i\,\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^i}{i!} = \lambda$; a
similar sum gives $E[X(X-1)] = \lambda^2$, so $\operatorname{Var}(X) = \lambda^2 + \lambda -
\lambda^2 = \lambda$ — mean and variance coincide.

**Exponential memorylessness.** $P\{X > s+t \mid X > s\} = \dfrac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda
s}} = e^{-\lambda t} = P\{X > t\}$ — the past is forgotten. The geometric satisfies the discrete
analogue.

**Normal moments and the 68–95–99.7 rule** follow from $\int \phi = 1$ and Gaussian integration;
the empirical rule is $\Phi(1)-\Phi(-1)\approx 0.6826$, $\Phi(2)-\Phi(-2)\approx 0.9544$,
$\Phi(3)-\Phi(-3)\approx 0.9974$.

:::
