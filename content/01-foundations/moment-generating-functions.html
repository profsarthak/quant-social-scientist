Moment Generating Functions

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

One function that remembers everything about a distribution — and turns the hard problem of adding random variables into easy multiplication.

A distribution has infinitely many features — mean, variance, skew, every higher moment. The moment generating function packs them all into a single function M(t)=\mathbb E[e^{tX}], a “convenient bookkeeping device.” Differentiate it to read off any moment; recognise its form to identify the distribution; and — its killer feature — multiply two MGFs to get the distribution of a sum, turning convolution from a slog into a one-liner and handing you the cleanest proof of the Central Limit Theorem.

Why it matters

Two operations are everywhere in statistics and both are awkward head-on: computing a distribution’s moments, and finding the distribution of a sum of random variables. The MGF makes both mechanical. It’s the tool behind the closure properties you rely on (“the sum of Normals is Normal”) and the engine of the limit theorems — a small piece of machinery with outsized reach. (Ross 2018)

The definition, and why “moment generating”

The MGF of a random variable X is the expected value of e^{tX}: (Ross 2018) M(t) = \mathbb E\!\left[e^{tX}\right], a sum over a pmf (discrete) or an integral over a density (continuous). The name comes from a small miracle: differentiate M and set t=0 to generate the moments. The n-th derivative gives the n-th moment, (Ross 2018) M^{(n)}(0) = \mathbb E[X^n]. So M'(0)=\mathbb E[X], M''(0)=\mathbb E[X^2], and the variance \sigma^2 = M''(0)-[M'(0)]^2 falls out far more easily than computing it from the definition. (Ross 2018) (The reason: e^{tX}=\sum_k \frac{t^k}{k!}X^k, so M(t) is a Taylor series whose coefficients are the moments.)

Uniqueness: the MGF is a fingerprint

The deep result: the MGF uniquely determines the distribution. (Ross 2018) If two variables have the same MGF, they have the same distribution — so you can identify a distribution just by recognising the form of its MGF, without ever touching its density. This is what makes the next property a proof technique rather than a curiosity.

The killer app: sums become products

Finding the distribution of a sum is normally a “rather complicated” convolution. (Ross 2018) The MGF collapses it: for independent X and Y, M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t)\,M_Y(t). The MGF of a sum is the product of the MGFs. (Ross 2018) (Because \mathbb E[e^{t(X+Y)}]=\mathbb E[e^{tX}e^{tY}]=\mathbb E[e^{tX}]\mathbb E[e^{tY}] by independence.) Combine this with uniqueness and you get closure theorems for free: the sum of independent Normals is Normal, of independent Poissons is Poisson, of independent Binomials (same p) is Binomial — each proved by multiplying MGFs and recognising the result. (Ross 2018)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Convolve the Uniform(0,1) density with itself n-1 times on a fine grid
dx = 0.01
base = np.ones(int(1/dx))            # Uniform(0,1) density (height 1 on [0,1])
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 4, figsize=(10.0, 2.9), sharey=False)
dens = base.copy()
for i, n in enumerate([1, 2, 3, 8]):
    d = base.copy()
    for _ in range(n - 1):
        d = np.convolve(d, base) * dx     # convolution = MGF product
    x = np.arange(len(d)) * dx
    axes[i].fill_between(x, d, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85)
    axes[i].set_title(f"sum of {n} uniform{'s' if n>1 else ''}", fontsize=9.5)
    axes[i].set_yticks([])
fig.suptitle("Adding independent variables (multiplying MGFs) → the Normal", fontsize=11)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Proving the Central Limit Theorem

The MGF is “the key to the proof” of the CLT. (Ross 2018) The argument: write the MGF of a standardized sum of n independent variables, and show that as n\to\infty it converges to e^{t^2/2} — which is exactly the MGF of the standard Normal. By uniqueness, the distribution must therefore converge to Normal. (Ross 2018) That is the entire CLT in three moves: standardize, take the MGF, watch it become the Gaussian’s. The bell isn’t an accident; it is what the product of many MGFs is forced to become.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Assuming the MGF always exists. \mathbb E[e^{tX}] can be infinite (heavy tails — the Cauchy has no MGF). When it fails, use the always-defined characteristic function \mathbb E[e^{itX}] instead. (Ross 2018)

  • Multiplying MGFs of dependent variables. M_{X+Y}=M_X M_Y needs independence; without it the product rule fails. (Ross 2018)

  • Forgetting to evaluate at t=0. Moments are M^{(n)}(0), not M^{(n)}(t) — the derivatives are taken, then set t=0. (Ross 2018)

  • Confusing the MGF with the distribution. The MGF encodes the distribution; you still recognise the family from its form (uniqueness), not read probabilities off M directly.

## Proving the Central Limit Theorem

The MGF is *"the key to the proof"* of the [CLT](../02-inference/sampling-distributions-clt.qmd).
[@ross_first_course] The argument: write the MGF of a **standardized sum** of $n$ independent variables, and
show that as $n\to\infty$ it converges to $e^{t^2/2}$ — which is exactly the MGF of the **standard Normal.**
By uniqueness, the *distribution* must therefore converge to Normal. [@ross_first_course] That is the entire
CLT in three moves: standardize, take the MGF, watch it become the Gaussian's. The bell isn't an accident; it
is what the product of many MGFs is *forced* to become.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Assuming the MGF always exists.** $\mathbb E[e^{tX}]$ can be infinite (heavy tails — the Cauchy has no
  MGF). When it fails, use the always-defined **characteristic function** $\mathbb E[e^{itX}]$ instead.
  [@ross_first_course]

- **Multiplying MGFs of *dependent* variables.** $M_{X+Y}=M_X M_Y$ needs **independence**; without it the
  product rule fails. [@ross_first_course]

- **Forgetting to evaluate at $t=0$.** Moments are $M^{(n)}(0)$, not $M^{(n)}(t)$ — the derivatives are taken,
  *then* set $t=0$. [@ross_first_course]

- **Confusing the MGF with the distribution.** The MGF *encodes* the distribution; you still recognise the
  family from its form (uniqueness), not read probabilities off $M$ directly.

:::
