Conditional Probability & Independence

How knowing one thing changes the odds of another — and the special case where it doesn’t.

Almost every interesting question in social science is conditional: not “what’s the chance of poverty?” but “what’s the chance of poverty given no high-school diploma?” Conditioning means shrinking the world to the cases where one thing is true and re-measuring inside it. Independence is the special — and often wrongly assumed — case where the shrinking changes nothing.

Why it matters

Conditional probability is the mathematical seed of the entire book. “Controlling for,” “holding constant,” “the effect of X on Y among people with the same Z” — every one of these is, underneath, a statement about a conditional distribution. Regression estimates a conditional mean; causal inference is a careful argument about which conditional comparisons are fair. If you understand P(A \mid B) deeply, the rest of the manual is elaboration.

Independence matters for the opposite reason: it is the assumption that makes probabilities multiply, and it is the assumption most often invoked without warrant. Knowing exactly what it claims — and how it differs from “mutually exclusive” — prevents a whole family of errors, from the gambler’s fallacy to overconfident standard errors.

Conditional probability: restricting the sample space

To condition on B is to assume B happened and ask about A inside that smaller world. Any outcome outside B now has probability 0; outcomes in A count only if they also lie in B (the intersection AB) (Ross 2018). Rescaling so the new world B has total probability 1 gives the definition:

P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(AB)}{P(B)}, \qquad P(B) > 0.

Worked example (Ross). Roll two dice. You’re told the first die is a 4 (event F). That discards the 30 outcomes starting with 1, 2, 3, 5, or 6, leaving 6 equally likely ones: (4,1) through (4,6). Let E = “the sum is 6.” Inside the restricted world only (4,2) works, so P(E \mid F) = 1/6 (Ross 2018).

Rearranging the definition gives the multiplication rule — often more useful than the definition itself, because real problems unfold in stages:

P(AB) = P(A \mid B)\,P(B).

“The chance both happen = the chance the first happens, times the chance the second happens given the first.” Chained across several stages (draw a card, then another, then another), it becomes the workhorse for sequential experiments (Ross 2018).

TipConditioning is the whole game

When a later chapter says it “controls for income,” picture this entry: it is restricting attention to people at the same income and re-measuring the relationship inside that slice. The power — and the peril — of all observational social science lives in which variable you condition on. (Conditioning on the wrong one can manufacture bias; see the cast of characters and Part V.)

Independence: when conditioning changes nothing

Two events are independent when knowing one occurred leaves the other’s probability untouched (Ross 2018). Equivalently, either of:

P(A \mid B) = P(A) \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad P(AB) = P(A)\,P(B).

The second form — probabilities multiply — is the one you’ll use constantly, because it’s symmetric and survives even when P(B)=0 edge cases bite. Independence is what licenses “and → multiply” for separate random events: the chance of heads then heads is \tfrac12 \times \tfrac12 = \tfrac14 because the tosses are independent.

WarningIndependent is not the same as mutually exclusive

This confusion is so common it deserves its own warning (Ross 2018). Mutually exclusive events with positive probability are the opposite of independent. If A and B are disjoint, then learning A occurred tells you B definitely did not — that’s perfect information, i.e. maximal dependence. Formally, P(B \mid A) = 0 \ne P(B). Disjoint events can’t co-occur; independent events carry no information about each other. They are different ideas, and for positive-probability events, never both true at once.

The most expensive assumption in statistics

Some things really are independent — successive fair coin flips, well-separated measurement errors. But independence is assumed far more often than it is checked, and Ross flags this directly: many quantities people treat as independent are not (Ross 2018). The cost is real. Standard errors, p-values, and the Central Limit Theorem lean on independent (or weakly dependent) observations; when data are clustered — students within schools, repeated measures on the same person, neighbours sharing a shock — pretending they’re independent makes you far more certain than you’ve earned.

The gambler’s fallacy (Albers). After eight heads in a row, the ninth toss is not more likely to be tails. The tosses are independent, so the ninth is still 50/50 — the coin has no memory of the streak (Albers 2017). The fallacy is exactly a failure to apply independence; it returns, formalised, in the Law of Large Numbers.

The law of total probability

Often you can’t get P(E) directly, but you can get it inside each of several scenarios. If F_1, F_2, \dots, F_n partition the sample space (mutually exclusive, and together exhaustive), then E is split across them and its probability is the weighted average of the conditionals (Ross 2018):

P(E) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(E \mid F_i)\,P(F_i).

Read it as: average the chance of E within each scenario, weighting each scenario by how likely it is. This “condition on which case you’re in, then average” move is one of the most useful in all of probability — and it is the denominator of Bayes’ theorem, the very next entry.

Quick illustration. Suppose 60% of voters are urban (turnout 70%) and 40% rural (turnout 55%). Overall turnout is 0.70(0.60) + 0.55(0.40) = 0.42 + 0.22 = 0.64 — a weighted average of the two conditional turnouts, not a naïve midpoint.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Transposing the conditional. P(A \mid B) and P(B \mid A) are different numbers. “Most rioters were young men” does not mean “most young men riot.” (This is the prosecutor’s fallacy, dissected in the Bayes entry.)
  • Assuming independence by default. Multiplying probabilities you haven’t earned the right to multiply is the structural error behind overconfident results. Ask “could one of these carry information about the other?” before invoking P(AB)=P(A)P(B).
  • Confusing mutually exclusive with independent. Disjoint events are maximally dependent, not independent (see the warning above).
  • The gambler’s fallacy and its inverse. Streaks don’t make the opposite “due” (gambler’s fallacy), and they also don’t prove the coin is rigged after only a few tosses (its hot-hand-style inverse). Independence means each trial is fresh.
  • Ignoring clustering. Treating clustered or repeated-measure observations as independent understates uncertainty — a quiet but serious error that later chapters fix with clustered standard errors and multilevel models.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Always ask “conditional on what?” Naming the conditioning set turns vague claims into precise ones and exposes hidden assumptions about who’s being compared.
  • Say the multiplication rule out loud. “Chance of both = chance of the first × chance of the second given the first” handles sequential problems that look hard when attacked head-on.
  • Earn your independence. Before multiplying, articulate why the events carry no information about each other. If you can’t, don’t multiply.
  • Watch for clusters. Repeated measures, geographic grouping, and shared shocks all break independence; flag them early so the right standard errors get used later.

Appendix · the mathematics

Conditional probability is itself a probability. Fix B with P(B)>0 and define Q(A) = P(A\mid B) = P(AB)/P(B). Then Q satisfies all three axioms: Q(A)\ge 0; Q(S) = P(SB)/P(B) = P(B)/P(B) = 1; and for disjoint A_1,A_2,\dots, additivity of P on the disjoint sets A_iB gives additivity of Q. So every theorem about probabilities applies verbatim to conditional probabilities.

The multiplication (chain) rule, general form. P(A_1 A_2 \cdots A_n) = P(A_1)\,P(A_2\mid A_1)\,P(A_3\mid A_1 A_2)\cdots P(A_n\mid A_1\cdots A_{n-1}).

Law of total probability, derivation. If \{F_i\} partitions S, then E = \bigcup_i (E F_i), a union of mutually exclusive events. By axiom 3 and the multiplication rule, P(E) = \sum_i P(E F_i) = \sum_i P(E\mid F_i)\,P(F_i).

Independence of several events is stronger than pairwise independence: A,B,C are (mutually) independent only if every subset multiplies, including P(ABC)=P(A)P(B)P(C). Pairwise independence does not imply this — a standard cautionary example, and a reason to state independence assumptions carefully.
