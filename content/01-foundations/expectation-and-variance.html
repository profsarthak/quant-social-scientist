Expectation & Variance

Two numbers that tame a whole distribution: where it balances, and how far it scatters.

A distribution can be complicated; two summaries do most of the work. The expectation is its balance point — the long-run average. The variance is its scatter — how far values stray from that balance. Master two algebraic facts about them — expectation is linear (always), variance of a sum adds only when things are independent — and the standard error, the heart of inference, falls out for free.

Why it matters

Expectation and variance are the atoms of quantitative reasoning. “The average treatment effect,” “the mean income,” “the expected return” — all expectations. “The standard deviation,” “the margin of error,” “the volatility” — all built from variance. More than that: the single most important formula in applied statistics, the standard error \text{SE}(\bar X) = \sigma/\sqrt{n} that powers the Central Limit Theorem, is just the variance-of-a-sum rule applied to an average. This entry is where that formula is earned.

Expectation: the balance point

The expected value E[X] is a weighted average of the values X can take, each weighted by its probability (Ross 2018): E[X] = \sum_x x\,p(x) \quad\text{(discrete)}, \qquad E[X] = \int x\,f(x)\,dx \quad\text{(continuous)}.

Two intuitions, both worth carrying:

  • Long-run average. Repeat the experiment endlessly and the running average of outcomes settles on E[X] (Ross 2018). (That this must happen is the Law of Large Numbers.)
  • Centre of gravity. Place a weight equal to each probability at its value along a rod; E[X] is the point where the rod balances (Ross 2018). Skew the weights right and the balance point slides right.

Worked examples (Ross).

  • Three fair coins, Y = number of heads. The masses are p(0,1,2,3) = \tfrac18,\tfrac38,\tfrac38,\tfrac18, so E[Y] = 0\cdot\tfrac18 + 1\cdot\tfrac38 + 2\cdot\tfrac38 + 3\cdot\tfrac18 = 1.5. Note the expectation, 1.5, is a value Y can never take — it’s a balance point, not a forecast (Ross 2018).
  • Uniform on (\alpha,\beta). With constant density, the balance point is the midpoint, E[X] = \dfrac{\alpha+\beta}{2} (Ross 2018).

Linearity of expectation — the workhorse

For any random variables X, Y and constants a, b (Ross 2018): E[aX + bY] = a\,E[X] + b\,E[Y].

TipWhy linearity needs no independence

This is the most useful fact in the chapter, and its power is that it holds even when X and Y are dependent (Ross 2018). The reason is mechanical: E[X+Y] sums the quantity (x+y) against the joint distribution P\{X=x, Y=y\}. That double sum splits into a sum of the x-part and a sum of the y-part, and each collapses to a marginal — \sum_x x\,P\{X=x\} and \sum_y y\,P\{Y=y\} — regardless of how X and Y are related. Dependence rearranges where the joint mass sits, but it can’t change the marginal totals. (Dependence does matter for the variance of a sum — just not the expectation.)

Linearity lets you compute the mean of a complicated sum without ever finding its distribution — the trick behind the unbiasedness of the sample mean, E[\bar X] = \mu, used in the flagship CLT article.

LOTUS: the mean of a function, the lazy way

You often want E[g(X)] — the expected value of some transformation, like X^2. The Law of the Unconscious Statistician says you can compute it directly from X’s distribution, without first deriving the distribution of g(X) (Ross 2018): E[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x)\,p(x) \quad\text{or}\quad \int g(x)\,f(x)\,dx.

Worked example (Ross). Let X take values -1, 0, 1 with probabilities 0.2, 0.5, 0.3. Then E[X^2] = (-1)^2(0.2) + 0^2(0.5) + 1^2(0.3) = 0.5 — just reweight the squared values (Ross 2018). (Note E[X^2] = 0.5 \ne (E[X])^2 = 0.01; squaring and averaging don’t commute — exactly the gap the variance measures.)

Variance: the scatter

The variance is the expected squared distance from the mean (Ross 2018): \operatorname{Var}(X) = E\big[(X-\mu)^2\big] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2, \qquad \mu = E[X]. The second form (via LOTUS) is the one you actually compute with. The standard deviation \operatorname{SD}(X) = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)} returns the scatter to the original units (squared dollars back to dollars).

If E[X] is the centre of gravity, variance is the moment of inertia — how far the distribution’s “mass” sits from the centre (Ross 2018). Same balance point, very different spread:

Worked examples (Ross).

  • Fair die. E[X] = 3.5 and E[X^2] = \tfrac{1+4+9+16+25+36}{6} = \tfrac{91}{6}, so \operatorname{Var}(X) = \tfrac{91}{6} - 3.5^2 = \tfrac{35}{12} \approx 2.917 (Ross 2018).
  • Same mean, different spread. Let W \equiv 0; Y = \pm 1 with probability \tfrac12 each; Z = \pm 100 with probability \tfrac12 each. All three have mean 0, yet \operatorname{Var}(W) = 0, \operatorname{Var}(Y) = 1, \operatorname{Var}(Z) = 10{,}000 — identical centres, wildly different scatter (Ross 2018). The mean alone never tells you the spread.

The variance of a sum — and the standard error

Unlike expectation, variance does not add freely. For two variables, \operatorname{Var}(X + Y) = \operatorname{Var}(X) + \operatorname{Var}(Y) + 2\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y). The covariance term is exactly where dependence re-enters (the extraction’s “correlation only affects the variance of a sum, not its expectation”) (Ross 2018). When X and Y are independent, the covariance is 0 and variances simply add — the fact that unlocks the standard error.

TipWhere SE = σ/√n comes from

Take an average of n independent draws, each with variance \sigma^2. Variances of independent things add, and the constant 1/n pulls out squared: \operatorname{Var}(\bar X) = \operatorname{Var}\!\Big(\tfrac1n \textstyle\sum_i X_i\Big) = \tfrac{1}{n^2}\sum_i \operatorname{Var}(X_i) = \tfrac{1}{n^2}(n\sigma^2) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}. Take the square root: \operatorname{SE}(\bar X) = \sigma/\sqrt{n}. The entire \sqrt{n} law of precision — quadruple the sample to halve the error — is this one line. It is why the CLT chapter can promise that averages get sharp.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Treating the expectation as a likely value. E[Y] = 1.5 heads is the balance point, not a prediction — the variable may never take it. Report spread alongside the mean.
  • Assuming E[g(X)] = g(E[X]). False in general (Jensen’s inequality). E[X^2] \ne (E[X])^2 is the very gap that defines variance. Squaring then averaging \ne averaging then squaring.
  • Adding variances that aren’t independent. \operatorname{Var}(X+Y) = \operatorname{Var}(X) + \operatorname{Var}(Y) holds only when \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=0. Forgetting the covariance term is how people understate uncertainty for correlated data.
  • Reporting variance in squared units. Variance is in units^2; convert to the standard deviation before interpreting “spread” in plain language.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Ross, A First Course in Probability — expectation as a probability-weighted average, with the long-run-average and centre-of-gravity intuitions (three-coins E[Y]=1.5; uniform midpoint); linearity of expectation for any X,Y and the joint-distribution argument for why independence isn’t needed; LOTUS (X\in\{-1,0,1\}, E[X^2]=0.5); variance as E[(X-\mu)^2]=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2 with the moment-of-inertia intuition (fair die \operatorname{Var}=35/12; the W/Y/Z same-mean/different-spread trio); variance of a sum and the role of covariance.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Compute means with linearity, not brute force. Break a complicated quantity into a sum and take expectations term by term — independence not required, so it almost always works.
  • Use \operatorname{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2. It’s nearly always less work than the definition, and it makes the “squaring doesn’t commute with averaging” point concrete.
  • Always pair a mean with a spread. A mean without a standard deviation hides whether the average is typical or just a balance point between extremes.
  • Track independence before adding variances. The covariance term is where clustered or correlated data quietly inflate true uncertainty — flag it early.

Appendix · the mathematics

Linearity, proof sketch. For discrete X,Y with joint pmf p(x,y), E[X+Y] = \sum_x\sum_y (x+y)\,p(x,y) = \sum_x x\sum_y p(x,y) + \sum_y y\sum_x p(x,y) = E[X] + E[Y], since \sum_y p(x,y) = p_X(x) and \sum_x p(x,y) = p_Y(y) are the marginals — true for any joint distribution. Scaling by constants is immediate, giving E[aX+bY]=aE[X]+bE[Y].

Computational form of the variance. Expand and use linearity: \operatorname{Var}(X) = E[(X-\mu)^2] = E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2] = E[X^2] - 2\mu E[X] + \mu^2 = E[X^2] - \mu^2.

Variance of a linear combination. With \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y], \operatorname{Var}(aX+bY) = a^2\operatorname{Var}(X) + b^2\operatorname{Var}(Y) + 2ab\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y). For independent X_1,\dots,X_n (all covariances 0) with common variance \sigma^2, the average \bar X = \tfrac1n\sum X_i has \operatorname{Var}(\bar X) = \sigma^2/n, hence \operatorname{SE}(\bar X)=\sigma/\sqrt n. Covariance and correlation get their own treatment in P8.

Jensen’s inequality (why E[g(X)] \ne g(E[X])). For a convex function g (one that curves upward, like x^2 or e^x), the average of the transformed values exceeds the transform of the average: E[g(X)] \;\ge\; g\big(E[X]\big), with equality only if g is linear or X is constant. The variance is the canonical case (g(x)=x^2 gives E[X^2] \ge (E[X])^2, the gap being \operatorname{Var}(X)\ge 0). For a concave g (like \log) the inequality flips: E[\log X] \le \log E[X] — which is exactly why the mean of log-income sits below the log of mean income, and why exponentiating a log-scale prediction under-states the level. The size of the gap grows with the curvature of g and the spread of X.

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Linearity, proof sketch.** For discrete $X,Y$ with joint pmf $p(x,y)$,
$$
E[X+Y] = \sum_x\sum_y (x+y)\,p(x,y) = \sum_x x\sum_y p(x,y) + \sum_y y\sum_x p(x,y) = E[X] + E[Y],
$$
since $\sum_y p(x,y) = p_X(x)$ and $\sum_x p(x,y) = p_Y(y)$ are the marginals — true for *any*
joint distribution. Scaling by constants is immediate, giving $E[aX+bY]=aE[X]+bE[Y]$.

**Computational form of the variance.** Expand and use linearity:
$$
\operatorname{Var}(X) = E[(X-\mu)^2] = E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2] = E[X^2] - 2\mu E[X] + \mu^2
= E[X^2] - \mu^2.
$$

**Variance of a linear combination.** With $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$,
$$
\operatorname{Var}(aX+bY) = a^2\operatorname{Var}(X) + b^2\operatorname{Var}(Y) + 2ab\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y).
$$
For independent $X_1,\dots,X_n$ (all covariances 0) with common variance $\sigma^2$, the average
$\bar X = \tfrac1n\sum X_i$ has $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X) = \sigma^2/n$, hence
$\operatorname{SE}(\bar X)=\sigma/\sqrt n$. Covariance and correlation get their own treatment in
[P8](covariance-and-correlation.qmd).

**Jensen's inequality** (why $E[g(X)] \ne g(E[X])$). For a **convex** function $g$ (one that curves
upward, like $x^2$ or $e^x$), the average of the transformed values exceeds the transform of the
average:
$$
E[g(X)] \;\ge\; g\big(E[X]\big),
$$
with equality only if $g$ is linear or $X$ is constant. The variance is the canonical case
($g(x)=x^2$ gives $E[X^2] \ge (E[X])^2$, the gap being $\operatorname{Var}(X)\ge 0$). For a **concave**
$g$ (like $\log$) the inequality flips: $E[\log X] \le \log E[X]$ — which is exactly why the mean of
log-income sits *below* the log of mean income, and why exponentiating a log-scale prediction
under-states the level. The size of the gap grows with the curvature of $g$ and the spread of $X$.

:::
