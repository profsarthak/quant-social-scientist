Joint Distributions, Covariance & Correlation

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

How two random quantities move together — and the famous trap of reading that movement as cause.

One variable at a time only gets you so far; the interesting questions are about pairs — education and income, temperature and crime, treatment and outcome. Covariance and correlation measure whether two variables move together. But they measure only linear co-movement, they can be zero for variables that are tightly related, and — the warning that echoes through the rest of this book — a correlation, however strong, is not a cause.

Why it matters

This entry is a hinge. Backward, it completes the probability machinery: the variance-of-a-sum formula had a covariance term, and here it finally gets defined. Forward, it is the gateway to all of applied social science. Regression is, at its core, structured correlation; the entire causal-inference summit exists because correlation alone cannot establish cause. The single most repeated mistake in empirical work — reading an association as a mechanism — is born exactly here, so this is where we inoculate against it.

Two variables at once: joint, marginal, conditional

When you track two random variables together, three distributions describe them (Ross 2018):

  • Joint distribution. The probability of value-pairs at once: the joint cdf F(a,b) = P\{X \le a,\, Y \le b\}, with a joint pmf p(x,y) = P\{X=x, Y=y\} (discrete) or a joint density f(x,y) whose volume over a region gives probability (continuous). It holds all the information about how X and Y relate.
  • Marginal distribution. Each variable on its own, recovered by summing (or integrating) out the other — collapsing the joint down onto one axis. The margin is what you’d see ignoring the partner variable.
  • Conditional distribution. The distribution of one variable given a specific value of the other — p(x \mid y) = p(x,y)/p_Y(y) — the same conditioning idea, now between numeric variables. This is the object regression will model: the conditional mean of Y given X.

Covariance: do they move together?

Covariance summarises the direction of co-movement (Ross 2018): \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) = E[XY] - E[X]\,E[Y].

Read it through the signs: it is positive when X being above its mean tends to coincide with Y being above its mean (they rise together), and negative when one tends to be high while the other is low (they move oppositely) (Ross 2018). Zero covariance means no linear tendency either way.

A key fact, and the one that closed the variance-of-a-sum loop: if X and Y are independent, then \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) = 0, because independence makes E[XY] = E[X]E[Y] (Ross 2018). (The converse fails — see below.)

The trouble with covariance is units. Measure height in inches versus feet and the covariance of height-and-weight changes by a factor of 12, though nothing about the relationship did (Ross 2018). Its sign is meaningful; its magnitude, on its own, is not.

Correlation: covariance made unitless

Correlation (Pearson’s \rho) fixes the units problem by standardising — dividing covariance by each variable’s standard deviation (Ross 2018): \rho(X,Y) = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \,\sigma_Y}.

This rescaling forces \rho into the interval [-1, +1] — a consequence of the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality (Ross 2018). Now the magnitude means something:

  • \rho = +1: a perfect positive linear relationship (points fall exactly on an upward line).
  • \rho = -1: a perfect negative linear relationship.
  • \rho \approx 0: no linear association.

The word linear is doing enormous, easily-missed work — as the next section shows.

The trap: zero correlation ≠ independence

Independence implies zero correlation. The reverse is false (Ross 2018). Correlation sees only the linear part of a relationship and is blind to everything else.

WarningThe canonical counterexample

Let X take values -1, 0, 1 each with probability 1/3, and set Y = X^2 (Ross 2018). Then Y is a perfect, deterministic function of X — knowing X tells you Y exactly, the strongest dependence possible. Yet \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) = E[X^3] - E[X]\,E[X^2] = 0 - 0\cdot\tfrac23 = 0, so \rho = 0. The relationship is entirely non-linear (a U-shape), and correlation, which only measures linear trend, reports “nothing here.” A correlation of zero means no straight-line association — not “no relationship.”

Show the code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
x = rng.uniform(-3, 3, size=400)
y = x**2 + rng.normal(0, 0.6, size=400)   # clear nonlinear dependence
r = np.corrcoef(x, y)[0, 1]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 4))
ax.scatter(x, y, s=12, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.6)
ax.set_title(f"Strong dependence, yet correlation r = {r:.2f}")
ax.set_xlabel("X"); ax.set_ylabel("Y = X² + noise")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The practical lesson: always plot the data. A near-zero correlation can hide a U-shape, a cycle, or a threshold — relationships that matter enormously and that a single coefficient erases.

The big one: correlation is not causation

Even a strong, genuinely linear correlation does not establish that one variable causes the other. The usual culprit is a lurking (confounding) variable that drives both, producing a spurious correlation (Bruce et al. 2020). The sources offer a rogues’ gallery:

  • Ice cream and shark attacks rise together at the beach — but ice cream doesn’t summon sharks. Temperature is the lurking variable: heat drives both beach attendance (more swimmers, more shark encounters) and ice-cream sales (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Marriages and deaths across U.S. states correlate strongly — a regression of deaths on marriages gives a coefficient of 0.888. Marriage is not lethal; state population drives both (bigger states have more of everything) (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Nicolas Cage films and pool drownings show a 66.6% correlation over 1999–2009 — pure coincidence surfaced by data mining across enough unrelated series (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Bathrooms in the home and student achievement correlate — not because plumbing teaches, but because both track parental economic status (Bruce et al. 2020).
TipWhy this is the seed of the whole book

Each example has the same shape: X and Y correlate because a third variable Z causes both (recall the confounders from the Overture). Correlation measures the association; it cannot tell you whether it came from X \to Y, Y \to X, or Z \to X and Z \to Y. Sorting those apart is impossible with correlation alone — which is the entire reason causal inference exists, and why randomisation (which severs the link to every Z) is so prized.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reading correlation as causation. The headline error of empirical social science. An association is a question, not an answer — ask “what third variable could produce both?”
  • Trusting \rho = 0 as ‘no relationship.’ It means no linear relationship. Plot the data; U-shapes and cycles hide in plain sight.
  • Comparing raw covariances. Covariance magnitude is unit-dependent and meaningless across variables; standardise to correlation before comparing strengths.
  • Mining for correlations. Test enough pairs and chance will hand you a striking, meaningless one (Nicolas Cage). Correlations found by trawling need far more skepticism than ones predicted in advance.
  • Forgetting correlation is symmetric. \rho(X,Y) = \rho(Y,X) carries no direction; it cannot even hint which variable came first, let alone which caused which.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Ross, A First Course in Probability — joint, marginal, and conditional distributions; covariance \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y], its sign intuition, and independence ⟹ zero covariance; correlation as standardised covariance, bounded in [-1,1] by Cauchy–Schwarz; the Y=X^2 counterexample showing zero correlation with perfect dependence.
  • Bruce & Bruce, Practical Statistics for Data Scientists — correlation captures only linear association; correlation-is-not-causation and spurious correlation via lurking variables (ice cream/sharks; marriages/deaths with coefficient 0.888; Nicolas Cage/drownings at 66.6%; bathrooms/achievement and parental status).

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Plot first, correlate second. A scatterplot reveals non-linearity, clusters, and outliers that a single coefficient hides. Never report \rho without having looked.
  • For every correlation, name a candidate confounder. Forcing yourself to propose the lurking Z is the fastest cure for causal over-reach.
  • Standardise before comparing. Use correlation, not covariance, when asking “which relationship is stronger” — covariance units make raw comparison meaningless.
  • Distrust mined correlations. A relationship you went looking for across many variables deserves far more doubt than one your theory predicted beforehand.

Appendix · the mathematics

Covariance, bilinearity, and the variance link. Expanding the definition, \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) = E[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)] = E[XY]-E[X]E[Y], and \operatorname{Cov}(X,X) = \operatorname{Var}(X). Covariance is bilinear: \operatorname{Cov}(aX+b,\,cY+d) = ac\,\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y), which is why \operatorname{Var}(X+Y) = \operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y).

Why |\rho| \le 1 (Cauchy–Schwarz). For any random variables, \big(E[UV]\big)^2 \le E[U^2]\,E[V^2]. Set U = X-\mu_X, V = Y-\mu_Y: \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)^2 \le \operatorname{Var}(X)\operatorname{Var}(Y) \;\Longrightarrow\; \rho^2 \le 1. Equality (so \rho = \pm 1) holds exactly when Y is an affine function of X, i.e. the points lie on a straight line — the precise sense in which correlation measures linearity.

The counterexample, computed. X \in \{-1,0,1\} each w.p. 1/3, Y = X^2. Then E[X]=0, E[X^2]=2/3, E[XY]=E[X^3]=0, so \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=0 and \rho=0, despite Y being a deterministic function of X. Zero correlation, total dependence.

Independence vs uncorrelatedness. Independence ⟹ E[XY]=E[X]E[Y] ⟹ \rho=0. The converse needs the whole joint distribution to factor, p(x,y)=p_X(x)p_Y(y) — far stronger than a single number vanishing. (One exception worth knowing: for a jointly Normal pair, zero correlation does imply independence.)
