Messy Real Data: What to Expect

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Textbook data is clean. Real data has holes, typos, monsters in the tails — and a well-designed study with bad data is still a failure.

Everything so far assumed tidy numbers. Real data arrives with missing values, outliers, mislabelled types, units silently mixed, and tails far heavier than any bell curve. Garbage in, garbage out: no model, however sophisticated, rescues conclusions built on bad data. This entry is the reality check that closes Part I — what to expect, how to diagnose it, and why the unglamorous work of structuring and cleaning data is as decisive as the analysis it precedes.

Why it matters

A common, costly belief among beginners is that analysis is the hard part and the data “is just there.” The opposite is closer to the truth: experienced researchers spend the majority of their time getting data into shape, because the cleanest regression in the world is worthless if it runs on a column where someone typed 500 for 50. Albers puts it bluntly — a well-designed study with bad data is still a failure (Albers 2017). This entry connects the chapter’s theory to the field’s practice, and it is where the robust statistics of D1 and the diagnostic plots of D2 finally earn their keep.

Data types and structure

Raw inputs — text, sensor streams, logs — must first be turned into structured data (Bruce et al. 2020). The standard structure is rectangular data, the data frame: a two-dimensional table where rows are records (observations, cases) and columns are features (variables, attributes) (Bruce et al. 2020). Almost every tool you’ll use expects this shape.

Within it, every variable has a type, and the type dictates what is legal to do with it (Bruce et al. 2020; Albers 2017):

  • Numeric — continuous (any value in a range, e.g. wind speed) or discrete (integer counts, e.g. number of complaints).
  • Categorical — a fixed set of values: nominal (unordered, e.g. department names), binary (two values, e.g. yes/no), or ordinal (ordered but with unknown gaps, e.g. a 1–5 Likert item).
TipType decides what’s valid — the Overture’s measurement levels, operationalised

Knowing the type isn’t bookkeeping; it determines which statistics and plots even mean something (Bruce et al. 2020). A scatterplot illuminates two numeric variables but collapses into an uninformative “stack” of points for ordinal data. An arithmetic mean is meaningless for nominal data — the “average” of Accounting and Shipping is nothing at all (Bruce et al. 2020). This is the measurement-levels lesson from the Overture, now as a daily practical constraint: match the method to the type, or compute nonsense precisely.

Missing data

Missingness is a fact of social-science life — participants refuse questions, drop out over time (attrition), or a researcher mis-records a value (Albers 2017). Why data is missing matters enormously:

  • Missing Completely At Random (MCAR) — no systematic pattern; the gaps are unrelated to anything. The benign case (Albers 2017).
  • Missing Not At Random (MNAR) — the missingness is systematic and informative. If high-income respondents disproportionately refuse to report income, the holes themselves are biased, creating hidden rival explanations for whatever you find (Albers 2017).

Two common fixes, both with risks (Albers 2017):

  • Listwise deletion — drop any record with a missing value. Simple, but it shrinks the sample and, if data is MNAR, leaves a non-representative remainder.
  • Imputation — fill the gaps. Mean substitution (insert the average) is widely criticised because it artificially shrinks variance and dampens correlations with other variables; regression imputation predicts the missing value from other variables and is usually better (Albers 2017).

Worked example (Albers). In a birth-weight study, 197 of 1,388 observations were dropped because parental-education data was missing — those cases simply fell out of the regression (Albers 2017). If the missingness was related to the outcome, that deletion quietly biased the result.

Outliers

An outlier is an observation lying an abnormal distance from the rest (Bruce et al. 2020). Standard detectors: values more than 3 standard deviations from the mean, or the boxplot’s 1.5 × IQR rule beyond the quartiles (Bruce et al. 2020; Albers 2017).

The temptation is to delete them. Resist it. Outliers matter doubly because OLS regression is acutely sensitive to them — least squares minimises squared residuals, so one extreme point carries enormous weight (Bruce et al. 2020). But Albers warns against blind deletion: an outlier may be a genuine anomaly worth studying, a sign of a bimodal mix of two populations, or just the tail of a long-tailed distribution — not an error (Albers 2017).

Worked example (Albers). In a typing-speed study where most scores cluster near 150 wpm, a single value of 54 wpm drags the group mean down to 133.67; delete it and the mean jumps to 149.6 — a materially different conclusion from one decision (Albers 2017). That single point deserves investigation, not a reflexive delete key.

Long-tailed distributions

The Normal curve is iconic, and — as P7 warned — a poor default for raw data (Bruce et al. 2020). Real variables are routinely skewed (asymmetric) or heavy-tailed (high kurtosis: a propensity for extreme values) (Bruce et al. 2020).

The stakes are captured by the black swan idea: extreme, anomalous events — market crashes, viral cascades, mega-claims — occur far more often than a Normal model predicts (Bruce et al. 2020). Tukey’s own observation is the useful mental model: data is often “normal in the middle” but with much longer tails than the bell allows (Bruce et al. 2020). Pricing risk, or any decision dominated by extremes, off a Normal assumption systematically understates the danger.

Worked example (Bruce & Bruce). A QQ-plot of Netflix (NFLX) stock returns bends sharply away from the straight Normal reference line at both ends — concrete evidence that extreme gains and losses strike far more often than Normality anticipates (Bruce et al. 2020).

Show the code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statistics import NormalDist

rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
data = rng.standard_t(df=3, size=2000)          # heavy-tailed (Student-t, 3 df)
data = (data - data.mean()) / data.std()

# Theoretical Normal quantiles vs sorted standardized data.
# inv_cdf is the standard-normal inverse CDF (no scipy needed).
n = data.size
probs = (np.arange(1, n + 1) - 0.5) / n
nd = NormalDist()
theo = np.array([nd.inv_cdf(p) for p in probs])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 4.2))
ax.scatter(theo, np.sort(data), s=10, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.6)
lim = [-4, 4]
ax.plot(lim, lim, color="#b5446e", lw=1.8)
ax.set_xlim(lim); ax.set_ylim(-8, 8)
ax.set_xlabel("Normal theoretical quantiles")
ax.set_ylabel("observed quantiles (standardized)")
ax.set_title("QQ-plot: tails peel off the Normal line")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Data cleaning and data quality

The governing principle is GIGO — garbage in, garbage out: even the most powerful algorithm cannot salvage valid findings from invalid data (Albers 2017). The usual culprits (Albers 2017):

  • Data-entry errors — typing 500 for 50.
  • Measurement errors — poorly calibrated instruments.
  • Bad units — mixing meters and kilometres in one column.

These are not cosmetic. Messy data threatens both internal validity (did the variable really cause the effect?) and external validity (do the results generalise?) — the credibility criteria from the Overture (Albers 2017).

Worked example (Albers). In a mindfulness study, a variable’s standard deviation (9.9) exceeded its mean (8.6) — a red flag. Chasing it down revealed a data-entry error: a score of 50 had been recorded as 500, single-handedly inflating the variance (Albers 2017). Note the method: a robust summary check from D1 caught the error — EDA and clean data are the same discipline.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Deleting cases with missing data without asking why. If the data is MNAR, listwise deletion bakes the bias in. Diagnose the missingness mechanism before dropping anything.
  • Mean-imputing blindly. It shrinks variance and weakens correlations — flattering your precision while distorting relationships.
  • Reflexively removing outliers. They may be the real signal — an anomaly, a second population, or a fat tail. Investigate first; document any removal.
  • Assuming Normality for raw data. Heavy tails are the rule, not the exception, for money, counts, and durations; a QQ-plot reveals them in seconds.
  • Skipping the units/entry sanity checks. SD larger than a positive mean, impossible values, suspiciously round numbers — these red flags catch the errors that wreck conclusions.
  • Treating cleaning as menial. It is where most real errors are made or caught. Garbage in, garbage out — no model fixes it downstream.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Budget real time for cleaning. Plan for it to dominate the project; it usually does, and it’s where validity is won or lost.
  • Diagnose missingness before handling it. Ask whether gaps could relate to the outcome (MNAR); if so, deletion and naïve imputation both bias the result.

  • Run cheap sanity checks. SD vs mean, min/max, value counts, and a QQ-plot catch a surprising share of fatal errors in minutes — the same robust summaries from D1.
