Famous Paradoxes: Simpson, Monty Hall & the Prosecutor

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The same data can say opposite things depending on how you slice it — and only a causal story tells you which slice is right. Four puzzles where intuition fails, and why they matter.

Numbers do not speak for themselves. A drug can help every subgroup yet appear to harm the whole; a 1% match can mean 99% guilt or near-innocence; switching doors can double your odds. These famous paradoxes are not parlour tricks — they are the clearest demonstrations that probability alone is not enough, and that deciding what to condition on is a causal judgment, not a statistical one.

Why it matters

Every paradox here is a trap a working analyst can fall into with real data: aggregating when you should stratify (or vice versa), confusing a conditional with its reverse, or reading a correlation manufactured by your own sample selection. They are the intuition pumps behind Bayes’ theorem, confounding and colliders, and the whole case for causal reasoning in the social sciences.

Simpson’s paradox: the great reversal

Simpson’s paradox is when an association that holds in the whole population reverses in every subpopulation — a puzzle about confounding that “statistics alone cannot solve” (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018).

Pearl’s worked example — a fictional heart-attack drug (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018):

Group Control heart-attack rate Drug rate Drug better?
Women 5% 7.5% ✗ worse
Men 30% 40% ✗ worse
Everyone (aggregated) 22% 18% ✓ better?!

The drug looks worse for women and worse for men, yet better overall. Why? Unequal subgroup sizes plus a lurking confounder: women (lower baseline risk) were much more likely to take the drug, so the aggregate “drug group” was disproportionately low-risk, mixing the drug’s effect with gender’s (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018).

Pearl’s central point: only a causal model resolves it (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018). The numbers cannot tell you which table to trust:

  • If the third variable is a confounder (like gender, a cause of both taking the drug and the outcome), you must adjust — trust the partitioned data (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018).
  • If the third variable is a mediator (e.g. blood pressure that the drug lowers on the way to helping), you must not adjust — adjusting would mask the drug’s real benefit (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018).

Same data, opposite correct answers, decided entirely by the causal story.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
def group(x_lo, x_hi, intercept, n):
    x = rng.uniform(x_lo, x_hi, n)
    y = intercept - 1.0*x + rng.normal(0, 0.6, n)   # within-group slope = -1
    return x, y

xA, yA = group(1, 5, 10, 120)     # group A: low x, lower band
xB, yB = group(5, 9, 16, 120)     # group B: high x, higher band  -> positive aggregate

x_all = np.concatenate([xA, xB]); y_all = np.concatenate([yA, yB])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.3))
for x, y, c, lab in [(xA, yA, "#1f4e79", "group A"), (xB, yB, "#d98a29", "group B")]:
    ax.scatter(x, y, s=14, color=c, alpha=0.55, label=lab)
    b1, b0 = np.polyfit(x, y, 1)
    xs = np.linspace(x.min(), x.max(), 20)
    ax.plot(xs, b0+b1*xs, "--", color=c, lw=2)
b1, b0 = np.polyfit(x_all, y_all, 1)
xs = np.linspace(1, 9, 30)
ax.plot(xs, b0+b1*xs, "-", color="black", lw=2.6, label=f"overall fit (slope +{b1:.2f})")
ax.text(2, 4.2, "within groups: slope −1.0", fontsize=9, color="#444")
ax.set_xlabel("x"); ax.set_ylabel("y")
ax.set_title("Simpson's paradox: within-group ↓ but aggregate ↑")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Monty Hall: the host’s informed action

Three doors: a car behind one, goats behind two (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018). You pick Door 1; the host — who knows where the car is — opens a different door (say Door 3) revealing a goat, and offers a switch to Door 2. Should you? Yes — switching wins 2/3 of the time (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018).

Intuition screams 50/50 (two doors left), but that’s wrong: your initial pick had only a 1/3 chance, and the host’s action is not random — he must open a goat door and cannot open yours, so his choice “pushes” the remaining 2/3 probability onto the other closed door (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018). Pearl frames the host’s opened door as a collider — it is caused by both your choice and the car’s location — so conditioning on it induces a dependence between your door and the prize, making the other door twice as likely (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018).

The prosecutor’s fallacy: base-rate neglect

The prosecutor’s fallacy is the transposed conditional — confusing P(\text{evidence}\mid \text{innocent}) with P(\text{innocent}\mid\text{evidence}) (Wheelan 2013).

The example: a crime-scene blood type found in 1% of the population (Wheelan 2013). The prosecutor argues: “only a 1% chance an innocent person matches, so 99% chance the defendant is guilty.” Wrong. In a city of 800,000, about 8,000 people match — so the defendant, absent other evidence, is 1 of 8,000 suspects, and the probability of guilt is tiny (Wheelan 2013). The lesson is pure Bayes: you must start from the base rate (prior) and update; ignoring it yields nonsense (Wheelan 2013).

Berkson’s paradox: collider bias by selection

Berkson’s paradox shows how conditioning on a common effect manufactures a spurious association between unrelated causes (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018). Wheelan and Pearl’s dating example: why do attractive people seem to be “jerks”? If your dating pool admits anyone who is either very attractive or very nice (conditioning on the common effect “someone you’d date”), then mean-and-unattractive people are excluded — leaving a negative correlation between looks and personality that doesn’t exist in the population (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018). The hospital version: two unrelated diseases look correlated among admitted patients, because people with neither aren’t in the hospital (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018).

The thread: conditioning is a causal act

All four share one moral. Whether you should condition on a variable — stratify, control, select — depends on its causal role:

  • Confounder (common cause) → do condition (Simpson’s: adjust for gender).
  • Mediator (on the causal path) → don’t condition (Simpson’s: don’t adjust for blood pressure).
  • Collider (common effect) → don’t condition; doing so creates bias (Monty Hall, Berkson’s).

No amount of data tells you which is which — that knowledge comes from a causal model. This is the Book of Why’s thesis in miniature.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Always trusting the aggregate (or always the subgroups). Simpson’s shows either can be right — decide by the causal role of the grouping variable. (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018)
  • Treating the host’s door (or any collider) as uninformative. Conditioning on a common effect changes the probabilities; ignore that and you misjudge Monty Hall and selection-biased samples. (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018)
  • Transposing the conditional. P(\text{match}\mid\text{innocent})\ne P(\text{innocent}\mid\text{match}); always bring in the base rate. (Wheelan 2013)
  • Reading correlations in a selected sample as real. Berkson’s bias is everywhere data is filtered on an outcome (hospitalised, admitted, hired, dated). (Pearl and Mackenzie 2018)

Appendix · the mathematics

Simpson’s algebra. The aggregate rate is a size-weighted average of subgroup rates. With drug-group women heavily outnumbering drug-group men (and women low-risk), the drug average \approx w\cdot(\text{low women rate}) + (1-w)\cdot(\text{men rate}) puts large weight w on the low value; the control average reverses the weights, putting more weight on high-risk men. Different weights on the same two risk levels flip the comparison — even though, level-for-level, the drug is worse. The reversal is a weighting artefact; the fix is to compare like with like (the causal adjustment).

Monty Hall by Bayes. Let the car be behind Door 1, 2, or 3 with prior 1/3 each; you pick Door 1. The host opens Door 3. P(\text{host opens 3}\mid \text{car in 1})=1/2, P(\cdot\mid\text{car in 2})=1, P(\cdot\mid\text{car in 3})=0. By Bayes, P(\text{car in 2}\mid\text{opens 3}) = \dfrac{1\cdot\frac13}{\frac12\cdot\frac13 + 1\cdot\frac13} = \dfrac{1/3}{1/6 + 1/3} = \dfrac{1/3}{1/2} = \tfrac{2}{3}, versus P(\text{car in 1}\mid\text{opens 3})=1/3. Switch.

Prosecutor by Bayes. With prior guilt P(G)=1/800{,}000 (one true culprit) and a match that is certain if guilty, P(M\mid G)=1, and P(M\mid \bar G)=0.01: P(G\mid M)=\frac{1\cdot\frac1{800{,}000}}{1\cdot\frac1{800{,}000} + 0.01\cdot\frac{799{,}999}{800{,}000}} \approx \frac{1}{8001} — about 1/8000, not 0.99. The likelihood is not the posterior.

Collider bias, formally. If A\to C\leftarrow B with A\perp B marginally, then conditioning on the collider C generally makes A\not\perp B\mid C — an induced dependence. Berkson’s “you’d date them” and Monty’s “opened door” are both C.

  not a mediator); **Monty Hall** (1/3 vs 2/3, the host's informed action, the **collider** framing);
  **Berkson's paradox / collider bias** (the attractive-jerks dating pool and the hospital example).
- **Wheelan, *Naked Statistics*** — the **prosecutor's fallacy** / transposed conditional and **base-rate
  neglect** (the 1%-blood-type, 8,000-suspects example).

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Simpson's algebra.** The aggregate rate is a **size-weighted average** of subgroup rates. With drug-group
women heavily outnumbering drug-group men (and women low-risk), the drug average
$\approx w\cdot(\text{low women rate}) + (1-w)\cdot(\text{men rate})$ puts large weight $w$ on the low value;
the control average reverses the weights, putting more weight on high-risk men. Different weights on the same
two risk levels flip the comparison — even though, level-for-level, the drug is worse. The reversal is a
weighting artefact; the fix is to compare **like with like** (the causal adjustment).

**Monty Hall by Bayes.** Let the car be behind Door 1, 2, or 3 with prior $1/3$ each; you pick Door 1. The
host opens Door 3. $P(\text{host opens 3}\mid \text{car in 1})=1/2$, $P(\cdot\mid\text{car in 2})=1$,
$P(\cdot\mid\text{car in 3})=0$. By Bayes, $P(\text{car in 2}\mid\text{opens 3}) =
\dfrac{1\cdot\frac13}{\frac12\cdot\frac13 + 1\cdot\frac13} = \dfrac{1/3}{1/6 + 1/3} = \dfrac{1/3}{1/2} = \tfrac{2}{3}$,
versus $P(\text{car in 1}\mid\text{opens 3})=1/3$. Switch.

**Prosecutor by Bayes.** With prior guilt $P(G)=1/800{,}000$ (one true culprit) and a match that is certain
if guilty, $P(M\mid G)=1$, and $P(M\mid \bar G)=0.01$:
$P(G\mid M)=\frac{1\cdot\frac1{800{,}000}}{1\cdot\frac1{800{,}000} + 0.01\cdot\frac{799{,}999}{800{,}000}}
\approx \frac{1}{8001}$ — about $1/8000$, not $0.99$. The likelihood is not the posterior.

**Collider bias, formally.** If $A\to C\leftarrow B$ with $A\perp B$ marginally, then conditioning on the
collider $C$ generally makes $A\not\perp B\mid C$ — an induced dependence. Berkson's "you'd date them" and
Monty's "opened door" are both $C$.

:::
