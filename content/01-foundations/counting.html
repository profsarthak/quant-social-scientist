Counting: The Arithmetic of Possibility

When outcomes are equally likely, probability is counting — so learn to count without listing.

The classical formula P(E) = |E|/|S| turns every probability into two counts: the favourable outcomes over the total. The trouble is that real sample spaces are huge — a 9-person batting order has 362,880 arrangements — so you must count without enumerating. Three tools do almost all the work: the multiplication principle, permutations (order matters), and combinations (order doesn’t).

Why it matters

This is the most mechanical entry in the chapter, and the most quietly important. The combination count \binom{n}{r} you meet here is the same \binom{n}{r} that sits at the front of the binomial distribution in P7, which in turn drives the analysis of yes/no survey data, polling, and A/B tests. Sampling with and without replacement — the backbone of survey sampling — is a counting question. Learn to count cleanly now and several later results arrive pre-assembled.

The deeper habit is the one from the De Méré story: intuition about “how many ways” is unreliable, and a small slip in the count becomes a wrong probability. The tools below replace guessing with bookkeeping.

The multiplication principle

The foundation: if one choice can be made in m ways and a second independent choice in n ways, the two together can be made in m \times n ways. Extend it to any number of stages by multiplying across them (Ross 2018; Grinstead and Snell 1997).

Worked example (Grinstead & Snell). A meal has 2 appetiser choices, 3 main courses, and 2 desserts. The number of distinct meals is 2 \times 3 \times 2 = 12 (Grinstead and Snell 1997).

This single idea — multiply the choices at each stage — generates both of the formulas below; they are just the multiplication principle applied to specific selection problems.

Permutations — when order matters

A permutation is an ordered arrangement. To order n distinct objects, you have n choices for the first slot, n-1 for the second, and so on — so by the multiplication principle there are n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1 arrangements (Ross 2018).

Worked example (Ross). A baseball team of 9 players can bat in 9! = 362{,}880 different orders (Ross 2018).

If you arrange only r of the n objects (still caring about order), you stop after r slots: {}_nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times (n-r+1).

When objects repeat, some orderings become indistinguishable, and you divide them out:

Worked example (Ross). The word PEPPER has 6 letters, but with 3 P’s and 2 E’s identical, the number of distinguishable arrangements is \frac{6!}{3!\,2!\,1!} = 60, not 6! = 720 (Ross 2018). Dividing by 3! and 2! removes the rearrangements of identical letters that don’t produce a new “word.”

Combinations — when order doesn’t matter

A combination is an unordered selection of r objects from n. Because order no longer distinguishes selections, you take the ordered count {}_nP_r and divide by the r! orderings of each chosen group: \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}, read “n choose r” (Ross 2018; Grinstead and Snell 1997). This is the binomial coefficient.

Worked examples.

  • Choosing a committee of 5 from 10 people: \binom{10}{5} = \dfrac{10 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7 \cdot 6}{5 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1} = 252 (Grinstead and Snell 1997).
  • Choosing a committee of 3 from 20 people: \binom{20}{3} = \dfrac{20 \cdot 19 \cdot 18}{3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1} = 1{,}140 (Ross 2018).
TipThe one question that picks the tool

Before counting, ask: does order matter? A batting order, a ranked podium, a PIN — order matters, use permutations. A committee, a hand of cards, a random sample of respondents — order is irrelevant, use combinations. Most counting mistakes are really this-question-answered-wrong: counting arrangements when you wanted selections (overcounting by a factor of r!), or vice versa.

Why social scientists care: sampling

Drawing a sample of n people from a population of N — without replacement and without regard to order — is exactly a combination: there are \binom{N}{n} possible samples. Every statement that a simple random sample is “equally likely to be any subset” rests on this count, and the machinery of sampling distributions (the flagship CLT article) is built on top of it. Counting is not a detour from social science; it is the hidden floor under survey inference.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Using permutations when order is irrelevant. Counting ordered arrangements for an unordered selection overcounts by exactly r!. A “committee” is a combination, not a permutation.
  • Forgetting to divide out repeats. Treating PEPPER’s identical letters as distinct inflates the count more than tenfold (720 vs 60). Whenever objects are interchangeable, divide by the factorials of their multiplicities.
  • Confusing with- and without-replacement. Sampling the same person twice (with replacement) and sampling two different people (without) give different counts and different probabilities — be explicit about which the design uses.
  • Multiplying when you should add (or vice versa). Multiply across independent stages of a single arrangement (“and”); add across mutually exclusive cases (“or”). Mixing these up is the structural error beneath most wrong counts.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Decide “order or not?” before touching a formula. That single question selects permutation vs combination and prevents the most common error outright.
  • Translate “and” → multiply, “or” → add. Stages joined by “and” multiply; mutually exclusive cases joined by “or” add. Narrate the problem and the arithmetic follows.
  • Use the complement when “at least one” appears. Counting the complement (“none”) is usually far smaller than counting every favourable case directly — the same trick as in the axioms entry.
  • Recognise \binom{n}{r} when it returns. The same coefficient reappears in the binomial distribution and Pascal’s triangle; spotting it early makes P7 feel like review rather than new material.

Appendix · the mathematics

Permutations of r from n. By the multiplication principle, the number of ways to fill r ordered slots from n distinct objects is {}_nP_r = n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}.

Combinations from permutations. Each unordered selection of r objects can be ordered in r! ways, so {}_nP_r = r! \binom{n}{r}, giving \binom{n}{r} = \frac{{}_nP_r}{r!} = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}.

Symmetry and Pascal’s rule. Directly from the formula, \binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r}, \qquad \binom{n}{r} = \binom{n-1}{r-1} + \binom{n-1}{r}. The second identity (Pascal’s rule) is the engine of Pascal’s triangle: choosing r from n either includes a fixed element (then choose r-1 from the rest) or excludes it (choose r from the rest).

The binomial theorem. The same coefficients expand powers of a sum: (x + y)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} x^{r} y^{\,n-r}, because forming an x^r y^{n-r} term means choosing which r of the n factors contribute an x — a combination. This is exactly why \binom{n}{r} heads the binomial distribution in P7: it counts the number of sequences with r successes.

Connections

  • Builds on: Sample spaces & the axioms — counting is how you evaluate P(E) = |E|/|S| when outcomes are equally likely.
  • Leads to: Conditional probability, where counting restricted sample spaces becomes second nature.
  • Pays off in: the common distributions (the binomial coefficient is the heart of the binomial distribution) and survey sampling (a random sample is a combination).

**Permutations of $r$ from $n$.** By the multiplication principle, the number of ways to fill
$r$ ordered slots from $n$ distinct objects is
$$
