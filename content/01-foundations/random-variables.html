Random Variables (Discrete & Continuous)

From ‘which outcome happened’ to ‘what number did we get’ — the move that lets us do arithmetic on chance.

A random variable is not a variable in the algebra sense and not really random — it’s a function that attaches a number to each outcome of an experiment. That small reframing is what turns probability into something you can average, add, and plot. Two species cover everything you need: discrete variables you can count, and continuous ones you can only measure.

Why it matters

Up to now we’ve talked about events — sets of outcomes. But almost every quantity a social scientist studies is a number: a test score, an income, a count of protests, a vote share. The random variable is the bridge: it lets us stop describing outcomes one set at a time and start summarising them with a single distribution, then compress that distribution into an expectation and a variance. Every histogram you’ll ever draw, every “the average effect was…”, every regression coefficient is downstream of this definition.

The discrete/continuous split also previews a practical fork: discrete data invite counts and proportions, continuous data invite densities and areas, and the two call for different tools throughout the book.

What a random variable is

Formally, a random variable is a real-valued function defined on the sample space — it maps each outcome to a number (Ross 2018). The motivation is honest convenience: we are usually more interested in a function of the outcome than the raw outcome itself. Roll two dice and you rarely care about the ordered pair (4,2); you care about the sum, 6. “Sum of the dice” is a random variable (Ross 2018).

The two species:

  • Discrete — takes at most a countable set of values. The number of heads in ten flips, the count of yes-responses in a survey, the number of children in a household. You can list the possible values (Ross 2018).
  • Continuous — takes values in an uncountable continuum. A person’s exact weight, the lifetime of a device, a precise income or waiting time. You can measure it to arbitrary precision but never enumerate the possibilities (Ross 2018).

Describing the distribution: pmf, pdf, cdf

How you assign probability depends on the species.

Discrete → probability mass function (pmf). The pmf gives the probability of each value, p(a) = P\{X = a\}, and these masses must sum to 1 over all possible values (Ross 2018). Picture a bar at each value whose height is its probability.

Continuous → probability density function (pdf). Here probability is an area. A density f(x) is a curve such that the probability X lands in a region B is the area under f over B (Ross 2018): P\{X \in B\} = \int_B f(x)\,dx, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = 1.

TipWhy P(X = x) = 0 for continuous variables

For a continuous variable, the probability of any exact value is zero — the “width” of a single point is zero, so the area above it is zero (Ross 2018). This sounds paradoxical (some value does occur!) but it’s the right bookkeeping: for continuous quantities, only intervals carry probability. “The chance someone weighs exactly 70.000… kg” is 0; “the chance they weigh between 69.5 and 70.5 kg” is positive. The density f(x) is not a probability — it’s probability per unit length, which is why it can exceed 1.

Both → cumulative distribution function (cdf). The cdf works for any random variable: F(b) = P\{X \le b\}. It accumulates probability from the left. For a discrete variable it’s a step function (jumping at each value by that value’s mass); for a continuous variable it’s a smooth curve, and the pdf is exactly its derivative, f(x) = F'(x) (Ross 2018). The cdf is the one description that unifies the two species — handy whenever you need “the probability of at most” or want to compare distributions on equal footing.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reading a density as a probability. f(x) can be greater than 1; it is probability per unit, and only becomes a probability once integrated over an interval. Heights of a pdf are not chances.
  • Asking for P(X = x) on continuous data. It’s always 0. Rephrase as an interval, or use the cdf for “at most”/“at least.”
  • Forcing continuous data into the discrete mold (or vice versa). Counts (protests, children) are genuinely discrete; binning them or modelling them as Normal can mislead, especially for rare counts (see the Poisson).
  • Forgetting the distribution must sum/integrate to 1. A proposed pmf or pdf that doesn’t normalise isn’t a valid distribution — a quick, error-catching sanity check.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Ross, A First Course in Probability — random variable as a real-valued function on the sample space (the “sum of two dice” motivation); discrete (countable) vs continuous (uncountable) variables; pmf and its summing to 1; pdf with P\{X\in B\}=\int_B f; P(X=x)=0 for continuous variables; the cdf F(b)=P\{X\le b\} as a step function (discrete) and the pdf as the derivative of the cdf (continuous).

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Name the random variable explicitly. Writing “Let X = number of yes-responses” forces you to fix the support and the species before computing anything.
  • Decide discrete vs continuous early. It selects your whole toolkit — masses vs densities, sums vs integrals, bar charts vs histograms.
  • Use the cdf for “at least / at most” questions. It sidesteps fiddly summation and is the natural object for quantiles and percentiles later.
  • Always check it normalises. If your masses don’t sum to 1 (or your density doesn’t integrate to 1), something is wrong before you’ve even started.

Appendix · the mathematics

Properties of the cdf. For any random variable, F is (i) non-decreasing, (ii) right- continuous, with (iii) \lim_{b\to-\infty}F(b)=0 and \lim_{b\to\infty}F(b)=1. Probabilities of intervals come straight from it: P\{a < X \le b\} = F(b) - F(a).

Discrete cdf as a sum of jumps. F(b) = \sum_{a \le b} p(a); the jump at each value a is exactly p(a) = F(a) - F(a^-), recovering the pmf from the cdf.

Continuous cdf and pdf. F(b) = \int_{-\infty}^{b} f(x)\,dx, so by the fundamental theorem of calculus f(x) = F'(x) wherever F is differentiable. The probability of an exact point is P\{X=x\} = F(x) - F(x^-) = 0 because F is continuous.

Functions of a random variable. If Y = g(X), its distribution can be found from X’s — but remarkably, you don’t need Y’s distribution to get its mean: that’s the job of LOTUS in the next entry.

Connections

  • Builds on: Sample spaces & the axioms — a random variable is a function on the sample space, and its probabilities inherit the axioms.
  • Leads to: Expectation & variance — once you have a distribution, you summarise it by a centre and a spread.
  • Pays off in: the common distributions (named pmfs and pdfs) and the sampling distribution of a statistic, which is itself the distribution of a random variable.
