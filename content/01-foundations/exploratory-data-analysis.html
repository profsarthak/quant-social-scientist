Exploratory Data Analysis & Visual Summaries

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Look before you model. The five-minute habit that catches the errors no test will.

Before a single hypothesis is tested, you look. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is the discipline of summarising and graphing data to build intuition, spot errors, find outliers, and check whether the data even fits the methods you planned. A histogram or boxplot routinely reveals in seconds what a table of numbers hides — skew, gaps, two-populations, a data-entry typo. EDA is not a preliminary to the “real” analysis; it is the step that decides whether the real analysis is valid.

Why it matters

The previous entry reduced a variable to a few numbers; this one insists you also see it, because numbers alone deceive. A mean and standard deviation are identical for a clean bell and for a skewed mess with an outlier — but a glance at the picture is not. EDA is where you catch the data-entry error, notice the bimodal split, and discover that your “Normal” variable is a long-tailed monster that will break the tests ahead. Every later chapter assumes you looked first. Skipping EDA is how confident analyses quietly go wrong.

The EDA philosophy: look before you model

EDA is younger than most of statistics. For its first century the field was almost entirely about inference — using small samples to draw conclusions about large populations (Bruce et al. 2020). In 1962 John Tukey called for a reformation in his paper The Future of Data Analysis, arguing that inference should be only one component of a broader practice; he established the discipline fully in his 1977 classic Exploratory Data Analysis (Bruce et al. 2020).

The creed is simple: the most important first step in any data project is to look at the data (Bruce et al. 2020). Summarise it, graph it, and you gain intuition you can get no other way. Albers makes the practical case: EDA surfaces patterns the human eye cannot see in raw numbers, flags errors like data-entry mistakes, locates outliers, and reveals whether the distribution (Normal? skewed?) actually meets the assumptions of the test you intended to run (Albers 2017). That last function — checking assumptions before committing to a method — is what makes EDA a safeguard, not a formality.

Percentiles and the five-number summary

A percentile is a value below which a given percentage of the data falls — the p-th percentile has p\% of values at or beneath it (Bruce et al. 2020). A quantile is the same idea indexed by fractions (the 0.8 quantile is the 80th percentile). Percentiles are inherently robust: they describe positions in the sorted data, untouched by how extreme the extremes are.

The five-number summary captures a distribution’s location and spread in five robust values (Bruce et al. 2020): \text{Min} \;\le\; Q_1\,(25\text{th}) \;\le\; \text{Median}\,(50\text{th}) \;\le\; Q_3\,(75\text{th}) \;\le\; \text{Max}.

Worked example (Bruce & Bruce). State murder rates: the 5th percentile is 1.6 per 100,000, the median is 4.0, and the 95th percentile is 6.51 (Bruce et al. 2020). Three numbers already sketch the whole distribution’s centre and reach.

The boxplot

Tukey’s boxplot (box-and-whisker) turns the five-number summary into a picture (Bruce et al. 2020):

  • The box spans Q_1 to Q_3 — the middle 50% of the data, i.e. the IQR; the line inside marks the median.
  • The whiskers extend to the furthest points within 1.5 \times \text{IQR} of the box (the common R convention); Albers notes other variants reach to the absolute min/max or to one standard deviation (Bruce et al. 2020; Albers 2017).
  • Points beyond the whiskers are drawn individually as outliers (Bruce et al. 2020).

Worked example (Bruce & Bruce). A boxplot of state populations sits with its median near 4.4 million, the box skewed toward the bottom (small states vary little), while a few giants like California float up top as individual outlier points (Bruce et al. 2020). The asymmetry is the skew, made visible.

Histograms, frequency tables, and density plots

To see a numeric variable’s whole distribution (Bruce et al. 2020):

  • A frequency table tallies counts into equal-width bins.
  • A histogram plots that table — bins on the x-axis, counts on the y-axis, bars contiguous (no gaps), with empty bins shown so you can see where data is missing (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • A density plot is a smoothed histogram (a kernel density estimate), drawn as a continuous curve with the y-axis as a proportion rather than a raw count (Bruce et al. 2020).

Worked example (Bruce & Bruce). A 10-bin histogram of state populations puts 24 states in the smallest bin and 14 in the next, while the largest bin — holding only California — is separated from the rest by several empty bins, visually screaming “extreme value” (Bruce et al. 2020).

Show the code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(11)
data = rng.lognormal(mean=1.0, sigma=0.8, size=2000)   # right-skewed

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 3.8),
                         gridspec_kw={"width_ratios": [2.4, 1]})

axes[0].hist(data, bins=50, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85)
axes[0].set_title("Histogram: shape and the long tail")
axes[0].set_xlabel("value"); axes[0].set_yticks([])

bp = axes[1].boxplot(data, vert=True, widths=0.5, patch_artist=True)
bp["boxes"][0].set(facecolor="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.6)
bp["medians"][0].set(color="#b5446e", linewidth=2)
axes[1].set_title("Boxplot: five-number summary")
axes[1].set_xticks([])

fig.suptitle("Same data, two lenses — and the skew is obvious in both", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Categorical and binary data

Numeric summaries don’t apply to categories; you summarise them differently (Bruce et al. 2020):

  • Proportions — the share of records in each category — and the mode, the most frequent category, are the basic summaries.
  • Bar charts display them, with gaps between bars to signal the categories are discrete and mutually exclusive — the deliberate visual opposite of a histogram’s touching bars (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • When categories map to numbers (price tiers, say), the expected value acts as a weighted mean, the category values weighted by their probabilities (Bruce et al. 2020) — the empirical echo of expectation.

Worked example (Bruce & Bruce). Causes of Dallas/Fort Worth airport delays: Carrier 23.02%, ATC 30.40%, Weather 4.03%, Security 0.12% — with the mode, inbound-aircraft delays, at 42.43% (Bruce et al. 2020). Proportions and a mode tell the whole categorical story.

Two or more variables at once

To explore relationships (Bruce et al. 2020):

  • The scatterplot is the standard view of two numeric variables.
  • The correlation coefficient r \in [-1, +1] measures linear strength and direction (the covariance/correlation entry, now as an EDA tool); a correlation matrix tabulates it for every pair at once — a fast scan for structure.
  • For large data, where a scatterplot becomes an ink blot: hexagonal binning shades hexagons by point density, contour plots draw density like a topographic map, and contingency tables cross-tabulate two categorical variables with row/column percentages (Bruce et al. 2020).

Worked examples (Bruce & Bruce). A scatterplot of daily returns for AT&T and Verizon shows a strong positive correlation — the stocks move in tandem. A hexbin plot of home size versus tax-assessed value reveals a main peak and a distinct secondary cluster of high-value, small-size homes — structure a raw scatterplot would have buried (Bruce et al. 2020).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Modelling before looking. Jumping to a test without a histogram is how skew, outliers, and bimodality silently break assumptions. Always plot first.
  • Confusing a bar chart with a histogram. Touching bars = a binned numeric variable; gapped bars = categorical. The spacing carries meaning.
  • Over-binning or under-binning a histogram. Too few bins hide structure; too many turn it to noise. Try several bin widths before believing a shape.
  • Trusting one summary plot for big, dense data. An overplotted scatter looks like a blob; reach for hexbin or contours to recover the density.
  • Treating outliers flagged by a boxplot as errors. The 1.5\timesIQR rule is a flag, not a verdict — investigate before deleting (next entry).

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Make a histogram and a boxplot your reflex. Two plots, thirty seconds, and most data-quality surprises surface before they cost you.
  • Read the boxplot for skew. A median line low in the box with a long upper whisker and high outliers is right skew at a glance.
  • Vary the bin width. Shapes that survive several binnings are real; shapes that don’t are artefacts.
  • Use a correlation matrix as a map, not a verdict. It points to relationships worth a closer scatterplot — but remember it only sees linear association (P8).

  contour plots, contingency tables (AT&T/Verizon; home size vs tax value).
- **Albers, *Quantitative Data Analysis*** — EDA reveals patterns the eye misses in raw numbers,
  flags data-entry errors and outliers, and checks whether the distribution fits the intended test;
  boxplot whisker variations.
:::

## Practitioner's notes — tips & tricks {.unnumbered}

- **Make a histogram and a boxplot your reflex.** Two plots, thirty seconds, and most data-quality
  surprises surface before they cost you.
- **Read the boxplot for skew.** A median line low in the box with a long upper whisker and high
  outliers is right skew at a glance.
- **Vary the bin width.** Shapes that survive several binnings are real; shapes that don't are
  artefacts.
- **Use a correlation matrix as a map, not a verdict.** It points to relationships worth a closer
  scatterplot — but remember it only sees *linear* association
  ([P8](covariance-and-correlation.qmd)).
