Conditional Expectation

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The best guess about one thing once you know another — and the exact idea that becomes regression.

Plain expectation answers “what’s the average?” Conditional expectation answers the question researchers actually ask: “what’s the average for people like this?” That small addition — an average that depends on what you already know — turns out to be the single most important object in applied social science. It is, quite literally, what a regression estimates.

Why it matters

Almost every empirical question is conditional. Not “what is income?” but “what is income given education?” Not “what is recidivism?” but “what is recidivism given the treatment?” The tool that makes “given” precise is conditional expectation, and it is the hinge between the probability you have learned and the regression and causal inference still ahead.

Two results in this entry do enormous work. The law of iterated expectations lets you rebuild a whole-population average from subgroup averages. And the fact that conditional expectation is the best possible predictor is the reason regression — which estimates exactly this object — sits at the centre of the field. Master this and regression stops being a formula and becomes inevitable.

Conditional expectation: the average given what you know

For a specific known value Y=y, the conditional expectation of X is just the mean of the conditional distribution — the ordinary average, but computed with conditional probabilities (Ross 2018):

E[X \mid Y=y] = \sum_x x\,P(X=x \mid Y=y) \quad\text{(discrete)}, \qquad E[X \mid Y=y] = \int x\, f_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)\,dx \quad\text{(continuous)}.

So far this is one number for each y. Now the conceptual leap that trips up every newcomer and unlocks everything:

ImportantE[X | Y] is itself a random variable

E[X\mid Y=y] is a number. But E[X\mid Y] — before you fix a value of Y — is a function of the random variable Y, and therefore a random variable in its own right, with its own distribution, mean, and variance. (Ross 2018) It is the rule “whatever Y turns out to be, report the average of X among cases with that Y.” Because Y is random, that reported value is random too.

A clean Ross example: if X and Y are independent binomials with the same parameters and you are told only their sum X+Y=m, then by symmetry E[X \mid X+Y=m] = m/2 — the total splits evenly in expectation. (Ross 2018) Knowing the sum updates your best guess for the part.

The law of iterated expectations

Here is the first powerhouse. The unconditional average equals the average of the conditional averages: (Ross 2018)

E[X] = E\big[\,E[X \mid Y]\,\big].

The intuition is exactly how you’d compute a national average from regional ones: take each subgroup’s mean and average them, weighting by subgroup size. (Ross 2018)

TipTwo quick worked examples

GPA. If women average a 3.5 GPA and men a 3.2, and each is 50% of the population, the overall average is 0.5(3.5) + 0.5(3.2) = 3.35. The whole is the size-weighted blend of the parts. (Ross 2018)

Misprints. Sam is equally likely to grab a probability book (mean 2 misprints) or a history book (mean 5). The expected number he finds is E[X] = 0.5(2) + 0.5(5) = 3.5 — average over which book, each weighted by how often it’s chosen. (Ross 2018)

Computing expectations by conditioning

The law of iterated expectations is not just bookkeeping — it is a problem-solving engine. When a quantity is hard to average directly, condition on a helpful first step, then average over the steps. (Ross 2018) Ross’s classic shows the trick at its most elegant.

NoteRoss’s trapped miner

A miner is at a junction with three doors. Door 1 leads out after 3 hours. Door 2 loops back to the junction after 5 hours. Door 3 loops back after 7 hours. Each is equally likely, and a returning miner forgets and chooses afresh. What is the expected time to safety?

Condition on the first door Y. Given door 1, E[X\mid Y{=}1]=3. Given door 2, he spends 5 hours and is back where he started, facing the same problem: E[X\mid Y{=}2]=5+E[X]. Likewise E[X\mid Y{=}3]=7+E[X]. By iterated expectations,

E[X] = \tfrac13(3) + \tfrac13\big(5+E[X]\big) + \tfrac13\big(7+E[X]\big).

Solve: E[X] = 5 + \tfrac23 E[X] \Rightarrow E[X] = \mathbf{15} hours. (Ross 2018) A seemingly hard infinite-loop problem collapses to one line of algebra — the power of conditioning.

The law of total variance

If iterated expectations splits the mean, its partner splits the variance. The law of total variance decomposes total variation into two interpretable pieces: (Ross 2018)

\operatorname{Var}(X) = \underbrace{E\big[\operatorname{Var}(X \mid Y)\big]}_{\text{within-group}} + \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}\big(E[X \mid Y]\big)}_{\text{between-group}}.

In words: total spread = the average leftover spread within groups + the spread of the group means themselves. (Ross 2018) This is precisely the logic that ANOVA formalises — “between-group versus within-group variation” — and it is the same split that the bias–variance decomposition will echo. Knowing Y can only reduce your remaining uncertainty about X: the between-group part is variation that conditioning explains away.

A compound example shows its reach: if a random number N of customers each spend a random X, the variance of total daily revenue is \operatorname{Var}\!\big(\sum_{i=1}^N X_i\big) = E[N]\operatorname{Var}(X) + (E[X])^2\operatorname{Var}(N) — one part from variation in spending, one from variation in the number of customers. (Ross 2018)

Conditional expectation is the best predictor

Now the result that makes this entry the gateway to modelling. Suppose you must predict Y with some function g(X) of the information X, and you are graded by mean squared error, E\big[(Y-g(X))^2\big]. Which g is best? (Ross 2018)

The answer is unique and beautiful: g(X) = E[Y\mid X]. No other function of X achieves a smaller expected squared error. (Ross 2018) The two limiting cases pin down the intuition:

  • With no information about X, the best constant predictor of Y is its unconditional mean E[Y] (the variance entry’s balance point).
  • Once you observe X=x, the best prediction shifts to the conditional mean E[Y\mid X=x].

So conditioning is literally the act of improving your prediction with data.

See it: the regression curve is conditional expectation

The picture below makes the abstract concrete. The cloud is data; the flat grey line is the best guess that ignores X (the unconditional mean E[Y]); the blue curve is E[Y\mid X] — the best guess that uses X. The blue curve threads the conditional averages (the dots), and it is the target every regression aims at.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(4)
n = 600
x = rng.uniform(0, 10, n)
cef = 20 + 5 * np.sqrt(x)              # the true E[Y|X]: a curved, increasing CEF
y = cef + rng.normal(0, 3.5, n)        # data scatters around the CEF

# binned sample conditional means
bins = np.linspace(0, 10, 11)
idx = np.digitize(x, bins)
centres = 0.5 * (bins[:-1] + bins[1:])
cond_means = [y[idx == k].mean() for k in range(1, len(bins))]

xs = np.linspace(0.05, 10, 200)
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.0))
ax.scatter(x, y, s=10, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.5, label="data")
ax.axhline(y.mean(), color="#7a7f87", ls="--", lw=1.6,
           label=f"E[Y] = {y.mean():.0f}  (best guess ignoring X)")
ax.plot(xs, 20 + 5 * np.sqrt(xs), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.6,
        label="E[Y | X]  (best guess using X)")
ax.scatter(centres, cond_means, color="#b5446e", s=36, zorder=5,
           label="average of Y within each X-slice")
ax.set_xlabel("X"); ax.set_ylabel("Y"); ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_title("The regression curve is the conditional-expectation function")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The foundation of regression

This is why the entry sits where it does. The conditional expectation function (CEF) E[Y\mid X] describes how the population average of Y moves across values of X. (Ross 2018) When you run a regression, you are estimating the CEF: if the true relationship is linear, the regression line is the CEF; if it is non-linear, regression delivers the best linear approximation to it. (Ross 2018) Every regression coefficient you will ever interpret is a statement about how a conditional average changes — which is why “regression” and “conditional expectation” are, at bottom, the same idea wearing different clothes.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Forgetting that E[X\mid Y] is random. E[X\mid Y=y] is a number; E[X\mid Y] is a random variable. The whole machinery (iterated expectations, total variance) depends on the second reading. (Ross 2018)

  • Averaging subgroup means without weighting by size. The law of iterated expectations weights each conditional mean by how common that subgroup is. An unweighted average of group means is a different — and usually wrong — number. (Ross 2018)

  • Ignoring within-group variance. A difference in group means (between-group) can be swamped by large within-group spread. The total-variance decomposition is exactly what keeps both in view — and what ANOVA tests. (Ross 2018)

  • Reading the CEF as causal. E[Y\mid X] is the average of Y among units with a given X — an association, not a mechanism. The correlation-isn’t-causation warning applies in full force; closing that gap is the job of Part V.

NoteWho says what

Appendix · the mathematics

Law of iterated expectations. For discrete variables, E\big[E[X\mid Y]\big] = \sum_y E[X\mid Y=y]\,P(Y=y) = \sum_y \sum_x x\,P(X=x\mid Y=y)\,P(Y=y) = \sum_x x \sum_y P(X=x, Y=y) = \sum_x x\,P(X=x) = E[X], using P(X=x\mid Y=y)P(Y=y) = P(X=x,Y=y) and then summing out y to the marginal. The continuous case replaces sums with integrals.

Law of total variance. Apply \operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2 and iterated expectations to E[X^2]=E\big[E[X^2\mid Y]\big]. Writing \operatorname{Var}(X\mid Y)=E[X^2\mid Y]-(E[X\mid Y])^2 and m(Y)=E[X\mid Y], \operatorname{Var}(X) = E\big[\operatorname{Var}(X\mid Y)\big] + \big(E[m(Y)^2]-(E[m(Y)])^2\big) = E\big[\operatorname{Var}(X\mid Y)\big] + \operatorname{Var}\big(E[X\mid Y]\big).

Conditional expectation minimises MSE. For any predictor g(X), insert and subtract m(X)=E[Y\mid X]: E\big[(Y-g(X))^2\big] = E\big[(Y-m(X))^2\big] + E\big[(m(X)-g(X))^2\big] + 2\,E\big[(Y-m(X))(m(X)-g(X))\big]. The cross term vanishes: conditioning on X, E[(Y-m(X))\mid X]=0, so by iterated expectations the whole cross term is 0. The first term is fixed; the second is \ge 0 and is minimised (to 0) exactly when g(X)=m(X)=E[Y\mid X]. Hence the CEF is the unique minimum-MSE predictor.

The miner, solved. E[X]=\tfrac13\cdot3+\tfrac13(5+E[X])+\tfrac13(7+E[X]) = 5 + \tfrac23 E[X], so \tfrac13 E[X]=5 and E[X]=15.

Connections

  • Builds on: expectation & variance (conditional expectation is expectation under the conditional distribution), conditional probability (the conditioning operation), and joint distributions & covariance (the conditional distribution lives inside the joint).
  • Leads to: the Law of Large Numbers and, decisively, regression — which estimates the CEF — and the potential-outcomes machinery, where average treatment effects are differences of conditional expectations.
  • Reappears as: the “between vs within” logic of ANOVA and the bias–variance decomposition in predictive modelling.

```{python}
cond_means = [y[idx == k].mean() for k in range(1, len(bins))]

xs = np.linspace(0.05, 10, 200)
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.0))
ax.scatter(x, y, s=10, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.5, label="data")
ax.axhline(y.mean(), color="#7a7f87", ls="--", lw=1.6,
           label=f"E[Y] = {y.mean():.0f}  (best guess ignoring X)")
ax.plot(xs, 20 + 5 * np.sqrt(xs), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.6,
        label="E[Y | X]  (best guess using X)")
ax.scatter(centres, cond_means, color="#b5446e", s=36, zorder=5,
           label="average of Y within each X-slice")
ax.set_xlabel("X"); ax.set_ylabel("Y"); ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_title("The regression curve is the conditional-expectation function")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## The foundation of regression

This is why the entry sits where it does. The **conditional expectation function (CEF)** $E[Y\mid X]$
describes how the *population average* of $Y$ moves across values of $X$. [@ross_first_course] When
you run a regression, you are **estimating the CEF**: if the true relationship is linear, the
regression line *is* the CEF; if it is non-linear, regression delivers the **best linear
approximation** to it. [@ross_first_course] Every regression coefficient you will ever interpret is a
statement about how a conditional average changes — which is why "regression" and "conditional
expectation" are, at bottom, the same idea wearing different clothes.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Forgetting that $E[X\mid Y]$ is random.** $E[X\mid Y=y]$ is a number; $E[X\mid Y]$ is a random
  variable. The whole machinery (iterated expectations, total variance) depends on the second
  reading. [@ross_first_course]

- **Averaging subgroup means without weighting by size.** The law of iterated expectations weights
  each conditional mean by how common that subgroup is. An unweighted average of group means is a
  different — and usually wrong — number. [@ross_first_course]

- **Ignoring within-group variance.** A difference in group means (between-group) can be swamped by
  large within-group spread. The total-variance decomposition is exactly what keeps both in view —
  and what [ANOVA](../02-inference/comparing-groups.qmd) tests. [@ross_first_course]

- **Reading the CEF as causal.** $E[Y\mid X]$ is the *average of $Y$ among units with a given $X$* —
  an association, not a mechanism. The [correlation-isn't-causation](covariance-and-correlation.qmd)
  warning applies in full force; closing that gap is the job of [Part V](../05-causal-inference/index.qmd).

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Law of iterated expectations.** For discrete variables,
$$
E\big[E[X\mid Y]\big] = \sum_y E[X\mid Y=y]\,P(Y=y)
= \sum_y \sum_x x\,P(X=x\mid Y=y)\,P(Y=y)
= \sum_x x \sum_y P(X=x, Y=y) = \sum_x x\,P(X=x) = E[X],
$$
using $P(X=x\mid Y=y)P(Y=y) = P(X=x,Y=y)$ and then summing out $y$ to the marginal. The continuous
case replaces sums with integrals.

**Law of total variance.** Apply $\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$ and iterated expectations
to $E[X^2]=E\big[E[X^2\mid Y]\big]$. Writing $\operatorname{Var}(X\mid Y)=E[X^2\mid Y]-(E[X\mid Y])^2$
and $m(Y)=E[X\mid Y]$,
$$
\operatorname{Var}(X) = E\big[\operatorname{Var}(X\mid Y)\big] + \big(E[m(Y)^2]-(E[m(Y)])^2\big)
= E\big[\operatorname{Var}(X\mid Y)\big] + \operatorname{Var}\big(E[X\mid Y]\big).
$$

**Conditional expectation minimises MSE.** For any predictor $g(X)$, insert and subtract
$m(X)=E[Y\mid X]$:
$$
E\big[(Y-g(X))^2\big] = E\big[(Y-m(X))^2\big] + E\big[(m(X)-g(X))^2\big] + 2\,E\big[(Y-m(X))(m(X)-g(X))\big].
$$
The cross term vanishes: conditioning on $X$, $E[(Y-m(X))\mid X]=0$, so by iterated expectations the
whole cross term is 0. The first term is fixed; the second is $\ge 0$ and is minimised (to 0) exactly
when $g(X)=m(X)=E[Y\mid X]$. Hence the CEF is the unique minimum-MSE predictor.

**The miner, solved.** $E[X]=\tfrac13\cdot3+\tfrac13(5+E[X])+\tfrac13(7+E[X]) = 5 + \tfrac23 E[X]$,
so $\tfrac13 E[X]=5$ and $E[X]=15$.

:::
