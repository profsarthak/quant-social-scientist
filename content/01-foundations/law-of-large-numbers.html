The Law of Large Numbers

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Why averages settle down — and why that does not mean a coin owes you a tail.

Average enough independent draws and the average stops wandering: it homes in on the true mean. That is the Law of Large Numbers, and it is what makes the frequentist interpretation of probability true rather than merely assumed. But it works by swamping past deviations, not by compensating for them — which is exactly why the gambler’s fallacy is a fallacy.

Why it matters

This is the theorem that closes the chapter’s loop. Way back in the interpretations entry, we said the frequentist view defines probability as a long-run frequency, and flagged that taking this as a definition is awkward. The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) repays that debt: starting only from the axioms, it proves that long-run frequencies converge to probabilities. The frequentist picture is a result, not an assumption.

It also seats the Central Limit Theorem. The LLN says where the sample average is heading (the mean); the CLT says what shape the cloud of averages takes on the way there (a bell). Together they are the theoretical engine of all of inference — and the LLN, properly understood, is also the definitive cure for one of the most stubborn errors about chance.

The law, weak and strong

Let X_1, X_2, \dots be independent draws from the same distribution with mean \mu. Form the running average \bar X_n = \tfrac1n \sum_{i=1}^n X_i. The LLN says \bar X_n \to \mu — in two precise senses (Ross 2018; Grinstead and Snell 1997):

  • Weak law (WLLN). For any margin \varepsilon > 0, the probability that the average is more than \varepsilon from the mean goes to zero as n \to \infty: P\big(|\bar X_n - \mu| > \varepsilon\big) \longrightarrow 0. For a specific, large n, the average is very likely close to \mu.
  • Strong law (SLLN). With probability 1, the entire sequence of averages converges to \mu. Stronger than the weak law: it guarantees the average gets close and stays close for all larger n, not just that any single large n is probably fine (Ross 2018).

Simulation (Grinstead & Snell). Toss a fair coin and watch the proportion of heads. At 20 tosses they got 5 heads (0.25); at 1,000 tosses, 494 heads (0.494); at 10,000 tosses, 5,039 heads (0.5039) — marching toward the theoretical 0.5 (Grinstead and Snell 1997). A fair die behaves the same way: the running average of rolls converges to E[X] = 3.5 (Grinstead and Snell 1997).

Show the code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
n = 10_000
flips = rng.integers(0, 2, size=n)          # 1 = heads
heads_cumsum = np.cumsum(flips)
trials = np.arange(1, n + 1)

proportion = heads_cumsum / trials
abs_dev = np.abs(heads_cumsum - trials / 2)  # |heads - n/2|

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(11, 3.6))

axes[0].plot(trials, proportion, color="#1f3a5f", lw=1)
axes[0].axhline(0.5, color="#b5446e", ls="--", lw=1.4)
axes[0].set_xscale("log")
axes[0].set_title("Proportion of heads → 0.5")
axes[0].set_xlabel("number of tosses (log scale)")
axes[0].set_ylabel("proportion heads")
axes[0].set_ylim(0, 1)

axes[1].plot(trials, abs_dev, color="#9a6a2f", lw=1)
axes[1].set_title("Absolute deviation |heads − n/2| grows")
axes[1].set_xlabel("number of tosses")
axes[1].set_ylabel("|heads − n/2|")

fig.suptitle("The average settles while the raw gap widens", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The tools behind the proof: Markov and Chebyshev

Two inequalities bound probabilities using only a mean (Markov) or a mean and variance (Chebyshev) — remarkable because they hold for any distribution with those moments (Ross 2018).

Markov’s inequality. For a non-negative random variable, the chance it reaches a high value a is capped by its mean over a: P\{X \ge a\} \le \frac{E[X]}{a}.

Ross’s example. A factory averages 50 items a week. Then P\{X \ge 75\} \le 50/75 = 2/3 — the best you can say knowing only the mean (Ross 2018).

Chebyshev’s inequality. Bounds deviation from the mean using the variance: P\{|X - \mu| \ge k\} \le \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}.

Ross’s example. If that factory’s production has variance 25, then production lands within 10 of the mean (between 40 and 60) with probability at least 1 - 25/10^2 = 0.75 (Ross 2018).

TipHow Chebyshev proves the weak law — in one line

Apply Chebyshev to the average \bar X_n. Its mean is still \mu, but its variance is \sigma^2/n (the variance-of-a-sum result). So P\{|\bar X_n - \mu| \ge \varepsilon\} \le \frac{\sigma^2/n}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \longrightarrow 0. The averaging shrinks the variance, which strangles the deviation probability. That’s the entire weak law (Ross 2018; Grinstead and Snell 1997).

The crucial distinction: swamping, not compensating

Here is where almost everyone’s intuition goes wrong, and where the LLN earns its keep.

WarningThe gambler’s fallacy

After eight heads in a row, a tail is not “due.” The flips are independent, so the ninth is still exactly 50/50 — the coin has no memory and no debt to repay (Albers 2017).

So if the average converges to 0.5, how does it get there without tails “catching up”? By swamping, not compensating (Grinstead and Snell 1997). Suppose after some flips you’re “ahead” by 100 extra heads. The LLN does not summon 100 extra tails to cancel them. Instead, as you flip millions more times, that fixed surplus of 100 becomes a vanishing fraction of the total. The deviation isn’t erased — it’s diluted.

This produces a genuinely surprising fact:

The average stabilises while the raw gap grows. The proportion S_n/n settles toward 0.5, but the absolute deviation — the literal (heads - n/2) — tends to grow with n. Off by 10 heads in 100 flips is a 10% error; off by 500 heads in 1,000,000 flips is a larger number but only a 0.05% error (Grinstead and Snell 1997). The right panel of the figure above shows exactly this: the gap widens even as the ratio converges.

That second fact is not a footnote — it’s the seed of the CLT. The absolute deviation grows like \sqrt{n}, which is precisely why the standard error scales as \sigma/\sqrt{n} and why the sampling distribution has the width it does.

What the LLN buys, and what it doesn’t

It justifies frequentist probability. Apply the strong law to an event’s indicator (1 if it happens, 0 if not): its long-run relative frequency converges, with probability 1, to P(E) (Ross 2018). This is the theorem that makes “probability = long-run frequency” consistent with the axioms rather than a separate article of faith — the promissory note from the first entry, paid in full.

It pairs with the CLT. The LLN gives the destination (the average converges to a point); the CLT gives the travel pattern (the averages scatter Normally around that point, with a width that shrinks like 1/\sqrt n) (Ross 2018). The LLN says the variance of the average vanishes; the CLT says how much probability sits within any given distance for finite n.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • The gambler’s fallacy. Believing past outcomes make future ones “due.” Independence means each trial is fresh; the law works by dilution, not by debt collection.
  • Confusing the average with the absolute count. The proportion converges; the raw gap (heads minus n/2) typically grows. Expecting the literal counts to equalise is the fallacy in another costume.
  • Expecting the LLN at small n. It is an asymptotic statement. Twenty tosses can sit far from 50% (Grinstead & Snell’s run hit 25%); “the law of small numbers” — trusting tiny samples — is a classic error the inference chapters attack directly.
  • Assuming convergence without finite variance. Heavy-tailed distributions (no finite variance) can break the usual guarantees — the same caveat that makes the CLT fail for long tails.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Trust averages, distrust streaks. The LLN governs the long-run average, never the next trial. Any “it’s due” reasoning is the gambler’s fallacy.
  • Remember “swamping, not compensating.” Deviations don’t get cancelled; they get diluted. Keeping this straight immunises you against a whole family of chance errors.
  • Respect small samples. Convergence is asymptotic; at small n, averages can stray far. Don’t read a stable-looking mean off twenty data points.
  • Use Chebyshev for a quick, assumption-light bound. When you know only a mean and variance, it gives a guaranteed (if loose) probability bound for any distribution — handy as a sanity check before invoking Normality.

Appendix · the mathematics

Markov’s inequality. For X \ge 0 and a > 0, let \mathbf{1}_{\{X \ge a\}} be the indicator of the event \{X \ge a\}. Since X \ge 0, we have X \ge a\,\mathbf{1}_{\{X \ge a\}} (it holds with equality-or-more when X \ge a, and trivially when X < a). Taking expectations, E[X] \ge a\,P\{X \ge a\}, hence P\{X \ge a\} \le E[X]/a.

Chebyshev’s inequality. Apply Markov to the non-negative variable (X-\mu)^2 with threshold k^2: P\{|X-\mu| \ge k\} = P\{(X-\mu)^2 \ge k^2\} \le \frac{E[(X-\mu)^2]}{k^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}.

Weak law of large numbers. With \bar X_n = \tfrac1n\sum_{i=1}^n X_i, independence gives E[\bar X_n] = \mu and \operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n) = \sigma^2/n. Chebyshev then yields, for any \varepsilon > 0, P\{|\bar X_n - \mu| \ge \varepsilon\} \le \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{} 0. This is convergence in probability. The strong law strengthens it to convergence almost surely (P\{\lim_n \bar X_n = \mu\} = 1); its proof is harder and needs more than the second moment, but the conclusion is the one that licenses defining probability as a long-run frequency.

Why the absolute deviation grows. For fair coins, S_n - n/2 has standard deviation \tfrac12\sqrt n, so the typical absolute gap grows like \sqrt n even as the proportion gap |S_n/n - \tfrac12| shrinks like 1/\sqrt n. The \sqrt n is the same one that runs through the standard error and the CLT.

::: {.math-appendix}

**Markov's inequality.** For $X \ge 0$ and $a > 0$, let $\mathbf{1}_{\{X \ge a\}}$ be the
indicator of the event $\{X \ge a\}$. Since $X \ge 0$, we have $X \ge a\,\mathbf{1}_{\{X \ge a\}}$
(it holds with equality-or-more when $X \ge a$, and trivially when $X < a$). Taking expectations,
$E[X] \ge a\,P\{X \ge a\}$, hence $P\{X \ge a\} \le E[X]/a$.

**Chebyshev's inequality.** Apply Markov to the non-negative variable $(X-\mu)^2$ with threshold
$k^2$:
$$
P\{|X-\mu| \ge k\} = P\{(X-\mu)^2 \ge k^2\} \le \frac{E[(X-\mu)^2]}{k^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}.
$$

**Weak law of large numbers.** With $\bar X_n = \tfrac1n\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$, independence gives
$E[\bar X_n] = \mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n) = \sigma^2/n$. Chebyshev then yields, for any
$\varepsilon > 0$,
$$
P\{|\bar X_n - \mu| \ge \varepsilon\} \le \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{} 0.
$$
This is convergence *in probability*. The **strong law** strengthens it to convergence *almost
surely* ($P\{\lim_n \bar X_n = \mu\} = 1$); its proof is harder and needs more than the second
moment, but the conclusion is the one that licenses defining probability as a long-run frequency.

**Why the absolute deviation grows.** For fair coins, $S_n - n/2$ has standard deviation
$\tfrac12\sqrt n$, so the typical *absolute* gap grows like $\sqrt n$ even as the *proportion*
gap $|S_n/n - \tfrac12|$ shrinks like $1/\sqrt n$. The $\sqrt n$ is the same one that runs through
the standard error and the [CLT](../02-inference/sampling-distributions-clt.qmd).

:::
