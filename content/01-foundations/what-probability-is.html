What Probability Actually Is

Three different answers to ‘what does a probability mean?’ — and why a social scientist needs all three.

A probability is a number between 0 and 1, but the meaning of that number is contested. “The coin has probability ½ of heads” can mean three different things: a count of symmetric possibilities, a long-run frequency, or a degree of belief. The mathematics is identical across all three; the interpretation changes what kind of question you’re answering — and which research tradition you’re standing in.

Why it matters

You will not get far in quantitative social science before all three readings show up. When a methods text says a fair die lands on each face with probability ⅙, that’s the classical reading. When a pollster says their estimator “is right 95% of the time,” that’s the frequentist reading — the one that underwrites confidence intervals and p-values. When a forecaster says a candidate has a “70% chance of winning” an election that happens exactly once, that’s the subjective reading — the one that underwrites Bayesian inference.

Beginners trip because they assume “probability” means one thing. It doesn’t. Knowing which interpretation is in play tells you what would count as evidence, and it is the quiet reason the frequentist and Bayesian chapters later in this book feel like different philosophies rather than different formulas. The good news, established at the end of this entry: whichever reading you adopt, the rules are the same.

The three interpretations

1. Classical — counting equally likely outcomes

The oldest view: if a situation has a finite number of outcomes and symmetry makes them equally likely, then the probability of an event is just a ratio of counts (Ross 2018; Grinstead and Snell 1997):

P(E) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in } E}{\text{number of outcomes in } S}.

This is “probability as careful counting.” Grinstead & Snell credit Gerolamo Cardano (a 16th-century gambler-mathematician) with the crucial first move — choosing the correct sample space for dice problems (Grinstead and Snell 1997).

Worked example (Ross). Roll two fair dice. There are 6 \times 6 = 36 equally likely outcomes. Exactly 6 of them sum to 7 — (1,6),(6,1),(2,5),(5,2),(3,4),(4,3) — so P(\text{sum}=7) = 6/36 = 1/6 (Ross 2018).

Where it shows up in social science: randomised assignment. When you flip a coin to decide who gets a treatment, the equally-likely model is literally true by construction — which is exactly why randomisation gives causal inference its clean footing (Part V).

Its limit: the classical view requires symmetry. It can tell you about fair dice and shuffled cards, but it is mute about “the probability it rains tomorrow” or “the probability this defendant is guilty” — situations with no obvious set of equally likely outcomes.

2. Frequentist — the long-run fraction

The frequentist reading defines probability operationally: P(E) is the fraction of times E occurs as the experiment is repeated a large number of times (Ross 2018). The coin is “fair” because, tossed forever, the proportion of heads settles to ½.

Worked example (Grinstead & Snell). If a drug is effective 30% of the time, we assign probability 0.3 that it works for the next patient. In a simulation of 10,000 coin tosses, they obtained 5,039 heads — close to the “true” 0.5, and closer the longer you run it (Grinstead and Snell 1997).

NoteA subtle point worth pausing on

Ross notes that defining probability as a limiting frequency is philosophically awkward — it quietly assumes the long-run fraction settles to a fixed number, which is a strong, unprovable premise if taken as a definition. The modern fix (next entry) is to start from a few simple axioms and then prove that averages stabilise. That theorem is the Law of Large Numbers, and it is why the frequentist picture is a result of the theory rather than its foundation (Ross 2018).

Where it shows up: essentially all of classical inference. A 95% confidence interval is a frequentist promise — the procedure covers the truth 95% of the time across hypothetical repetitions of the study. Hold that phrasing; Part II makes it precise.

Its limit: it needs the event to be repeatable, at least in principle. It strains on genuinely one-off events — a particular election, a specific war, this firm’s chance of default next year.

3. Subjective (Bayesian) — a degree of belief

The subjective reading interprets probability as a measure of one person’s confidence in a statement, given what they know (Ross 2018). It applies happily to one-off events because it isn’t about repetition at all — it’s about the strength of a belief.

Worked example (Ross). Someone may be 70% certain Shakespeare wrote Julius Caesar and 10% certain Marlowe did. Crucially, to stay coherent, they should then be 80% certain it was one of the two — the beliefs must obey the same arithmetic as any other probabilities (Ross 2018).

Where it shows up: Bayesian inference (the dedicated chapter built on Lambert), risk forecasting, expert elicitation, and any setting where you want to attach a probability to a hypothesis itself (“how likely is this theory, given the data?”) rather than to data given a hypothesis.

Its limit (and its strength): the number is personal — two reasonable people with different information can assign different probabilities to the same event. Bayesians treat that not as a bug but as honesty about the role of prior knowledge.

TipThe unifying move

These are three interpretations, not three theories. All three agree that probabilities must obey the same rules — they live in [0,1], the certain event has probability 1, and the probabilities of mutually exclusive events add. That shared rulebook is the set of axioms in the next entry. So you can switch interpretive hats freely: compute with the axioms, and interpret the answer according to the question you’re asking.

Where it all started — and a cautionary tale

Probability theory was born from gambling. Grinstead & Snell trace the field to a 17th-century correspondence between Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat, prompted by betting puzzles from the Chevalier de Méré (Grinstead and Snell 1997).

One puzzle is a perfect parable for this whole book. De Méré reasoned by a sloppy proportionality: a single die shows a 6 with decent odds in 4 rolls, so — scaling up — a pair of dice should show a double-6 with decent odds in 4 \times 6 = 24 rolls. He bet on it and lost money. Pascal and Fermat showed by exact calculation that you actually need 25 rolls for that bet to favour you (Grinstead and Snell 1997).

The lesson isn’t the arithmetic; it’s that plausible intuition about chance is often wrong, and only careful counting (the next two entries) sets it right. Probability is, in large part, the discipline of distrusting your gut about uncertainty.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Assuming “equally likely” without earning it. The classical formula is only valid when symmetry genuinely holds. Real survey responses, real people, real outcomes are rarely uniform — assuming they are is a silent modelling error.
  • Reading a frequentist statement as a belief. “95% confidence” is a property of the procedure over many repetitions, not “95% probability this particular interval contains the truth.” Conflating the two is the single most common misreading in applied statistics (Part II returns to it with force).
  • Demanding repetition where there is none. Insisting on a strict frequentist reading makes one-off probabilities (“chance of recession next year”) sound meaningless. The subjective reading exists precisely for these.
  • Trusting proportional intuition about odds. De Méré’s mistake in modern dress: “twice the rolls, twice the chance” almost never holds. Compute, don’t extrapolate.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Name your interpretation before you argue. Most “is this probability right?” disputes dissolve once both sides say whether they mean a frequency or a belief.
  • Use the classical model as a sanity check. Whenever symmetry genuinely holds (fair coins, random assignment, shuffled data), counting gives an exact answer to test simulations against.
  • Default to frequentist language in classical inference, Bayesian language for one-off risk. The two chapters ahead are not rivals so much as tools for different question shapes.
  • When intuition and calculation disagree about odds, calculation wins. That instinct — the De Méré lesson — is most of what separates a careful analyst from a confident one.
