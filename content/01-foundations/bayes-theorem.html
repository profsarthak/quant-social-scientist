Bayes’ Theorem: Updating Beliefs

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

How to invert a conditional probability — and why a positive test for a rare disease is usually a false alarm.

You know P(\text{test positive} \mid \text{sick}) — that’s the test’s accuracy. You want P(\text{sick} \mid \text{test positive}) — that’s what the patient cares about. Bayes’ theorem flips one into the other, and the flip is governed by the base rate: how common the condition was to begin with. Ignore the base rate and you will systematically over-read your evidence.

Why it matters

Bayes’ theorem is two things at once. Mechanically, it is the rule for inverting a conditional probability — turning P(E \mid H) into P(H \mid E). Philosophically, it is the engine of learning from evidence: it tells you exactly how a prior belief should change once data arrive. That second reading is the entire foundation of the Bayesian-inference chapter (built on Lambert), and the reason this entry is the bridge out of pure probability.

It also defuses one of the most consequential reasoning errors in applied work — the base-rate fallacy — which infects medical screening, forensic evidence, fraud detection, and any setting where a fairly accurate test hunts for a fairly rare thing. Getting Bayes into your bones is what stops you from confusing “the test rarely lies” with “a positive result is probably true.” Those are different claims, and the gap between them is the base rate.

The theorem

Combine the multiplication rule two ways — P(HE) = P(E \mid H)P(H) = P(H \mid E)P(E) — and solve for the conditional you want (Ross 2018):

P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H)\,P(H)}{P(E)}.

When the hypotheses H_1,\dots,H_m partition the world, the denominator is supplied by the law of total probability, giving the full form (Ross 2018):

P(H_i \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H_i)\,P(H_i)}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{m} P(E \mid H_k)\,P(H_k)}.

The vocabulary that carries into Bayesian statistics (Lambert 2018):

  • P(H) — the prior: what you believed before seeing the evidence.
  • P(E \mid H) — the likelihood: how well each hypothesis predicts the evidence.
  • P(H \mid E) — the posterior: your updated belief, after.
  • P(E) — the marginal likelihood (the denominator): a normaliser that makes the posterior sum to 1.

In one line: posterior \propto likelihood \times prior. Belief after is belief before, re-weighted by how well it predicted what you saw.

The base-rate fallacy: a positive test that usually isn’t

This is the example that makes Bayes unforgettable (Grinstead and Snell 1997). A cancer test:

  • Prevalence (prior): 1 person in 1,000 has the cancer — P(C) = 0.001.
  • Sensitivity: if you have it, the test is positive 99% of the time — P(+ \mid C) = 0.99.
  • Specificity: if you don’t, the test is negative 95% of the time — so the false-positive rate is 5%, P(+ \mid \text{no } C) = 0.05.

The test sounds excellent. Yet if you test positive, your probability of actually having the cancer is only about 1.9% (Grinstead and Snell 1997):

P(C \mid +) = \frac{(0.99)(0.001)}{(0.99)(0.001) + (0.05)(0.999)} \approx \frac{0.00099}{0.05094} \approx 0.019.

Why — see it in natural frequencies

The algebra hides the intuition; counts reveal it. Imagine 100,000 people:

Test positive Test negative Total
Has cancer 99 1 100
No cancer 4,995 94,905 99,900
Total 5,094 94,906 100,000

Only 100 people are truly sick, and 99 of them test positive. But 5% of the 99,900 healthy people — nearly 5,000 — also test positive. So among the 5,094 positives, the 99 real cases are a small minority: 99 / 5{,}094 \approx 1.9\% (Grinstead and Snell 1997). The false positives, drawn from a vastly larger healthy pool, swamp the true positives. That is the base-rate fallacy: a rare condition means even an accurate test produces mostly false alarms.

Show the code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

sens, spec = 0.99, 0.95          # sensitivity, specificity
prev = np.logspace(-4, 0, 400)   # prevalence from 0.01% to 100%

# Posterior P(disease | positive) via Bayes' theorem
ppv = (sens * prev) / (sens * prev + (1 - spec) * (1 - prev))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.plot(prev, ppv, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2)
ax.axvline(0.001, color="#b5446e", ls="--", lw=1.4)
ax.scatter([0.001], [(sens*0.001)/(sens*0.001 + (1-spec)*0.999)],
           color="#b5446e", zorder=5)
ax.annotate("prevalence 0.1%\n→ only ~1.9% true",
            xy=(0.001, 0.019), xytext=(0.0025, 0.45),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#b5446e"), fontsize=9)
ax.set_xscale("log")
ax.set_xlabel("Prevalence (base rate), log scale")
ax.set_ylabel("P(disease | positive test)")
ax.set_title("A good test + a rare disease = a positive that usually isn't")
ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The prosecutor’s fallacy: don’t transpose the conditional

The base-rate fallacy has a cousin that wrecks courtroom and forensic reasoning: confusing P(E \mid H) with P(H \mid E) (Albers 2017). “The probability of this DNA match if the suspect is innocent is one in a million” is not “the probability the suspect is innocent is one in a million.” The two differ by exactly the prior — the base rate of guilt in the relevant pool — which Bayes makes explicit and the fallacy quietly drops.

Albers’ homely version: a well-fed dog rarely barks, so a bark is weak evidence of hunger — the dog might bark 5% of the time even when full. P(\text{bark} \mid \text{hungry}) being high tells you little about P(\text{hungry} \mid \text{bark}) without knowing how often the dog is hungry in the first place (Albers 2017).

The bridge to Bayesian inference (Lambert)

Lambert frames Bayesian analysis as inversion: data are the effects, parameters are the causes, and Bayes runs the inference backward from effect to cause (Lambert 2018). The three ingredients:

  • Prior p(\theta) — a full distribution encoding pre-data uncertainty about a parameter. It is explicitly subjective and open to debate — a feature, not a flaw, since it makes assumptions visible (Lambert 2018).
  • Likelihood p(\text{data} \mid \theta) — how probable the observed data are for each parameter value. Lambert stresses a subtlety: viewed as a function of \theta (data fixed), the likelihood is not a probability distribution — its area need not integrate to 1 (Lambert 2018).
  • Posterior p(\theta \mid \text{data}) — the goal: a re-weighting of the prior by the likelihood. As data accumulate, the posterior narrows, reflecting growing confidence (Lambert 2018).
TipTwo of Lambert’s pictures worth keeping
  • The Bayesian coastguard. A ship is lost in a storm. The prior is a wide circle of possible locations; one radio signal sharpens it to an oval; combining a second tower’s data — using the first posterior as the new prior — collapses it to a tiny high-probability patch where the rescue happens. Updating is just yesterday’s posterior becoming today’s prior (Lambert 2018).
  • The fish game (a “Bayes’ box”). A bowl holds 5 fish, each red or white; you catch one and it’s red. Start with a flat prior — each count of red fish (0–5) equally likely, 1/6 each. Multiply each prior by the likelihood of catching red given that count (2 red fish → 2/5), sum the products to normalise, and read off the posterior. Counts that better explain “caught red” gain probability; “0 red fish” is annihilated (Lambert 2018).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Ignoring the base rate. Judging a positive result by the test’s accuracy alone, with no regard for how rare the condition is, is the classic error. For rare conditions, even great tests yield mostly false positives.
  • Transposing the conditional (prosecutor’s fallacy). P(\text{evidence} \mid \text{innocent}) is not P(\text{innocent} \mid \text{evidence}). Always ask what prior the claim is smuggling.
  • Treating the likelihood as a probability over \theta. It isn’t (Lambert) — it doesn’t integrate to 1 in \theta. The posterior is what’s normalised.
  • Forgetting to renormalise. “Posterior \propto likelihood \times prior” needs the denominator P(E) to become a real distribution; skipping it gives numbers that don’t sum to 1.
  • Pretending you have no prior. You always do — even “all equally likely” is a prior choice. Bayes makes it explicit; the fallacies above come from leaving it implicit.

  • Grinstead & Snell, Introduction to Probability — the rare-disease screening example (prevalence 0.001, sensitivity 0.99, specificity 0.95 → P(C\mid+)\approx 0.019) and the base-rate intuition.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Reach for natural frequencies. “Out of 100,000 people…” makes base-rate problems intuitive where formulas fog them. Build the 2×2 table before trusting any percentage.
  • Always ask for the base rate. Before reacting to any positive result — test, alert, red flag — ask how common the thing is. Rarity is half the answer.
  • Name your prior out loud. Even a flat prior is a choice; stating it converts hidden assumptions into debatable ones, which is the honest move.
  • Think in likelihood ratios. “How many times more likely is this evidence under H than under not-H?” times your prior odds gives the posterior odds — fast, and immune to the prosecutor’s fallacy (see the appendix).

Appendix · the mathematics

Derivation. From the multiplication rule, P(HE) = P(E\mid H)P(H) and also P(HE) = P(H\mid E)P(E). Equate and divide by P(E): P(H\mid E) = \frac{P(E\mid H)\,P(H)}{P(E)}. Expanding P(E) over a partition \{H_k\} via the law of total probability gives the full form.

Odds form (often the clearest). Write the ratio of posteriors for two hypotheses: \underbrace{\frac{P(H\mid E)}{P(H^c\mid E)}}_{\text{posterior odds}} = \underbrace{\frac{P(E\mid H)}{P(E\mid H^c)}}_{\text{likelihood ratio}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(H)}{P(H^c)}}_{\text{prior odds}}. The normaliser P(E) cancels. For the cancer test: prior odds = 0.001/0.999 \approx 1/999, likelihood ratio = 0.99/0.05 = 19.8, so posterior odds \approx 19.8/999 \approx 0.0198, i.e. probability 0.0198/(1+0.0198) \approx 0.0194 — the same \approx 1.9\%. This form makes the prosecutor’s fallacy obvious: a large likelihood ratio is worthless evidence of H if the prior odds are tiny.

Continuous (Bayesian) form. For a parameter \theta with prior density p(\theta) and likelihood p(\text{data}\mid\theta), p(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \frac{p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)}{\displaystyle\int p(\text{data}\mid\theta')\,p(\theta')\,d\theta'} \;\propto\; p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta). The integral in the denominator is what makes the posterior a genuine distribution — and is exactly the quantity that is hard to compute in practice, motivating the MCMC methods Lambert develops in the Bayesian chapter.

  denominator $P(E)$ to become a real distribution; skipping it gives numbers that don't sum to 1.
- **Pretending you have no prior.** You always do — even "all equally likely" is a prior choice.
  Bayes makes it explicit; the fallacies above come from leaving it implicit.
:::

## Practitioner's notes — tips & tricks {.unnumbered}

- **Reach for natural frequencies.** "Out of 100,000 people…" makes base-rate problems intuitive
  where formulas fog them. Build the 2×2 table before trusting any percentage.
- **Always ask for the base rate.** Before reacting to any positive result — test, alert, red
  flag — ask how common the thing is. Rarity is half the answer.
- **Name your prior out loud.** Even a flat prior is a choice; stating it converts hidden
  assumptions into debatable ones, which is the honest move.
- **Think in likelihood ratios.** "How many times more likely is this evidence under $H$ than
  under not-$H$?" times your prior odds gives the posterior odds — fast, and immune to the
  prosecutor's fallacy (see the appendix).

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Derivation.** From the [multiplication rule](conditional-probability.qmd),
$P(HE) = P(E\mid H)P(H)$ and also $P(HE) = P(H\mid E)P(E)$. Equate and divide by $P(E)$:
$$
P(H\mid E) = \frac{P(E\mid H)\,P(H)}{P(E)}.
$$
Expanding $P(E)$ over a partition $\{H_k\}$ via the law of total probability gives the full form.

**Odds form (often the clearest).** Write the ratio of posteriors for two hypotheses:
$$
\underbrace{\frac{P(H\mid E)}{P(H^c\mid E)}}_{\text{posterior odds}}
= \underbrace{\frac{P(E\mid H)}{P(E\mid H^c)}}_{\text{likelihood ratio}}
\times
\underbrace{\frac{P(H)}{P(H^c)}}_{\text{prior odds}}.
$$
The normaliser $P(E)$ cancels. For the cancer test: prior odds $= 0.001/0.999 \approx 1/999$,
likelihood ratio $= 0.99/0.05 = 19.8$, so posterior odds $\approx 19.8/999 \approx 0.0198$, i.e.
probability $0.0198/(1+0.0198) \approx 0.0194$ — the same $\approx 1.9\%$. This form makes the
prosecutor's fallacy obvious: a large likelihood ratio is worthless evidence of $H$ if the prior
odds are tiny.

**Continuous (Bayesian) form.** For a parameter $\theta$ with prior density $p(\theta)$ and
likelihood $p(\text{data}\mid\theta)$,
$$
p(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \frac{p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)}{\displaystyle\int p(\text{data}\mid\theta')\,p(\theta')\,d\theta'} \;\propto\; p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta).
$$
The integral in the denominator is what makes the posterior a genuine distribution — and is
exactly the quantity that is hard to compute in practice, motivating the MCMC methods Lambert
develops in the Bayesian chapter.

:::
