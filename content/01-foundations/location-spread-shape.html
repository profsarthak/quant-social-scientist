Location, Spread & Shape

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Summarising a variable in three questions: where is it centred, how far does it scatter, and what shape is it?

Before any model, you describe. A single variable is summarised by three things: a centre (the typical value), a spread (how much values vary), and a shape (symmetry, tails, peaks). The recurring theme is robustness — some summaries (mean, standard deviation) are hijacked by a single outlier, while others (median, IQR) shrug it off. Knowing which is which is the difference between a summary that informs and one that misleads.

Why it matters

This is the empirical mirror of the probability sub-chapter. There, distributions had theoretical means and variances; here, real data has estimated ones — and real data is messy, skewed, and dotted with outliers. Choosing the wrong summary is one of the most common and consequential mistakes in applied work: report a mean income and a few billionaires make the “typical” person look rich; report a median and you see the actual middle. Every chart, table, and model downstream rests on summaries chosen here, so choosing them well — matched to the data’s shape and to its measurement level — is foundational craft.

Measures of location: where is the centre?

A measure of location pins down a “typical” value (Bruce et al. 2020).

  • Mean — the arithmetic average, \bar x = \tfrac1n\sum x_i. The familiar choice, and the right one for roughly symmetric data without extremes (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Weighted mean — \bar x_w = \dfrac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}, used when some observations should count more (or less): when groups are unequally represented in the sample, or when some measurements are intrinsically noisier and should be down-weighted (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Median — the middle value of the sorted data. The better choice for skewed data or when outliers are present (household incomes are the canonical case) (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Trimmed mean — the average after dropping a fixed number p of the most extreme values at each end, \dfrac{1}{n-2p}\sum_{i=p+1}^{n-p} x_{(i)}. A compromise between mean and median; Olympic diving and gymnastics scores use it so a single rogue judge can’t swing the result (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Mode — the most frequent value. Mainly a summary for categorical data, where “average” has no meaning (Bruce et al. 2020).
TipRobustness: the one idea that organises this whole entry

A statistic is robust (or resistant) if a few extreme values barely change it. The mean is not robust: every point enters the sum, so one giant value drags it (Bruce et al. 2020). The median is robust: it depends only on the position of the middle observation, and an outlier at the end of the sorted list can’t move the middle (Bruce et al. 2020). The trimmed mean is robust because it deletes the extremes before averaging. This single distinction — does one weird value wreck the summary? — recurs for spread (below) and decides which numbers to trust on real, messy data.

Worked example (Bruce & Bruce). Across U.S. state populations, the mean is 6,162,876 but the median is only 4,436,370 (Bruce et al. 2020). The mean is much larger because a few highly populous states (California, Texas) inflate it; the median reports the genuine middle. The gap between them is itself a signal — of skew, which the shape section makes precise.

Measures of spread: how much does it scatter?

Spread asks whether values cluster tightly or range widely (Bruce et al. 2020). The same robust/non-robust split applies.

Measure Definition Robust?
Range \max - \min ✗ — defined by the two extremes
Variance s^2 \dfrac{1}{n-1}\sum (x_i - \bar x)^2 ✗ — squared deviations magnify outliers
Standard deviation s \sqrt{s^2} ✗ — same, in original units
Mean absolute deviation \tfrac1n\sum |x_i - \bar x| ✗ — still anchored to the mean
Median absolute deviation (MAD) \operatorname{median}(|x_i - m|), m= median ✓ — robust
Interquartile range (IQR) Q_{75} - Q_{25} ✓ — robust

The standard deviation is the default partner of the mean (and the quantity behind the standard error and CLT), but it is especially outlier-sensitive because it squares deviations — one extreme point, squared, dominates the sum (Bruce et al. 2020). When data is messy, the robust pair — median for location, IQR or MAD for spread — is the safer summary (Bruce et al. 2020).

NoteWhy divide by n − 1?

The sample variance divides by n-1, not n. Intuitively, you’ve “spent” one degree of freedom estimating the mean \bar x from the same data, so the deviations are slightly too small; dividing by n-1 corrects the bias, making s^2 an unbiased estimate of the true variance. The appendix makes this precise.

Shape: symmetry, tails, and peaks

Two distributions can share a mean and a standard deviation yet look completely different — shape is the missing third summary (Bruce et al. 2020; Albers 2017).

Skewness is asymmetry (Bruce et al. 2020):

  • Right (positive) skew — a long tail toward high values; a handful of large observations pull the mean above the median (\bar x > m). Income, city sizes, and insurance claims are classically right-skewed.
  • Left (negative) skew — a long tail toward low values; the median sits above the mean (m > \bar x).

The mean-versus-median comparison is therefore a quick skew detector: mean > median signals right skew, mean < median signals left skew, and the state-population example above (\bar x \gg m) is textbook right skew.

Modality counts the peaks (Albers 2017):

  • Unimodal — a single peak.
  • Bimodal (or multimodal) — two or more peaks, often a tell that the data secretly mixes two populations (e.g. heights pooled across men and women) (Albers 2017). A bimodal histogram is a standing invitation to disaggregate.

Worked example (Albers). Two typing methods are compared by mean alone — Method A at 42 wpm, Method B at 40 wpm — which would crown A the winner. But graphing the distributions reveals they have opposite skews, so their practical implications differ: judged on the full shape rather than the mean, the lower-mean method can be the better choice (Albers 2017). The lesson is the entry’s thesis: a mean without a shape can point you the wrong way.

Show the code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
data = rng.lognormal(mean=10.2, sigma=0.9, size=5000)   # right-skewed, income-like

mean, median = data.mean(), np.median(data)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.hist(data, bins=80, range=(0, np.quantile(data, 0.99)),
        color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85)
ax.axvline(median, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2, label=f"median = {median:,.0f}")
ax.axvline(mean, color="#b5446e", lw=2, ls="--", label=f"mean = {mean:,.0f}")
ax.set_title("Right skew: the mean chases the tail, the median holds the middle")
ax.set_xlabel("value (e.g. income)"); ax.set_yticks([])
ax.legend()
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reporting a mean for skewed data. With incomes, claims, or city sizes, the mean overstates the “typical” value. Use the median, and show both so the skew is visible.
  • Trusting the standard deviation on outlier-prone data. Because it squares deviations, one extreme point can dominate it. Reach for the IQR or MAD when robustness matters.
  • Summarising shape away. Mean and SD alone hide skew and bimodality. Always pair summaries with a histogram or boxplot (next entry).
  • Averaging the wrong measurement level. A mean of ordinal codes or nominal categories is meaningless — match the statistic to the measurement level (mode for nominal, median for ordinal).
  • Ignoring a bimodal peak. Two peaks usually mean two populations; a single summary of the pooled data describes neither.

  • Albers, Quantitative Data Analysis — descriptive framing; modality (unimodal/bimodal as a sign of mixed populations); the two-typing-methods example showing why the mean alone can mislead.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Default to median + IQR for messy data, mean + SD for clean symmetric data. Let the shape decide, not habit.
  • Always report mean and median together. Their gap is a free, instant read on skew.
  • Treat one outlier as a question, not a nuisance. If the mean and median disagree sharply, ask why before “fixing” anything — the outlier may be the most interesting datum.
  • Plot before you summarise. A five-minute histogram catches skew and bimodality that no single number reveals — the bridge to the EDA entry.

Appendix · the mathematics

Why the mean is the least-squares centre. The value c minimising \sum_i (x_i - c)^2 is c = \bar x (differentiate and set to zero). The value minimising \sum_i |x_i - c| is the median. So the mean is the centre under squared loss, the median under absolute loss — and squaring is exactly what makes the mean chase outliers.

The n-1 correction (Bessel’s correction). With \mu unknown and estimated by \bar x, E\!\left[\sum_i (x_i - \bar x)^2\right] = (n-1)\sigma^2, because the deviations are taken about the sample mean, which itself was fit to the data. Dividing by n-1 (not n) therefore makes E[s^2] = \sigma^2 — an unbiased estimator. The lost degree of freedom is the one used to estimate \bar x.

MAD as a robust σ-estimate. For Normally distributed data, \sigma \approx 1.4826 \times \operatorname{MAD}; the constant rescales the median absolute deviation so it matches the standard deviation under Normality while retaining robustness against outliers.

Percentiles. The p-th percentile is a value below which (roughly) p\% of the data falls; Q_{25}, Q_{50}=\text{median}, Q_{75} are the quartiles, and \text{IQR} = Q_{75} - Q_{25} spans the middle half — robust because it ignores the outer quarters entirely.
