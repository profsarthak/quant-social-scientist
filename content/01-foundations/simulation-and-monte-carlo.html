Simulation & Monte Carlo

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When the integral is too hard, roll dice instead. Using random numbers to compute probabilities, expectations, and integrals no formula can reach — and to see randomness work.

Most real probability questions have no closed-form answer. Monte Carlo methods sidestep the algebra: generate random draws, and let the Law of Large Numbers turn their average into the answer. Lambert calls it “abandoning brute force” — instead of grinding out an integral, you sample and count. It is how you estimate an intractable probability, integrate in a thousand dimensions, build a bootstrap sampling distribution, and — not least — see the limit theorems happen on screen.

Why it matters

Simulation is both a computational tool (approximate what you can’t derive) and a way of thinking. Lambert stresses it’s the most direct route to internalising the “repeated sample and sampling distribution” concepts at the heart of statistics: when you can simulate a process a million times, the abstract idea of a sampling distribution becomes something you can literally plot. (Lambert 2018) His image: lost in dense fog, you take random steps and record where you are and how high — and from enough random samples you build a reliable map of the mountain. (Lambert 2018) Sampling is how you map a landscape you can’t see all at once.

Generating random variables

Everything rests on turning a stream of uniform random numbers into draws from the distribution you want. Two workhorse methods: (Ross 2018)

  • Inverse-transform. The basic technique: to draw from a distribution with CDF F, generate U\sim \text{Uniform}(0,1) and set X = F^{-1}(U). (Ross 2018) (This is the probability integral transform run backwards.) An exponential variate, for instance, is X=-\ln(U)/\lambda. (Ross 2018) Clean when you can invert F.
  • Rejection sampling. When F^{-1} is intractable, draw from an easy proposal g(x) and accept the draw with probability proportional to f(x)/g(x), rejecting the rest. (Lambert 2018) It always works for a density you can evaluate, but Lambert warns it can be “pretty wasteful” — many draws thrown away — which is exactly the inefficiency that motivates MCMC for hard, high-dimensional targets. (Lambert 2018)

Monte Carlo integration

The central trick: an integral is an expectation, and an expectation is an average you can sample. To approximate \mathbb E[g(X)]=\int g(x)f(x)\,dx, draw X_1,\dots,X_n from f and take the sample mean: (Ross 2018) \mathbb E[g(X)] \approx \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g(X_i). The Law of Large Numbers guarantees this converges to the true value, and the CLT tells you the error shrinks like 1/\sqrt{n}. (Ross 2018) That 1/\sqrt n rate is the catch and the magic: to halve the error you need four times the samples — but the rate doesn’t depend on the dimension, which is why Monte Carlo crushes ordinary numerical integration in high dimensions.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
n = 20000
x = rng.random(n); y = rng.random(n)
inside = x**2 + y**2 <= 1.0
running = 4 * np.cumsum(inside) / np.arange(1, n + 1)

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.2, 4.2))
m = 2000
axL.scatter(x[:m][inside[:m]], y[:m][inside[:m]], s=4, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.6)
axL.scatter(x[:m][~inside[:m]], y[:m][~inside[:m]], s=4, color="#c9ccd1", alpha=0.6)
tt = np.linspace(0, np.pi/2, 100)
axL.plot(np.cos(tt), np.sin(tt), color="#c0392b", lw=2)
axL.set_aspect("equal"); axL.set_title(f"2,000 points → π ≈ {running[m-1]:.3f}")
axL.set_xlim(0, 1); axL.set_ylim(0, 1)

axR.plot(np.arange(1, n + 1), running, color="#1f3a5f", lw=1)
axR.axhline(np.pi, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.4)
axR.set_xscale("log"); axR.set_ylim(2.9, 3.4)
axR.set_xlabel("number of points (log scale)"); axR.set_ylabel("estimate of π")
axR.set_title(f"running estimate → π  (final ≈ {running[-1]:.4f})")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Classic experiments

The method’s pedigree is full of vivid examples: (Ross 2018; Grinstead and Snell 1997)

  • Estimating \pi — random points in a square, count the fraction inside the circle (above).
  • Buffon’s needle — drop needles on a ruled grid; the fraction crossing a line yields \pi. A physical Monte Carlo, two centuries before computers. (Grinstead and Snell 1997)
  • The bootstrap — “a simple Monte Carlo technique” that approximates a sampling distribution by resampling with replacement from the data you have. (Grinstead and Snell 1997)
  • Seeing the LLN — simulate trials and watch the running mean home in on the expectation, the law made visible. (Grinstead and Snell 1997)

This is also the engine of every executable figure in this book: each one is a small Monte Carlo, generating data to make an idea concrete.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Expecting fast convergence. The error is only O(1/\sqrt n): 100× the samples buys 10× the accuracy. Plan the sample size for the precision you need. (Ross 2018)

  • Forgetting Monte Carlo error is itself random. A simulation estimate has a standard error; report it (or use enough draws that it’s negligible). (Ross 2018)

  • Wasteful rejection sampling. A poorly-matched proposal rejects almost everything; in high dimensions this fails badly — reach for MCMC. (Lambert 2018)

  • A bad random-number generator. Garbage pseudo-randomness (correlations, short period) poisons every estimate; use a vetted generator and seed it for reproducibility.

## Why it matters

## Generating random variables

Everything rests on turning a stream of **uniform** random numbers into draws from the distribution you want.
Two workhorse methods: [@ross_first_course]

- **Inverse-transform.** The basic technique: to draw from a distribution with CDF $F$, generate $U\sim
  \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and set $X = F^{-1}(U)$. [@ross_first_course] (This is the [probability integral
  transform](transformations-and-sums.qmd) run backwards.) An exponential variate, for instance, is
  $X=-\ln(U)/\lambda$. [@ross_first_course] Clean when you can invert $F$.

## Monte Carlo integration

The central trick: **an integral is an expectation, and an expectation is an average you can sample.** To
approximate $\mathbb E[g(X)]=\int g(x)f(x)\,dx$, draw $X_1,\dots,X_n$ from $f$ and take the **sample mean**:
[@ross_first_course]
$$
\mathbb E[g(X)] \approx \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g(X_i).
$$
The [Law of Large Numbers](law-of-large-numbers.qmd) guarantees this converges to the true value, and the
[CLT](../02-inference/sampling-distributions-clt.qmd) tells you the error shrinks like $1/\sqrt{n}$.
[@ross_first_course] That $1/\sqrt n$ rate is the catch *and* the magic: to halve the error you need *four
times* the samples — but the rate **doesn't depend on the dimension**, which is why Monte Carlo crushes
ordinary numerical integration in high dimensions.
