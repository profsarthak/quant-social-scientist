Vector Autoregression: VAR, Granger & Impulse Responses

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Stop privileging one variable as ‘the’ outcome. Let every series explain every other, then ask the system how a shock ripples through it.

ARIMA models one series from its own past. But macro and social systems are webs: output, inflation, and interest rates each move the others. Vector autoregression (VAR) treats them symmetrically — every variable is regressed on the lagged values of all variables, none privileged as exogenous. From that one democratic system come three tools: Granger causality (does the past of X help predict Y?), impulse responses (how does a shock propagate?), and variance decomposition (which shocks drive which series?).

Why it matters

Old large-scale macro models hard-wired which variables were “causes” and which “effects” — restrictions Christopher Sims (1980) attacked as “incredible,” lacking real theoretical basis (Enders 2014). His atheoretical alternative: treat every variable as endogenous and let the data speak. The VAR became the workhorse of empirical macro and a staple of political science and finance — and its innovation accounting (impulse responses + variance decomposition) is how researchers read dynamic cause-and-effect within a system without imposing a dubious structural story.

The VAR(p) system

A VAR of order p models a vector of n variables \mathbf{x}_t as a linear function of its own p lags — each equation containing the lags of every variable (Enders 2014). The crux is the distinction between two forms:

Structural (primitive) VAR — includes contemporaneous effects among variables, with pure, uncorrelated structural shocks \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t:

B\mathbf{x}_t = \Gamma_0 + \Gamma_1 \mathbf{x}_{t-1} + \cdots + \Gamma_p \mathbf{x}_{t-p} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t .

Reduced-form VAR — premultiply by B^{-1} to get the estimable system:

\mathbf{x}_t = A_0 + \sum_{i=1}^p A_i\,\mathbf{x}_{t-i} + \mathbf{e}_t .

The catch: the reduced-form errors \mathbf{e}_t = B^{-1}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t are composites of the structural shocks, so they are correlated across equations and have no direct economic interpretation (Enders 2014). That correlation is the whole identification problem (below).

Estimation and lag length

Because every equation has the identical set of regressors (the same lags of all variables), OLS equation-by-equation is consistent and asymptotically efficient — no system estimator needed (Enders 2014). The real choice is the lag length p: too few causes misspecification, too many wastes degrees of freedom (Enders 2014). Pick p to minimise an information criterion, \text{AIC} = T\ln|\Sigma| + 2N or \text{SBC} = T\ln|\Sigma| + N\ln T (with N total parameters), or test nested lag lengths with a likelihood-ratio statistic (T-c)\,(\ln|\Sigma_r| - \ln|\Sigma_u|), which is \chi^2 (Enders 2014).

Granger causality: predictive precedence, not cause

In a VAR, Granger causality measures predictive precedence, not philosophical causation (Enders 2014). Variable y Granger-causes z if past values of y help predict z beyond what z’s own past predicts (Enders 2014). You test it with an F-test that the coefficients on all lags of y in the z-equation are jointly zero; block exogeneity is the multivariate version, asking whether a block of variables helps predict the rest (Enders 2014).

Warning‘Granger-causes’ is a loaded name

It means temporal predictability, full stop. y can Granger-cause z with no causal link — both driven by a third variable, or by anticipation (stock prices “Granger-cause” earnings because they price in the future). Granger causality is necessary-ish for, but very far from sufficient for, the causal claims of Part V. (Enders 2014)

Impulse-response functions

The impulse-response function (IRF) traces the effect of a one-time shock to one innovation on the current and future values of all variables (Enders 2014). It comes from the vector moving-average (VMA) representation — the VAR rewritten as an infinite sum of past shocks:

\mathbf{x}_t = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \sum_{i=0}^{\infty}\Phi_i\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_{t-i}.

The matrices \Phi_i are the impulse responses: element (j,k) of \Phi_i is the response of variable j, i periods after a shock to variable k. Plotting them shows how a shock propagates through the system and decays (provided the VAR is stationary) (Enders 2014).

Identification: why the ordering matters

Here is the subtlety. Because the reduced-form residuals \mathbf{e}_t are correlated, a “shock to one equation” can’t be cleanly attributed to one variable — you must impose identifying restrictions (Enders 2014). The standard device is the Choleski (Cholesky) decomposition: a recursive ordering that makes the contemporaneous-effects matrix triangular, imposing exactly (n^2-n)/2 restrictions (Enders 2014). Its meaning: the variable ordered first affects all others contemporaneously, but is itself moved by the others only with a lag (Enders 2014).

The consequence is unavoidable: when residuals are highly correlated, changing the ordering changes the impulse responses and variance decompositions — the Sims–Bernanke concern (Enders 2014). The ordering is an economic assumption, not a technicality.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

A = np.array([[0.5, 0.3],            # VAR(1) coefficient matrix (stable)
              [0.2, 0.4]])
Sigma = np.array([[1.0, 0.8],        # reduced-form residual covariance (corr 0.8)
                  [0.8, 1.0]])
H = 12

def orth_irf(A, Sigma, order):
    """Orthogonalized IRF to a 1-SD shock in the variable placed FIRST under `order`."""
    P = np.eye(2)[order]                       # permutation to chosen order
    Sig_o = P @ Sigma @ P.T
    L = np.linalg.cholesky(Sig_o)              # lower-triangular factor
    shock = P.T @ (L @ np.array([1.0, 0.0]))   # 1-SD shock to first-ordered var, back to original axes
    resp = np.empty((H, 2)); cur = shock.copy()
    for h in range(H):
        resp[h] = cur
        cur = A @ cur                          # Phi_i = A^i applied to the shock
    return resp

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(8.6, 3.8), sharey=True)
for ax, order, lab in [(axes[0], [0, 1], "Ordering X → Y"),
                       (axes[1], [1, 0], "Ordering Y → X")]:
    r = orth_irf(A, Sigma, order)
    ax.axhline(0, color="#999", lw=0.8)
    ax.plot(range(H), r[:, 0], "o-", color="#161613", lw=2, ms=4, label="response of X")
    ax.plot(range(H), r[:, 1], "s-", color="#e8531d", lw=2, ms=4, label="response of Y")
    ax.set_title(lab, fontsize=10); ax.set_xlabel("horizon (periods after shock)")
    ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)
axes[0].set_ylabel("response to a 1-SD shock")
fig.suptitle("Same VAR, two Cholesky orderings → different impulse responses", fontsize=11.5)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Variance decomposition

The forecast-error variance decomposition (FEVD) is the IRF’s complement: it partitions the variance of each variable’s forecast error into the share attributable to each structural shock (Enders 2014). It answers “what % of the movement in z comes from z’s own shocks versus shocks to other variables?” Typically a variable explains most of its own variance at short horizons, with other variables’ shocks growing more important at longer horizons as effects diffuse through the system (Enders 2014). IRFs and FEVD together are Sims’s “innovation accounting” (Enders 2014).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reading Granger causality as causation. It is predictive precedence; a significant F-test is not a causal license. (Enders 2014)
  • Ignoring the Cholesky ordering. Highly correlated residuals make IRFs/FEVD ordering-dependent; justify the ordering or report robustness across orderings. (Enders 2014)
  • Running a VAR on non-stationary levels. If the series are I(1) and cointegrated, you need a VECM, not a plain VAR; if I(1) and not cointegrated, model the differences. (Enders 2014)
  • Over-lagging. Each extra lag costs n^2 parameters; let AIC/SBC and LR tests discipline p. (Enders 2014)
  • Over-interpreting structural shocks from a reduced form. Reduced-form residuals have no economic meaning until identified. (Enders 2014)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Enders, Applied Econometric Time Series — Sims’s “incredible restrictions” critique and the atheoretical VAR; the structural vs reduced-form systems and why reduced-form errors are correlated composites; OLS equation-by-equation estimation and lag-length selection (AIC/SBC, the LR statistic); Granger causality as predictive precedence with the joint F-test and block exogeneity; impulse responses from the VMA representation \mathbf{x}_t=\mu+\sum\Phi_i\epsilon_{t-i}; the identification problem, the Choleski recursive ordering with (n^2-n)/2 restrictions, and why ordering matters (Sims–Bernanke); the FEVD and “innovation accounting.”

Appendix · the mathematics

From VAR to VMA. Write the VAR(1) \mathbf{x}_t = A\mathbf{x}_{t-1} + \mathbf{e}_t (demeaned). Back- substituting, \mathbf{x}_t = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} A^i \mathbf{e}_{t-i}, so the reduced-form impulse matrices are A^i. Stationarity requires every eigenvalue of A to lie inside the unit circle, which makes A^i \to 0 and the responses decay. A VAR(p) is recast as a VAR(1) in companion form to apply the same result.

Orthogonalizing the shocks. With reduced-form covariance \Sigma = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{e}_t\mathbf{e}_t'], the Cholesky factor L (lower-triangular, LL'=\Sigma) defines orthogonal shocks \mathbf{u}_t = L^{-1}\mathbf{e}_t with identity covariance. The orthogonalized IRF to a unit shock in the k-th orthogonal innovation is \Phi_i L\,\mathbf{s}_k (with \mathbf{s}_k the k-th unit vector). Triangularity is the recursive assumption — variable 1 reacts to none of the others contemporaneously, and so on — which is exactly why a different ordering yields a different L and different responses.

Counting restrictions. Identifying n^2 structural parameters from the n(n+1)/2 distinct elements of \Sigma requires (n^2 - n)/2 extra restrictions; a recursive (triangular) B supplies exactly that many, which is why Cholesky just identifies the system.

Connections

  • Builds on: ARIMA (the univariate AR generalised to a system), unit roots (VARs need stationary inputs), and multiple regression (each equation is an OLS).
  • Leads to: cointegration & error-correction — the VECM is a VAR for cointegrated I(1) series.
  • Contrasts with: causal inference — Granger causality is predictive, not the structural causality of potential outcomes and DAGs.

  causal license. [@enders_time_series]
- **Ignoring the Cholesky ordering.** Highly correlated residuals make IRFs/FEVD ordering-dependent;
  justify the ordering or report robustness across orderings. [@enders_time_series]
- **Running a VAR on non-stationary levels.** If the series are I(1) and *cointegrated*, you need a
  [VECM](cointegration-and-error-correction.qmd), not a plain VAR; if I(1) and not cointegrated, model the
  differences. [@enders_time_series]
- **Over-lagging.** Each extra lag costs $n^2$ parameters; let AIC/SBC and LR tests discipline $p$.
  [@enders_time_series]
- **Over-interpreting structural shocks from a reduced form.** Reduced-form residuals have no economic
  meaning until identified. [@enders_time_series]
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**From VAR to VMA.** Write the VAR(1) $\mathbf{x}_t = A\mathbf{x}_{t-1} + \mathbf{e}_t$ (demeaned). Back-
substituting, $\mathbf{x}_t = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} A^i \mathbf{e}_{t-i}$, so the reduced-form impulse
matrices are $A^i$. Stationarity requires every eigenvalue of $A$ to lie **inside the unit circle**, which
makes $A^i \to 0$ and the responses decay. A VAR($p$) is recast as a VAR(1) in *companion form* to apply
the same result.

**Orthogonalizing the shocks.** With reduced-form covariance $\Sigma = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{e}_t\mathbf{e}_t']$,
the Cholesky factor $L$ (lower-triangular, $LL'=\Sigma$) defines orthogonal shocks
$\mathbf{u}_t = L^{-1}\mathbf{e}_t$ with identity covariance. The **orthogonalized IRF** to a unit shock in
the $k$-th orthogonal innovation is $\Phi_i L\,\mathbf{s}_k$ (with $\mathbf{s}_k$ the $k$-th unit vector).
Triangularity is the recursive assumption — variable 1 reacts to none of the others contemporaneously, and
so on — which is exactly why a different ordering yields a different $L$ and different responses.

**Counting restrictions.** Identifying $n^2$ structural parameters from the $n(n+1)/2$ distinct elements of
$\Sigma$ requires $(n^2 - n)/2$ extra restrictions; a recursive (triangular) $B$ supplies exactly that
many, which is why Cholesky *just* identifies the system.

:::
