Forecasting with ARIMA

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Three letters — autoregression, integration, moving average — and a disciplined recipe for predicting what comes next.

Once a series is stationary, forecasting becomes tractable. The ARIMA family says the future is a blend of two memories — the series’ own past values (AR) and its past shocks (MA) — applied to a differenced (I) series. The Box–Jenkins method is the disciplined cycle for choosing, fitting, and checking such a model, and its forecasts come with one unavoidable honesty: the further ahead you look, the less you know.

Why it matters

Forecasting is the explicit goal for vast swaths of applied time-series work — central banks, firms, governments all need a number for next quarter. (Enders 2014) ARIMA models are the classical workhorse, and even where modern ML competes, the ARIMA concepts — autoregression, the memory of shocks, differencing, and widening forecast uncertainty — remain the vocabulary. This entry turns the stationarity foundations into actual predictions, and brings the bias–variance and parsimony themes of the chapter to bear on choosing a model.

The building blocks: AR, MA, ARMA

Autoregressive — AR(p). Model today as a regression on its own recent past: (Enders 2014)

y_t = a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1} + a_2 y_{t-2} + \cdots + a_p y_{t-p} + \epsilon_t .

The AR(1) case y_t = a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t depends only on yesterday. It is stationary iff |a_1| < 1; at a_1 = 1 it is the random walk. (Enders 2014) An AR process has infinite memory — a shock propagates forward through the chain of lagged y’s, decaying as a_1^h.

Moving average — MA(q). Model today as a weighted sum of recent shocks: (Enders 2014)

y_t = \epsilon_t + \beta_1 \epsilon_{t-1} + \cdots + \beta_q \epsilon_{t-q} .

The contrast with AR is the key intuition: MA has finite memory — a shock \epsilon_t affects the series for exactly q periods and then vanishes completely. (Enders 2014)

ARMA(p,q) combines both, often fitting a series with fewer total parameters than a pure AR or MA would need — the parsimony principle at work: (Enders 2014)

y_t = a_0 + \sum_{i=1}^p a_i\,y_{t-i} + \epsilon_t + \sum_{i=1}^q \beta_i\,\epsilon_{t-i}.

ARIMA: adding the “I” for integrated

Most economic series are non-stationary in levels — they trend. (Enders 2014) The I in ARIMA handles that: a series is integrated of order d (I(d)) if it must be differenced d times to become stationary. So ARIMA(p,d,q) = difference the series d times (usually d = 1 for economic data), then fit an ARMA(p,q) to the stationary result \Delta^d y_t. (Enders 2014) The whole pipeline: difference to stationarity, model the dependence, forecast, then un-difference.

The Box–Jenkins method

Box and Jenkins gave the field its disciplined, three-stage iterative cycle for finding the right model. (Enders 2014)

Stage 1 — Identification. On the stationary series, read the ACF and partial ACF (PACF) to propose tentative orders p and q. The PACF at lag s is the correlation between y_t and y_{t-s} after removing the linear influence of the intervening lags y_{t-1},\dots,y_{t-s+1} — it isolates the direct dependence at lag s, whereas the ordinary ACF blends direct and indirect channels together. The signature patterns: (Enders 2014)

Process ACF PACF
AR(p) decays geometrically (smoothly or oscillating) cuts off after lag p
MA(q) cuts off after lag q decays geometrically
ARMA(p,q) decays (after lag q) decays (after lag p)

The mnemonic: the PACF fingerprints AR order; the ACF fingerprints MA order.

Stage 2 — Estimation. Fit the tentative model by maximum likelihood or non-linear least squares. Enders stresses the principle of parsimony — the simplest adequate model, to avoid overfitting the sample’s idiosyncrasies — and compares candidates with the AIC and BIC/SBC information criteria (lower is better; both reward fit and penalise extra parameters). (Enders 2014)

Stage 3 — Diagnostic checking. A good model leaves residuals that are white noise — no structure left to exploit. The Ljung–Box Q-test checks whether a batch of residual autocorrelations is jointly non-zero; if it is, the model is inadequate and you loop back to Stage 1. (Enders 2014)

Forecasting: mean reversion and widening intervals

With a fitted model, point forecasts come from iterating the conditional expectation forward — for AR(1), the one-step forecast is \mathbb{E}_t[y_{t+1}] = a_0 + a_1 y_t, then feed it back in for two steps, and so on. (Enders 2014) Two properties define how those forecasts behave:

  • Mean reversion. For any stationary ARMA model, the forecast \mathbb{E}_t[y_{t+j}] converges to the series’ unconditional mean as the horizon j grows. (Enders 2014) Far enough out, your best guess is simply “the long-run average” — the dependence that made the near future predictable has decayed away.
  • Widening intervals. Forecasts are (approximately) unbiased but less certain the further out you look: each additional period adds a new unknown shock \epsilon_{t+j} to the accumulated error, so the forecast-error variance grows with the horizon. (Enders 2014)
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
a1, mu, sigma = 0.7, 10.0, 1.0
a0 = mu * (1 - a1)
T = 80
y = np.empty(T); y[0] = mu
for t in range(1, T):
    y[t] = a0 + a1 * y[t-1] + rng.normal(0, sigma)

H = 30
fc = np.empty(H); last = y[-1]
for h in range(H):
    last = a0 + a1 * last
    fc[h] = last
# forecast-error variance at horizon h+1: sigma^2 * (1-a1^{2(h+1)})/(1-a1^2)
var_h = sigma**2 * (1 - a1**(2*(np.arange(1, H+1)))) / (1 - a1**2)
band = 1.96 * np.sqrt(var_h)

t_hist = np.arange(T); t_fut = np.arange(T, T + H)
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.5, 4.0))
ax.plot(t_hist, y, color="#1f3a5f", lw=1.4, label="observed series")
ax.axhline(mu, color="#9aa0a6", ls="--", lw=1.3, label="unconditional mean")
ax.plot(t_fut, fc, color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.2, label="point forecast")
ax.fill_between(t_fut, fc - band, fc + band, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.18,
                label="95% forecast interval")
ax.axvline(T - 0.5, color="#c0392b", lw=0.8, ls=":")
ax.set_xlabel("time"); ax.set_ylabel("y")
ax.set_title("AR(1) forecast: mean reversion + widening uncertainty")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Honesty about the method

Enders is candid that Box–Jenkins is “a blend of skill, art, and perseverance,” not a mechanical science. (Enders 2014) The ambiguities are real: in his interest-rate-spread analysis, AIC and BIC disagree — one favouring an AR(7), the other an AR(6) — and only judgment resolves it. (Enders 2014) You must also watch for common factors — AR and MA terms that algebraically cancel. Writing L for the lag operator, which shifts a series back one period (Ly_t=y_{t-1}, so L^k y_t = y_{t-k}), a model such as (1-0.5L)y_t = (1-0.5L)\epsilon_t carries the same factor on both sides; it cancels to plain white noise y_t=\epsilon_t, signalling an over-parameterised model that a simpler one would capture. (Enders 2014) The discipline is the cycle; the craft is the choices within it.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Fitting ARMA to a non-stationary series. Difference to stationarity first (the “I”); modelling trending levels invites spurious results. (Enders 2014)

  • Over-fitting with too many lags. A high-order model fits the sample’s noise and forecasts worse. Favour parsimony; let AIC/BIC and the test of residual whiteness discipline p and q. (Enders 2014)

  • Trusting long-horizon point forecasts. Stationary forecasts revert to the mean, so far-out predictions carry little information beyond the average — and their intervals are wide. Report the interval, not just the point. (Enders 2014)

  • Skipping diagnostic checking. If the residuals aren’t white noise (Ljung–Box rejects), the model is missing structure — respecify, don’t forecast. (Enders 2014)

  • Ignoring the common-factor trap. Cancelling AR and MA roots mean you’ve over-parameterised; simplify. (Enders 2014)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Enders, Applied Econometric Time Series — the AR(p) model and the AR(1) stationarity condition |a_1|<1; the MA(q) model and its finite memory; ARMA parsimony; ARIMA(p,d,q) and the integrated/differencing component; the Box–Jenkins three stages (ACF/PACF identification with the cut-off/decay rules, ML/NLS estimation, parsimony and AIC/BIC, diagnostic checking with Ljung–Box); forecasting by iterated conditional expectation, mean reversion to the unconditional mean, and forecast-error variance growing with the horizon; the “skill, art, and perseverance” caveat, the interest-rate-spread AIC-vs-BIC ambiguity, and the common-factor problem.

Appendix · the mathematics

AR(1) moments. For |a_1|<1, taking expectations of y_t=a_0+a_1y_{t-1}+\epsilon_t gives the unconditional mean \mu = a_0/(1-a_1), and the variance \gamma_0=\sigma^2/(1-a_1^2). The ACF is \rho_s = a_1^{\,s} — geometric decay, the AR fingerprint.

Why intervals widen (AR(1)). Write y_{t+h}-\mu = a_1^h(y_t-\mu) + \sum_{i=0}^{h-1} a_1^{i}\epsilon_{t+h-i}. The conditional forecast is \mathbb{E}_t[y_{t+h}] = \mu + a_1^h(y_t-\mu) (→ \mu as h\to\infty: mean reversion), and the forecast-error variance is

\operatorname{Var}_t(y_{t+h}) = \sigma^2\sum_{i=0}^{h-1} a_1^{2i} = \sigma^2\,\frac{1-a_1^{2h}}{1-a_1^2},

which increases in h and, for |a_1|<1, converges to the unconditional variance \sigma^2/(1-a_1^2) — so the band widens toward a constant. For a unit root (a_1=1) the sum is h\sigma^2, growing without bound (\sqrt h intervals) — the forecasting signature of non-stationarity.

Information criteria. \text{AIC} = -2\ln L + 2k and \text{BIC} = -2\ln L + k\ln n for k parameters and likelihood L. BIC’s heavier penalty (\ln n > 2 for n>7) favours smaller models — the usual source of the AIC-vs-BIC disagreement Enders flags.

[@enders_time_series] You must also watch for **common factors** — AR and MA terms that algebraically
cancel. Writing $L$ for the **lag operator**, which shifts a series back one period ($Ly_t=y_{t-1}$,
so $L^k y_t = y_{t-k}$), a model such as $(1-0.5L)y_t = (1-0.5L)\epsilon_t$ carries the *same* factor
on both sides; it cancels to plain white noise $y_t=\epsilon_t$, signalling an over-parameterised
model that a simpler one would capture. [@enders_time_series] The discipline is the cycle; the craft is the
choices within it.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Fitting ARMA to a non-stationary series.** Difference to [stationarity first](time-series-fundamentals.qmd)
  (the "I"); modelling trending levels invites [spurious](time-series-fundamentals.qmd) results.
  [@enders_time_series]

- **Over-fitting with too many lags.** A high-order model fits the sample's noise and forecasts worse.
  Favour parsimony; let AIC/BIC and the [test of residual whiteness](#the-boxjenkins-method) discipline
  $p$ and $q$. [@enders_time_series]

- **Trusting long-horizon point forecasts.** Stationary forecasts revert to the mean, so far-out
  predictions carry little information beyond the average — and their intervals are wide. Report the
  interval, not just the point. [@enders_time_series]

- **Skipping diagnostic checking.** If the residuals aren't white noise (Ljung–Box rejects), the model
  is missing structure — respecify, don't forecast. [@enders_time_series]

- **Ignoring the common-factor trap.** Cancelling AR and MA roots mean you've over-parameterised;
  simplify. [@enders_time_series]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**AR(1) moments.** For $|a_1|<1$, taking expectations of $y_t=a_0+a_1y_{t-1}+\epsilon_t$ gives the
unconditional mean $\mu = a_0/(1-a_1)$, and the variance $\gamma_0=\sigma^2/(1-a_1^2)$. The ACF is
$\rho_s = a_1^{\,s}$ — geometric decay, the AR fingerprint.

**Why intervals widen (AR(1)).** Write $y_{t+h}-\mu = a_1^h(y_t-\mu) + \sum_{i=0}^{h-1} a_1^{i}\epsilon_{t+h-i}$.
The conditional forecast is $\mathbb{E}_t[y_{t+h}] = \mu + a_1^h(y_t-\mu)$ (→ $\mu$ as $h\to\infty$:
**mean reversion**), and the forecast-error variance is

$$
\operatorname{Var}_t(y_{t+h}) = \sigma^2\sum_{i=0}^{h-1} a_1^{2i} = \sigma^2\,\frac{1-a_1^{2h}}{1-a_1^2},
$$

which **increases in $h$** and, for $|a_1|<1$, **converges** to the unconditional variance
$\sigma^2/(1-a_1^2)$ — so the band widens toward a constant. For a unit root ($a_1=1$) the sum is
$h\sigma^2$, growing **without bound** ($\sqrt h$ intervals) — the forecasting signature of
non-stationarity.

**Information criteria.** $\text{AIC} = -2\ln L + 2k$ and $\text{BIC} = -2\ln L + k\ln n$ for $k$
parameters and likelihood $L$. BIC's heavier penalty ($\ln n > 2$ for $n>7$) favours smaller models —
the usual source of the AIC-vs-BIC disagreement Enders flags.

:::
