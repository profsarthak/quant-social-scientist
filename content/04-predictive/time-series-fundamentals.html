Time-Series Fundamentals

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When the data are ordered in time, yesterday predicts today — and the i.i.d. world you built on quietly collapses.

Everything so far assumed observations were a random sample — order irrelevant, each draw independent. Time series breaks both: the data come in order, and today is correlated with yesterday. That single change voids the i.i.d. machinery, introduces a spectacular trap (spurious regression), and forces a new central concept — stationarity — before any forecasting can begin.

Why it matters

GDP, inflation, public opinion, crime rates, stock prices — vast tracts of social data arrive as time series, and the tools of the previous chapters can mislead badly when applied to them. (Enders 2014) The defining feature is temporal ordering: in a cross-section the order of rows is meaningless, but in a time series the sequence is everything, because the past affects the future. (Wooldridge 2019) This breaks the i.i.d. assumption under all of Parts II–IV, and it opens a notorious trap — regressing two unrelated trending series yields a beautiful, entirely fake result. This entry builds the foundations (dependence, stationarity, unit roots) that the forecasting entry then turns into models.

What a time series is made of

A series is conventionally decomposed into four components: (Enders 2014)

  • Trend — long-run direction (real GDP’s steady climb).
  • Seasonality — regular fluctuations within a year (December retail, spring housing starts).
  • Cycles — periodic swings longer than a year (the business cycle).
  • Irregular / random — the unpredictable noise left after the rest.

But the deeper feature is dependence: today’s GDP is highly correlated with yesterday’s, so the observations are emphatically not independent. (Wooldridge 2019) Quantifying that dependence is the first task.

Autocorrelation and the ACF

Autocorrelation (serial correlation) is the correlation of a series with its own past. The autocorrelation function (ACF) reports it at each lag s: (Enders 2014)

\rho_s = \frac{\gamma_s}{\gamma_0},

where \gamma_s = \operatorname{Cov}(y_t, y_{t-s}) is the autocovariance at lag s and \gamma_0 = \operatorname{Var}(y_t). Plotting \rho_s against s gives the correlogram, the basic diagnostic of a series’ memory. For a well-behaved (stationary) series the correlogram decays toward zero as lags grow — the distant past eventually stops mattering. (Enders 2014)

Stationarity: the stability a series needs

For inference and forecasting to be possible at all, the process must be stable over time — stationary. The working definition is weak (covariance) stationarity: (Enders 2014)

\mathbb{E}[y_t] = \mu, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(y_t) = \sigma^2, \qquad \operatorname{Cov}(y_t, y_{t-s}) = \gamma_s \text{ (depends only on the lag } s, \text{ not on } t).

In words: constant mean, constant variance, and an autocovariance that depends only on the gap between observations, not on when you look. (Enders 2014) Why insist on this? Because if a process changes its basic nature at every instant, a single historical path tells you nothing about the future. (Enders 2014) Stationarity is precisely what lets the law of large numbers and the CLT apply to time averages — restoring the ground the i.i.d. assumption used to provide.

The atom of it all is white noise: a sequence \{\epsilon_t\} with mean zero, constant variance \sigma^2, and zero autocorrelation at every non-zero lag. (Enders 2014) White noise is the purest stationary series and the building block of every model in the next entry.

The random walk and the unit root

The most important non-stationary process — and the one social data so often resembles — is the random walk: (Enders 2014)

y_t = y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t.

Each value is last period’s plus a fresh shock. Embed it in y_t = \rho_1 y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t; the random walk is the knife-edge case \rho_1 = 1, a unit root. (Enders 2014) Two consequences make it treacherous:

  • The variance grows with time: \operatorname{Var}(y_t) = t\sigma^2. The series has no tendency to revert to any mean — it meanders wherever the shocks push it. (Enders 2014)
  • Shocks are permanent. In a stationary series a shock fades; in a random walk every shock shifts all future values forever. (Enders 2014)

Add a constant and you get a random walk with drift, y_t = a_0 + y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t, combining a deterministic trend with the stochastic wandering. (Enders 2014) To test for a unit root, the Dickey–Fuller test regresses the change on the lagged level, \Delta y_t = \gamma y_{t-1} + \cdots; failing to reject \gamma = 0 means a unit root is present. (Enders 2014)

The trap: spurious regression

Here is the warning that makes this entry essential. Regress one trending, non-stationary series on another, completely unrelated one, and you get a deceptively high R^2 and wildly significant t-statistics — pointing to a strong relationship that does not exist. (Wooldridge 2019) Both series wander under their own stochastic trends, and by sheer coincidence they appear to move together. First diagnosed by Granger and Newbold (1974), this is spurious regression, and its lesson is blunt: in time-series levels, a high R^2 is often a sign of trouble, not of a good fit. (Enders 2014)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(11)
T = 200
y = np.cumsum(rng.normal(0, 1, T))      # random walk 1
x = np.cumsum(rng.normal(0, 1, T))      # random walk 2 — INDEPENDENT of y

# regress y on x
b1 = np.cov(x, y, bias=True)[0, 1] / np.var(x)
b0 = y.mean() - b1 * x.mean()
fit = b0 + b1 * x
ss_res = np.sum((y - fit) ** 2); ss_tot = np.sum((y - y.mean()) ** 2)
r2 = 1 - ss_res / ss_tot
se = np.sqrt(ss_res / (T - 2) / np.sum((x - x.mean()) ** 2))
tstat = b1 / se

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.2, 3.8))
axL.plot(y, color="#1f3a5f", lw=1.6, label="series Y (random walk)")
axL.plot(x, color="#d98a29", lw=1.6, label="series X (random walk)")
axL.set_title("Two independent random walks"); axL.set_xlabel("time")
axL.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)

axR.scatter(x, y, s=12, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.6)
xs = np.linspace(x.min(), x.max(), 30)
axR.plot(xs, b0 + b1 * xs, color="#c0392b", lw=2.2)
axR.set_title(f"Regress Y on X:  R² = {r2:.2f},  t = {tstat:.1f}")
axR.set_xlabel("X"); axR.set_ylabel("Y")
fig.suptitle("Spurious regression: a strong 'relationship' that isn't there", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

A “t of 10” and an “R^2 of 0.7” between two series that are, by construction, unrelated — that is the whole danger in one picture. Standard inference simply does not work on non-stationary levels.

The fix: differencing and integration

The cure for a unit root is differencing. The first difference \Delta y_t = y_t - y_{t-1} of a random walk is — by construction — just \epsilon_t, i.e. white noise, which is stationary. (Enders 2014) More generally, a series is integrated of order d, written I(d), if it must be differenced d times to become stationary: (Enders 2014)

  • I(0): already stationary (white noise, a stationary AR series).
  • I(1): one unit root — a random walk. Most highly persistent economic series (interest rates, the S&P 500) are treated as I(1). (Enders 2014)

A series made stationary by differencing is difference-stationary. Once differenced to stationarity, the honest forecasting models of the next entry — the “I” in ARIMA — apply. (Enders 2014)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Running OLS on non-stationary levels. Spurious regression gives fake high R^2 and huge t-stats between unrelated series. Test for stationarity (Dickey–Fuller) and difference first. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Treating a high R^2 as success in time series. In levels it often signals shared trends, not a real relationship — the opposite of the cross-sectional intuition. (Enders 2014)

  • Assuming shocks fade. In an I(1) series shocks are permanent; “it’ll revert to trend” is false for a random walk. (Enders 2014)

  • Ignoring autocorrelation. Serially correlated errors make ordinary standard errors wrong (too small), inflating significance — the time-series cousin of clustering. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Over-differencing. Differencing an already-stationary series injects an artificial MA unit root and needless noise. Difference only as much as a unit-root test demands. (Enders 2014)

From the sources

NoteWho says what

  • Wooldridge, Introductory Econometrics — time series vs cross-section and the breakdown of i.i.d.; serial correlation and its effect on inference; the spurious-regression warning.

Appendix · the mathematics

Why a random walk’s variance grows. Iterating y_t=y_{t-1}+\epsilon_t from y_0 gives y_t = y_0 + \sum_{i=1}^t \epsilon_i. With i.i.d. shocks of variance \sigma^2, \operatorname{Var}(y_t)=t\sigma^2 — unbounded in t, so no stationary variance exists. The autocovariance \operatorname{Cov}(y_t,y_{t-s})=(t-s)\sigma^2 depends on t, not just s — violating stationarity directly.

Shocks are permanent. \partial y_{t+h}/\partial \epsilon_t = 1 for every horizon h\ge 0 (the shock enters the running sum and never leaves), versus a stationary AR(1) where the effect is \rho_1^h \to 0. Permanence is the signature of a unit root.

Why spurious regression happens. Regressing one I(1) series on an independent I(1) series, the OLS t-statistic does not converge to a standard normal; it diverges with the sample size, so “significance” grows with T no matter what. The fix — differencing to I(0), or cointegration when a genuine long-run relation exists — restores standard asymptotics. Two I(1) series are cointegrated when some linear combination of them is itself I(0)/stationary: each wanders without bound, yet they wander together, tethered by a long-run equilibrium (the textbook case is a drunk and her dog — both random walks, but never far apart). When that holds, a regression in levels is no longer spurious but estimates the genuine equilibrium relation.

First-differencing a random walk. \Delta y_t = y_t - y_{t-1} = \epsilon_t, which is white noise: mean 0, variance \sigma^2, zero autocorrelation — stationary by inspection. Hence a random walk is I(1).

Connections

  • Builds on: covariance & correlation (autocorrelation is correlation with the past), the LLN & CLT (which stationarity restores for time averages), and regression diagnostics (serial correlation as a cousin of heteroskedasticity/clustering).
  • Leads to: forecasting with ARIMA — AR, MA, and the “I” that this entry’s differencing supplies.

  • Contrast: nonlinear dynamics & chaos — irregular series need not be stochastic; deterministic chaos produces aperiodic paths from a fixed rule, and sets a hard prediction horizon no model can beat.

```{python}
xs = np.linspace(x.min(), x.max(), 30)
axR.plot(xs, b0 + b1 * xs, color="#c0392b", lw=2.2)
axR.set_title(f"Regress Y on X:  R² = {r2:.2f},  t = {tstat:.1f}")
axR.set_xlabel("X"); axR.set_ylabel("Y")
fig.suptitle("Spurious regression: a strong 'relationship' that isn't there", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

A "$t$ of 10" and an "$R^2$ of 0.7" between two series that are, by construction, **unrelated** —
that is the whole danger in one picture. Standard [inference](../02-inference/hypothesis-testing.qmd)
simply does not work on non-stationary levels.

## The fix: differencing and integration

The cure for a unit root is **differencing**. The **first difference**
$\Delta y_t = y_t - y_{t-1}$ of a random walk is — by construction — just $\epsilon_t$, i.e. **white
noise, which is stationary**. [@enders_time_series] More generally, a series is **integrated of order
$d$**, written **I($d$)**, if it must be differenced $d$ times to become stationary:
[@enders_time_series]

- **I(0):** already stationary (white noise, a stationary AR series).
- **I(1):** one unit root — a random walk. Most highly persistent economic series (interest rates,
  the S&P 500) are treated as I(1). [@enders_time_series]

A series made stationary by differencing is **difference-stationary**. Once differenced to
stationarity, the honest [forecasting models](arima-forecasting.qmd) of the next entry — the "I" in
ARIMA — apply. [@enders_time_series]

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Running OLS on non-stationary levels.** Spurious regression gives fake high $R^2$ and huge
  $t$-stats between unrelated series. Test for stationarity (Dickey–Fuller) and difference first.
  [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Treating a high $R^2$ as success in time series.** In levels it often signals shared trends, not
  a real relationship — the opposite of the cross-sectional intuition. [@enders_time_series]

- **Assuming shocks fade.** In an I(1) series shocks are *permanent*; "it'll revert to trend" is false
  for a random walk. [@enders_time_series]

- **Ignoring autocorrelation.** Serially correlated errors make ordinary
  [standard errors](../03-regression/regression-diagnostics.qmd) wrong (too small), inflating
  significance — the time-series cousin of clustering. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Over-differencing.** Differencing an already-stationary series injects an artificial MA unit root
  and needless noise. Difference only as much as a unit-root test demands. [@enders_time_series]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why a random walk's variance grows.** Iterating $y_t=y_{t-1}+\epsilon_t$ from $y_0$ gives
$y_t = y_0 + \sum_{i=1}^t \epsilon_i$. With i.i.d. shocks of variance $\sigma^2$,
$\operatorname{Var}(y_t)=t\sigma^2$ — unbounded in $t$, so no stationary variance exists. The
autocovariance $\operatorname{Cov}(y_t,y_{t-s})=(t-s)\sigma^2$ depends on $t$, not just $s$ —
violating stationarity directly.

**Shocks are permanent.** $\partial y_{t+h}/\partial \epsilon_t = 1$ for every horizon $h\ge 0$ (the
shock enters the running sum and never leaves), versus a stationary AR(1) where the effect is
$\rho_1^h \to 0$. Permanence is the signature of a unit root.

**Why spurious regression happens.** Regressing one I(1) series on an independent I(1) series, the
OLS $t$-statistic does **not** converge to a standard normal; it *diverges* with the sample size, so
"significance" grows with $T$ no matter what. The fix — differencing to I(0), or
**cointegration** when a genuine long-run relation exists — restores standard asymptotics. Two I(1)
series are **cointegrated** when some linear combination of them is itself I(0)/stationary: each
wanders without bound, yet they wander *together*, tethered by a long-run equilibrium (the textbook
case is a drunk and her dog — both random walks, but never far apart). When that holds, a regression
in *levels* is no longer spurious but estimates the genuine equilibrium relation.

**First-differencing a random walk.** $\Delta y_t = y_t - y_{t-1} = \epsilon_t$, which is white noise:
mean 0, variance $\sigma^2$, zero autocorrelation — stationary by inspection. Hence a random walk is
I(1).

:::
