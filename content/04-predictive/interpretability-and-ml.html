Interpretability & ML for Social Science

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When accuracy is worth a black box, how to pry the box open — and how ML re-enters the causal game.

The flexible methods that predict best are the hardest to read, and social science cares about why. This entry navigates that tension: the accuracy–interpretability frontier, the tools that partly open a black box (importance, partial dependence, SHAP), the iron rule that interpretable is not causal, and the genuinely exciting part — how machine learning, used carefully, comes back to serve the causal questions of Part V.

Why it matters

This entry closes the predictive sub-chapter by confronting its central discomfort for researchers: the most accurate models are black boxes, and a discipline built on explanation cannot simply accept “it predicts well, trust me.” (Jacobucci et al. 2023) So we do two things. First, survey the tools that make a complex model partly legible. Second — and more importantly — show that the right response isn’t to reject ML but to bend it back toward causation: ML is now a power tool inside modern causal inference, not a rival to it. This is the bridge from prediction to the summit.

The accuracy–interpretability frontier

The trade-off from the opening entry, now made central: as model flexibility rises, interpretability falls. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Restrictive, transparent — linear and logistic regression, the Lasso: each coefficient is a ceteris paribus effect you can hand a policymaker. Limited in shape, but legible.
  • Flexible, opaque — random forests, boosting, neural nets: they bend to complex non-linearities and high-order interactions and often predict far better, but their input–output map runs through thousands of parameters that defy human explanation. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

methods = [
    ("Subset selection / Lasso", 0.92, 0.22),
    ("Linear / logistic regression", 0.96, 0.32),
    ("Generalized additive models", 0.70, 0.50),
    ("Decision trees", 0.80, 0.45),
    ("Bagging / random forests", 0.34, 0.74),
    ("Boosting", 0.28, 0.80),
    ("Neural nets / SVM", 0.15, 0.95),
]
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.6))
for name, interp, flex in methods:
    ax.scatter(interp, flex, s=70, color="#1f3a5f", zorder=3)
    right = interp > 0.55                                   # label points inward
    ax.annotate(name, (interp, flex), textcoords="offset points",
                xytext=(-8 if right else 8, 4), fontsize=8.5,
                ha="right" if right else "left")
ax.annotate("", xy=(0.12, 0.97), xytext=(0.97, 0.20),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="-", color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.4))
ax.text(0.45, 0.50, "the frontier:\nno model is both", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8.5, rotation=-34)
ax.set_xlim(0.0, 1.05)                         # interpretability increasing rightward (ISL convention)
ax.set_ylim(0.1, 1.05)
ax.set_xlabel("Interpretability  →"); ax.set_ylabel("Flexibility / predictive accuracy  →")
ax.set_title("You choose where to sit — there's no free lunch")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Social science has historically clustered at the interpretable end, because a model that predicts perfectly but explains nothing is of limited value for theory-building. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) But that needn’t mean abandoning flexible models — only learning to interrogate them.

Opening the box: interpretable ML

A toolkit has grown up to extract human-readable insight from complex models. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Variable (feature) importance — a heuristic ranking of which predictors drive the model, via Gini/RSS reduction or permutation. Answers “which features matter?”
  • Partial dependence plots (PDP) — show how the prediction changes as one feature varies, with all others averaged out. They reveal the shape of a learned relationship — linear, S-shaped, threshold — even inside a forest. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • SHAP and LIME — local explanations of a single prediction. SHAP (Shapley Additive exPlanations) attributes a prediction to its features by their exact additive contributions, naming the “key drivers” of this case; LIME fits a simple linear model locally around one point to see what mattered there. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) They answer “why did the model predict this for this person?”

These make a black box accountable — but accountable about its predictions, not about the world.

The iron rule: interpretable ≠ causal

The most important caveat in the chapter. A feature-importance ranking or a partial-dependence plot is descriptive and predictive — never, by itself, a causal effect. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) It shows how the model uses information to guess, not how the world works. A predictor can rank high because it’s a proxy, a collider, or a confounded correlate.

Causation still requires an identification strategy — randomization, instruments, a credible design — to strip out selection bias. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) As Chernozhukov puts it: however impressive a model looks, its outputs carry no causal interpretation unless the data satisfy conditions like conditional exogeneity / unconfoundedness — the very assumptions Part III hammered. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) Accuracy is not identification.

ML comes back to causation

Here is the exciting turn. Rather than competing with causal inference, modern ML has become a power tool inside it. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • High-dimensional controls. With thousands of potential confounders, you can’t hand-pick controls. Use the Lasso to systematically select which ones are needed to make a “other things equal” comparison credible. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE). Instead of one average effect, causal forests (a causal adaptation of random forests) and BART automate the hunt for interactions — discovering for whom a treatment works best. Crucially, they are “honest”: they use separate subsamples for choosing the splits and for estimating effects, so they don’t manufacture illusory interactions (the garden of forking paths, defused by design). (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Double / debiased machine learning (DML). Use ML to flexibly learn the nuisance parts — how the outcome and the treatment each depend on the controls — then partial them out (an FWL-style residualisation) and estimate the treatment effect on what’s left. This keeps the final effect estimate valid for inference, dodging the bias that arises from plugging ML directly into a coefficient. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Prediction-policy problems. Some policy questions are genuinely predictive, not causal: targeting interventions to those most at risk — of suicide, of loan default, of dropping out. Here a good black-box predictor is exactly what’s wanted. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

This is the substance of the causal summit’s modern wing — and why prediction sits between regression and causation in this book.

Risks: bias, fishing, and gaming

Power brings hazards social scientists must manage. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Encoded bias. Models learn the prejudices in their training data — text embeddings absorb gender and racial bias from the corpus — and can propagate or amplify them. Audit and correct. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Overfitting and fishing. Flexible models excel at mistaking noise for signal; without cross-validation and a sacred test set, “discoveries” won’t replicate. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Gaming (Goodhart’s law). As The Tyranny of Metrics warns, when a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure — people game the predictor. Combined with the duty to report everything (every algorithm, transformation, and tuning choice) to prevent p-hacking, this is the transparency the next part demands. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reading importance or a PDP as a causal effect. They explain the model, not the world. Causal claims need identification, not a high feature ranking. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Rejecting ML wholesale because it’s a black box. Interpretable-ML tools and causal-ML methods (DML, causal forests) make flexible models genuinely useful to researchers. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Plugging ML predictions straight into a regression coefficient. Naïvely using ML for inference biases the effect; DML’s cross-fitting and orthogonalisation exist precisely to fix this. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Trusting a fair-looking model on biased data. ML faithfully reproduces the discrimination in its training data. Fairness must be checked, not assumed. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Optimising a deployed metric and forgetting Goodhart. Once people know the predictor, they game its inputs; a model that worked in validation can rot in deployment. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Appendix · the mathematics

Partial dependence, defined. The partial dependence of the prediction on feature x_S averages the fitted model over the empirical distribution of the other features x_C:

\widehat{\text{PD}}_S(x_S) = \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \hat f\big(x_S,\, x_{C,i}\big).

Sweeping x_S traces the marginal shape the model learned — interpretable, but still a property of \hat f, not a causal dose–response.

Why naïve ML biases an effect (the DML idea). For Y=\theta D + g(X)+\epsilon, D=m(X)+v, regressing Y on D with an ML estimate \hat g plugged in carries the regularisation bias of \hat g into \hat\theta. DML instead forms residuals \tilde Y = Y-\hat g(X) and \tilde D = D-\hat m(X) (each fit on a separate fold — cross-fitting) and regresses \tilde Y on \tilde D. The resulting

\hat\theta = \frac{\sum_i \tilde D_i \tilde Y_i}{\sum_i \tilde D_i^2}

is Neyman-orthogonal: first-order insensitive to errors in \hat g,\hat m, so it stays \sqrt n-consistent and supports valid confidence intervals. It is exactly Frisch–Waugh–Lovell partialling-out, with ML doing the partialling and sample-splitting protecting the inference.

- **Rejecting ML wholesale because it's a black box.** Interpretable-ML tools and causal-ML methods
  (DML, causal forests) make flexible models genuinely useful to researchers. [@jacobucci_ml]

- **Plugging ML predictions straight into a regression coefficient.** Naïvely using ML for inference
  biases the effect; DML's cross-fitting and orthogonalisation exist precisely to fix this.
  [@jacobucci_ml]

- **Trusting a fair-looking model on biased data.** ML faithfully reproduces the discrimination in its
  training data. Fairness must be checked, not assumed. [@jacobucci_ml]

- **Optimising a deployed metric and forgetting Goodhart.** Once people know the predictor, they game
  its inputs; a model that worked in validation can rot in deployment. [@jacobucci_ml]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Partial dependence, defined.** The partial dependence of the prediction on feature $x_S$ averages
the fitted model over the *empirical* distribution of the other features $x_C$:

$$
\widehat{\text{PD}}_S(x_S) = \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \hat f\big(x_S,\, x_{C,i}\big).
$$

Sweeping $x_S$ traces the marginal shape the model learned — interpretable, but still a property of
$\hat f$, not a causal dose–response.

**Why naïve ML biases an effect (the DML idea).** For $Y=\theta D + g(X)+\epsilon$,
$D=m(X)+v$, regressing $Y$ on $D$ with an ML estimate $\hat g$ plugged in carries the *regularisation
bias* of $\hat g$ into $\hat\theta$. DML instead forms residuals $\tilde Y = Y-\hat g(X)$ and
$\tilde D = D-\hat m(X)$ (each fit on a *separate* fold — **cross-fitting**) and regresses $\tilde Y$
on $\tilde D$. The resulting

$$
\hat\theta = \frac{\sum_i \tilde D_i \tilde Y_i}{\sum_i \tilde D_i^2}
$$

is *Neyman-orthogonal*: first-order insensitive to errors in $\hat g,\hat m$, so it stays
$\sqrt n$-consistent and supports valid [confidence intervals](../02-inference/confidence-intervals.qmd).
It is exactly [Frisch–Waugh–Lovell](../03-regression/multiple-regression.qmd) partialling-out, with ML
doing the partialling and sample-splitting protecting the inference.

:::
