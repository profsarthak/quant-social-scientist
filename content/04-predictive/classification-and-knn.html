Classification: When the Outcome Is a Category

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Most social-science prediction is which class, not how much — so accuracy, the Bayes rule, and the ROC curve replace the squared error.

Half of supervised learning predicts a label, not a number: voted or not, defaulted or not, relapsed or not. The squared-error machinery of bias–variance still governs the trade-offs, but the scoreboard changes. A single accuracy number lies when one class is rare; the honest picture is a confusion matrix and the sensitivity–specificity trade it encodes. And just as regression had an irreducible-error floor, classification has the Bayes error rate — the best any classifier could ever do.

Why it matters

Everything so far measured a predictor by mean squared error — a regression loss. But the outcomes social scientists most often predict are categorical: a yes/no, a class, a diagnosis. (James et al. 2021) Classification is arguably more common than regression in applied social science — predicting loan defaults, vote choice, dropout, recidivism, clinical diagnosis — and it changes two things at once: the loss you minimise and the metrics you report. (Bruce et al. 2020) The modelling philosophy is unchanged — fit on a training set, judge on unseen data, tune flexibility by the bias–variance trade — but you need a new vocabulary to say what “good” means when there is no residual to square.

The Bayes classifier: the floor for labels

Regression had irreducible error \operatorname{Var}(\epsilon) — a floor no model beats. (James et al. 2021) Classification has the exact analog: the Bayes classifier. Given the predictors X=x_0, it assigns the class with the highest conditional probability \Pr(Y=j \mid X=x_0) — for two classes, predict 1 whenever \Pr(Y=1\mid X=x_0) > 0.5. (James et al. 2021) This rule is unbeatable: no classifier can have a lower expected error rate.

Its error — the Bayes error rate — is the floor, and it is usually above zero because the classes overlap in the population: at the same x_0 you sometimes see a 1 and sometimes a 0, so even perfect knowledge of the probabilities cannot separate them. (James et al. 2021) This is the lesson to carry: just as no model predicts noise, no classifier achieves 100% accuracy when classes overlap — and chasing it means overfitting. The catch is that the Bayes rule needs the true \Pr(Y\mid X), which we never have; real methods estimate it (as logistic regression does directly) or approximate the rule by looking at nearby points — which is exactly what K-nearest neighbours does.

K-nearest neighbours: the simplest learner

K-nearest neighbours (KNN) is the canonical non-parametric classifier and the natural first method to meet. (James et al. 2021) It makes no assumption about the shape of the boundary. To classify a new point x_0: find the K training observations closest to it, and take a majority vote of their labels. (James et al. 2021) That is the whole algorithm — it estimates the Bayes rule locally, using the neighbourhood’s class proportions as a stand-in for \Pr(Y=j\mid X=x_0).

KNN is worth knowing not because it always wins but because it makes two universal lessons concrete:

  • K is a flexibility dial. K=1 is maximally flexible — every point gets its nearest neighbour’s label, carving a jagged, overfit boundary (low bias, high variance). A large K averages over a wide neighbourhood, giving a smooth, rigid boundary (high bias, low variance). (James et al. 2021) The bias–variance U-curve reappears, now indexed by 1/K.
  • Scaling is not optional. Because KNN measures distance, a predictor on a large numeric scale (income in dollars) swamps one on a small scale (years of age) — the large-scale variable silently dominates “closeness.” You must standardise the predictors first so each contributes comparably. (James et al. 2021; Bruce et al. 2020) (This distance-sensitivity is why KNN degrades in high dimensions, where “nearest” neighbours are not actually near — the curse of dimensionality.)

The confusion matrix: the heart of classification metrics

You cannot summarise a classifier with one number. Start from the confusion matrix — for a binary outcome, the 2\times2 table cross-tabulating predictions against truth: (Bruce et al. 2020)

Actual 1 (positive) Actual 0 (negative)
Predicted 1 True Positive (TP) False Positive (FP)
Predicted 0 False Negative (FN) True Negative (TN)

Every metric is a ratio drawn from this table. The reason it matters — and the reason raw accuracy (\text{TP}+\text{TN})/n is dangerous — is the rare-class problem (the accuracy paradox). (Bruce et al. 2020) If only 1% of students attempt self-harm, a model that predicts “never” for everyone is 99% accurate and completely useless: it never flags the cases you built it to catch. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) Accuracy hides the failure; the confusion matrix exposes it.

Sensitivity, specificity, precision

The table yields the metrics that actually matter, each answering a different question: (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Sensitivity (= recall, the true-positive rate) =\dfrac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP}+\text{FN}} — of the actual positives, what fraction did we catch? How good are we at finding the 1s?
  • Specificity (the true-negative rate) =\dfrac{\text{TN}}{\text{TN}+\text{FP}} — of the actual negatives, what fraction did we correctly clear?
  • Precision (positive predictive value) =\dfrac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP}+\text{FP}} — when we predict positive, how often are we right?

Which one rules depends on the costs of the two errors, and in social science they are rarely equal. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) Missing a high-risk student (a false negative) may be far costlier than offering unneeded tutoring to a safe one (a false positive) — so you would prize sensitivity even at the price of precision. A spam filter inverts the costs: a false positive (a real email lost to the spam folder) hurts more, so you prize precision. The metric is a policy choice, not a statistical default.

The threshold and the ROC curve

Here is the move that ties it together. A classifier like logistic regression outputs a probability, and turning that into a label requires a cutoff. The default 0.5 is just the Bayes rule under equal costs — but you can move it. (Bruce et al. 2020) Lower the threshold and you flag more positives: sensitivity rises, but more false alarms drop specificity. Raise it and the reverse. Sensitivity and specificity are on a see-saw, and the threshold is the fulcrum.

Plot that entire trade-off and you get the ROC curve (Receiver Operating Characteristic): the true-positive rate against the false-positive rate (1-\text{specificity}) as the threshold sweeps from high to low. (Bruce et al. 2020) A useless (coin-flip) classifier rides the 45° diagonal; a good one bows toward the top-left corner (high sensitivity and high specificity at once). The single summary is the AUC — the Area Under the Curve: 0.5 is no better than chance, 1.0 is perfect separation. (Bruce et al. 2020) AUC has a clean interpretation — the probability that the model scores a randomly chosen positive above a randomly chosen negative — and, crucially, it judges the model across all thresholds at once, so you compare classifiers before committing to an operating point.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
n = 2000
# Latent scores: positives shifted up, but the classes OVERLAP (Bayes error > 0).
neg = rng.normal(0.0, 1.0, n)
pos = rng.normal(1.6, 1.0, n)
scores = np.concatenate([neg, pos])
labels = np.concatenate([np.zeros(n), np.ones(n)])

# Sweep thresholds high -> low, build the ROC by confusion-matrix counts.
thr = np.linspace(scores.max() + 0.5, scores.min() - 0.5, 250)
tpr = np.array([(pos >= t).mean() for t in thr])   # sensitivity
fpr = np.array([(neg >= t).mean() for t in thr])   # 1 - specificity
auc = np.sum(np.diff(fpr) * (tpr[:-1] + tpr[1:]) / 2)  # area under ROC (trapezoid)

t0 = 0.8                                            # one illustrative cutoff
tpr0, fpr0 = (pos >= t0).mean(), (neg >= t0).mean()

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(7.6, 3.7))

# Left: overlapping score distributions with a threshold.
axL.hist(neg, bins=40, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.7, label="actual 0")
axL.hist(pos, bins=40, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.6, label="actual 1")
axL.axvline(t0, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.6)
axL.text(t0 + 0.1, axL.get_ylim()[1] * 0.9, "threshold", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axL.set_xlabel("model score"); axL.set_ylabel("count")
axL.set_title("Overlapping scores"); axL.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)

# Right: the ROC curve.
axR.plot(fpr, tpr, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.2)
axR.fill_between(fpr, tpr, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.10)
axR.plot([0, 1], [0, 1], color="#9aa0a6", ls="--", lw=1.3)
axR.plot(fpr0, tpr0, "o", color="#c0392b", ms=7)
axR.text(fpr0 + 0.03, tpr0 - 0.08, "this cutoff", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axR.text(0.55, 0.25, f"AUC = {auc:.2f}", color="#1f3a5f", fontsize=11, fontweight="bold")
axR.set_xlabel("false-positive rate (1 − specificity)")
axR.set_ylabel("true-positive rate (sensitivity)")
axR.set_title("ROC curve"); axR.set_xlim(0, 1); axR.set_ylim(0, 1)

fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Validation: same loop, different scoreboard

Does any of the cross-validation machinery change? The process does not: you still split train/validation/test, still use k-fold CV to tune flexibility, and still treat the test set as sacred. (James et al. 2021) Two things differ: (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • The metric. Replace MSE with a classification loss — the error rate, or better, a probability-aware loss like log-loss (cross-entropy), which penalises confident mistakes more than hesitant ones and rewards well-calibrated probabilities.
  • The threshold is itself a tuning choice. Pick the operating point to match your misclassification costs — and, like every data-driven choice, do it inside the CV loop, never on the test set, or you inherit the same optimism/leakage that inflates every other accuracy estimate. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reporting accuracy on imbalanced data. With a rare positive, “95% accurate” can mean “never finds the thing.” Always show the confusion matrix and the sensitivity for the class you care about. (Bruce et al. 2020; Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Treating 0.5 as sacred. The cutoff is a policy lever set by the relative cost of false negatives vs false positives — not a fixed constant. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Forgetting to scale before KNN (or any distance method). Unstandardised predictors let the largest-scale variable dictate “nearness,” silently. (James et al. 2021)

  • Chasing zero error. When classes overlap, the Bayes error rate is a hard floor; a classifier near it is finished, not deficient. Pushing past it fits noise. (James et al. 2021)

  • Tuning the threshold on the test set. That is leakage by another name. Choose it inside cross-validation. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Appendix · the mathematics

The Bayes rule and its error. For classes j, the Bayes classifier is \hat C_{\text{Bayes}}(x_0) = \arg\max_{j}\ \Pr(Y=j \mid X=x_0). At a point x_0 its error is 1-\max_j \Pr(Y=j\mid X=x_0), and the overall Bayes error rate is the average of this over the distribution of X: 1 - \mathbb{E}_X\!\Big[\max_j \Pr(Y=j\mid X)\Big]. It is zero only if some class has probability 1 everywhere — i.e. the classes never overlap. KNN estimates the inner probability by the local frequency \hat\Pr(Y=j\mid X=x_0)=\frac1K\sum_{i\in \mathcal N_K(x_0)} \mathbb 1(y_i=j) over the K nearest neighbours \mathcal N_K(x_0), then applies the same \arg\max. As n\to\infty with K growing slowly, KNN’s error approaches the Bayes rate.

Log-loss (cross-entropy). For predicted probabilities \hat p_i=\hat\Pr(Y_i=1\mid X_i) and labels y_i\in\{0,1\}, \text{log-loss} = -\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \big[y_i\ln \hat p_i + (1-y_i)\ln(1-\hat p_i)\big]. It is minimised (at 0) only by confident, correct probabilities and explodes when the model is confidently wrong (\hat p_i\to0 while y_i=1). This is exactly the negative log-likelihood that logistic regression maximises — the bridge from the explanatory model to the predictive scoreboard.

AUC as a probability. The area under the ROC equals \Pr\big(\hat s(X^+) > \hat s(X^-)\big) — the chance the model scores a random positive above a random negative — which is the Mann–Whitney U statistic rescaled. A coin flip gives 0.5; perfect ranking gives 1.

Connections

  • Builds on: bias–variance (the Bayes error rate is the classification floor, K is its flexibility dial) and logistic regression (the probability model whose output a threshold turns into a label).
  • Leads to: cross-validation (the same tuning loop, with a classification metric) and trees & forests (classifiers split on Gini/entropy, scored by these same metrics).
  • Reappears in: interpretability & ML and the prediction problems of Part V, where propensity scores are themselves classifiers whose calibration matters.

true-positive rate against the false-positive rate $(1-\text{specificity})$ as the threshold sweeps
from high to low. [@bruce_practical] A useless (coin-flip) classifier rides the 45° diagonal; a good
one bows toward the **top-left corner** (high sensitivity *and* high specificity at once). The single
summary is the **AUC** — the **Area Under the Curve**: $0.5$ is no better than chance, $1.0$ is
perfect separation. [@bruce_practical] AUC has a clean interpretation — the probability that the model
scores a randomly chosen positive *above* a randomly chosen negative — and, crucially, it judges the
model **across all thresholds at once**, so you compare classifiers before committing to an operating
point.

```{python}
#| label: fig-roc
#| fig-cap: "From overlapping scores to the ROC curve. Left: a classifier outputs higher scores for positives (green) than negatives (grey), but the distributions overlap, so no single cutoff separates them perfectly — that overlap is the Bayes error made visible. Sliding the threshold (dashed line) trades false negatives for false positives. Right: sweeping every threshold traces the ROC curve; the area under it (AUC) summarises the model's separating power across all cutoffs at once. The diagonal is a coin flip."
#| code-fold: true
#| code-summary: "Show the figure code"

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
n = 2000
# Latent scores: positives shifted up, but the classes OVERLAP (Bayes error > 0).
neg = rng.normal(0.0, 1.0, n)
pos = rng.normal(1.6, 1.0, n)
scores = np.concatenate([neg, pos])
labels = np.concatenate([np.zeros(n), np.ones(n)])

# Sweep thresholds high -> low, build the ROC by confusion-matrix counts.
thr = np.linspace(scores.max() + 0.5, scores.min() - 0.5, 250)
tpr = np.array([(pos >= t).mean() for t in thr])   # sensitivity
fpr = np.array([(neg >= t).mean() for t in thr])   # 1 - specificity
auc = np.sum(np.diff(fpr) * (tpr[:-1] + tpr[1:]) / 2)  # area under ROC (trapezoid)

t0 = 0.8                                            # one illustrative cutoff
tpr0, fpr0 = (pos >= t0).mean(), (neg >= t0).mean()

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(7.6, 3.7))

# Left: overlapping score distributions with a threshold.
axL.hist(neg, bins=40, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.7, label="actual 0")
axL.hist(pos, bins=40, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.6, label="actual 1")
axL.axvline(t0, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.6)
axL.text(t0 + 0.1, axL.get_ylim()[1] * 0.9, "threshold", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axL.set_xlabel("model score"); axL.set_ylabel("count")
axL.set_title("Overlapping scores"); axL.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)

# Right: the ROC curve.
axR.plot(fpr, tpr, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.2)
axR.fill_between(fpr, tpr, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.10)
axR.plot([0, 1], [0, 1], color="#9aa0a6", ls="--", lw=1.3)
axR.plot(fpr0, tpr0, "o", color="#c0392b", ms=7)
axR.text(fpr0 + 0.03, tpr0 - 0.08, "this cutoff", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axR.text(0.55, 0.25, f"AUC = {auc:.2f}", color="#1f3a5f", fontsize=11, fontweight="bold")
axR.set_xlabel("false-positive rate (1 − specificity)")
axR.set_ylabel("true-positive rate (sensitivity)")
axR.set_title("ROC curve"); axR.set_xlim(0, 1); axR.set_ylim(0, 1)

fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Validation: same loop, different scoreboard

Does any of the [cross-validation](cross-validation.qmd) machinery change? The **process** does not:
you still split train/validation/test, still use **k-fold CV** to tune flexibility, and still treat
the test set as sacred. [@islr] Two things differ: [@bruce_practical]

- **The metric.** Replace MSE with a classification loss — the **error rate**, or better, a
  probability-aware loss like **log-loss** (cross-entropy), which penalises *confident* mistakes more
  than hesitant ones and rewards well-calibrated probabilities.
- **The threshold is itself a tuning choice.** Pick the operating point to match your
  misclassification costs — and, like every data-driven choice, do it **inside** the CV loop, never on
  the test set, or you inherit the same [optimism/leakage](cross-validation.qmd) that inflates every
  other accuracy estimate. [@jacobucci_ml]

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Reporting accuracy on imbalanced data.** With a rare positive, "95% accurate" can mean "never
  finds the thing." Always show the confusion matrix and the sensitivity for the class you care
  about. [@bruce_practical; @jacobucci_ml]

- **Treating 0.5 as sacred.** The cutoff is a policy lever set by the relative cost of false
  negatives vs false positives — not a fixed constant. [@bruce_practical]

- **Forgetting to scale before KNN (or any distance method).** Unstandardised predictors let the
  largest-scale variable dictate "nearness," silently. [@islr]

- **Chasing zero error.** When classes overlap, the **Bayes error rate** is a hard floor; a classifier
  near it is finished, not deficient. Pushing past it fits noise. [@islr]

- **Tuning the threshold on the test set.** That is leakage by another name. Choose it inside
  cross-validation. [@jacobucci_ml]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The Bayes rule and its error.** For classes $j$, the Bayes classifier is
$$
\hat C_{\text{Bayes}}(x_0) = \arg\max_{j}\ \Pr(Y=j \mid X=x_0).
$$
At a point $x_0$ its error is $1-\max_j \Pr(Y=j\mid X=x_0)$, and the overall **Bayes error rate** is
the average of this over the distribution of $X$:
$$
1 - \mathbb{E}_X\!\Big[\max_j \Pr(Y=j\mid X)\Big].
$$
It is zero only if some class has probability 1 everywhere — i.e. the classes never overlap. KNN
estimates the inner probability by the local frequency
$\hat\Pr(Y=j\mid X=x_0)=\frac1K\sum_{i\in \mathcal N_K(x_0)} \mathbb 1(y_i=j)$ over the $K$ nearest
neighbours $\mathcal N_K(x_0)$, then applies the same $\arg\max$. As $n\to\infty$ with $K$ growing
slowly, KNN's error approaches the Bayes rate.

**Log-loss (cross-entropy).** For predicted probabilities $\hat p_i=\hat\Pr(Y_i=1\mid X_i)$ and labels
$y_i\in\{0,1\}$,
$$
\text{log-loss} = -\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \big[y_i\ln \hat p_i + (1-y_i)\ln(1-\hat p_i)\big].
$$
It is minimised (at $0$) only by confident, *correct* probabilities and explodes when the model is
confidently wrong ($\hat p_i\to0$ while $y_i=1$). This is exactly the negative log-likelihood that
[logistic regression](../03-regression/limited-dependent-variables.qmd) maximises — the bridge from
the explanatory model to the predictive scoreboard.

**AUC as a probability.** The area under the ROC equals
$\Pr\big(\hat s(X^+) > \hat s(X^-)\big)$ — the chance the model scores a random positive above a
random negative — which is the Mann–Whitney $U$ statistic rescaled. A coin flip gives $0.5$; perfect
ranking gives $1$.

:::
