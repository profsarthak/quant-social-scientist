From Explaining to Predicting: Two Goals, Two Cultures

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The same regression output, two incompatible scorecards — and the paradigm shift between them.

Everything so far asked why: estimate an effect, defend it, attach a standard error. Predictive modelling asks a different question — what next? — and keeps score completely differently. A coefficient that’s wrong-but-significant fails the first goal; a black box that forecasts perfectly fails the second’s interpretability but wins its accuracy. Knowing which game you’re playing is the whole of this chapter.

Why it matters

The same fitted regression can be a triumph or a disaster depending on what you wanted from it, and conflating the two goals is the source of endless confusion across academia and industry. (James et al. 2021) Social science has traditionally lived in the explanatory culture — theory, hypotheses, interpretable effects. The predictive culture — machine learning — optimises something else entirely, and judges success on data the model has never seen. This entry draws the line cleanly, because every method in Part IV (cross-validation, regularization, trees) only makes sense once you accept the predictive scorecard, and because modern causal inference now borrows these tools.

Two goals

Explanatory (statistical / inferential) Predictive (machine learning)
Asks Why? — the mechanism What next? — the forecast
Wants unbiased, interpretable effects with valid uncertainty (p-values, CIs) the most accurate prediction on new data
Will trade away predictive accuracy, flexibility interpretability, unbiasedness — happily treats the model as a black box
A “failure” is a biased or uninterpretable coefficient poor accuracy on unseen data

(James et al. 2021; Jacobucci et al. 2023) The point is not that one is better — it’s that they answer different questions and reward different models. A wrong-but-significant coefficient is a failure for explanation even if it predicts; a black box is a success for prediction even if no coefficient means anything. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

The statistical-learning frame: Y = f(X) + \epsilon

ISL gives the unifying notation. Assume the inputs X (features, predictors) relate to the output Y (the response) through (James et al. 2021)

Y = f(X) + \epsilon,

where f is the systematic information X carries about Y, and \epsilon is a random error, independent of X with mean zero. All of statistical learning is estimating f from training data to get \hat f — and there are exactly two reasons to do it: (James et al. 2021)

  • Prediction — produce a guess \hat Y = \hat f(X) for new observations. Here \hat f can be a black box; you only care that \hat Y is close to Y.
  • Inference — understand the form of f: which predictors matter, in what direction, linearly or not. Here you must be able to read \hat f.

This single fork — predict with \hat f vs interpret \hat f — is the two-cultures divide, formalised. (James et al. 2021)

Reducible vs irreducible error

Even a perfect estimate of f can’t predict perfectly, and ISL’s decomposition says why. The expected prediction error splits into two pieces: (James et al. 2021)

  • Reducible error — because \hat f \neq f. Better methods and more data shrink it; in principle it can go to zero. This is the part we work on.
  • Irreducible error — the \epsilon itself. Since Y depends on \epsilon, and \epsilon is by definition unpredictable from X, this error never goes away. It is an absolute ceiling on accuracy. (James et al. 2021)

Why does \epsilon exist? It holds the unmeasured and unmeasurable — a patient’s mood that day, a shopper’s whim, all the causes you didn’t (or couldn’t) record. (James et al. 2021) No algorithm, however clever, predicts what it has no information about.

The in-sample / out-of-sample paradox

Here is the crux of why a great explanatory model can be a terrible predictor. (James et al. 2021)

  • In-sample fit measures performance on the same data used to train — the R^2 and MSE you’ve optimised all through Part III.
  • Out-of-sample accuracy measures performance on a held-out test set (or via cross-validation) — data the model has never seen.

The two can diverge sharply through overfitting: a flexible model can fit the training data perfectly by memorising its noise — the random idiosyncrasies of those particular cases — rather than the signal f. (James et al. 2021)

TipLambert’s exam analogy

Overfitting is like cheating on a test by memorising the answer key beforehand: the student aces that exam and flunks the next one. (James et al. 2021) A model that has memorised its training data looks brilliant in-sample and fails the moment it meets new data — which is the only test that counts for prediction.

Watch it happen: training error always falls, test error doesn’t

Fit polynomials of rising flexibility to the same noisy data. The training error falls monotonically — more flexibility always fits the seen data better. But the test error is U-shaped: it improves, bottoms out at a sweet spot, then climbs as the model starts fitting noise. That gap is overfitting.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
def f_true(x):  return np.sin(2.2 * x) + 0.4 * x          # smooth signal

x_tr = np.sort(rng.uniform(-3, 3, 30))
y_tr = f_true(x_tr) + rng.normal(0, 0.45, x_tr.size)
x_te = np.sort(rng.uniform(-3, 3, 1000))
y_te = f_true(x_te) + rng.normal(0, 0.45, x_te.size)

degrees = range(1, 14)
train_mse, test_mse = [], []
for d in degrees:
    coef = np.polyfit(x_tr, y_tr, d)
    train_mse.append(np.mean((y_tr - np.polyval(coef, x_tr)) ** 2))
    test_mse.append(np.mean((y_te - np.polyval(coef, x_te)) ** 2))

train_mse, test_mse = np.array(train_mse), np.array(test_mse)
best = degrees[int(np.argmin(test_mse))]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.0))
ax.plot(list(degrees), train_mse, "o-", color="#9aa0a6", lw=2, label="training error (in-sample)")
ax.plot(list(degrees), test_mse, "o-", color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.2, label="test error (out-of-sample)")
ax.axvline(best, color="#1f8a4c", ls="--", lw=1.5)
ax.text(best + 0.2, test_mse.max() * 0.7, f"sweet spot\n(degree {best})", color="#1f8a4c", fontsize=8)
ax.axvspan(best + 0.5, 13.5, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.06)
ax.text(10.5, test_mse.max() * 0.9, "overfitting", color="#c0392b", fontsize=9, ha="center")
ax.set_xlabel("model flexibility (polynomial degree)"); ax.set_ylabel("mean squared error")
ax.set_yscale("log")
ax.set_title("Training error always falls; test error is U-shaped")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper center")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The lesson that organises the whole chapter: in-sample fit is not the goal — it can be made perfect and meaningless. The goal is the bottom of that blue curve, and finding it is what bias–variance and cross-validation are for.

Flexibility vs interpretability: no free lunch

A second trade-off runs through everything: as flexibility rises, interpretability falls. (James et al. 2021)

  • Restrictive, interpretable methods — linear regression, the Lasso — can only bend so far, but you can read exactly how each predictor acts. Ideal for inference.
  • Flexible, opaque methods — boosting, random forests, neural networks — fit wildly non-linear relationships and often predict better, but produce an \hat f so complex that no single predictor’s role is legible. Ideal for prediction. (James et al. 2021)

There is no method that is both maximally flexible and maximally interpretable — choosing where to sit on that frontier is choosing your culture. (We return to it in interpretability.)

Breiman’s two cultures

Leo Breiman named the divide. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • The data-modelling culture assumes Nature follows a specific stochastic model (a linear regression, say) and estimates its parameters. This has dominated social science.
  • The algorithmic-modelling culture treats Nature’s mechanism as unknown and uses algorithms to find whatever function maps X to Y best, judged by prediction.

For social scientists this is more than a technical choice — it’s a methodological shift. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) Used well, predictive tools let researchers test whether existing theories actually hold up out of sample, discover heterogeneous treatment effects (how an intervention helps different people differently), and — because out-of-sample validation is built in — provide a principled guard against the p-hacking and fishing that plague the explanatory culture. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) The cultures are complements, not enemies — which is exactly why this part sits between regression and the causal summit.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Judging a predictive model by in-sample R^2. Training fit can be made perfect by overfitting; it says nothing about new data. Evaluate out-of-sample, always. (James et al. 2021)

  • Judging an explanatory model by predictive accuracy (or vice versa). They optimise different things. A model can predict well with biased, uninterpretable coefficients — useless for a causal question — and an unbiased causal model can predict poorly. Pick the scorecard first. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Forgetting the irreducible error. No model beats the \epsilon ceiling; chasing zero error guarantees overfitting. Some of the world is genuinely unpredictable from your features. (James et al. 2021)

  • Assuming flexible = better. More flexibility lowers bias but raises variance and destroys interpretability; the right amount is a choice, not “as much as possible.” (James et al. 2021)

  • Treating a black box as an explanation. A forest can predict default beautifully and tell you nothing trustworthy about why — reading mechanism off a predictive model is a category error. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Appendix · the mathematics

The reducible/irreducible split. For a fixed \hat f and test point X=x_0, with Y=f(x_0)+\epsilon,

\mathbb{E}\big[(Y-\hat f(x_0))^2\big] = \underbrace{\big(f(x_0)-\hat f(x_0)\big)^2}_{\text{reducible}} + \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}(\epsilon)}_{\text{irreducible}} .

The cross term vanishes because \epsilon has mean zero and is independent of \hat f(x_0). No choice of \hat f touches \operatorname{Var}(\epsilon) — hence the ceiling. Averaging the reducible part over training sets is what produces the bias–variance decomposition of the next entry.

Why training error understates test error. Training error is \frac1n\sum_i (y_i-\hat f(x_i))^2 evaluated at the same points used to choose \hat f, so \hat f is tuned to those \epsilon_i. A flexible enough \hat f can drive it to 0 by interpolating the noise, while test error — evaluated at independent points with fresh \epsilon — rises. The gap is the optimism of the training error, and it grows with flexibility.

## Flexibility vs interpretability: no free lunch

A second trade-off runs through everything: **as flexibility rises, interpretability falls.** [@islr]

- **Restrictive, interpretable** methods — linear regression, the [Lasso](regularization.qmd) — can
  only bend so far, but you can read exactly how each predictor acts. Ideal for *inference*.
- **Flexible, opaque** methods — boosting, [random forests](trees-and-forests.qmd), neural networks
  — fit wildly non-linear relationships and often predict better, but produce an $\hat f$ so complex
  that no single predictor's role is legible. Ideal for *prediction*. [@islr]

There is no method that is both maximally flexible and maximally interpretable — choosing where to
sit on that frontier is choosing your culture. (We return to it in
[interpretability](interpretability-and-ml.qmd).)

## Breiman's two cultures

Leo Breiman named the divide. [@jacobucci_ml]

- The **data-modelling culture** assumes Nature follows a specific stochastic model (a linear
  regression, say) and estimates its parameters. This has **dominated social science**.
- The **algorithmic-modelling culture** treats Nature's mechanism as unknown and uses *algorithms*
  to find whatever function maps $X$ to $Y$ best, judged by prediction.

For social scientists this is more than a technical choice — it's a **methodological shift**.
[@jacobucci_ml] Used well, predictive tools let researchers test whether existing theories actually
*hold up out of sample*, discover **heterogeneous treatment effects** (how an intervention helps
different people differently), and — because out-of-sample validation is built in — provide a
principled guard against the [p-hacking and fishing](../02-inference/replication-crisis.qmd) that
plague the explanatory culture. [@jacobucci_ml] The cultures are complements, not enemies — which is
exactly why this part sits between regression and the causal summit.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Judging a predictive model by in-sample $R^2$.** Training fit can be made perfect by overfitting;
  it says nothing about new data. Evaluate out-of-sample, always. [@islr]

- **Judging an explanatory model by predictive accuracy (or vice versa).** They optimise different
  things. A model can predict well with biased, uninterpretable coefficients — useless for a causal
  question — and an unbiased causal model can predict poorly. Pick the scorecard first. [@jacobucci_ml]

- **Forgetting the irreducible error.** No model beats the $\epsilon$ ceiling; chasing zero error
  guarantees overfitting. Some of the world is genuinely unpredictable from your features. [@islr]

- **Assuming flexible = better.** More flexibility lowers bias but raises variance and destroys
  interpretability; the right amount is a choice, not "as much as possible." [@islr]

- **Treating a black box as an explanation.** A forest can predict default beautifully and tell you
  nothing trustworthy about *why* — reading mechanism off a predictive model is a category error.
  [@jacobucci_ml]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The reducible/irreducible split.** For a fixed $\hat f$ and test point $X=x_0$, with $Y=f(x_0)+\epsilon$,

$$
\mathbb{E}\big[(Y-\hat f(x_0))^2\big]
= \underbrace{\big(f(x_0)-\hat f(x_0)\big)^2}_{\text{reducible}}
+ \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}(\epsilon)}_{\text{irreducible}} .
$$

The cross term vanishes because $\epsilon$ has mean zero and is independent of $\hat f(x_0)$. No
choice of $\hat f$ touches $\operatorname{Var}(\epsilon)$ — hence the ceiling. Averaging the
reducible part over training sets is what produces the
[bias–variance decomposition](bias-variance-tradeoff.qmd) of the next entry.

**Why training error understates test error.** Training error is
$\frac1n\sum_i (y_i-\hat f(x_i))^2$ evaluated at the *same* points used to choose $\hat f$, so $\hat
f$ is tuned to those $\epsilon_i$. A flexible enough $\hat f$ can drive it to 0 by interpolating the
noise, while test error — evaluated at independent points with fresh $\epsilon$ — rises. The gap is
the **optimism** of the training error, and it grows with flexibility.

:::
