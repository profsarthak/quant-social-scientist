Unit Roots & Stationarity Testing

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

One decision governs every time-series model — difference, or don’t? — and the tests that answer it are subtler, and weaker, than they look.

Spurious regression makes the question unavoidable: is this series stationary, or does it have a unit root? Get it right and standard inference returns; get it wrong and you either chase phantom trends or destroy real ones. But the tests that decide are non-standard (the usual t-table doesn’t apply), directional (which hypothesis is the null matters), and famously low-powered (they often can’t tell a near-unit-root from a unit root). This entry is the careful machinery behind a deceptively simple “to difference or not.”

Why it matters

The previous entry introduced the unit root and the one-line Dickey–Fuller test. Real data force three complications it glossed over. First, economic errors are serially correlated, so the basic test is biased. Second, the choice between trend-stationary and difference-stationary changes your long-run forecasts entirely — and the wrong “fix” injects new pathologies. Third, the tests are weak enough that a confident verdict often isn’t available. Knowing how the tests work — and how they fail — is what separates mechanical differencing from judgement.

The Augmented Dickey–Fuller test

The basic Dickey–Fuller regression \Delta y_t = a_0 + \gamma y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t assumes \epsilon_t is white noise. Most economic series violate that — and if the residuals are correlated, \gamma and its standard error are poorly estimated (Enders 2014). The fix is to augment the regression with lagged differences of y, which “soak up” the serial correlation until the remaining error is white noise (Enders 2014):

\Delta y_t = a_0 + \gamma\,y_{t-1} + \sum_{i=2}^{p}\beta_i\,\Delta y_{t-i+1} + \epsilon_t .

The null of a unit root is \gamma = 0 (since \gamma = \rho_1 - 1); the test statistic is the t-ratio on \gamma, but read against special critical values (below).

The three test regressions. Which deterministic terms to include is a real decision, because the critical values differ for each (Enders 2014):

Specification Regression Statistic
Pure random walk (no constant) \Delta y_t = \gamma y_{t-1} + \sum \beta_i \Delta y_{t-i+1} + \epsilon_t \tau
With drift (constant) \Delta y_t = a_0 + \gamma y_{t-1} + \cdots \tau_\mu
With drift & linear trend \Delta y_t = a_0 + \gamma y_{t-1} + a_2 t + \cdots \tau_\tau

Choosing among them is crucial and asymmetric: including unnecessary deterministic regressors reduces power, but omitting a needed one (e.g. a trend in a trending series) gives a test with zero power as the sample grows (Enders 2014). Enders’ advice: plot the series first, match the specification to what you see, and use a general-to-specific strategy — start generously lagged and prune insignificant lags (Enders 2014).

Why the t-table doesn’t apply

You cannot read the ADF statistic against the normal or t distribution, because their theory assumes stationary variables (Enders 2014). Under the null, the series’ variance grows without bound, the OLS estimator of \rho is biased below unity, and the statistic does not converge to a standard normal (Enders 2014). Its true distribution — the Dickey–Fuller distribution — is obtained by Monte Carlo simulation and is skewed left: for the drift case the statistic centres near -1.53, not 0, so the 5% critical value sits around -2.9 rather than the familiar -1.65 (Enders 2014). Using the wrong table massively over-rejects.

KPSS: testing stationarity as the null

ADF puts the unit root on trial (null = non-stationary). The KPSS test (Kwiatkowski–Phillips–Schmidt–Shin) reverses the burden of proof: its null is stationarity, the alternative a unit root (Enders 2014). Because both tests are low-powered, they are best used together, as confirmatory analysis (Enders 2014):

ADF result KPSS result Conclusion
Fails to reject unit root Rejects stationarity Strong evidence of a unit root
Rejects unit root Fails to reject stationarity Strong evidence of stationarity
Both fail to reject (or both reject) — Data “not sufficiently informative”

That last row is the honest one: sometimes the series simply cannot adjudicate between the two, and the right answer is “we can’t tell.”

Phillips–Perron: the nonparametric route

The Phillips–Perron (PP) test attacks the same serial-correlation problem differently. Instead of adding lagged differences, it runs the basic DF regression and applies a nonparametric correction to the statistic itself, based on the residuals (Enders 2014). PP was once favoured for series with large negative MA components, but Enders notes it generally does not perform as well as the ADF test with a modified-AIC lag choice (Enders 2014).

Two ways to be non-stationary: TS vs DS

A trending series can be non-stationary in two fundamentally different ways, and distinguishing them is vital for long-run forecasting (Enders 2014):

  • Difference-stationary (DS). An I(1) series with a stochastic trend; stationary only after differencing (y_t - y_{t-1}). Shocks are permanent.
  • Trend-stationary (TS). Stationary once a deterministic trend is removed (y_t - a_0 - a_2 t). Shocks are transitory — the series returns to its trend line.

The mis-specification costs run both ways (Enders 2014):

  • Differencing a TS series (over-differencing) injects a non-invertible (unit) root into the MA part of the model — a needless pathology that wrecks estimation.
  • Detrending a DS series fails to remove the stochastic trend, leaving the series still non-stationary.

You must diagnose which kind you have; you cannot safely apply both fixes.

The low-power problem

Here is the practical warning. Unit-root tests have low power: when a series is genuinely stationary but its largest root is close to 1, they routinely fail to reject the false unit-root null (Enders 2014). Enders’ example is sobering — with 100 observations and a true AR coefficient of 0.95, the ADF test correctly rejects a unit root only 12.7% of the time (Enders 2014). A “fail to reject” is therefore weak evidence: it may mean a unit root, or merely a persistent stationary series the test can’t resolve.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
T, n_sim = 100, 600
crit_5 = -2.89                      # DF τ_μ 5% critical value, n≈100
rhos = np.array([1.00, 0.99, 0.97, 0.95, 0.90, 0.85, 0.80, 0.70])

def df_tau(y):
    """Drift-form Dickey–Fuller t-stat on gamma, OLS by hand."""
    dy = np.diff(y)                 # Delta y_t
    ylag = y[:-1]                   # y_{t-1}
    X = np.column_stack([np.ones_like(ylag), ylag])   # [const, level]
    beta, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(X, dy, rcond=None)
    resid = dy - X @ beta
    s2 = resid @ resid / (len(dy) - 2)
    covb = s2 * np.linalg.inv(X.T @ X)
    return beta[1] / np.sqrt(covb[1, 1])            # gamma / se(gamma)

power = []
for rho in rhos:
    rejects = 0
    for _ in range(n_sim):
        y = np.empty(T); y[0] = 0.0
        e = rng.normal(0, 1, T)
        for t in range(1, T):
            y[t] = rho * y[t-1] + e[t]
        if df_tau(y) < crit_5:
            rejects += 1
    power.append(rejects / n_sim)
power = np.array(power)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.plot(rhos, power * 100, "o-", color="#161613", lw=2.2, ms=6)
ax.axhline(5, color="#b7b6a8", ls=":", lw=1.2)
ax.text(0.70, 7, "test size ≈ 5%", color="#3a3a33", fontsize=8.5)
ax.axvline(1.0, color="#e8531d", ls="--", lw=1.3)
ax.text(0.985, 80, "true unit root", color="#e8531d", fontsize=8.5, rotation=90, va="top")
i95 = int(np.where(rhos == 0.95)[0][0])
ax.annotate(f"ρ=0.95: rejects only {power[i95]*100:.0f}%",
            xy=(0.95, power[i95]*100), xytext=(0.86, 55),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#e8531d"), fontsize=8.5, color="#e8531d")
ax.set_xlabel("true AR(1) coefficient  ρ  (1 = unit root)")
ax.set_ylabel("% of samples rejecting the unit-root null")
ax.set_title("ADF power collapses near a unit root (T = 100)")
ax.invert_xaxis(); ax.set_ylim(0, 100)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Structural breaks: the Perron critique

The low-power problem has a famous twist. Perron (1989) argued that many macroeconomic series are really trend-stationary with a few structural breaks, not unit-root processes (Enders 2014). The mechanism: a single permanent shift in the mean makes a stationary series look non-stationary to an ADF test, biasing it toward not rejecting the unit root (Enders 2014). Perron’s remedy is to build the known break date into the test regression with dummy variables for the pulse (DP, a one-period spike) and level (DL, a permanent shift) changes (Enders 2014):

y_t = a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1} + a_2 t + \mu_1 DP + \mu_2 DL + \epsilon_t .

The lesson generalises: what looks like a unit root may be a stable process with a break — always ask whether a regime change, not a stochastic trend, is doing the work.

Seasonal unit roots and SARIMA

Seasonality comes in two flavours: deterministic (captured by seasonal dummies) or stochastic (requiring seasonal differencing) (Enders 2014). For quarterly data the seasonal difference is \Delta_4 y_t = y_t - y_{t-4} (Enders 2014). The HEGY test (Hylleberg et al. 1990) distinguishes non-seasonal, semi-annual, and seasonal unit roots (Enders 2014). A model with both regular and seasonal structure is a multiplicative SARIMA, written \text{ARIMA}(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)_s — the uppercase (P,D,Q) are the seasonal AR, differencing, and MA orders and s the seasonal period (Enders 2014).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reading the ADF statistic off the t-table. The distribution is non-standard and left-skewed; use Dickey–Fuller critical values, or you will massively over-reject. (Enders 2014)
  • Omitting a needed trend term. A trending series tested without a trend regressor yields a test with zero power asymptotically. Match the specification to a plot of the data. (Enders 2014)
  • Treating “fail to reject” as proof of a unit root. With low power, non-rejection is weak evidence; confirm with KPSS, and accept “not informative” when that’s the truth. (Enders 2014)
  • Over-differencing a trend-stationary series. It plants a non-invertible MA unit root; difference only when the data are difference-stationary. (Enders 2014)
  • Ignoring structural breaks. A break can manufacture a spurious unit root (Perron); test for the break rather than blindly differencing. (Enders 2014)

Appendix · the mathematics

Why \gamma = 0 is the unit-root null. Start from y_t = \rho y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t and subtract y_{t-1}: \Delta y_t = (\rho-1)y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t = \gamma y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t. A unit root \rho = 1 is exactly \gamma = 0; stationarity (|\rho|<1) is \gamma < 0. The one-sided alternative \gamma < 0 is why the DF rejection region is the left tail.

Why OLS is biased downward. Under \rho = 1 the regressor y_{t-1} is itself I(1), so the usual \sqrt T-asymptotics fail; \hat\rho converges at rate T (super-consistency) to a non-normal functional of Brownian motion, with a mean below 1 in finite samples — the Dickey–Fuller distribution. Its quantiles must be tabulated by simulation.

ADF lag choice. Choose p (number of augmenting lags) large enough that \epsilon_t is white noise — checked with the Ljung–Box test — but no larger, since extra lags cost power. Information criteria (AIC, or Enders’ preferred MAIC) or general-to-specific pruning both work.

The HEGY idea. Seasonal differencing \Delta_s = 1 - L^s factors into (1-L) and seasonal-frequency roots; HEGY tests each factor separately, so you difference only at the frequencies that actually carry a unit root — avoiding the over-differencing \Delta_s would force everywhere.

- **Trend-stationary (TS).** Stationary once a *deterministic* trend is removed ($y_t - a_0 - a_2 t$).
  Shocks are **transitory** — the series returns to its trend line.

The mis-specification costs run **both ways** [@enders_time_series]:

- **Differencing a TS series (over-differencing)** injects a **non-invertible (unit) root into the MA
  part** of the model — a needless pathology that wrecks estimation.
- **Detrending a DS series** fails to remove the stochastic trend, leaving the series **still
  non-stationary.**

You must diagnose *which* kind you have; you cannot safely apply both fixes.

## The low-power problem

Here is the practical warning. Unit-root tests have **low power**: when a series is genuinely stationary
but its largest root is *close* to 1, they routinely **fail to reject** the false unit-root null
[@enders_time_series]. Enders' example is sobering — with 100 observations and a true AR coefficient of
$0.95$, the ADF test correctly rejects a unit root only **12.7% of the time** [@enders_time_series]. A
"fail to reject" is therefore **weak** evidence: it may mean a unit root, or merely a persistent
stationary series the test can't resolve.

```{python}
#| label: fig-adf-power
#| fig-cap: "The low power of the ADF test, simulated by hand. For each true AR(1) coefficient ρ we generate 600 series of length 100, run the drift-form Dickey–Fuller regression Δy = a₀ + γ·y₋₁ + ε by hand, and reject the unit-root null when the τ-statistic falls below the 5% critical value (≈ −2.89). At ρ = 1 the rejection rate is the test's size (~5%); as ρ falls the test *should* reject, but power climbs only slowly — near ρ = 0.95 it is dismal (Enders reports ~12.7%). 'Fail to reject' is weak evidence: a near-unit-root stationary series looks just like a unit root."
#| code-fold: true
#| code-summary: "Show the figure code"

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
T, n_sim = 100, 600
crit_5 = -2.89                      # DF τ_μ 5% critical value, n≈100
rhos = np.array([1.00, 0.99, 0.97, 0.95, 0.90, 0.85, 0.80, 0.70])

def df_tau(y):
    """Drift-form Dickey–Fuller t-stat on gamma, OLS by hand."""
    dy = np.diff(y)                 # Delta y_t
    ylag = y[:-1]                   # y_{t-1}
    X = np.column_stack([np.ones_like(ylag), ylag])   # [const, level]
    beta, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(X, dy, rcond=None)
    resid = dy - X @ beta
    s2 = resid @ resid / (len(dy) - 2)
    covb = s2 * np.linalg.inv(X.T @ X)
    return beta[1] / np.sqrt(covb[1, 1])            # gamma / se(gamma)

power = []
for rho in rhos:
    rejects = 0
    for _ in range(n_sim):
        y = np.empty(T); y[0] = 0.0
        e = rng.normal(0, 1, T)
        for t in range(1, T):
            y[t] = rho * y[t-1] + e[t]
        if df_tau(y) < crit_5:
            rejects += 1
    power.append(rejects / n_sim)
power = np.array(power)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.plot(rhos, power * 100, "o-", color="#161613", lw=2.2, ms=6)
ax.axhline(5, color="#b7b6a8", ls=":", lw=1.2)
ax.text(0.70, 7, "test size ≈ 5%", color="#3a3a33", fontsize=8.5)
ax.axvline(1.0, color="#e8531d", ls="--", lw=1.3)
ax.text(0.985, 80, "true unit root", color="#e8531d", fontsize=8.5, rotation=90, va="top")
i95 = int(np.where(rhos == 0.95)[0][0])
ax.annotate(f"ρ=0.95: rejects only {power[i95]*100:.0f}%",
            xy=(0.95, power[i95]*100), xytext=(0.86, 55),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#e8531d"), fontsize=8.5, color="#e8531d")
ax.set_xlabel("true AR(1) coefficient  ρ  (1 = unit root)")
ax.set_ylabel("% of samples rejecting the unit-root null")
ax.set_title("ADF power collapses near a unit root (T = 100)")
ax.invert_xaxis(); ax.set_ylim(0, 100)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Structural breaks: the Perron critique

The low-power problem has a famous twist. **Perron (1989)** argued that many macroeconomic series are
really **trend-stationary with a few structural breaks**, *not* unit-root processes [@enders_time_series].
The mechanism: a single permanent shift in the mean makes a stationary series *look* non-stationary to an
ADF test, **biasing it toward not rejecting** the unit root [@enders_time_series]. Perron's remedy is to
build the **known break date** into the test regression with **dummy variables** for the pulse ($DP$, a
one-period spike) and level ($DL$, a permanent shift) changes [@enders_time_series]:

$$
y_t = a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1} + a_2 t + \mu_1 DP + \mu_2 DL + \epsilon_t .
$$

The lesson generalises: **what looks like a unit root may be a stable process with a break** — always ask
whether a regime change, not a stochastic trend, is doing the work.

## Seasonal unit roots and SARIMA

Seasonality comes in two flavours: **deterministic** (captured by seasonal dummies) or **stochastic**
(requiring **seasonal differencing**) [@enders_time_series]. For quarterly data the seasonal difference is
$\Delta_4 y_t = y_t - y_{t-4}$ [@enders_time_series]. The **HEGY** test (Hylleberg et al. 1990) distinguishes
non-seasonal, semi-annual, and seasonal unit roots [@enders_time_series]. A model with both regular and
seasonal structure is a **multiplicative SARIMA**, written $\text{ARIMA}(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)_s$ — the uppercase
$(P,D,Q)$ are the seasonal AR, differencing, and MA orders and $s$ the seasonal period
[@enders_time_series].

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}
- **Reading the ADF statistic off the $t$-table.** The distribution is non-standard and left-skewed; use
  Dickey–Fuller critical values, or you will massively over-reject. [@enders_time_series]
- **Omitting a needed trend term.** A trending series tested without a trend regressor yields a test with
  *zero* power asymptotically. Match the specification to a plot of the data. [@enders_time_series]
- **Treating "fail to reject" as proof of a unit root.** With low power, non-rejection is weak evidence;
  confirm with KPSS, and accept "not informative" when that's the truth. [@enders_time_series]
- **Over-differencing a trend-stationary series.** It plants a non-invertible MA unit root; difference
  only when the data are difference-stationary. [@enders_time_series]
- **Ignoring structural breaks.** A break can manufacture a spurious unit root (Perron); test for the
  break rather than blindly differencing. [@enders_time_series]
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why $\gamma = 0$ is the unit-root null.** Start from $y_t = \rho y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t$ and subtract
$y_{t-1}$: $\Delta y_t = (\rho-1)y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t = \gamma y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t$. A unit root
$\rho = 1$ is exactly $\gamma = 0$; stationarity ($|\rho|<1$) is $\gamma < 0$. The one-sided alternative
$\gamma < 0$ is why the DF rejection region is the *left* tail.

**Why OLS is biased downward.** Under $\rho = 1$ the regressor $y_{t-1}$ is itself I(1), so the usual
$\sqrt T$-asymptotics fail; $\hat\rho$ converges at rate $T$ (super-consistency) to a *non-normal*
functional of Brownian motion, with a mean below 1 in finite samples — the **Dickey–Fuller distribution**.
Its quantiles must be tabulated by simulation.

**ADF lag choice.** Choose $p$ (number of augmenting lags) large enough that $\epsilon_t$ is white noise —
checked with the [Ljung–Box test](arima-forecasting.qmd) — but no larger, since extra lags cost power.
Information criteria (AIC, or Enders' preferred **MAIC**) or general-to-specific pruning both work.

**The HEGY idea.** Seasonal differencing $\Delta_s = 1 - L^s$ factors into $(1-L)$ and seasonal-frequency
roots; HEGY tests each factor separately, so you difference *only* at the frequencies that actually carry
a unit root — avoiding the over-differencing $\Delta_s$ would force everywhere.

:::
