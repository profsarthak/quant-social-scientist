Out-of-Sample Honesty: The Test Set & Cross-Validation

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

You can’t pick the sweet spot you can’t see — so estimate test error honestly, by hiding data from yourself.

The bias–variance sweet spot is invisible: it lives in test error, and you don’t have infinite test data. Cross-validation is the trick that manufactures honest test error from a limited sample — by repeatedly hiding part of the data, fitting on the rest, and scoring on what was hidden. It is how every flexibility dial in this chapter actually gets set.

Why it matters

The previous two entries established the goal — minimise error on unseen data — and warned that training error is a liar, falling toward zero as you overfit. (James et al. 2021) But if you can’t trust training error and don’t have a giant separate test set, how do you choose a model’s flexibility, or a regularization penalty, or which variables to keep? Cross-validation is the answer, and it is the most important procedure in predictive modelling: the disciplined way to estimate test error and tune a model without fooling yourself. Get its one golden rule wrong and every result downstream is optimistic fiction.

The sacred test set

The cleanest discipline splits data three ways: (James et al. 2021)

  • Training set — fit the model’s parameters.
  • Validation set — tune its complexity (flexibility, penalty, which variables).
  • Test set — sacred: held out, untouched, never seen by the algorithm until a single, final, once-and-for-all evaluation.

The test set exists to give an honest estimate of real-world performance, and it only stays honest if you never peek. (James et al. 2021) The cardinal sin is data leakage — letting information from the validation or test data seep into training. A classic leak: standardising the whole dataset before splitting, so the training fold has secretly “seen” the held-out data’s mean and scale. The result is an artificially optimistic score that collapses in production. (James et al. 2021)

The validation-set approach (and why it’s not enough)

The simplest estimate of test error: randomly split the data once into a training half and a validation (hold-out) half, fit on the first, score on the second. (James et al. 2021)

NoteISL’s Auto example

Predicting a car’s gas mileage from horsepower, ISL splits 392 observations into 196 train / 196 validation. A linear fit gives a validation MSE of 23.30; adding a quadratic term drops it to 18.90 — clear evidence the relationship curves. (James et al. 2021)

But the single-split approach has two flaws: (James et al. 2021)

  1. High variability. The estimate swings depending on which observations land in which half — ISL shows ten different random splits producing ten different error curves.
  2. Wasted data. Training on only half the sample, models perform worse than they would on the full data, so the validation approach overestimates the true test error.

Cross-validation fixes both by reusing every observation for both roles.

k-fold cross-validation

The modern standard. Split the data into k roughly-equal folds; hold out one fold as validation, train on the other k-1, score on the held-out fold; rotate so each fold is the validation set exactly once; average the k scores. (James et al. 2021)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

k = 5
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.2))
for row in range(k):                       # one round per row
    for col in range(k):                   # the k folds
        val = (col == row)
        ax.add_patch(plt.Rectangle((col, k - 1 - row), 0.96, 0.96,
                     facecolor="#d98a29" if val else "#1f3a5f",
                     edgecolor="white", lw=2))
    ax.text(-0.25, k - 1 - row + 0.48, f"round {row+1}", ha="right", va="center", fontsize=9)
ax.add_patch(plt.Rectangle((k + 0.4, k - 1.5), 0.5, 0.5, facecolor="#1f3a5f"))
ax.text(k + 1.05, k - 1.25, "train", va="center", fontsize=9)
ax.add_patch(plt.Rectangle((k + 0.4, k - 2.3), 0.5, 0.5, facecolor="#d98a29"))
ax.text(k + 1.05, k - 2.05, "validate", va="center", fontsize=9)
ax.set_xlim(-1.5, k + 2.5); ax.set_ylim(-0.3, k + 0.3)
ax.set_aspect("equal"); ax.axis("off")
ax.set_title("5-fold cross-validation: every fold is held out once")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Why k = 5 or 10? They are the standard compromise. (James et al. 2021) The extreme case k = n is leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV) — fit on n-1 points, test on the one left out, repeat n times. LOOCV has very low bias (each fit uses almost all the data) and is perfectly stable (no random splits), but two costs push practitioners toward 5 or 10 folds: (James et al. 2021)

  • Computation — LOOCV fits the model n times (though OLS has a magic closed-form shortcut).
  • Higher variance of the estimate — a subtle point. LOOCV’s n models are trained on nearly identical data, so their errors are highly correlated, and averaging strongly-correlated quantities yields a higher-variance average than averaging the 5 or 10 less-correlated estimates of k-fold. So 10-fold often estimates test error better than LOOCV. (James et al. 2021)

Using CV to tune the model

This is what cross-validation is for: pick the flexibility that minimises estimated test error. (James et al. 2021) Compute the CV error across a grid of the tuning knob — polynomial degree, the regularization penalty \lambda, tree depth — and find the bottom of the U-shaped curve.

TipThe one-standard-error rule

When the CV curve is flat near its minimum, the lowest point is barely better than its neighbours and is itself noisy. The one-standard-error rule says: compute the standard error of the CV estimates, and pick the simplest (most parsimonious) model whose CV error is within one standard error of the minimum. (James et al. 2021) You give up a sliver of estimated accuracy for a simpler, more robust model — a principled lean toward parsimony.

The golden rule: keep choices inside the loop

The single most common — and most damaging — mistake. Any data-dependent choice must be made inside the cross-validation loop. (James et al. 2021) If you use CV to select variables, you must redo the variable selection separately within each training fold, so the validation fold stays genuinely unseen.

The tempting shortcut — screen all the variables (or standardise, or impute) on the whole dataset first, then cross-validate the chosen model — lets the model peek at the answers. The validation folds influenced the choices being “validated,” so the CV score is over-optimistic and won’t generalise. (James et al. 2021) If a decision touches the data, it goes inside the loop. No exceptions.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Pre-processing before splitting. Standardising, imputing, or selecting variables on the full dataset leaks the held-out data into training. Fit every data-dependent step on the training fold only. (James et al. 2021)

  • Touching the test set more than once. The moment you use the test set to make a choice, it becomes a validation set and stops being an honest estimate. Hold it sacred for the final number. (James et al. 2021)

  • Trusting a single train/validation split. Its estimate is high-variance and pessimistic; use k-fold to average over splits and use all the data. (James et al. 2021)

  • Assuming LOOCV is best because it has lowest bias. Its highly-correlated fits give a higher-variance estimate than 10-fold; the standard k=5 or 10 usually wins. (James et al. 2021)

  • Reporting the CV error of the selected model as its test error. Having chosen the model to minimise CV error, that minimum is itself optimistic — the honest number comes from the untouched test set.

Appendix · the mathematics

The k-fold estimate. Partition the indices into folds C_1,\dots,C_k of size \approx n/k. Let \hat f^{-\kappa} be the model fit with fold \kappa removed. Then

\text{CV}_{(k)} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{\kappa=1}^{k} \text{MSE}_\kappa, \qquad \text{MSE}_\kappa = \frac{1}{|C_\kappa|}\sum_{i\in C_\kappa}\big(y_i - \hat f^{-\kappa}(x_i)\big)^2 .

LOOCV is the case k=n, |C_\kappa|=1.

LOOCV’s magic shortcut (OLS). For linear models fit by least squares, LOOCV needs only one fit:

\text{CV}_{(n)} = \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{y_i-\hat y_i}{1-h_i}\right)^2,

where \hat y_i is the full-sample fitted value and h_i is observation i’s leverage. The leverage inflates each residual by exactly the amount that point pulled the fit toward itself — recovering the leave-one-out error without refitting.

Why LOOCV has higher variance. The estimate is a mean of n fold-errors. For correlated terms, \operatorname{Var}(\bar T) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\big(1+(n-1)\bar\rho\big) — the same clustering inflation (Moulton) logic. LOOCV’s fits are almost identical, so \bar\rho is large and the average is high-variance; k-fold’s fits overlap less, lowering \bar\rho and the variance of the estimate.

  / [resampling](../02-inference/resampling-and-nonparametric-tests.qmd) (CV is a resampling method).
- **Leads to:** [regularization](regularization.qmd) (CV chooses the penalty $\lambda$) and
  [trees & forests](trees-and-forests.qmd) (CV tunes depth and the number of trees).
- **Reappears in:** [causal machine learning](../05-causal-inference/index.qmd), where **cross-fitting**
  (sample-splitting) is what keeps ML-estimated nuisances from biasing the treatment effect.
