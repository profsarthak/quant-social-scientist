Regularization: Ridge & Lasso

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Deliberately bias your estimates to slash their variance — and let the data switch off the variables that don’t matter.

OLS is unbiased — and when predictors are many or collinear, that unbiasedness comes at a ruinous price in variance. Regularization makes a trade the bias–variance logic predicts will pay: shrink the coefficients toward zero, accept a little bias, and buy a large drop in variance. The Lasso goes further, setting some coefficients exactly to zero — doing variable selection automatically.

Why it matters

The estimation entry’s shrinkage estimator — biased but lower-MSE — was a curiosity there. Here it becomes a workhorse. When the number of predictors p is large relative to n, OLS overfits badly; when p > n, OLS has no unique solution at all. (James et al. 2021) Regularization rescues both, and the Lasso throws in a bonus social scientists prize: interpretability, by zeroing out the variables that don’t earn their place. (James et al. 2021) This is also the on-ramp to high-dimensional and causal ML, where regularised estimates of many nuisance parameters are routine.

The idea: penalise big coefficients

Both methods keep OLS’s goal — minimise the residual sum of squares — but add a penalty on the size of the coefficients, so the fit must now balance accuracy against coefficient magnitude. (James et al. 2021) A tuning parameter \lambda \ge 0 sets the exchange rate:

  • \lambda = 0: no penalty — you get plain OLS.
  • \lambda \to \infty: the penalty dominates — all coefficients are crushed toward zero (the null model).

In between, \lambda is a bias–variance dial: as it rises, variance falls (the model is less sensitive to the training sample) and bias rises (it assumes coefficients are small). The test error is U-shaped in \lambda, and you pick the bottom by cross-validation. (James et al. 2021)

Ridge regression (\ell_2)

Ridge adds the sum of squared coefficients: (James et al. 2021)

\min_{\beta}\ \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n\Big(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_j \beta_j x_{ij}\Big)^2}_{\text{RSS}} \;+\; \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j^2 .

The \ell_2 penalty shrinks every coefficient toward zero but never to zero (except in the limit \lambda=\infty). (James et al. 2021) So Ridge keeps all predictors — it tames their magnitudes rather than dropping any. Two practical notes: Ridge is not scale-invariant (the penalty treats all \beta_j alike, so a variable measured in dollars is penalised differently from one in years), so you must standardise predictors to mean 0, SD 1 before fitting; and Ridge is especially good under multicollinearity, spreading weight smoothly across correlated predictors instead of letting OLS swing them wildly. (James et al. 2021)

The Lasso (\ell_1)

The Lasso changes one thing — squared coefficients become absolute values: (James et al. 2021)

\min_{\beta}\ \text{RSS} \;+\; \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p |\beta_j| .

That small change has a dramatic effect: for large enough \lambda, the \ell_1 penalty forces some coefficients to be exactly zero. (James et al. 2021) The Lasso therefore performs automatic variable selection, yielding a sparse model — only the predictors that earn their keep survive — which is far easier for a social scientist to interpret than Ridge’s dense vector of small-but-nonzero coefficients. (James et al. 2021)

Why Lasso zeroes coefficients but Ridge doesn’t

The reason is geometry, and it is beautiful. Each method can be written as “minimise RSS subject to a budget on coefficient size.” The budget’s shape is everything: (James et al. 2021)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

bhat = np.array([1.9, 1.1])                         # OLS estimate
A = np.array([[1.0, 0.5], [0.5, 1.3]])              # RSS contour shape
g1, g2 = np.meshgrid(np.linspace(-0.5, 3, 400), np.linspace(-0.5, 2.6, 400))
Z = (A[0,0]*(g1-bhat[0])**2 + 2*A[0,1]*(g1-bhat[0])*(g2-bhat[1]) + A[1,1]*(g2-bhat[1])**2)

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 4.4))
for ax in (axL, axR):
    ax.contour(g1, g2, Z, levels=8, colors="#c0392b", linewidths=0.8, alpha=0.8)
    ax.scatter(*bhat, color="#c0392b", zorder=5); ax.text(bhat[0]+0.06, bhat[1]+0.05, "β̂", color="#c0392b")
    ax.axhline(0, color="#999", lw=0.7); ax.axvline(0, color="#999", lw=0.7)
    ax.set_xlim(-0.5, 3); ax.set_ylim(-0.5, 2.6); ax.set_aspect("equal")
    ax.set_xlabel("β₁"); ax.set_ylabel("β₂")

s = 1.15
axL.add_patch(plt.Polygon([[s,0],[0,s],[-s,0],[0,-s]], closed=True,
              facecolor="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.3, edgecolor="#1f3a5f", lw=2))
axL.scatter(0, s, color="#1f8a4c", zorder=6, s=60)
axL.text(0.06, s+0.06, "solution: β₁ = 0", color="#1f8a4c", fontsize=8)
axL.set_title("Lasso (ℓ₁): diamond → hits a corner")

axR.add_patch(plt.Circle((0,0), 1.15, facecolor="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.3, edgecolor="#1f3a5f", lw=2))
ang = 0.62
axR.scatter(1.15*np.cos(ang), 1.15*np.sin(ang), color="#1f8a4c", zorder=6, s=60)
axR.text(0.55, 0.78, "both β ≠ 0", color="#1f8a4c", fontsize=8)
axR.set_title("Ridge (ℓ₂): circle → no corner")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The diamond has corners sitting exactly on the axes (where a coefficient is zero), and the expanding RSS ellipse tends to touch the budget at one of those corners — zeroing that coefficient. The circle is smooth and corner-free, so contact almost always lands where both coefficients are non-zero. (James et al. 2021) Sparsity is a consequence of the \ell_1 ball’s pointy shape.

Choosing \lambda, and which method wins

Pick \lambda by k-fold cross-validation: score a grid of \lambda values, take the one minimising CV error (or the one-standard-error rule’s most parsimonious model within one SE). (James et al. 2021)

NoteISL’s Credit data and the verdict

On the Credit data (predicting card balance), the Ridge paths show Rating, Limit, Income, Student all shrinking smoothly toward zero but staying non-zero; the Lasso paths show variables switching on one at a time as \lambda falls — Rating first, then Student, Limit, finally Income — a readable ranking of importance, with the rest pinned at zero. (James et al. 2021)

Neither method universally wins. In a sparse world (a few predictors really matter) the Lasso excels; in a dense world (many small effects) Ridge does. So: Lasso for interpretability and selection, Ridge for robustness under multicollinearity. (James et al. 2021) (The elastic net blends both.)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Forgetting to standardise. Ridge and Lasso are scale-dependent; an unstandardised variable is penalised by its units, not its importance. Standardise predictors first. (James et al. 2021)

  • Reading shrunken coefficients as unbiased effects. Regularization deliberately biases coefficients toward zero — they are tuned for prediction, not for an honest causal magnitude. Don’t interpret a Lasso coefficient like an OLS one. (James et al. 2021)

  • Treating Lasso’s selection as the “true model.” Which variables survive depends on \lambda, the sample, and correlations among predictors; a dropped variable isn’t proven irrelevant, and with correlated predictors the Lasso picks one somewhat arbitrarily. (James et al. 2021)

  • Choosing \lambda by training error. Training error falls as \lambda\to 0 (toward OLS); only cross-validated error reveals the right amount of shrinkage. (James et al. 2021)

  • Using Ridge when you wanted selection (or Lasso under heavy collinearity). Match the tool to the world — sparse vs dense, correlated vs not. (James et al. 2021)

Appendix · the mathematics

Ridge has a closed form. In matrix form, minimising \lVert y-X\beta\rVert^2+\lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2 gives

\hat\beta^{\text{ridge}} = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y .

The added \lambda I makes X^\top X+\lambda I invertible even when X^\top X is singular (e.g. p>n) — which is precisely why Ridge works where OLS fails. As \lambda\to\infty, \hat\beta^{\text{ridge}}\to 0.

The constrained form. Each penalty is equivalent (by Lagrangian duality) to minimising RSS subject to a budget: Ridge to \sum\beta_j^2\le s, Lasso to \sum|\beta_j|\le s, with a one-to-one \lambda\leftrightarrow s. The geometry of those budget sets — the smooth \ell_2 ball vs the cornered \ell_1 ball — is what the figure draws, and is the exact reason for (no) sparsity.

Soft-thresholding (orthonormal case). When the predictors are orthonormal, the Lasso solution is explicit: \hat\beta_j^{\text{lasso}} = \operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta_j)\,(|\hat\beta_j|-\lambda/2)_+ — each OLS coefficient is pulled toward zero by \lambda/2 and clipped to exactly zero once it’s smaller than that. Ridge instead scales each by 1/(1+\lambda) — shrinking proportionally, never to zero. That contrast — subtract-and-clip vs scale — is the algebra behind the geometry.

Connections

  • Builds on: the bias–variance trade-off (regularization buys variance reduction with bias), the shrinkage estimator (lower MSE via bias), cross-validation (how \lambda is chosen), and OLS / Gauss–Markov (the unbiased baseline being traded away).
  • Leads to: trees & forests (another route to low variance, via averaging) and high-dimensional causal ML (Lasso for selecting controls; double/debiased ML).
  • Contrasts with: logistic regression, which the Lasso also regularises for high-dimensional classification.

  via bias), [cross-validation](cross-validation.qmd) (how $\lambda$ is chosen), and
  [OLS / Gauss–Markov](../03-regression/gauss-markov-assumptions.qmd) (the unbiased baseline being
  traded away).
- **Leads to:** [trees & forests](trees-and-forests.qmd) (another route to low variance, via
  averaging) and [high-dimensional causal ML](../05-causal-inference/index.qmd) (Lasso for selecting
  controls; double/debiased ML).
- **Contrasts with:** [logistic regression](../03-regression/limited-dependent-variables.qmd), which
  the Lasso also regularises for high-dimensional classification.
