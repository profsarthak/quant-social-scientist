The Bias–Variance Trade-off

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Why the best model is never the one that fits your data best — the single idea that governs all of prediction.

Every predictive method faces the same tug-of-war. Too rigid and it misses the signal (bias); too flexible and it chases the noise (variance). The expected error on new data is literally the sum of those two plus an irreducible floor — and that sum is minimised not at maximum flexibility but somewhere in the middle. Finding that middle is what predictive modelling is.

Why it matters

The last entry showed the U-shaped test-error curve. This one explains why it’s U-shaped, and the answer — the bias–variance decomposition — is the most important single idea in statistical learning. (James et al. 2021) It is the seed planted back in estimation (MSE = variance + bias²) now grown into the organising principle of an entire field: every method choice, every tuning parameter, every regularization penalty is, underneath, a position on this trade-off. Understand it and the rest of Part IV is commentary.

Flexibility, overfitting, underfitting

Flexibility is the range of shapes a method can take to estimate the unknown f. (James et al. 2021) Too little or too much, and you fail — in opposite ways:

  • Underfitting (high bias). The model is too rigid to capture the signal — fitting a straight line to a curved truth. It misses the same way in every sample. (James et al. 2021)
  • Overfitting (high variance). The model is so flexible it follows the training data too closely, mistaking that sample’s noise for signal. It nails the data it trained on and fails miserably on new data. (James et al. 2021)

The art is the flexibility between these — enough to catch the signal, not so much as to memorise the noise.

The decomposition

ISL makes it exact. For a test point x_0, the expected squared prediction error splits into three non-negative pieces: (James et al. 2021)

\mathbb{E}\big[(y_0 - \hat f(x_0))^2\big] = \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}\big(\hat f(x_0)\big)}_{\text{variance}} + \underbrace{\big[\operatorname{Bias}(\hat f(x_0))\big]^2}_{\text{bias}^2} + \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}(\epsilon)}_{\text{irreducible}}.

Each term has a clean meaning: (James et al. 2021)

  • Bias — the error from approximating a complex reality with a simpler model. Assume linearity when the world is curved and you build in a systematic miss. High for rigid models.
  • Variance — how much \hat f would change if you re-estimated it on a different training set. A stable method barely moves; a flexible one pivots wildly to chase each sample’s points. High for flexible models.
  • Irreducible error — the noise \operatorname{Var}(\epsilon) that X simply cannot predict — the floor no model beats. (James et al. 2021)

Because all three are non-negative, the irreducible term is an absolute lower bound: the best possible test MSE is \operatorname{Var}(\epsilon), achieved only with zero bias and zero variance — generally impossible.

The trade-off and the U-shape

Here is the tension. As flexibility rises, bias falls but variance rises — a tug-of-war. (James et al. 2021) And it plays out in two acts:

  1. Early on, added flexibility cuts bias faster than it adds variance, so total test MSE falls.
  2. Eventually, bias is nearly gone and there’s little left to gain, while variance keeps climbing as the model fits noise — so total MSE rises.

The result is the U-shaped test-error curve, and the optimal model sits at the bottom of the U, where bias² + variance is smallest. (James et al. 2021) This is also exactly why training error misleads: training error tracks bias down but is blind to variance, so it falls forever while test error turns back up.

Watch the trade-off decompose

The figure below runs the decomposition by brute force: fit polynomials of each flexibility to hundreds of fresh training sets, then measure, at held-out points, how much the predictions miss on average (bias²) and how much they scatter across training sets (variance). Bias falls, variance rises, and their sum — plus the irreducible floor — traces the familiar U.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
def f_true(x):  return np.sin(1.5 * x)
sigma = 0.4
x_grid = np.linspace(-2, 2, 40)                 # test points
truth = f_true(x_grid)

degrees = range(1, 11)
n_train, reps = 25, 300
bias2, variance = [], []
for d in degrees:
    preds = np.empty((reps, x_grid.size))
    for r in range(reps):
        xt = rng.uniform(-2, 2, n_train)
        yt = f_true(xt) + rng.normal(0, sigma, n_train)
        preds[r] = np.polyval(np.polyfit(xt, yt, d), x_grid)
    mean_pred = preds.mean(axis=0)
    bias2.append(np.mean((mean_pred - truth) ** 2))
    variance.append(np.mean(preds.var(axis=0)))

bias2, variance = np.array(bias2), np.array(variance)
total = bias2 + variance + sigma ** 2
best = list(degrees)[int(np.argmin(total))]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.0))
ax.plot(list(degrees), bias2, "o-", color="#d98a29", lw=2, label="bias²")
ax.plot(list(degrees), variance, "o-", color="#1f3a5f", lw=2, label="variance")
ax.plot(list(degrees), total, "o-", color="#111", lw=2.4, label="total test MSE")
ax.axhline(sigma ** 2, color="#9aa0a6", ls="--", lw=1.4, label="irreducible error")
ax.axvline(best, color="#1f8a4c", ls=":", lw=1.6)
ax.text(best + 0.15, total.max() * 0.8, f"sweet spot\n(degree {best})", color="#1f8a4c", fontsize=8)
ax.set_xlabel("model flexibility (polynomial degree)"); ax.set_ylabel("error")
ax.set_title("Bias falls, variance rises, total error is U-shaped")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

It depends on the truth

A subtlety worth carrying: where the sweet spot sits depends on how complex reality actually is. ISL’s three simulated cases make the point: (James et al. 2021)

  • When the truth is nearly linear, a rigid linear model is already near-best — extra flexibility only adds variance, so test error rises almost immediately. (ISL Fig. 2.10.)
  • When the truth is mildly non-linear, an intermediate flexibility wins; both the rigid line (too biased) and the wiggly spline (too variable) lose. (ISL Fig. 2.9.)
  • When the truth is highly non-linear, flexibility pays off for a long way — bias reduction is huge — and the optimum is far more flexible before variance finally bites. (ISL Fig. 2.11.)

There is no universally best flexibility; it must be learned from the data, which is precisely the job of cross-validation. (James et al. 2021)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Chasing the lowest training error. Training error tracks bias only; driving it to zero maximises variance and overfits. The target is the bottom of the test-error U. (James et al. 2021)

  • Believing more flexible is always better. It lowers bias but raises variance; past the sweet spot it makes predictions worse. Flexibility is a dial to tune, not a quantity to maximise. (James et al. 2021)

  • Ignoring that the optimum depends on the unknown truth. You can’t pick flexibility from theory alone; estimate test error (CV) and let the data choose. (James et al. 2021)

  • Forgetting the irreducible floor. No model beats \operatorname{Var}(\epsilon). A test error near the floor means you’re done, not that you need a fancier model. (James et al. 2021)

  • Reading low variance as “good.” A rigid model has low variance and high bias — confidently wrong. Both terms matter; only their sum does. (James et al. 2021)

Appendix · the mathematics

Deriving the decomposition. Let y_0=f(x_0)+\epsilon with \mathbb{E}[\epsilon]=0, \operatorname{Var}(\epsilon)=\sigma^2, \epsilon\perp\hat f. Add and subtract \mathbb{E}[\hat f(x_0)]:

\mathbb{E}[(y_0-\hat f(x_0))^2] = \mathbb{E}\big[(f(x_0)+\epsilon-\hat f(x_0))^2\big].

Expand; the \epsilon cross-terms vanish (mean zero, independent), leaving \sigma^2 + \mathbb{E}[(f(x_0)-\hat f(x_0))^2]. Now split the second term with \bar f \equiv \mathbb{E}[\hat f(x_0)]:

\mathbb{E}[(f-\hat f)^2] = \underbrace{(f-\bar f)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[(\hat f-\bar f)^2]}_{\text{Var}(\hat f)},

its cross-term zero because \mathbb{E}[\hat f-\bar f]=0. Hence the three-way split. This is the same bias–variance identity as for a single estimator, now applied to the prediction \hat f(x_0) and averaged over training sets.

Why flexibility trades them. Intuitively, a model class with more free parameters can sit closer to any target (bias \downarrow) but is pinned down less firmly by n fixed observations, so it swings more across resamples (variance \uparrow). Roughly, for many methods variance grows with the effective number of parameters p like \sigma^2 p/n, while bias shrinks as the class enriches — their sum is minimised at finite p.

Connections

  • Builds on: estimation (the MSE = variance + bias² identity, the seed) and explaining vs predicting (the U-shaped test error this explains).
  • Leads to: classification (the same trade-off for categorical outcomes, where the irreducible floor becomes the Bayes error rate), cross-validation (how to find the sweet spot from data), regularization (deliberately adding bias to slash variance), and trees & forests (a high-variance method tamed by ensembling).
  • Reappears in: causal machine learning, where regularised, cross-fitted nuisance estimates trade bias for variance in service of an unbiased effect.
