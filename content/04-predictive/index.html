IV · The Computational Turn: Predictive Modelling

From explaining why to predicting what next — and the bridge to computational thinking.

This is the hinge of the manual: where statistical thinking (estimate an effect, defend it, quantify uncertainty) meets computational thinking (build something that predicts well on data it has never seen). It is the on-ramp to the causal summit — you meet cross-validation, regularization, and the bias–variance trade-off here, because modern causal inference uses these tools — and it gathers the field’s running debates in one place.

There are two distinct goals people pursue with the same regression output, and conflating them is the source of endless confusion in both academia and industry:

  • Explanatory (statistical) modelling asks why. It wants unbiased estimates of effects, valid standard errors, and a defensible causal story. A wrong-but-significant coefficient is a failure even if the model predicts well.
  • Predictive modelling asks what next. It wants accurate forecasts on unseen data and will happily trade interpretability and unbiasedness for it. A black box that predicts well is a success even if no coefficient is interpretable.
