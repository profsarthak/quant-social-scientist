Volatility: ARCH & GARCH Models

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Every model so far forecast the mean. Finance lives and dies by the variance — and variance, it turns out, is forecastable too.

ARIMA and VAR model the conditional mean. But for asset returns the mean is nearly unforecastable while the variance is strikingly predictable: calm and turbulent periods cluster. ARCH/GARCH models seize that regularity by letting today’s variance depend on yesterday’s shocks — turning “risk” from a constant nuisance into a modelled, forecastable quantity. This is the foundation of modern financial econometrics and risk management.

Why it matters

Classical regression assumes homoskedasticity — constant error variance (Enders 2014). For returns that assumption is simply false, and not randomly so: volatility arrives in clusters. Engle’s insight (1982, a Nobel-winning one) was that the variance could be given its own equation and forecast like a mean. The payoff is everywhere risk is priced: option valuation, Value-at-Risk, portfolio choice, and any social-science series — exchange rates, inflation, polls — where uncertainty itself ebbs and flows.

Volatility clustering and conditional variance

The motivating stylised fact: “large changes tend to be followed by large changes, of either sign, and small changes by small changes” — volatility clustering (Enders 2014). The key is a distinction:

  • Unconditional (long-run) variance — constant. Over the whole sample, the series has one variance.
  • Conditional variance h_t — the variance at time t given past information — time-varying and forecastable (Enders 2014).

A series with constant unconditional but time-varying conditional variance is conditionally heteroskedastic. Homoskedasticity fails for returns because rational agents use all available information — including recent volatility — to assess current risk (Enders 2014).

The ARCH(q) model

Engle (1982) showed you can model the mean and the variance simultaneously (Enders 2014). Write the error as

\epsilon_t = v_t\sqrt{h_t}, \qquad v_t \sim \text{white noise},\ \operatorname{Var}(v_t)=1,

so h_t is the conditional variance. The ARCH(q) variance equation makes it a function of past squared shocks (Enders 2014):

h_t = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 \epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \alpha_2 \epsilon_{t-2}^2 + \cdots + \alpha_q \epsilon_{t-q}^2 .

The mean equation (say an ARMA, y_t = a_0 + a_1 y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t) and the variance equation work in tandem: a large shock in the mean equation raises the conditional variance for subsequent periods — exactly the clustering mechanism (Enders 2014).

Testing for ARCH effects. The standard diagnostic is the McLeod–Li (1983) LM test: regress the squared residuals on a constant and q of their own lags; a significant TR^2 statistic (\sim \chi^2_q) signals ARCH errors (Enders 2014).

The GARCH(p,q) model

Bollerslev (1986) generalised ARCH by letting the conditional variance depend on its own past values — an ARMA for the variance (Enders 2014):

h_t = \alpha_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{q}\alpha_i \epsilon_{t-i}^2 + \sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j h_{t-j}.

GARCH(1,1) dominates practice — h_t = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1\epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 h_{t-1} — because it is extremely parsimonious, mimicking a very high-order ARCH with just three parameters (Enders 2014).

  • Persistence condition. For a finite, stable unconditional variance you need \sum\alpha_i + \sum\beta_j < 1 (Enders 2014).
  • IGARCH and long memory. If \alpha_1 + \beta_1 = 1, the model is Integrated GARCH (IGARCH): the unconditional variance is infinite, volatility shocks are permanent, and today’s variance is the best forecast of all future variances (Enders 2014). Empirically \alpha_1+\beta_1 is often just below 1 — volatility is highly persistent.
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(20)
a0, a1, b1 = 0.05, 0.10, 0.85          # persistence a1+b1 = 0.95
T = 600
h = np.empty(T); eps = np.empty(T)
h[0] = a0 / (1 - a1 - b1)              # start at the unconditional variance
for t in range(T):
    eps[t] = rng.normal(0, 1) * np.sqrt(h[t])
    if t + 1 < T:
        h[t+1] = a0 + a1 * eps[t]**2 + b1 * h[t]

fig, (axT, axB) = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(7.6, 5.0), sharex=True,
                               gridspec_kw={"height_ratios": [2, 1]})
axT.plot(eps, color="#1f4e79", lw=0.8)
axT.axhline(0, color="#999", lw=0.6)
axT.set_title("GARCH(1,1) returns: volatility clusters (α₁+β₁ = 0.95)")
axT.set_ylabel("return  εₜ")

axB.plot(np.sqrt(h), color="#c0392b", lw=1.2)
uncond_sd = np.sqrt(a0 / (1 - a1 - b1))
axB.axhline(uncond_sd, color="#444", ls="--", lw=1.1)
axB.text(8, uncond_sd*1.05, "unconditional SD", fontsize=8, color="#444")
axB.set_title("Conditional volatility  √hₜ  (the forecastable part)")
axB.set_xlabel("time"); axB.set_ylabel("√hₜ")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Estimation by maximum likelihood

ARCH/GARCH models are non-linear and cannot be estimated by OLS (Enders 2014). They are fit by maximum likelihood (usually assuming conditional normality), maximising the log-likelihood over the mean and variance parameters simultaneously, with a non-linear search algorithm (BFGS, simplex) to find the peak (Enders 2014).

Asymmetry: the leverage effect

Another stylised fact: the leverage effect — bad news (negative shocks) raises volatility more than good news of the same size (Enders 2014). Symmetric GARCH (which uses \epsilon_{t-1}^2, blind to sign) misses this, so two extensions add asymmetry:

  • TARCH / GJR-GARCH — add a dummy d_{t-1} = 1 when \epsilon_{t-1} < 0: h_t = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1\epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \lambda_1 d_{t-1}\epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 h_{t-1}. If \lambda_1 > 0, negative shocks hit harder (Enders 2014).
  • EGARCH (Nelson 1991) — model \ln h_t, which keeps h_t > 0 automatically (no non-negativity constraints on the coefficients) and captures asymmetry through standardised shocks (Enders 2014).

ARCH-M: when risk enters the mean

The ARCH-in-Mean (ARCH-M) model lets the conditional mean depend on the conditional variance (Enders 2014) — the econometric form of “no risk premium without risk”:

y_t = \beta + \delta h_t + \epsilon_t, \qquad \delta > 0 .

Risk-averse investors demand a higher expected return when risk is high, so a shock raises h_t, which raises the required risk premium \delta h_t (Enders 2014). It closes the loop between the two equations — variance feeds the mean.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Modelling variance before the mean is right. ARCH tests on the residuals of a misspecified mean equation can falsely signal ARCH; get the mean model right first. (Enders 2014)
  • Forgetting the persistence constraint. If \sum\alpha+\sum\beta \ge 1 the unconditional variance is infinite (IGARCH); check it before trusting long-horizon variance forecasts. (Enders 2014)
  • Using symmetric GARCH on equity returns. The leverage effect is real; a symmetric model understates the volatility surge after bad news — use GJR/EGARCH. (Enders 2014)
  • Estimating with OLS. These are non-linear; OLS is invalid — use MLE. (Enders 2014)
  • Assuming normality blindly. Returns are fat-tailed even after GARCH; consider a t-distributed conditional density (Enders notes the normal is the usual but not mandatory choice).

Appendix · the mathematics

Unconditional variance of GARCH(1,1). Take expectations of h_t = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1\epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 h_{t-1}. Since \mathbb{E}[\epsilon_{t-1}^2] = \mathbb{E}[h_{t-1}] = \sigma^2 in the stationary state, \sigma^2 = \alpha_0 + (\alpha_1+\beta_1)\sigma^2, giving

\sigma^2 = \frac{\alpha_0}{1 - \alpha_1 - \beta_1}.

This is positive and finite iff \alpha_1 + \beta_1 < 1 — the persistence condition. At \alpha_1+\beta_1 = 1 the denominator is zero: the unconditional variance diverges (IGARCH).

Why GARCH(1,1) ≈ ARCH(∞). Recursively substitute h_{t-1}: h_t = \alpha_0(1+\beta_1+\beta_1^2+\cdots) + \alpha_1\sum_{k\ge 0}\beta_1^{k}\epsilon_{t-1-k}^2. The single \beta_1 encodes a geometrically declining weight on all past squared shocks — an infinite ARCH captured by one extra parameter. That is the source of its parsimony.

Volatility forecasting. The s-step-ahead forecast of the conditional variance reverts to the unconditional level: \mathbb{E}_t[h_{t+s}] = \sigma^2 + (\alpha_1+\beta_1)^{s}(h_{t+1}-\sigma^2), decaying at rate (\alpha_1+\beta_1). Near 1 (high persistence) the decay is slow — calm and storm both last.

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Unconditional variance of GARCH(1,1).** Take expectations of
$h_t = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1\epsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 h_{t-1}$. Since
$\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_{t-1}^2] = \mathbb{E}[h_{t-1}] = \sigma^2$ in the stationary state,
$\sigma^2 = \alpha_0 + (\alpha_1+\beta_1)\sigma^2$, giving

$$
\sigma^2 = \frac{\alpha_0}{1 - \alpha_1 - \beta_1}.
$$

This is positive and finite **iff $\alpha_1 + \beta_1 < 1$** — the persistence condition. At
$\alpha_1+\beta_1 = 1$ the denominator is zero: the unconditional variance **diverges** (IGARCH).

**Why GARCH(1,1) ≈ ARCH(∞).** Recursively substitute $h_{t-1}$:
$h_t = \alpha_0(1+\beta_1+\beta_1^2+\cdots) + \alpha_1\sum_{k\ge 0}\beta_1^{k}\epsilon_{t-1-k}^2$. The
single $\beta_1$ encodes a **geometrically declining** weight on *all* past squared shocks — an infinite
ARCH captured by one extra parameter. That is the source of its parsimony.

**Volatility forecasting.** The $s$-step-ahead forecast of the conditional variance reverts to the
unconditional level: $\mathbb{E}_t[h_{t+s}] = \sigma^2 + (\alpha_1+\beta_1)^{s}(h_{t+1}-\sigma^2)$, decaying
at rate $(\alpha_1+\beta_1)$. Near 1 (high persistence) the decay is slow — calm and storm both last.

:::
