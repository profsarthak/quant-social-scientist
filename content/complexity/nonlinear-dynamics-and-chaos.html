Nonlinear Dynamics & Chaos

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A single line of arithmetic can produce behaviour so intricate it looks random — yet is perfectly deterministic. Chaos is the discovery that determinism and predictability are not the same thing.

Take the simplest nonlinear rule for a population — x_{n+1} = r\,x_n(1-x_n) — and turn one knob. At low r the population settles to a steady value; raise r and it splits into a 2-cycle, then 4, then 8, then chaos: aperiodic, never-repeating, and exponentially sensitive to its starting point. The system is fully deterministic — no randomness anywhere — yet long-range prediction is impossible in principle. That is the revolution of chaos theory, and it bounds what any model of a complex system can forecast.

Why it matters

Chaos sets a hard ceiling on prediction for weather, ecosystems, heart rhythms, and economies (Strogatz 2015). It dissolves the comforting equation deterministic = predictable: you can know the exact rule and still not know the future, because you can never know the present to infinite precision. For a forecaster or modeller, knowing which regime a system is in — stable, periodic, or chaotic — is the difference between a useful forecast and false confidence.

Dynamical systems and attractors

A dynamical system evolves over time by a fixed rule — a differential equation (continuous time) or an iterated map (discrete) (Strogatz 2015). Its states live in phase space, and over time the system often settles onto an attractor (Strogatz 2015). Stable fixed points (sinks) pull nearby states back after a disturbance; unstable ones (sources) repel (Strogatz 2015). The question chaos answers is: what happens when the attractor is neither a point nor a cycle?

The logistic map: a road to chaos

The canonical example — simple rule, astounding complexity (Mitchell 2009; Strogatz 2015):

x_{n+1} = r\,x_n(1-x_n),

with x_n the population as a fraction of carrying capacity and r the growth rate. As r rises (Mitchell 2009):

  • small r → a stable fixed point (one steady value);
  • higher r → period-doubling: oscillation between 2 values, then 4, then 8, … each split a bifurcation;
  • the cascade culminates in chaos at r \approx 3.5699 (Mitchell 2009).
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Bifurcation diagram (vectorised over r)
rs = np.linspace(2.5, 4.0, 2000)
x = np.full_like(rs, 0.5)
for _ in range(400): x = rs*x*(1-x)            # discard transient
pts_r, pts_x = [], []
for _ in range(160):
    x = rs*x*(1-x); pts_r.append(rs.copy()); pts_x.append(x.copy())
pts_r = np.concatenate(pts_r); pts_x = np.concatenate(pts_x)

# Sensitive dependence at r = 3.9
r = 3.9; a = np.empty(60); b = np.empty(60); a[0], b[0] = 0.5, 0.5 + 1e-9
for n in range(59):
    a[n+1] = r*a[n]*(1-a[n]); b[n+1] = r*b[n]*(1-b[n])

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.4, 4.3), gridspec_kw={"width_ratios":[1.5,1]})
axL.plot(pts_r, pts_x, ",k", alpha=0.25)
axL.axvline(3.5699, color="#c0392b", lw=1, ls="--"); axL.text(3.58, 0.05, "onset of chaos", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axL.set_xlabel("growth rate  r"); axL.set_ylabel("long-run population  x"); axL.set_title("Bifurcation diagram: period-doubling → chaos")

axR.plot(a, "o-", color="#1f4e79", ms=3, lw=1, label="x₀ = 0.5")
axR.plot(b, "o-", color="#c0392b", ms=3, lw=1, label="x₀ = 0.5 + 10⁻⁹")
axR.set_xlabel("step n"); axR.set_ylabel("x"); axR.set_title("Sensitive dependence (r = 3.9)")
axR.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

A stunning twist: Feigenbaum found the bifurcations arrive at a universal rate — the gaps shrink by a constant factor \delta \approx 4.6692 for a huge class of maps (Mitchell 2009). The road to chaos has the same shape across utterly different systems.

Chaos and sensitive dependence

Chaos is aperiodic long-term behaviour in a deterministic system with sensitive dependence on initial conditions (Strogatz 2015). Sensitive dependence — the “butterfly effect” — means two trajectories from nearly identical states diverge exponentially fast, so any measurement error, however tiny, makes long-term prediction vastly inaccurate (Mitchell 2009). The Lyapunov exponent \lambda measures that divergence rate; a positive \lambda is the signature of chaos (Strogatz 2015). Crucially, chaos is deterministic, not random: the rule is exact with no random inputs — it only appears random because we can’t know the start to infinite precision (Mitchell 2009).

Strange attractors

A bounded chaotic system that settles onto neither a point nor a cycle lands on a strange attractor — e.g. the butterfly-shaped Lorenz attractor, a fractal with fine structure at every scale (Strogatz 2015). Its geometry comes from stretching (magnifying microscopic differences) and folding (keeping trajectories bounded), like kneading dough (Strogatz 2015).

The limits of prediction

The practical payload (Strogatz 2015):

  • A prediction horizon. Because errors grow exponentially, forecasts fail beyond a finite time — about a week for weather (Strogatz 2015).
  • Precision barely helps. A million-fold improvement in initial-measurement accuracy extends the horizon only slightly (Strogatz 2015).
  • Predict statistics, not specifics. You can forecast the climate (statistical properties) even when the weather (the specific path) is fundamentally obscured (Strogatz 2015).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Equating deterministic with predictable. A known exact rule can still be unforecastable; chaos is the reason. (Mitchell 2009)
  • Calling noisy data ‘chaotic’. Chaos is low-dimensional determinism, not randomness; the claim requires evidence (a positive Lyapunov exponent, a strange attractor), not just irregularity. (Strogatz 2015)
  • Chasing long-range point forecasts. Past the prediction horizon, report distributions/statistics, not a single trajectory. (Strogatz 2015)
  • Assuming more data fixes it. Exponential error growth means precision yields only logarithmic horizon gains. (Strogatz 2015)

  • Mitchell, Complexity: A Guided Tour — the logistic map x_{n+1}=rx_n(1-x_n), the period-doubling cascade and bifurcation diagram, the onset of chaos at r\approx3.57, the Feigenbaum universal constant, and that chaos is deterministic, not random.

Appendix · the mathematics

Logistic-map stability. The map f(x)=rx(1-x) has a nonzero fixed point x^*=1-1/r, stable while |f'(x^*)|=|2-r|<1, i.e. 1<r<3. At r=3, |f'|=1 and the fixed point loses stability — the first period-doubling bifurcation, where a stable 2-cycle is born. Successive doublings occur at r_1=3,\,r_2\approx3.449,\,r_3\approx3.544,\dots, accumulating at r_\infty\approx3.5699.

Feigenbaum universality. The ratios \delta_k=(r_k-r_{k-1})/(r_{k+1}-r_k) converge to \delta=4.66920\ldots for any smooth unimodal map — the route to chaos is universal, independent of the specific equation, which is why a single toy map illuminates real systems.

Lyapunov exponent. \lambda=\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\ln|f'(x_n)|. A small initial separation \delta_0 grows as \delta_n\approx\delta_0 e^{\lambda n}; \lambda>0 ⇒ chaos. The prediction horizon scales as \sim\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(1/\delta_0) — logarithmic in precision, hence the futility of buying accuracy.

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Logistic-map stability.** The map $f(x)=rx(1-x)$ has a nonzero fixed point $x^*=1-1/r$, stable while
$|f'(x^*)|=|2-r|<1$, i.e. $1<r<3$. At $r=3$, $|f'|=1$ and the fixed point loses stability — the first

$r_1=3,\,r_2\approx3.449,\,r_3\approx3.544,\dots$, accumulating at $r_\infty\approx3.5699$.

**Feigenbaum universality.** The ratios $\delta_k=(r_k-r_{k-1})/(r_{k+1}-r_k)$ converge to

the specific equation, which is why a single toy map illuminates real systems.

**Lyapunov exponent.** $\lambda=\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac1N\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\ln|f'(x_n)|$. A small initial
separation $\delta_0$ grows as $\delta_n\approx\delta_0 e^{\lambda n}$; $\lambda>0$ ⇒ chaos. The prediction
horizon scales as $\sim\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(1/\delta_0)$ — *logarithmic* in precision, hence the futility of
buying accuracy.

:::
