Spreading on Networks: Contagion & Diffusion

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A disease, a rumour, a behaviour — none spreads in a well-mixed soup. It travels along the edges of a contact network, and the network’s shape decides whether it fizzles or sweeps the world.

Classical epidemiology assumes everyone mixes with everyone. Real contagion — viral, informational, behavioural — moves node by node along the edges of a contact network, so structure is destiny. The hubs are superspreaders that abolish the usual safety threshold; weak ties carry novelty between communities; and behaviours spread by a different rule than germs. This entry — the capstone of the Network Analysis sub-track — is where everything before it pays off.

Why it matters

Epidemics, viral marketing, the diffusion of innovations, the cascade of a protest or a bank run — all are spreading processes on networks, and the network determines who to vaccinate, who to seed, and why some good ideas never catch on. It also connects straight back to causal inference: peer effects and contagion are why SUTVA (no interference between units) so often fails in social data.

Epidemic models, run on a graph

The compartmental models, but evolving node by node with transmission along edges at probability \beta (Barabási 2016; Menczer et al. 2020):

  • SI — susceptible → infected, and infected forever. The simplest baseline.
  • SIS — no immunity (a cold): infected nodes recover back to susceptible and can be reinfected.
  • SIR — permanent immunity or removal: susceptible → infectious → removed (recovered/dead, no longer transmitting).

Hubs and the missing threshold

Network topology sets the speed and reach (Barabási 2016):

  • Hubs are superspreaders. A high-degree node is infected early (many exposures) and, once infected, rebroadcasts to enormous numbers of others; many shortest paths run through hubs, shrinking the distance the contagion must travel (Barabási 2016).
  • No epidemic threshold on scale-free networks (Pastor-Satorras & Vespignani). In a random network a disease must be “contagious enough” to persist; on a scale-free network the hubs let a disease linger and spread no matter how low its infectiousness (Barabási 2016). The usual safety margin simply vanishes.

Simple vs complex contagion

Crucially, information and behaviour spread by different rules (Menczer et al. 2020):

  • Simple contagion — germs and simple information (a rumour) transmit on a single contact (Menczer et al. 2020).
  • Complex contagion — behaviours, norms, and technology adoption need multiple reinforcing contacts: a node adopts only when a threshold fraction q of its neighbours already has (Granovetter threshold models, the Watts global-cascade model) (Menczer et al. 2020).

This flips the value of network structure (Menczer et al. 2020): the long-range shortcuts that make a small world great for simple contagion are ineffective for complex contagion, which needs social reinforcement and so spreads better through densely clustered ties where neighbours share friends (Menczer et al. 2020). Tight communities can block a cascade — why some innovations never diffuse.

The strength of weak ties

Granovetter’s classic insight (Menczer et al. 2020): strong ties (close friends) are redundant — they sit inside the same dense cluster, all knowing each other. Weak ties (acquaintances) act as local bridges between communities, and so are the main conduits of novel information — job leads, news from distant parts of the network (Menczer et al. 2020). The counterintuitive moral: for reach and novelty, your acquaintances matter more than your friends.

Show the figure code (BA network + SIR by hand) 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
N, m = 1500, 2
# grow a Barabási–Albert network
deg = np.zeros(N, int); repeated = []; nbrs = [set() for _ in range(N)]
for i in range(m+1):
    for j in range(i+1, m+1):
        nbrs[i].add(j); nbrs[j].add(i); deg[i]+=1; deg[j]+=1; repeated += [i, j]
for new in range(m+1, N):
    tg = set()
    while len(tg) < m: tg.add(repeated[rng.integers(len(repeated))])
    for t in tg:
        nbrs[new].add(t); nbrs[t].add(new); deg[new]+=1; deg[t]+=1; repeated += [new, t]
adj = [np.fromiter(s, int) for s in nbrs]

def sir(immune, beta=0.06, gamma=0.12, T=60):
    state = np.zeros(N, int)                  # 0=S 1=I 2=R
    state[list(immune)] = 2
    ever = np.zeros(N, bool)
    seed = rng.choice(np.where(state == 0)[0]); state[seed] = 1; ever[seed] = True
    curve = []
    for _ in range(T):
        inf = np.where(state == 1)[0]
        ns = state.copy()
        for u in inf:
            for v in adj[u]:
                if state[v] == 0 and rng.random() < beta: ns[v] = 1; ever[v] = True
            if rng.random() < gamma: ns[u] = 2
        state = ns; curve.append(ever.mean())
    return np.array(curve)

nv = int(0.08*N)
rand_immune = set(rng.choice(N, nv, replace=False))
hub_immune = set(np.argsort(deg)[-nv:])
none = np.mean([sir(set()) for _ in range(5)], axis=0)
randc = np.mean([sir(rand_immune) for _ in range(5)], axis=0)
hubc = np.mean([sir(hub_immune) for _ in range(5)], axis=0)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.plot(none, color="#c0392b", lw=2.2, label="no immunisation")
ax.plot(randc, color="#d98a29", lw=2.2, label="random 8% immunised")
ax.plot(hubc, color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.4, label="top-8% HUBS immunised")
ax.set_xlabel("time"); ax.set_ylabel("cumulative fraction infected")
ax.set_title("Vaccinate the hubs: same budget, the epidemic collapses")
ax.legend(fontsize=8.5, frameon=False, loc="center right"); ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Practical implications

The structure points straight at policy (Barabási 2016; Menczer et al. 2020):

  • Targeted immunisation. Vaccinating a small fraction of hubs fragments the network and stops a pandemic far more effectively than random vaccination (Barabási 2016) — the figure’s lesson.
  • Seeding diffusion. In marketing, target influentials with access to easily-influenced others to seed a cascade; but for complex contagion, seed clustered communities for reinforcement (Menczer et al. 2020).
  • Why innovations stall. Densely closed communities can block cascades — a diagnosis for failed diffusion (Menczer et al. 2020).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Assuming well-mixing. Network structure changes the threshold, the speed, and who matters; the mean-field SIR can badly mislead. (Barabási 2016)
  • Treating behaviour like a virus. Adoption is complex contagion (needs reinforcement); long-range shortcuts that spread rumours fast may fail to spread behaviour. (Menczer et al. 2020)
  • Random vaccination on a scale-free network. It wastes doses on low-degree nodes and misses the hubs doing the spreading. (Barabási 2016)
  • Ignoring interference in causal studies. Contagion violates SUTVA — one unit’s treatment spills to its neighbours. (Menczer et al. 2020)

  • Menczer, Fortunato & Davis, A First Course in Network Science — simple vs complex contagion (Granovetter thresholds, Watts cascades; clusters vs shortcuts), the strength of weak ties (bridges, novel information), and seeding/diffusion implications.

Appendix · the mathematics

SIR on a network. Each infected node u infects each susceptible neighbour independently with probability \beta per step and recovers with probability \gamma — exactly the loop in the code. The mean-field epidemic threshold is \lambda_c=\beta/\gamma \sim \langle k\rangle/\langle k^2\rangle: an outbreak grows only if \lambda>\lambda_c. On scale-free networks \langle k^2\rangle\to\infty, so \lambda_c\to0 — no threshold. Targeted immunisation works by removing the high-k nodes that make \langle k^2\rangle explode, restoring a finite threshold.

Complex-contagion threshold. In Watts’ model a node with fraction \phi of adopting neighbours adopts iff \phi\ge q. A global cascade requires a connected “vulnerable” cluster (nodes with low enough threshold relative to degree); too few or too many connections both suppress cascades, so there is a cascade window in (q,\langle k\rangle) — unlike simple contagion, more connectivity is not always more spread.

Why weak ties carry novelty. A strong tie sits in a triangle (its endpoints share neighbours), so its information is redundant. A weak tie that is a local bridge is the only short path between two clusters, so it is the unique conduit for whatever one cluster knows and the other doesn’t.

Connections

  • Builds on: scale-free networks (hubs, no threshold), small worlds (shortcuts speed simple contagion), and community structure (clusters reinforce complex contagion).
  • Connects to: Erdős–Rényi percolation (the \langle k\rangle=1 threshold is the epidemic threshold) and causal interference / SUTVA.
  • Leads to: the Dynamics cluster — agent-based models make these spreading rules into full simulations of social behaviour.

$\phi\ge q$. A global cascade requires a connected **"vulnerable" cluster** (nodes with low enough threshold
relative to degree); too few or too many connections both *suppress* cascades, so there is a cascade *window*
in $(q,\langle k\rangle)$ — unlike simple contagion, more connectivity is not always more spread.

**Why weak ties carry novelty.** A strong tie sits in a triangle (its endpoints share neighbours), so its
information is redundant. A weak tie that is a **local bridge** is the *only* short path between two clusters,
so it is the unique conduit for whatever one cluster knows and the other doesn't.

:::
