Centrality: Who Matters in a Network

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

‘Important’ has many meanings — popular, well-placed, reachable, prestigious — and the measures that capture them genuinely disagree. The disagreement is the insight.

Ask “who is the most important node?” and the network gives different answers depending on what you mean. Degree rewards popularity; betweenness rewards brokerage; closeness rewards reach; eigenvector and PageRank reward prestige. A low-degree person who is the only bridge between two communities is nearly invisible to one measure and dominant in another — and knowing which to use is the whole skill.

Why it matters

“Who should we vaccinate / target / monitor / promote?” is a centrality question, and choosing the wrong measure points you at the wrong node. The broker who controls information flow, the hub everyone follows, and the node that reaches everyone fastest are different people — centrality makes the distinction precise.

Degree centrality — popularity

The simplest measure: the number of connections, normalised by the maximum possible (Menczer et al. 2020):

c_D(i) = \frac{k_i}{N-1}, \qquad k_i = \sum_j A_{ij}.

It captures popularity, activity, exposure — the hubs of a social network, the major airports of a flight network (Menczer et al. 2020). Local and cheap, but blind to where in the structure those edges sit.

Betweenness centrality — brokerage

Betweenness measures how often a node sits on the shortest paths between other pairs (Menczer et al. 2020):

b_i = \sum_{h\ne j\ne i} \frac{\sigma_{hj}(i)}{\sigma_{hj}},

where \sigma_{hj} is the number of shortest paths from h to j and \sigma_{hj}(i) how many pass through i. It quantifies brokerage, gatekeeping, control of flow — the bridges between communities (Menczer et al. 2020). Its signature case: a low-degree individual who is the only link between two social circles (spanning a structural hole) has very high betweenness, because all communication between the groups must pass through them (Menczer et al. 2020). It is computed from BFS all-pairs shortest paths (Menczer et al. 2020).

Closeness centrality — reach

Closeness is the inverse of the total distance to everyone else (Menczer et al. 2020):

g_i = \frac{1}{\sum_{j\ne i}\ell_{ij}}.

High closeness means a node can reach the whole network in few hops — the most efficient seed for spreading a rumour or diffusing information fast (Menczer et al. 2020).

Eigenvector centrality — prestige by association

Degree counts neighbours; eigenvector centrality weights them by their importance — “a node is important if its neighbours are important” (Menczer et al. 2020). An endorsement from a high-status node counts more than one from the periphery; an academic is influential for being cited by the highly cited (Menczer et al. 2020). Formally it is the leading eigenvector of the adjacency matrix:

A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} \quad(\lambda = \text{largest eigenvalue}),

and c_E(i) = v_i (Menczer et al. 2020).

PageRank — the directed, random-walk variant

PageRank (Google’s founders) adapts eigenvector centrality to directed networks via a random walker: importance is the long-run probability the walker is at a node (Barabási 2016). The fix that makes it work is a damping factor \alpha (≈0.85): with probability \alpha the walker follows an out-link, and with probability 1-\alpha it teleports to a random node — which handles dangling nodes (no out-links) that would otherwise trap the walker and drain importance (Barabási 2016). The transition operator is T = \alpha A D^{-1} + \tfrac{1-\alpha}{n}J, and PageRank is its stationary distribution (Barabási 2016). A page can have high degree but low PageRank if its links come only from unimportant pages (Barabási 2016).

When centralities disagree — and how to choose

Show the figure code (betweenness via Brandes; eigenvector via power iteration — by hand) 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from collections import deque

rng = np.random.default_rng(2)
A = np.zeros((15, 15), int)
def connect(nodes, p):
    for a in range(len(nodes)):
        for b in range(a+1, len(nodes)):
            if rng.random() < p: A[nodes[a], nodes[b]] = A[nodes[b], nodes[a]] = 1
cluA, cluB, bridge = list(range(0, 7)), list(range(7, 14)), 14
connect(cluA, 0.6); connect(cluB, 0.6)
for u in (cluA[0], cluA[1]): A[u, cluA[(cluA.index(u)+1)]] = A[cluA[(cluA.index(u)+1)], u] = 1  # ensure connected
A[bridge, 3] = A[3, bridge] = 1; A[bridge, 10] = A[10, bridge] = 1   # the ONLY A-B link
N = 15; adj = [np.where(A[i])[0] for i in range(N)]

# degree
deg = A.sum(1).astype(float)

# betweenness via Brandes (unweighted)
btw = np.zeros(N)
for s in range(N):
    S = []; P = [[] for _ in range(N)]; sigma = np.zeros(N); sigma[s] = 1
    d = np.full(N, -1); d[s] = 0; Q = deque([s])
    while Q:
        v = Q.popleft(); S.append(v)
        for w in adj[v]:
            if d[w] < 0: d[w] = d[v]+1; Q.append(w)
            if d[w] == d[v]+1: sigma[w] += sigma[v]; P[w].append(v)
    delta = np.zeros(N)
    while S:
        w = S.pop()
        for v in P[w]: delta[v] += (sigma[v]/sigma[w])*(1+delta[w])
        if w != s: btw[w] += delta[w]
btw /= 2

# eigenvector centrality via power iteration
v = np.ones(N)
for _ in range(200):
    v = A @ v; v /= np.linalg.norm(v)
eig = v

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(10.2, 3.8))
ang = {}; posA = np.c_[0.2+0.12*np.cos(np.linspace(0,2*np.pi,7,endpoint=False)),
                       0.5+0.22*np.sin(np.linspace(0,2*np.pi,7,endpoint=False))]
posB = np.c_[0.8+0.12*np.cos(np.linspace(0,2*np.pi,7,endpoint=False)),
             0.5+0.22*np.sin(np.linspace(0,2*np.pi,7,endpoint=False))]
pos = np.vstack([posA, posB, [[0.5, 0.5]]])
for ax, score, name in zip(axes, [deg, btw, eig], ["degree", "betweenness", "eigenvector"]):
    for i in range(N):
        for j in adj[i]:
            if j > i: ax.plot(*zip(pos[i], pos[j]), color="#dadee2", lw=0.7, zorder=1)
    s = 60 + 1400*(score/score.max())
    ax.scatter(pos[:,0], pos[:,1], s=s, color="#1f4e79", zorder=2)
    ax.scatter(*pos[bridge], s=60+1400*(score[bridge]/score.max()), facecolor="#1f4e79",
               edgecolor="#c0392b", linewidth=2.5, zorder=3)
    ax.set_title(name); ax.axis("off"); ax.set_xlim(0,1); ax.set_ylim(0.15,0.85)
fig.suptitle("Same network, three notions of 'important' — watch the bridge node (red outline)", fontsize=11)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The measures disagree because they capture different things (Barabási 2016). The selection rule (Menczer et al. 2020; Barabási 2016):

Use… …to find Example
Degree local popularity / activity most-followed account
Betweenness chokepoints / flow controllers the broker between cliques
Closeness fast reach / accessibility best seed for diffusion
Eigenvector / PageRank global prestige / ranking most-cited-by-the-cited; web ranking

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Defaulting to degree. It misses brokers and prestige; the most structurally important node often isn’t the most connected. (Menczer et al. 2020)
  • Betweenness on disconnected graphs. Undefined across components and dominated by a few bridges; compute within the giant component and interpret carefully. (Menczer et al. 2020)
  • Eigenvector centrality on directed/disconnected graphs. It can concentrate on one component or fail to converge — use PageRank (with damping) for directed networks. (Barabási 2016)
  • Forgetting normalisation. Raw betweenness scales with N^2; normalise before comparing networks of different size. (Menczer et al. 2020)
  • Equating high degree with influence. A hub linked only by unimportant nodes has low PageRank — quantity isn’t quality. (Barabási 2016)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Menczer, Fortunato & Davis, A First Course in Network Science — degree c_D=k_i/(N-1); betweenness b_i=\sum \sigma_{hj}(i)/\sigma_{hj} (brokerage / structural holes, via BFS); closeness g_i=1/\sum_j\ell_{ij}; eigenvector centrality as the leading eigenvector of A; and that the measures disagree, with a selection rule by research question.
  • Barabási, Network Science — PageRank as the damped random-walk variant for directed graphs (T=\alpha AD^{-1}+\tfrac{1-\alpha}{n}J), handling dangling nodes, and high-degree-but-low-PageRank.

Appendix · the mathematics

Brandes’ algorithm (the code). Naïvely, betweenness needs all shortest paths through each node — O(N^3). Brandes computes it in O(NL) by, for each source s: a BFS that records the shortest-path counts \sigma_s(v) and predecessors, then a reverse-BFS accumulation of the dependency \delta_s(v)=\sum_{w:\,v\in\text{pred}(w)} \tfrac{\sigma_s(v)}{\sigma_s(w)}(1+\delta_s(w)). Summing \delta_s(v) over all sources s gives b_v (halved for undirected graphs) — exactly the loop in the figure.

Eigenvector centrality and power iteration. A\mathbf v=\lambda\mathbf v for the largest \lambda; by Perron–Frobenius, for a connected graph the leading eigenvector is positive, so v_i\ge0 are valid scores. Power iteration — repeatedly \mathbf v\leftarrow A\mathbf v/\|A\mathbf v\| — converges to it because the dominant eigendirection grows fastest. PageRank is the same idea on the stochastic operator T, whose stationary distribution (leading eigenvector, \lambda=1) is guaranteed unique by the teleportation term.


The measures **disagree because they capture different things** [@barabasi_network]. The selection rule
[@menczer_network; @barabasi_network]:

| Use… | …to find | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Degree** | local popularity / activity | most-followed account |
| **Betweenness** | chokepoints / flow controllers | the broker between cliques |
| **Closeness** | fast reach / accessibility | best seed for diffusion |
| **Eigenvector / PageRank** | global prestige / ranking | most-cited-by-the-cited; web ranking |

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}
- **Defaulting to degree.** It misses brokers and prestige; the most *structurally* important node often
  isn't the most connected. [@menczer_network]
- **Betweenness on disconnected graphs.** Undefined across components and dominated by a few bridges; compute
  within the giant component and interpret carefully. [@menczer_network]
- **Eigenvector centrality on directed/disconnected graphs.** It can concentrate on one component or fail to
  converge — use **PageRank** (with damping) for directed networks. [@barabasi_network]
- **Forgetting normalisation.** Raw betweenness scales with $N^2$; normalise before comparing networks of
  different size. [@menczer_network]
- **Equating high degree with influence.** A hub linked only by unimportant nodes has low PageRank — quantity
  isn't quality. [@barabasi_network]
:::

## From the sources {.unnumbered}

::: {.callout-note collapse="true" title="Who says what"}
- **Menczer, Fortunato & Davis, *A First Course in Network Science*** — **degree** $c_D=k_i/(N-1)$;
  **betweenness** $b_i=\sum \sigma_{hj}(i)/\sigma_{hj}$ (brokerage / structural holes, via BFS);
  **closeness** $g_i=1/\sum_j\ell_{ij}$; **eigenvector** centrality as the leading eigenvector of $A$; and
  that the measures disagree, with a selection rule by research question.
- **Barabási, *Network Science*** — **PageRank** as the damped random-walk variant for directed graphs
  ($T=\alpha AD^{-1}+\tfrac{1-\alpha}{n}J$), handling dangling nodes, and high-degree-but-low-PageRank.
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Brandes' algorithm (the code).** Naïvely, betweenness needs all shortest paths through each node — $O(N^3)$.
Brandes computes it in $O(NL)$ by, for each source $s$: a BFS that records the shortest-path counts
$\sigma_s(v)$ and predecessors, then a **reverse-BFS accumulation** of the *dependency*
$\delta_s(v)=\sum_{w:\,v\in\text{pred}(w)} \tfrac{\sigma_s(v)}{\sigma_s(w)}(1+\delta_s(w))$. Summing
$\delta_s(v)$ over all sources $s$ gives $b_v$ (halved for undirected graphs) — exactly the loop in the figure.

**Eigenvector centrality and power iteration.** $A\mathbf v=\lambda\mathbf v$ for the largest $\lambda$; by
Perron–Frobenius, for a connected graph the leading eigenvector is positive, so $v_i\ge0$ are valid scores.
**Power iteration** — repeatedly $\mathbf v\leftarrow A\mathbf v/\|A\mathbf v\|$ — converges to it because the
dominant eigendirection grows fastest. PageRank is the same idea on the *stochastic* operator $T$, whose
stationary distribution (leading eigenvector, $\lambda=1$) is guaranteed unique by the teleportation term.

:::
