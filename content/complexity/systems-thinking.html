Thinking in Systems: Feedback, Stocks & Leverage

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Why a well-measured intervention can still backfire — because the thing you’re acting on pushes back. The structure, not the event, drives the behaviour.

Every estimate in this book isolates one effect: what does X do to Y, all else equal? But the world you act on is a system — stocks, flows, and feedback loops that respond to your intervention and often push back. Donella Meadows’ Thinking in Systems is the lens for that: it explains why policies provoke resistance, why problems persist despite our efforts, and why the highest-leverage place to intervene is almost never the one our instincts reach for. It is the structural complement to everything you’ve learned about estimating effects.

Why it matters

A causal estimate is a statement about a nudge to one variable. A policy is a sustained shove against a living structure that adapts. (Meadows 2008) The reason good interventions disappoint — and the reason a metric gets gamed the moment it becomes a target — is that you are not acting on an inert quantity but on a network of feedback loops that responds. Meadows’ core warning is blunt: “the system causes its own behavior.” (Meadows 2008) The quantitative researcher who can estimate an effect and read the system it lives in is the one whose recommendations survive contact with reality.

What a system is

A system is not a heap of parts; it is “an interconnected set of elements coherently organized in a way that achieves something.” It has three components, in rising order of importance and falling order of visibility: (Meadows 2008)

  • Elements — the tangible parts you notice first (a university’s students, faculty, buildings).
  • Interconnections — the relationships that bind them, usually flows of information or signals (admission standards, grading rules, the chemical messages in a tree).
  • Purpose / function — the “most crucial but least obvious” part. (Meadows 2008)

The deep move is how you find the purpose: “purposes are deduced from behavior, not from rhetoric or stated goals.” (Meadows 2008) Swap every player on a team and the system is barely changed; change its purpose — a university shifting from “disseminating knowledge” to “making a profit” — and it transforms, even with the same people in the same buildings. Meadows’ compressed version, and a razor for cutting through any mission statement: “the purpose of a system is what it does.” (Meadows 2008)

Stocks and flows

The building blocks. A stock is an accumulation — water in a bathtub, a population, a bank balance, a “reserve of good will.” A flow is the action that fills or drains it (the tap and the plughole). (Meadows 2008) Two properties make stocks the heart of system behaviour:

  • A stock is “the memory of the history of changing flows within the system.” (Meadows 2008) It carries the past forward; you read history in the level, not the moment.
  • Stocks decouple inflow from outflow. Because the bathtub buffers, the two flows need not match instant to instant — an oil company keeps running even when refinery output doesn’t equal the rate cars burn fuel. (Meadows 2008) Stocks act as delays, buffers, and shock absorbers — and those delays are exactly what makes systems hard to steer.

Feedback loops

A feedback loop is “a closed chain of causal connections” from a stock, through a flow, back to the stock. (Meadows 2008) There are only two kinds, and almost all dynamics are their interplay:

  • Balancing (stabilizing) loops are goal-seeking: they oppose whatever direction of change is imposed, holding a stock near a target. A thermostat returning a room to its setpoint; a hot coffee cooling to room temperature. (Meadows 2008)
  • Reinforcing (amplifying) loops are self-multiplying: the more there is in the stock, the faster it grows — exponential growth or runaway collapse. An interest-bearing account; a breeding population of rabbits. (Meadows 2008)
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

T = 60
# Reinforcing loop: stock grows in proportion to itself (dS = r*S dt)
r = 0.06
reinforcing = np.empty(T); reinforcing[0] = 2.0
for t in range(1, T):
    reinforcing[t] = reinforcing[t-1] + r * reinforcing[t-1]

# Balancing loop: stock seeks a goal (dS = k*(goal - S) dt)
goal, k = 8.0, 0.12
balancing = np.empty(T); balancing[0] = 0.5
for t in range(1, T):
    balancing[t] = balancing[t-1] + k * (goal - balancing[t-1])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.0))
ax.plot(reinforcing, color="#161613", lw=2.4, label="reinforcing loop (amplifies)")
ax.plot(balancing, color="#5e5e54", lw=2.4, label="balancing loop (goal-seeking)")
ax.axhline(goal, color="#5e5e54", ls="--", lw=1.2, alpha=0.7)
ax.text(T*0.62, goal + 0.4, "balancing loop's goal", color="#5e5e54", fontsize=8.5)
ax.set_ylim(0, 16)
ax.set_xlabel("time"); ax.set_ylabel("stock level")
ax.set_title("Reinforcing vs balancing feedback")
ax.legend(fontsize=8.5, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The system causes its own behaviour

Here is the insight that reorders how you read evidence. We instinctively blame events — a single bad number, a single villain. Meadows insists we climb from events to behaviour (trends over time) to structure (the arrangement of stocks, flows, and loops). (Meadows 2008) “The system causes its own behavior.” A political leader doesn’t cause a recession; “ups and downs are inherent in the structure of the market economy.” (Meadows 2008) Persistent ills — poverty, war — endure because they are “intrinsically systems problems,” generated by structures that recreate them regardless of who occupies the roles. (Meadows 2008)

This is why “systems fool us”: they behave beautifully for a while, then surprise us with terrible results. (Meadows 2008) And the surprise is rarely anyone’s malice. Under bounded rationality, every actor behaves “dutifully and rationally” on the local information they can see, yet the aggregate is “a perfectly terrible result.” (Meadows 2008) No bad guys required — just a structure that makes sensible local choices add up to collective failure.

Leverage points — and why we push them backwards

A leverage point is “a place in a system where a small shift… can produce big changes in everything.” (Meadows 2008) The famous, humbling observation: people are often good at intuiting where the leverage points are — and then push them in the wrong direction. (Meadows 2008) The hierarchy explains why our instincts mislead. The interventions that feel powerful — changing a parameter (swap the leader, tweak a number, adjust a tax rate) — are usually the weakest. (Meadows 2008) The strongest leverage sits where we rarely look: in the interconnections, the goals, and the paradigm — the system’s purpose. Change the rules and the purpose and you change everything; change the people at the top and the structure quietly reasserts itself.

What this means for the quantitative researcher

Systems thinking does not contradict the causal toolkit — it frames it. Three consequences for applied work:

  • Policy resistance is predictable. A balancing loop will oppose your intervention; the system pushes back toward its prior state. An effect estimated in a trial can be neutralised at scale by feedback the trial never exercised — a structural reason external validity fails.
  • Goodhart’s law is a feedback loop. “When a measure becomes a target it ceases to be a good measure” is precisely a system responding to the stock you chose to optimise. Metric fixation is systems-blindness with a dashboard. (Meadows 2008)
  • Look for structure, not villains. When a result disappoints or an organisation misbehaves, the systems question — what structure makes this behaviour the natural output? — beats the search for someone to blame, and points at the high-leverage fix. This is the “feel for the whole” that no metric supplies. (Meadows 2008)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Treating a stock like a flow. A one-time payment (flow) doesn’t fix a depleted reserve (stock) that took years to drain; mind the difference, and the delays. (Meadows 2008)

  • Blaming the event, missing the structure. Reacting to each data point in isolation keeps you forever fire-fighting symptoms a feedback loop keeps regenerating. Climb to behaviour and structure. (Meadows 2008)

  • Pulling the obvious lever. Changing a parameter (the number, the person at the top) feels decisive and usually changes little; the goals and rules are where the leverage hides. (Meadows 2008)

  • Pushing leverage the wrong way. Even when you find the leverage point, intuition often has the sign backwards — e.g. relieving a pressure that was actually holding the system in check. Test the direction. (Meadows 2008)

  • Mistaking stated purpose for real purpose. Read behaviour, not rhetoric: “the purpose of a system is what it does.” A mission statement is not evidence. (Meadows 2008)

Appendix · the mathematics

The stock–flow integral. A stock is the accumulation of net flow over time: S(t)=S(0)+\int_0^t \big[\text{inflow}(\tau)-\text{outflow}(\tau)\big]\,d\tau, the continuous statement of “the level is the memory of past flows.” Its discrete cousin is S_{t}=S_{t-1}+(\text{in}-\text{out}) — the update rule in the figure.

Why the two loops give exponential vs goal-seeking curves. A reinforcing loop makes net flow proportional to the stock, \dot S = rS, whose solution S(t)=S_0 e^{rt} is exponential — runaway growth (or, with r<0, collapse). A balancing loop makes net flow proportional to the gap from a goal G, \dot S = k(G-S), whose solution S(t)=G-(G-S_0)e^{-kt} decays toward G and stays there — goal-seeking stability. Every system trajectory is a superposition of these two motifs, shaped by the delays that the stocks impose.

  backwards — e.g. relieving a pressure that was actually holding the system in check. Test the direction.
  [@meadows_systems]

- **Mistaking stated purpose for real purpose.** Read behaviour, not rhetoric: "the purpose of a system is
  what it does." A mission statement is not evidence. [@meadows_systems]

:::

## From the sources {.unnumbered}

::: {.callout-note collapse="true" title="Who says what"}
- **Meadows, *Thinking in Systems: A Primer*** — a system as **elements + interconnections + purpose**, with
  purpose "deduced from behavior, not rhetoric" and "the purpose of a system is what it does"; **stocks**
  (the "memory of the history of changing flows") and **flows**, stocks as buffers/delays that decouple
  inflow and outflow; **feedback loops** — **balancing** (goal-seeking: thermostat, cooling coffee) vs
  **reinforcing** (amplifying: interest, rabbits); **"the system causes its own behavior"** and the
  **events → behavior → structure** ladder; **bounded rationality** ("dutifully and rationally" → "a
  perfectly terrible result"); **leverage points**, the claim that people push them **in the wrong
  direction**, and the hierarchy in which **parameters are weak** and **goals/paradigms are strong**; and
  "systems fool us" / "you can't see the forest for the trees."
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The stock–flow integral.** A stock is the accumulation of net flow over time:
$$
S(t)=S(0)+\int_0^t \big[\text{inflow}(\tau)-\text{outflow}(\tau)\big]\,d\tau,
$$
the continuous statement of "the level is the memory of past flows." Its discrete cousin is
$S_{t}=S_{t-1}+(\text{in}-\text{out})$ — the update rule in the figure.

**Why the two loops give exponential vs goal-seeking curves.** A **reinforcing** loop makes net flow
proportional to the stock, $\dot S = rS$, whose solution $S(t)=S_0 e^{rt}$ is **exponential** — runaway
growth (or, with $r<0$, collapse). A **balancing** loop makes net flow proportional to the *gap* from a
goal $G$, $\dot S = k(G-S)$, whose solution $S(t)=G-(G-S_0)e^{-kt}$ **decays toward $G$** and stays there —
goal-seeking stability. Every system trajectory is a superposition of these two motifs, shaped by the
**delays** that the stocks impose.

:::
