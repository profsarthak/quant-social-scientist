Power Laws & Scaling

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Some quantities have no typical size. Cities, words, earthquakes, fortunes — most are tiny, a few are colossal, and there is no ‘average’ that means anything. The power law is the signature of a world without a characteristic scale.

Pick a person at random and you can guess their height to within a few inches — there is a typical human, and nobody is ten times the average. Now pick a city, a word, or a personal fortune at random: the “typical” value tells you almost nothing, because a handful of cases are thousands of times larger than the median. That is a power law — p(x)\propto x^{-\alpha} — and it describes a startling number of social and natural systems. Its defining feature is what it lacks: a characteristic scale. The bell curve has a centre of gravity; the power law has none.

Why it matters

If you assume a bell curve where a power law rules, you will be blindsided. Bell-curve thinking says extreme events are so rare they can be ignored — the hundred-year flood, the market crash, the runaway bestseller. Power-law thinking says the extremes are where the action is: they are rare, but not nearly rare enough to ignore, and they can dominate the total (Mitchell 2009). Wealth, city sizes, web traffic, word usage, earthquake energy, and the casualties of wars all live in this regime. Getting the shape of the tail wrong is the difference between a risk model that survives and one that detonates.

Two kinds of randomness: the bell curve vs the heavy tail

The normal (bell) distribution has a characteristic scale: values cluster around the mean, and the probability of an outlier dies off exponentially fast. There is a representative member — the “average” — and deviations more than a few standard deviations out are effectively impossible. Human height, measurement error, and sums of many small independent effects look like this (Mitchell 2009).

A power-law distribution is scale-free: there is no representative member (Barabási 2016). Zoom in on the small values or out to the large ones and the distribution looks the same — self-similar across scales. Its tail is heavy (or “fat”): extreme events, while still rare, are many orders of magnitude more likely than a bell curve would ever predict (Mitchell 2009). The practical upshot is unsettling: in a scale-free system with exponent 2<\alpha<3, the mean is finite but the variance diverges — fluctuations are so large that the average is “meaningless as a scale” (Barabási 2016).

What a power law is

A power law says the probability of an observation of size x falls off as a power of x:

p(x) \;\propto\; x^{-\alpha},

with \alpha the exponent (typically between 2 and 3 for the things social scientists study). Take logs of both sides and the magic appears:

\log p(x) \;=\; \text{const} - \alpha\,\log x .

On a log-log plot, a power law is a straight line with slope -\alpha (Mitchell 2009). That is the eyeball test, and the source of the term scaling: multiply x by a factor and p(x) changes by a fixed ratio, no matter where you started — the hallmark of having no special scale.

Where they show up

Power laws turn up across wildly different domains (Mitchell 2009; Barabási 2016):

  • Zipf’s law — the frequency of words in a language and the sizes of cities: rank a city r-th by population and its size is roughly proportional to 1/r.
  • The Pareto principle — wealth and income (the “80/20 rule”): a small fraction of people hold most of the wealth.
  • Earthquakes — the Gutenberg–Richter law relating magnitude to frequency.
  • Networks — the number of links to a web page or the connections of a hub (a scale-free network; see scale-free networks).
  • Catastrophes — the casualties of wars and the sizes of forest fires.
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)

def survival(x):
    """Empirical P(X > x): sort ascending, plot complementary rank."""
    xs = np.sort(x)
    n = xs.size
    ccdf = 1.0 - np.arange(1, n + 1) / n
    return xs, ccdf

# --- power-law sample via inverse transform: P(X>x)=(x/xmin)^-(a-1) ---
def sample_powerlaw(n, alpha, xmin=1.0):
    u = rng.random(n)
    return xmin * (1.0 - u) ** (-1.0 / (alpha - 1.0))

n = 20000
pl   = sample_powerlaw(n, alpha=2.5, xmin=1.0)
# normal with a matched median (~1), folded to positive support
nrm  = np.abs(rng.normal(1.0, 0.35, n))
logn = rng.lognormal(mean=0.0, sigma=1.3, size=n)   # heavy-ish but NOT power law

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.4, 4.3))

for ax in (axL, axR):
    ax.set_xscale("log"); ax.set_yscale("log")
    ax.set_xlabel("value  x"); ax.set_ylabel("P(X > x)")

xs, cc = survival(pl);  axL.plot(xs, cc, color="#1f4e79", lw=2, label="power law (α=2.5)")
xs, cc = survival(nrm); axL.plot(xs, cc, color="#888888", lw=2, label="normal (bell curve)")
axL.set_ylim(1e-4, 1.5); axL.set_xlim(0.2, 1e3)
axL.set_title("Heavy tail vs thin tail")
axL.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower left")
axL.text(20, 0.2, "extremes\nrare but\nnot negligible", color="#1f4e79", fontsize=8)

xs, cc = survival(pl);   axR.plot(xs, cc, color="#1f4e79", lw=2, label="true power law")
xs, cc = survival(logn); axR.plot(xs, cc, color="#d97706", lw=2, label="lognormal (impostor)")
axR.set_ylim(1e-4, 1.5); axR.set_xlim(0.2, 1e3)
axR.set_title("Why 'looks straight' isn't proof")
axR.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower left")

fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The over-claiming caution

Because a straight line on a log-log plot is so easy to want to see, power laws are among the most over-claimed patterns in science (Mitchell 2009). Many distributions that look like power laws over a decade or two are really lognormal, stretched-exponential, or some other heavy tail (Barabási 2016) — and a lognormal can masquerade as a straight line across a surprising range before it curves away (right panel above).

The discipline here is non-negotiable: a power law is a statistical hypothesis, not a doodle. Rigorous fitting and goodness-of-fit testing — the Clauset–Shalizi–Newman methodology of maximum-likelihood estimation of \alpha, a principled lower cutoff x_{\min}, and a likelihood-ratio test against alternatives like the lognormal — is what separates a real claim from wishful log-log eyeballing (Mitchell 2009). The mechanism matters too: a power law is interesting because it hints at an underlying generative process (preferential attachment, self-organised criticality), but only once the statistics actually hold up.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reporting a mean as if it’s typical. In a heavy-tailed sample the mean is dragged around by the largest few values and the variance may be effectively infinite; the median and the tail exponent describe the system, the mean does not. (Barabási 2016)
  • Bell-curve risk models. Assuming normal tails systematically under-prices catastrophe — the crash that “should happen once in millennia” arrives every decade. (Mitchell 2009)
  • Declaring a power law from a straight-ish line. Two or three decades that look linear are not proof; fit it properly and test against a lognormal before you claim it. (Mitchell 2009)
  • Confusing scale-free with structureless. “No characteristic scale” does not mean “no structure” — it means the same structure repeats at every scale. (Barabási 2016)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Mitchell, Complexity: A Guided Tour — power laws as scale-free, heavy-tailed distributions p(x)\propto x^{-\alpha}; the contrast with the bell curve’s characteristic scale; the log-log straight line; the canonical examples (Zipf, Pareto, earthquakes, web links); and the strong over-claiming caution with the Clauset–Shalizi–Newman testing discipline.
  • Barabási, Network Science — the scale-free property and the divergence of the variance for 2<\alpha<3 (“the mean is meaningless as a scale”); power-law degree distributions in real networks; and the warning that many apparent power laws are lognormal.

Appendix · the mathematics

Normalisation and the cutoff. A power law cannot hold all the way to x=0 (the density would not integrate). One writes it for x\ge x_{\min}: p(x)=\frac{\alpha-1}{x_{\min}}\left(\frac{x}{x_{\min}}\right)^{-\alpha},\qquad x\ge x_{\min}, which integrates to 1 for \alpha>1. The survival function is the clean object to plot: P(X>x)=\left(\frac{x}{x_{\min}}\right)^{-(\alpha-1)}, a straight line of slope -(\alpha-1) on log-log axes — which is exactly the inverse-transform used to generate the sample in the figure: x=x_{\min}(1-u)^{-1/(\alpha-1)} for u\sim\text{Uniform}(0,1).

Divergent moments. The k-th moment is \langle x^k\rangle\propto\int_{x_{\min}}^{\infty} x^{k-\alpha}\,dx, which diverges unless \alpha>k+1. So the mean is finite only for \alpha>2, and the variance only for \alpha>3. In the common social-science regime 2<\alpha<3 the mean exists but the variance is infinite — formal justification for distrusting the average.

ML estimation (Clauset–Shalizi–Newman). For continuous data above x_{\min}, the maximum-likelihood exponent is \hat\alpha = 1 + n\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln\frac{x_i}{x_{\min}}\right]^{-1}, with x_{\min} chosen to minimise the Kolmogorov–Smirnov distance between data and fit, and the whole power-law hypothesis then tested against alternatives (e.g. lognormal) by a likelihood-ratio test. A small slope estimate alone proves nothing.

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Normalisation and the cutoff.** A power law cannot hold all the way to $x=0$ (the density would not integrate). One writes it for $x\ge x_{\min}$:
$$
p(x)=\frac{\alpha-1}{x_{\min}}\left(\frac{x}{x_{\min}}\right)^{-\alpha},\qquad x\ge x_{\min},
$$
which integrates to 1 for $\alpha>1$. The **survival function** is the clean object to plot:
$$
P(X>x)=\left(\frac{x}{x_{\min}}\right)^{-(\alpha-1)},
$$
a straight line of slope $-(\alpha-1)$ on log-log axes — which is exactly the inverse-transform used to generate the sample in the figure: $x=x_{\min}(1-u)^{-1/(\alpha-1)}$ for $u\sim\text{Uniform}(0,1)$.

**Divergent moments.** The $k$-th moment is $\langle x^k\rangle\propto\int_{x_{\min}}^{\infty} x^{k-\alpha}\,dx$, which **diverges** unless $\alpha>k+1$. So the mean is finite only for $\alpha>2$, and the variance only for $\alpha>3$. In the common social-science regime $2<\alpha<3$ the mean exists but the variance is infinite — formal justification for distrusting the average.

**ML estimation (Clauset–Shalizi–Newman).** For continuous data above $x_{\min}$, the maximum-likelihood exponent is
$$
\hat\alpha = 1 + n\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ln\frac{x_i}{x_{\min}}\right]^{-1},
$$
with $x_{\min}$ chosen to minimise the Kolmogorov–Smirnov distance between data and fit, and the whole power-law hypothesis then tested against alternatives (e.g. lognormal) by a likelihood-ratio test. A small slope estimate alone proves nothing.
