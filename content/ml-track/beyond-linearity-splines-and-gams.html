Beyond Linearity: Splines & GAMs

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Bend the regression line into any smooth shape — without surrendering the one thing that makes a model useful to a social scientist: the ability to read it.

Linear regression assumes a straight line; the world is rarely straight. The flexible methods of this entry — splines, local regression, and generalized additive models — let each predictor’s effect curve freely, while keeping the model interpretable: you can still plot what each variable does, holding the others fixed. This is the sweet spot between a rigid line and a black-box forest — flexibility you can read.

Why it matters

Most flexible ML buys accuracy by becoming opaque. Splines and GAMs are the exception: they capture genuine non-linearity and stay legible, which is exactly what a social scientist needs when the shape of an effect is the finding (wages rise with age, then fall). (James et al. 2021) They also generalise the ad-hoc tricks of functional form — logs, quadratics, dummies — into a principled toolkit for learning the shape from data.

The simple extensions, and why they fall short

Two first attempts, both limited: (James et al. 2021)

  • Polynomial regression adds powers (\text{age},\text{age}^2,\text{age}^3,\dots). Easy, but non-local: a wiggle in one region flexes the whole curve, and high-degree polynomials go wild at the boundaries, producing absurd tail predictions. (James et al. 2021)
  • Step functions bin X into regions and fit a constant in each (dummy variables). Simple, but unless the breakpoints are natural they miss trends within a bin — a single “age 20–40” bin hides the steady wage rise inside it. (James et al. 2021)

Splines fix both by being piecewise and smooth.

Regression splines

A regression spline fits separate low-degree polynomials over regions divided by knots, stitched together with smoothness constraints. (James et al. 2021)

  • A cubic spline is piecewise cubic, constrained so the curve and its first and second derivatives are continuous at every knot — no kinks, no jumps, visually seamless. (James et al. 2021)
  • Flexibility = knots. More knots ⇒ more wiggle. A cubic spline with K knots uses K+4 degrees of freedom. (James et al. 2021)
  • Natural splines fix the boundary problem: ordinary splines have high variance at the tails, so a natural spline forces the curve to be linear beyond the boundary knots, giving stable, trustworthy tail estimates. (James et al. 2021)
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
n = 120
x = np.sort(rng.uniform(0, 1, n))
f_true = lambda t: np.sin(2.2 * np.pi * t) * (t < 0.7) + (0.5 - 0.8 * (t - 0.7)) * (t >= 0.7)
y = f_true(x) + rng.normal(0, 0.25, n)

# Natural cubic spline via a truncated-power basis with linear tail constraints.
knots = np.quantile(x, [0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8])
def trunc(t, k):
    d = (t - k); return np.where(d > 0, d**3, 0.0)
def nat_basis(t):
    # natural cubic spline basis (Hastie et al. form): linear + truncated-cubic differences
    K = knots; last = K[-1]
    cols = [np.ones_like(t), t]
    dK = trunc(t, K[-2]) - trunc(t, last)
    for k in K[:-1]:
        cols.append((trunc(t, k) - trunc(t, last)) / (last - k) - dK / (last - K[-2]))
    return np.column_stack(cols)

B = nat_basis(x); coef, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(B, y, rcond=None)
xs = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
spline = nat_basis(xs) @ coef
poly = np.polyval(np.polyfit(x, y, 10), xs)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.scatter(x, y, s=10, color="#c9ccd1", alpha=0.6, label="data")
ax.plot(xs, f_true(xs), color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.0, ls="--", label="true function")
ax.plot(xs, poly, color="#d98a29", lw=1.8, label="degree-10 polynomial")
ax.plot(xs, spline, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.4, label="natural cubic spline")
for k in knots: ax.axvline(k, color="#9aa0a6", ls=":", lw=0.8)
ax.set_ylim(-1.8, 1.8)
ax.set_xlabel("x"); ax.set_ylabel("y")
ax.set_title("Splines bend smoothly; polynomials thrash at the edges")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Smoothing splines

Regression splines make you choose knots. Smoothing splines sidestep that with a “loss + penalty” formulation — fit every point but penalise wiggliness: (James et al. 2021) \min_g \ \sum_{i=1}^n \big(y_i-g(x_i)\big)^2 \;+\; \lambda \int g''(t)^2\,dt . The roughness penalty \int g''(t)^2 dt measures total curvature; \lambda dials the trade-off between fitting the data and staying smooth. (James et al. 2021) As \lambda runs from 0 to \infty, the effective degrees of freedom fall from n (interpolate every point) to 2 (a straight line) — so \lambda is a continuous flexibility knob. It’s chosen by leave-one-out cross-validation, which (as in the CV entry) has an efficient single-pass formula. (James et al. 2021)

Local regression (LOESS)

Local regression fits the curve at a target point x_0 using only nearby observations, weighted by distance — closest points count most. (James et al. 2021) The key knob is the span s, the fraction of data in each local neighbourhood: a small span gives a wiggly, very local fit; a large span approaches a global line. (James et al. 2021) It’s the KNN idea applied to regression — smooth, intuitive, and a natural way to let the data speak locally.

Generalized additive models (GAMs)

The payoff: extend all of this to many predictors while keeping interpretability, via a generalized additive model: (James et al. 2021) y_i=\beta_0+f_1(x_{i1})+f_2(x_{i2})+\dots+f_p(x_{ip})+\epsilon_i , each f_j a smooth function (spline, LOESS) learned from data. The magic is additivity: because the effects add, you can examine each f_j separately — plot the (non-linear) effect of one predictor holding the others fixed, exactly the ceteris-paribus reading of a regression coefficient, now curved. (James et al. 2021) GAMs handle classification too, via a logit link (e.g. \Pr(\text{wage}>250\text{k})). (James et al. 2021)

The cost of additivity is its blind spot: a GAM misses interactions between predictors unless you add them by hand (an x_j\times x_k term or a bivariate smooth). (James et al. 2021) It sits one notch below trees/forests in flexibility (which find interactions automatically) and well above them in interpretability — the GAM’s reason for being.

NoteISL’s Wage GAM

Predicting wages from year, age, education: a smoothing spline for year (4 df), a smoothing spline for age (5 df), and a step function for the categorical education. The fitted effects read like a story: wage rises with age until ~60, then declines; rises slowly but steadily across 2003–2009; and climbs with education. (James et al. 2021) Three legible curves — non-linear, yet as interpretable as a table of coefficients.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • High-degree global polynomials. They thrash at the boundaries; prefer splines (and natural splines for stable tails). (James et al. 2021)

  • Too many knots / too small \lambda or span. Over-flexible fits chase noise; tune flexibility (df, \lambda, span) by cross-validation. (James et al. 2021)

  • Forgetting GAM additivity. No interactions unless you add them; if effects depend on combinations, a GAM will quietly miss them. (James et al. 2021)

  • Reading a smooth effect as causal. A GAM’s f_j is a flexible conditional shape — still a correlation with controls, not an effect. (James et al. 2021)

  • Over-interpreting wiggles at the data’s edges. Sparse-tail regions have high variance; natural splines and honest uncertainty bands matter most there. (James et al. 2021)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • James, Witten, Hastie & Tibshirani, ISL (Ch 7) — polynomial regression (non-local, wild boundaries) and step functions (binning); regression splines (knots; cubic splines with continuous first/second derivatives; natural splines linear beyond boundary knots; K knots =K+4 df); smoothing splines (RSS +\lambda\int g''^2, effective df from n to 2, LOOCV); local regression / LOESS (distance weights, the span); GAMs (y=\beta_0+\sum_j f_j(x_j), additive interpretability, logit for classification, missing interactions); the Wage GAM (year 4 df, age 5 df, education step function; wage peaks near 60).

Appendix · the mathematics

Truncated-power spline basis. A cubic spline with knots \xi_1,\dots,\xi_K can be written as a linear model in the basis \{1,x,x^2,x^3,(x-\xi_1)_+^3,\dots,(x-\xi_K)_+^3\} where (t)_+=\max(t,0). Each truncated term adds curvature only past its knot, which is what makes the fit piecewise while continuity of g,g',g'' is automatic (the cubic truncation has two vanishing derivatives at the knot). Fitting is then ordinary least squares on these K+4 columns — flexibility with the linear model’s machinery intact.

The smoothing-spline solution. Remarkably, the minimiser of \sum_i (y_i-g(x_i))^2+\lambda\int g''(t)^2dt over all functions is a natural cubic spline with knots at every unique x_i — an n-dimensional problem with a finite, closed-form answer. The fit is linear in y: \hat g=S_\lambda y for a smoother matrix S_\lambda, and the effective degrees of freedom are \operatorname{trace}(S_\lambda), decreasing in \lambda. LOOCV has the leverage shortcut \text{CV}=\frac1n\sum_i\big(\frac{y_i-\hat g(x_i)}{1-S_{\lambda,ii}}\big)^2 — the same leverage-corrected residual as for linear models.

GAM fitting (backfitting). With \hat f_j smooth, GAMs are fit by backfitting: cycle through predictors, each time smoothing the partial residual y-\beta_0-\sum_{k\ne j}\hat f_k(x_k) against x_j to update \hat f_j, until convergence — a coordinate-descent that reduces a p-dimensional smooth to a sequence of one-dimensional ones.

Connections

  • Builds on: functional form (logs/quadratics generalised), bias–variance (df/\lambda/span as flexibility), and cross-validation (tuning).
  • Leads to: support vector machines (non-linearity via kernels) and neural networks (non-linearity via learned features) — the same goal by other means, trading away the GAM’s legibility.
  • Bridges back: a flexible f_j is still descriptive; the shape is a conditional association, not a cause.

## Smoothing splines

Regression splines make you *choose* knots. **Smoothing splines** sidestep that with a **"loss +
penalty"** formulation — fit *every* point but penalise wiggliness: [@islr]
$$
\min_g \ \sum_{i=1}^n \big(y_i-g(x_i)\big)^2 \;+\; \lambda \int g''(t)^2\,dt .
$$
The **roughness penalty** $\int g''(t)^2 dt$ measures total curvature; $\lambda$ dials the trade-off
between fitting the data and staying smooth. [@islr] As $\lambda$ runs from $0$ to $\infty$, the
**effective degrees of freedom** fall from $n$ (interpolate every point) to $2$ (a straight line) — so
$\lambda$ is a continuous [flexibility knob](../04-predictive/bias-variance-tradeoff.qmd). It's chosen by
**[leave-one-out cross-validation](../04-predictive/cross-validation.qmd)**, which (as in the
[CV entry](../04-predictive/cross-validation.qmd)) has an efficient single-pass formula. [@islr]

## Local regression (LOESS)

**Local regression** fits the curve at a target point $x_0$ using only **nearby** observations, weighted
by distance — closest points count most. [@islr] The key knob is the **span** $s$, the fraction of data
in each local neighbourhood: a **small span** gives a wiggly, very local fit; a **large span** approaches
a global line. [@islr] It's the [KNN](../04-predictive/classification-and-knn.qmd) idea applied to
regression — smooth, intuitive, and a natural way to let the data speak locally.

## Generalized additive models (GAMs)

The payoff: extend all of this to **many predictors** while keeping interpretability, via a
**generalized additive model**: [@islr]
$$
y_i=\beta_0+f_1(x_{i1})+f_2(x_{i2})+\dots+f_p(x_{ip})+\epsilon_i ,
$$
each $f_j$ a smooth function (spline, LOESS) learned from data. The magic is **additivity**: because the
effects *add*, you can **examine each $f_j$ separately** — plot the (non-linear) effect of one predictor
*holding the others fixed*, exactly the [ceteris-paribus](../03-regression/multiple-regression.qmd)
reading of a regression coefficient, now curved. [@islr] GAMs handle **classification** too, via a
[logit link](../03-regression/limited-dependent-variables.qmd) (e.g. $\Pr(\text{wage}>250\text{k})$).
[@islr]

The cost of additivity is its blind spot: a GAM **misses interactions** between predictors unless you
add them by hand (an $x_j\times x_k$ term or a bivariate smooth). [@islr] It sits one notch below
[trees/forests](../04-predictive/trees-and-forests.qmd) in flexibility (which find interactions
automatically) and well above them in interpretability — the GAM's reason for being.

spline for **age** (5 df), and a **step function** for the categorical **education**. The fitted effects
read like a story: wage **rises with age until ~60, then declines**; rises **slowly but steadily** across
2003–2009; and **climbs with education**. [@islr] Three legible curves — non-linear, yet as interpretable
as a table of coefficients.
:::

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **High-degree global polynomials.** They thrash at the boundaries; prefer splines (and *natural*
  splines for stable tails). [@islr]

- **Too many knots / too small $\lambda$ or span.** Over-flexible fits chase noise; tune flexibility
  (df, $\lambda$, span) by [cross-validation](../04-predictive/cross-validation.qmd). [@islr]

- **Forgetting GAM additivity.** No interactions unless you add them; if effects depend on combinations,
  a GAM will quietly miss them. [@islr]

- **Reading a smooth effect as causal.** A GAM's $f_j$ is a flexible *conditional* shape — still a
  [correlation with controls](../03-regression/regression-and-causality.qmd), not an effect. [@islr]

- **Over-interpreting wiggles at the data's edges.** Sparse-tail regions have high variance; natural
  splines and honest uncertainty bands matter most there. [@islr]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Truncated-power spline basis.** A cubic spline with knots $\xi_1,\dots,\xi_K$ can be written as a
linear model in the basis $\{1,x,x^2,x^3,(x-\xi_1)_+^3,\dots,(x-\xi_K)_+^3\}$ where
$(t)_+=\max(t,0)$. Each truncated term adds curvature *only past its knot*, which is what makes the fit
**piecewise** while continuity of $g,g',g''$ is automatic (the cubic truncation has two vanishing
derivatives at the knot). Fitting is then ordinary least squares on these $K+4$ columns — flexibility
with the linear model's machinery intact.

**The smoothing-spline solution.** Remarkably, the minimiser of
$\sum_i (y_i-g(x_i))^2+\lambda\int g''(t)^2dt$ over *all* functions is a **natural cubic spline with knots
at every unique $x_i$** — an $n$-dimensional problem with a finite, closed-form answer. The fit is linear
in $y$: $\hat g=S_\lambda y$ for a smoother matrix $S_\lambda$, and the **effective degrees of freedom**
are $\operatorname{trace}(S_\lambda)$, decreasing in $\lambda$. LOOCV has the leverage shortcut
$\text{CV}=\frac1n\sum_i\big(\frac{y_i-\hat g(x_i)}{1-S_{\lambda,ii}}\big)^2$ — the same
[leverage-corrected residual](../04-predictive/cross-validation.qmd) as for linear models.

**GAM fitting (backfitting).** With $\hat f_j$ smooth, GAMs are fit by **backfitting**: cycle through
predictors, each time smoothing the **partial residual** $y-\beta_0-\sum_{k\ne j}\hat f_k(x_k)$ against
$x_j$ to update $\hat f_j$, until convergence — a coordinate-descent that reduces a $p$-dimensional smooth
to a sequence of one-dimensional ones.

:::
