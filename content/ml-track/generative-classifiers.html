Generative Classifiers: LDA, QDA & Naive Bayes

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Instead of drawing a boundary directly, model what each class looks like — then let Bayes’ theorem turn those pictures into predictions.

Logistic regression draws the boundary between classes directly. The generative classifiers take the opposite route: model the distribution of the predictors within each class — what a defaulter’s finances look like, what a non-defaulter’s look like — attach the class priors, and invert with Bayes’ theorem to get the probability of each class. The payoff is stability where logistic regression wobbles: well-separated classes, small samples, and many classes. LDA, QDA, and Naive Bayes are three points on this generative spectrum.

Why it matters

Classification so far meant logistic regression and KNN. The generative classifiers complete the toolkit — and they’re not merely historical: LDA is often more stable than logistic regression exactly where the latter fails, and Naive Bayes thrives in the high-dimensional, p>n settings (text, genomics) where modelling a full covariance is hopeless. (James et al. 2021) They also make the Bayes classifier concrete: each is an attempt to approximate that gold standard by plugging estimates into Bayes’ theorem.

Generative vs discriminative

The fork in the road: (James et al. 2021)

  • Discriminative (logistic regression) models \Pr(Y=k\mid X) directly — straight to the boundary.
  • Generative (LDA/QDA/Naive Bayes) models the density of X within each class, f_k(x)=\Pr(X=x\mid Y=k), together with the priors \pi_k (each class’s overall share), then uses Bayes’ theorem to flip them into the posterior: \Pr(Y=k\mid X=x)=\frac{\pi_k\,f_k(x)}{\sum_{l=1}^K \pi_l\,f_l(x)} .

It is called generative because, knowing each f_k, you could in principle generate synthetic examples of each class. (James et al. 2021) The three methods differ only in how they model f_k.

The probabilistic perspective

This fork is the doorway to Kevin Murphy’s larger thesis in Machine Learning: A Probabilistic Perspective: that machine learning is best seen not as a “cookbook” of ad-hoc recipes but as “a unified view of the field through the lens of probabilistic modeling and inference.” (Murphy 2012) Every method in this track is, underneath, a way of estimating a probability — supervised learning is conditional density estimation, p(y\mid x). (Murphy 2012) Seen this way, the generative/discriminative split is a genuine modelling choice with real trade-offs Murphy spells out: (Murphy 2012)

  • Generative models specify how the data were made — the joint p(x,y) via class-conditionals p(x\mid y) and priors p(y). They are often easy to fit (counting and averaging), and — their signature advantage — they handle missing features (marginalise them out) and unlabelled data (semi-supervised learning), because they model x itself. (Murphy 2012)
  • Discriminative models fit the posterior p(y\mid x) directly. Murphy’s verdict: they are usually more accurate, because their “job” is easier — they never have to model the complicated distribution of the features x — and they permit arbitrary feature preprocessing (basis expansions). (Murphy 2012)

The same lens reframes regularization: a MAP estimate is just penalised MLE, with the log-prior acting as the penalty, and plain MLE’s overfitting shows up as the “zero-count problem” — an unseen category assigned probability zero. (Murphy 2012) And it gives a principled Occam’s razor: a complex model must “spread its probability mass thinly” over the many datasets it could explain, so a simpler model that fits is preferred automatically — Bayesian model selection. (Murphy 2012) Two of Murphy’s borrowed maxims sit behind the whole track: “there is no universally best model” (the No Free Lunch theorem) and George Box’s “all models are wrong, but some models are useful.” (Murphy 2012)

Linear discriminant analysis (LDA)

The oldest statistical classifier (R. A. Fisher, 1936). (James et al. 2021) LDA assumes that within each class the predictors are multivariate normal, N(\mu_k,\Sigma), with class-specific means \mu_k but — the key restriction — a common covariance matrix \Sigma shared across all classes. (James et al. 2021) Plugging Gaussian densities into Bayes’ theorem and taking logs, an observation is assigned to the class with the largest discriminant function: \delta_k(x)=x^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k-\tfrac12\mu_k^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k+\log\pi_k . Because x enters only linearly, the boundaries \{\delta_k=\delta_l\} are lines (or hyperplanes) — hence linear discriminant analysis. (James et al. 2021) With the true parameters this is the Bayes classifier; LDA approximates it by substituting sample means, priors, and a pooled covariance. (James et al. 2021)

Quadratic discriminant analysis (QDA)

QDA relaxes the one strong assumption: let each class have its own covariance \Sigma_k. (James et al. 2021) Now x enters the discriminant quadratically, so the boundaries curve — QDA can carve shapes LDA cannot. The cost is a classic bias–variance trade: (James et al. 2021)

  • LDA estimates far fewer parameters (one shared \Sigma) → lower variance, higher bias if the covariances really differ. Best for small training sets.
  • QDA estimates a separate covariance per class — K\frac{p(p+1)}{2} parameters → lower bias, higher variance. Worth it only when the training set is large (or the classes’ shapes genuinely differ). (James et al. 2021)

The figure shows the difference directly: a straight LDA boundary vs a curved QDA one on classes with unequal spreads.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(2)
# Class 0: compact; class 1: elongated and rotated — different covariances.
mu0 = np.array([-1.2, 0.0]); C0 = np.array([[0.5, 0.0], [0.0, 0.5]])
mu1 = np.array([1.4, 0.6]);  C1 = np.array([[1.6, 0.9], [0.9, 0.8]])
n = 250
X0 = rng.multivariate_normal(mu0, C0, n); X1 = rng.multivariate_normal(mu1, C1, n)

def gauss_loglik(x, mu, C):
    Ci = np.linalg.inv(C); d = x - mu
    return -0.5 * np.einsum("...i,ij,...j->...", d, Ci, d) - 0.5 * np.log(np.linalg.det(C))

# estimates
m0, m1 = X0.mean(0), X1.mean(0)
S0, S1 = np.cov(X0.T), np.cov(X1.T)
Spool = 0.5 * (S0 + S1)                     # LDA: shared covariance
gx, gy = np.meshgrid(np.linspace(-4, 5, 300), np.linspace(-3.5, 4, 300))
grid = np.c_[gx.ravel(), gy.ravel()]
lda = (gauss_loglik(grid, m1, Spool) - gauss_loglik(grid, m0, Spool)).reshape(gx.shape)
qda = (gauss_loglik(grid, m1, S1) - gauss_loglik(grid, m0, S0)).reshape(gx.shape)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.4))
ax.scatter(X0[:, 0], X0[:, 1], s=10, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.5, label="class 0")
ax.scatter(X1[:, 0], X1[:, 1], s=10, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.5, label="class 1")
ax.contour(gx, gy, lda, levels=[0], colors="#c0392b", linewidths=2.4)
ax.contour(gx, gy, qda, levels=[0], colors="#1f6fb2", linewidths=2.4, linestyles="--")
ax.plot([], [], color="#c0392b", lw=2.4, label="LDA boundary (linear)")
ax.plot([], [], color="#1f6fb2", lw=2.4, ls="--", label="QDA boundary (quadratic)")
ax.set_xlabel("x₁"); ax.set_ylabel("x₂")
ax.set_title("Shared vs class-specific covariance")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Naive Bayes

Naive Bayes simplifies the generative problem to the extreme: assume the predictors are conditionally independent within each class, so the joint density factorises into a product of one-dimensional densities: (James et al. 2021) f_k(x)=f_{k1}(x_1)\times f_{k2}(x_2)\times\cdots\times f_{kp}(x_p) . The independence assumption is almost always false — income and education are correlated among voters — yet Naive Bayes works remarkably well in practice, because the drastic parameter reduction slashes variance. (James et al. 2021) You never estimate a p\times p covariance (impossible when p>n); you estimate p separate univariate densities. That is why Naive Bayes is a staple of text classification and other high-dimensional tasks. (James et al. 2021)

When to prefer a generative classifier

LDA especially earns its place over logistic regression in three situations: (James et al. 2021)

  • Well-separated classes. When the classes barely overlap, logistic regression’s coefficients become unstable (they run off to infinity); LDA stays well-behaved.
  • Small n with roughly normal predictors. LDA’s stronger assumptions buy stability and accuracy when data is scarce and approximately Gaussian.
  • More than two classes. LDA handles K>2 naturally and efficiently, where multinomial logistic regression is less commonly used. (James et al. 2021)
NoteISL’s classifiers in action
  • Default (predict credit-card default from balance and student status). At the default 0.5 threshold, LDA classified 99.8% of non-defaulters correctly but missed 75.7% of actual defaulters — a vivid reminder (from classification) that on imbalanced data you must move the threshold to the cost of the errors you care about. (James et al. 2021)
  • Smarket (predict the market Up/Down). Here QDA won — ~60% held-out accuracy — its quadratic flexibility capturing structure the linear methods missed. (James et al. 2021)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • QDA on small data. Its per-class covariances are variance-prone; prefer LDA unless n is large or the class shapes clearly differ. (James et al. 2021)

  • Trusting the normality assumption blindly. LDA/QDA assume Gaussian predictors; badly non-normal or categorical features may favour Naive Bayes or logistic regression. (James et al. 2021)

  • Reading Naive Bayes’ independence as literal truth. It’s a deliberately wrong, useful simplification — fine for prediction, not a claim about the world. (James et al. 2021)

  • Default 0.5 threshold on imbalanced classes. As the Default example shows, it misses the rare class; tune the threshold to the costs. (James et al. 2021)

  • Treating a discriminant coefficient as a causal effect. These are predictive classifiers; the weights are not effects. (James et al. 2021)

Appendix · the mathematics

From Gaussian density to the LDA discriminant. With f_k(x)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp(-\tfrac12(x-\mu_k)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_k)) and a shared \Sigma, take \log[\pi_k f_k(x)] and drop terms common to all classes (the x^\top\Sigma^{-1}x term cancels because \Sigma is shared): \delta_k(x)=x^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k-\tfrac12\mu_k^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k+\log\pi_k , linear in x. For QDA, \Sigma_k differs by class, so the x^\top\Sigma_k^{-1}x term survives, leaving a quadratic discriminant — the source of curved boundaries.

Parameter counts (the bias–variance ledger). LDA estimates K mean vectors plus one shared \Sigma (\tfrac{p(p+1)}2 entries); QDA estimates K separate \Sigma_k, i.e. K\cdot\tfrac{p(p+1)}2 — growing fast in p, hence QDA’s variance and its need for large n.

Naive Bayes as diagonal QDA. Assuming within-class independence makes each class covariance diagonal, \Sigma_k=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_{k1}^2,\dots,\sigma_{kp}^2), so f_k factorises into univariate densities and the parameter count drops from O(p^2) to O(p) per class — the reason it survives high dimensions where full LDA/QDA cannot.

Connections

  • Builds on: Bayes’ theorem (the inversion at its heart), logistic regression (the discriminative alternative), classification & the Bayes classifier (what these approximate, and the threshold lesson), and common distributions (the Gaussian assumption).

```{python}
#| label: fig-lda-qda
#| fig-cap: "LDA vs QDA. Two classes with different covariance shapes (green is tighter, navy is elongated). LDA (red) assumes a shared covariance and can only draw a straight boundary — it misreads the differing spreads. QDA (blue) estimates each class's covariance and bends the boundary into a curve that tracks the real shapes. LDA is steadier on small data; QDA is more accurate when the shapes truly differ and n is large."
#| code-fold: true
#| code-summary: "Show the figure code"

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(2)
# Class 0: compact; class 1: elongated and rotated — different covariances.
mu0 = np.array([-1.2, 0.0]); C0 = np.array([[0.5, 0.0], [0.0, 0.5]])
mu1 = np.array([1.4, 0.6]);  C1 = np.array([[1.6, 0.9], [0.9, 0.8]])
n = 250
X0 = rng.multivariate_normal(mu0, C0, n); X1 = rng.multivariate_normal(mu1, C1, n)

def gauss_loglik(x, mu, C):
    Ci = np.linalg.inv(C); d = x - mu
    return -0.5 * np.einsum("...i,ij,...j->...", d, Ci, d) - 0.5 * np.log(np.linalg.det(C))

# estimates
m0, m1 = X0.mean(0), X1.mean(0)
S0, S1 = np.cov(X0.T), np.cov(X1.T)
Spool = 0.5 * (S0 + S1)                     # LDA: shared covariance
gx, gy = np.meshgrid(np.linspace(-4, 5, 300), np.linspace(-3.5, 4, 300))
grid = np.c_[gx.ravel(), gy.ravel()]
lda = (gauss_loglik(grid, m1, Spool) - gauss_loglik(grid, m0, Spool)).reshape(gx.shape)
qda = (gauss_loglik(grid, m1, S1) - gauss_loglik(grid, m0, S0)).reshape(gx.shape)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.4))
ax.scatter(X0[:, 0], X0[:, 1], s=10, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.5, label="class 0")
ax.scatter(X1[:, 0], X1[:, 1], s=10, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.5, label="class 1")
ax.contour(gx, gy, lda, levels=[0], colors="#c0392b", linewidths=2.4)
ax.contour(gx, gy, qda, levels=[0], colors="#1f6fb2", linewidths=2.4, linestyles="--")
ax.plot([], [], color="#c0392b", lw=2.4, label="LDA boundary (linear)")
ax.plot([], [], color="#1f6fb2", lw=2.4, ls="--", label="QDA boundary (quadratic)")
ax.set_xlabel("x₁"); ax.set_ylabel("x₂")
ax.set_title("Shared vs class-specific covariance")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Naive Bayes

**Naive Bayes** simplifies the generative problem to the extreme: assume the predictors are
**conditionally independent within each class**, so the joint density **factorises** into a product of
one-dimensional densities: [@islr]
$$
f_k(x)=f_{k1}(x_1)\times f_{k2}(x_2)\times\cdots\times f_{kp}(x_p) .
$$
The independence assumption is almost always **false** — income and education are correlated among
voters — yet Naive Bayes works **remarkably well in practice**, because the drastic parameter reduction
slashes variance. [@islr] You never estimate a $p\times p$ covariance (impossible when
[$p>n$](model-selection-and-dimension-reduction.qmd)); you estimate $p$ separate univariate densities.
That is why Naive Bayes is a staple of **text classification** and other high-dimensional tasks. [@islr]

## When to prefer a generative classifier

LDA especially earns its place over [logistic regression](../03-regression/limited-dependent-variables.qmd)
in three situations: [@islr]

- **Well-separated classes.** When the classes barely overlap, logistic regression's coefficients become
  **unstable** (they run off to infinity); LDA stays well-behaved.
- **Small $n$ with roughly normal predictors.** LDA's stronger assumptions buy **stability and
  accuracy** when data is scarce and approximately Gaussian.
- **More than two classes.** LDA handles $K>2$ naturally and efficiently, where multinomial logistic
  regression is less commonly used. [@islr]

::: {.callout-note title="ISL's classifiers in action"}
- **Default** (predict credit-card default from balance and student status). At the default **0.5
  threshold**, LDA classified **99.8% of non-defaulters** correctly but **missed 75.7% of actual
  defaulters** — a vivid reminder (from [classification](../04-predictive/classification-and-knn.qmd))
  that on **imbalanced** data you must **move the threshold** to the cost of the errors you care about.
  [@islr]
- **Smarket** (predict the market Up/Down). Here **QDA won** — ~**60%** held-out accuracy — its quadratic
  flexibility capturing structure the linear methods missed. [@islr]
:::

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **QDA on small data.** Its per-class covariances are variance-prone; prefer LDA unless $n$ is large or
  the class shapes clearly differ. [@islr]

- **Trusting the normality assumption blindly.** LDA/QDA assume Gaussian predictors; badly non-normal or
  categorical features may favour Naive Bayes or logistic regression. [@islr]

- **Reading Naive Bayes' independence as literal truth.** It's a deliberately wrong, useful
  simplification — fine for prediction, not a claim about the world. [@islr]

- **Default 0.5 threshold on imbalanced classes.** As the Default example shows, it misses the rare
  class; tune the [threshold](../04-predictive/classification-and-knn.qmd) to the costs. [@islr]

- **Treating a discriminant coefficient as a causal effect.** These are
  [predictive](../04-predictive/explaining-vs-predicting.qmd) classifiers; the weights are not effects.
  [@islr]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**From Gaussian density to the LDA discriminant.** With $f_k(x)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}
\exp(-\tfrac12(x-\mu_k)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_k))$ and a *shared* $\Sigma$, take $\log[\pi_k f_k(x)]$ and
drop terms common to all classes (the $x^\top\Sigma^{-1}x$ term cancels because $\Sigma$ is shared):
$$
\delta_k(x)=x^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k-\tfrac12\mu_k^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k+\log\pi_k ,
$$
**linear in $x$**. For QDA, $\Sigma_k$ differs by class, so the $x^\top\Sigma_k^{-1}x$ term **survives**,
leaving a **quadratic** discriminant — the source of curved boundaries.

**Parameter counts (the bias–variance ledger).** LDA estimates $K$ mean vectors plus **one** shared
$\Sigma$ ($\tfrac{p(p+1)}2$ entries); QDA estimates $K$ separate $\Sigma_k$, i.e. $K\cdot\tfrac{p(p+1)}2$
— growing fast in $p$, hence QDA's variance and its need for large $n$.

**Naive Bayes as diagonal QDA.** Assuming within-class independence makes each class covariance
**diagonal**, $\Sigma_k=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_{k1}^2,\dots,\sigma_{kp}^2)$, so $f_k$ factorises into
univariate densities and the parameter count drops from $O(p^2)$ to $O(p)$ per class — the reason it
survives high dimensions where full LDA/QDA cannot.

:::
