ML for Social Science: Workflow & the Bridge Home

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

How to actually use this toolkit on real social data — and the one discipline that keeps it honest: a prediction is not an explanation, and an important feature is not a cause.

You now have the full predictive toolkit. This closing entry is about using it well as a social scientist — the disciplined workflow, the awkward truth that social data is usually small and noisy, the genuine jobs ML does superbly, and the boundary it must never cross. The whole track converges on one sentence: machine learning answers “what is Y given X?”, not “what happens to Y if I change D?” Keep those questions apart and ML is a powerful ally; conflate them and a model that predicts beautifully can recommend something disastrous.

Why it matters

ML is a magnificent shovel for digging through data — but, as Judea Pearl puts it, “you are smarter than your data: data do not understand causes and effects; humans do.” (Jacobucci et al. 2023) The master of metrics uses the shovel without ever mistaking the hole for the foundation of the house. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) This entry consolidates the workflow lessons scattered across Part IV, confronts the realities of social data, and draws the firm bridge back to the causal summit that the whole book defends.

The workflow

Everything rests on a disciplined pipeline that guards against overfitting — fitting the sample’s noise instead of the signal: (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Train / validation / test. Fit parameters on train, choose models and tune hyperparameters on validation, and reserve test for a single, once-and-for-all honest assessment on truly unseen data. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Avoid data leakage. The cardinal sin is selecting or tuning on the same data you report on — “optimism bias.” The fix is nested cross-validation: tune in an inner loop, assess in an outer loop, so the reported number never saw the tuning. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Cross-fitting (for causal applications) ensures the nuisance models don’t contaminate the treatment-effect estimate — the discipline double ML is built on. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

This is the cross-validation entry’s “choices inside the loop” rule, scaled up to a whole project.

The realities of social data

Industry ML lives on millions of rows. Social science usually doesn’t — and that changes everything. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Small n, wide p. Psychology and sociology datasets are often small with many correlated predictors — exactly where flexible methods overfit. The flexibility trap is “beating the data until it confesses.” (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • No Free Lunch. There is no single best algorithm for all problems. On noisy tabular social data, simple regularized models (Lasso/Ridge, well-tuned linear models) frequently match or beat black-box learners — with interpretability thrown in. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • The replication ethic. Because patterns in wide data can appear by chance, “an ounce of replication is worth more than a ton of inferential statistics.” (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

The lesson echoes the bias–variance trade-off: match the flexibility to the data you actually have, not the data you wish you had.

What ML is genuinely good for

Used for the right jobs, ML adds real value to social research: (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Prediction-policy problems. When the payoff comes from an accurate forecast to target a scarce resource — flagging students at risk of dropout, defendants’ recidivism risk, patients’ health risk — to direct interventions. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Measurement (text-as-data). Turning unstructured text or images into numbers with embeddings/transformers, creating variables that then feed ordinary causal models. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Exploratory pattern-finding. Tree-based methods surface interactions and nonlinearities you didn’t pre-specify — hypotheses to test later. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Heterogeneous treatment effects. Causal forests estimate the CATE — for whom a treatment works — the explicit ML-for-causation bridge. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Notice the pattern: ML supplies predictions and features; the causal question is answered by a design.

The central discipline: prediction is not explanation

Here is the line the entire book defends. Accurate prediction does not imply causal understanding. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) Two different questions: (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  • Prediction: what is the most likely Y given X? (ML’s home turf.)
  • Causation: what happens to Y if I intervene and change D? (needs a research design.)

A model can predict superbly by leaning on markers rather than causes — and a policy built on it can be harmful. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) The figure sorts problems into the two camps.

NoteThe asthma–pneumonia trap

The classic cautionary tale: a model predicting death from pneumonia learned that asthmatic patients had lower risk — and was, as a prediction, correct. The reason was causal and inverted: asthmatics were rushed to the ICU and treated more aggressively, which lowered their death rate. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) A policy reading the model literally — treat asthmatics less urgently because they’re low-risk — would be lethal. The model captured a marker shaped by treatment, not a cause. This is collider/selection logic wearing a machine- learning coat, and the reason feature importance ≠ causal effect: importance tells you what helped the algorithm reduce error in this dataset, never “the causes.” (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

The ethical dimension

One last duty, pointing to Part VI: a deployed model encodes and amplifies the prejudices in its training data, so fairness checks across subgroups are mandatory — acting on a biased model produces systemic injustice at scale. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) Predictive power without fairness auditing is not neutral; it is harm, automated.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Tuning and reporting on the same data. Leakage inflates every number; use nested CV, keep the test set sacred. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • A flexible model on small wide data. It overfits; prefer regularized/simple models and replicate. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Treating a causal question as a prediction problem. “Should we change D?” needs a design, not a better classifier. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  • Reading feature importance as a list of causes. It ranks predictive contribution in one dataset, not effects. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Deploying without a fairness audit. Average accuracy hides subgroup harm; check it. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Appendix · the boundary, formally

Why a great predictor can mislead a policy. ML targets \mathbb E[Y\mid X=x] — the observational conditional mean. A policy that intervenes needs \mathbb E[Y\mid \operatorname{do}(D=d)] — the interventional mean. These coincide only under unconfoundedness. In the asthma case, \Pr(\text{death}\mid \text{asthma}) is genuinely low, but \Pr(\text{death}\mid \operatorname{do}(\text{de-prioritise asthmatics})) is high — because the observed association ran through aggressive treatment, a path the intervention destroys. The model estimated the first; the policy needed the second.

Nested CV. Let the inner loop pick hyperparameters \hat\lambda(\mathcal D_{\text{in}}) by CV on a training fold, and the outer loop evaluate the whole procedure (including that selection) on a held-out fold. The outer estimate is unbiased for the generalisation error of the pipeline, whereas a single CV that both tunes and reports is optimistic by roughly the spread of the inner search — the multiplicity of trying many \lambda.

- **Tuning and reporting on the same data.** Leakage inflates every number; use nested CV, keep the test
  set sacred. [@jacobucci_ml]

- **A flexible model on small wide data.** It overfits; prefer regularized/simple models and
  [replicate](../06-field-realities/open-and-reproducible-science.qmd). [@jacobucci_ml]

- **Treating a causal question as a prediction problem.** "Should we change $D$?" needs a
  [design](../05-causal-inference/index.qmd), not a better classifier. [@angrist_mostly_harmless]

- **Reading feature importance as a list of causes.** It ranks predictive contribution in *one dataset*,
  not effects. [@jacobucci_ml]

- **Deploying without a fairness audit.** Average accuracy hides subgroup harm; check it.
  [@jacobucci_ml]

:::

## Appendix · the boundary, formally {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why a great predictor can mislead a policy.** ML targets $\mathbb E[Y\mid X=x]$ — the *observational*
conditional mean. A policy that *intervenes* needs $\mathbb E[Y\mid \operatorname{do}(D=d)]$ — the
[interventional](../05-causal-inference/directed-acyclic-graphs.qmd) mean. These coincide only under
[unconfoundedness](../05-causal-inference/matching-and-propensity-scores.qmd). In the asthma case,
$\Pr(\text{death}\mid \text{asthma})$ is genuinely low, but
$\Pr(\text{death}\mid \operatorname{do}(\text{de-prioritise asthmatics}))$ is *high* — because the
observed association ran through aggressive treatment, a path the intervention destroys. The model
estimated the first; the policy needed the second.

**Nested CV.** Let the inner loop pick hyperparameters $\hat\lambda(\mathcal D_{\text{in}})$ by CV on a
training fold, and the outer loop evaluate the *whole procedure* (including that selection) on a held-out
fold. The outer estimate is unbiased for the generalisation error of the **pipeline**, whereas a single
CV that both tunes and reports is optimistic by roughly the spread of the inner search — the
[multiplicity](../06-field-realities/questionable-research-practices.qmd) of trying many $\lambda$.

:::
