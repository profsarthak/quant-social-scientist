Support Vector Machines

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Classify by drawing the widest possible street between the classes — then bend that street into any shape with a kernel, without ever leaving the math of a straight line.

A support vector machine classifies with a single geometric idea: find the boundary that sits as far as possible from the nearest points of either class — the widest “street” between them. Only the points on the street edge (the support vectors) matter; the rest are irrelevant. A soft margin lets a few points trespass for robustness, and the kernel trick bends the straight boundary into curves while keeping the computation linear. It is one of the most elegant ideas in machine learning.

Why it matters

SVMs were the dominant classifier of the 2000s and remain a sharp tool — especially when predictors vastly outnumber observations, the p>n regime where they shine. (James et al. 2021) They’re also conceptually important: the margin formalises “a confident boundary,” and the kernel trick shows how to get non-linearity for free — both ideas recur far beyond SVMs. This is the geometric counterpart to the probabilistic logistic regression you already know.

The maximal margin classifier

Start with the separable case. A hyperplane in p dimensions is a flat (p{-}1)-dimensional surface \beta_0+\beta_1X_1+\dots+\beta_pX_p=0; its sign classifies a point. (James et al. 2021) If the classes are linearly separable, infinitely many separating hyperplanes exist — so which? The maximal margin classifier picks the one with the largest margin: the greatest perpendicular distance to the nearest training points. (James et al. 2021) The widest street is the most confident, most generalisable cut.

The points lying exactly on the margin are the support vectors — and they alone define the boundary. Move any other point and nothing changes (as long as it stays off the street); move a support vector and the boundary shifts. (James et al. 2021) But the pure maximal margin classifier is brittle: it needs perfect separability, and it’s hypersensitive — a single new point can swing the hyperplane wildly, overfitting. (James et al. 2021) The fix is to allow a few violations.

The support vector classifier (soft margin)

The support vector classifier relaxes the rule: let some points sit inside the margin, or even on the wrong side, via slack variables \epsilon_i. (James et al. 2021)

  • \epsilon_i=0: point is on the correct side, outside the margin.
  • \epsilon_i>0: inside the margin (a violation).
  • \epsilon_i>1: on the wrong side of the hyperplane (misclassified).

A budget C caps the total violation, \sum_i\epsilon_i\le C — and C is a bias–variance dial chosen by cross-validation: (James et al. 2021)

  • Small budget → narrow margins, few support vectors → low bias, high variance (the boundary hugs the data).
  • Large budget → wide margins, many support vectors → high bias, low variance (a stable, smooth boundary).

Crucially, only the support vectors (points on or violating the margin) shape the classifier — making SVMs robust to far-away outliers, unlike methods that fit every point. (James et al. 2021)

The kernel trick: curved boundaries, linear math

Real boundaries are often non-linear. The SVM’s move: enlarge the feature space so a linear boundary in the bigger space becomes a curved one back in the original. (James et al. 2021) The genius is that the classifier depends on the data only through inner products \langle x_i,x_{i'}\rangle — so replace that inner product with a kernel K(x_i,x_{i'}), a measure of similarity that implicitly computes the inner product in a high- (even infinite-) dimensional space without ever building the features. (James et al. 2021) This is the kernel trick: (James et al. 2021)

  • Polynomial kernel K(x_i,x_{i'})=(1+\sum_j x_{ij}x_{i'j})^d — boundaries up to degree d.
  • Radial (RBF) kernel K(x_i,x_{i'})=\exp(-\gamma\sum_j (x_{ij}-x_{i'j})^2) — and because the exponential decays with distance, only nearby training points influence a test point’s prediction, giving the radial kernel a local character (a cousin of KNN). (James et al. 2021)

The figure shows both ends: a linear margin with its support vectors, and an RBF boundary curving around non-separable classes.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)

# ---- Left: linearly separable, illustrate margin + support vectors ----
A = rng.normal([-1.6, 0], 0.55, (25, 2))
B = rng.normal([1.6, 0], 0.55, (25, 2))
w = np.array([1.0, 0.0])                      # separating direction (vertical street)
# margin half-width = distance from boundary (x=0) to nearest point each side
mA = -A[:, 0].max(); mB = B[:, 0].min(); m = min(mA, mB)
svA = A[np.argmax(A[:, 0])]; svB = B[np.argmin(B[:, 0])]

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(7.8, 3.9))
axL.scatter(A[:, 0], A[:, 1], s=18, color="#1f3a5f", label="class −1")
axL.scatter(B[:, 0], B[:, 1], s=18, color="#1f8a4c", label="class +1")
axL.axvline(0, color="#111", lw=2.0)
axL.axvline(-m, color="#9aa0a6", ls="--"); axL.axvline(m, color="#9aa0a6", ls="--")
for sv in (svA, svB):
    axL.scatter(*sv, s=120, facecolors="none", edgecolors="#c0392b", lw=2, zorder=5)
axL.text(0, 1.9, "margin", ha="center", color="#555", fontsize=8)
axL.text(svB[0]+0.1, svB[1], "support\nvectors", color="#c0392b", fontsize=7.5)
axL.set_title("Maximal margin (linear)"); axL.legend(fontsize=7, frameon=False, loc="lower left")
axL.set_xlim(-3.4, 3.4); axL.set_ylim(-2.2, 2.4)

# ---- Right: RBF kernel decision function (sum of Gaussians), non-separable ----
t = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, 60); r = rng.normal(0.6, 0.12, 60)
inner = np.c_[r*np.cos(t), r*np.sin(t)]           # class +1: inner ring
t2 = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, 60); r2 = rng.normal(1.8, 0.18, 60)
outer = np.c_[r2*np.cos(t2), r2*np.sin(t2)]       # class -1: outer ring
Xk = np.vstack([inner, outer]); yk = np.r_[np.ones(60), -np.ones(60)]
gamma = 1.2
def decision(grid):
    d2 = ((grid[:, None, :] - Xk[None, :, :])**2).sum(2)
    return (np.exp(-gamma*d2) * yk).sum(1)
gx, gy = np.meshgrid(np.linspace(-2.6, 2.6, 120), np.linspace(-2.6, 2.6, 120))
Z = decision(np.c_[gx.ravel(), gy.ravel()]).reshape(gx.shape)
axR.contourf(gx, gy, Z, levels=[-1e9, 0, 1e9], colors=["#dfeae5", "#e7ecf2"], alpha=0.6)
axR.contour(gx, gy, Z, levels=[0], colors="#111", linewidths=2.0)
axR.scatter(inner[:, 0], inner[:, 1], s=14, color="#1f8a4c")
axR.scatter(outer[:, 0], outer[:, 1], s=14, color="#1f3a5f")
axR.set_title("Radial (RBF) kernel — non-linear"); axR.set_xticks([]); axR.set_yticks([])

fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

SVM vs logistic regression

The SVM isn’t as alien to your toolkit as it looks. The support vector classifier minimises a “loss + penalty” with the hinge loss \max[0,\,1-y_i f(x_i)] — and the hinge loss behaves much like the logistic loss, so the two methods often give similar results. (James et al. 2021) The differences are characterful: (James et al. 2021)

  • SVM gives a hard classification — its loss is exactly zero for points comfortably on the right side, so far-away points are ignored entirely.
  • Logistic regression gives probabilities — useful when you need calibrated risk, not just a label.

For multiclass problems SVMs use one-vs-one (fit \binom{K}{2} pairwise SVMs, majority vote of the “duels”) or one-vs-all (fit K class-vs-rest SVMs, assign to the largest margin). (James et al. 2021)

NoteISL’s two SVM datasets
  • Heart disease. The support vector classifier did slightly better than LDA; with a radial kernel, raising \gamma improved training accuracy but overfit — \gamma=10^{-1} looked near-perfect in training yet was worst on the test set, a vivid bias–variance lesson. (James et al. 2021)
  • Khan gene expression (p=2{,}308 genes \gg n). The classes were perfectly linearly separable — because with so many features a separating hyperplane almost always exists — so a linear kernel sufficed; added kernel flexibility would only overfit. (James et al. 2021) In high dimensions, keep it linear.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Using the hard maximal margin. It demands perfect separability and is hypersensitive; use the soft-margin classifier and tune C. (James et al. 2021)

  • Not tuning C and \gamma by CV. Both control flexibility; the Heart example shows large \gamma overfits badly. (James et al. 2021)

  • Forgetting to scale features. Kernels measure distance, so unstandardised predictors let the largest-scale variable dominate — the same scaling rule as KNN. (James et al. 2021)

  • Reaching for a fancy kernel in high dimensions. When p\gg n data are often already separable; a linear kernel is usually best. (James et al. 2021)

  • Wanting probabilities from a raw SVM. It outputs a hard label; use logistic regression (or post-hoc calibration) when you need risk. (James et al. 2021)

NoteWho says what
  • James, Witten, Hastie & Tibshirani, ISL (Ch 9) — the maximal margin classifier (separating hyperplane, margin, support vectors, brittleness); the support vector classifier (slack \epsilon_i, budget C, the bias–variance of C, only support vectors matter); the SVM with kernels — the kernel trick (inner products), polynomial (1+\langle x,x'\rangle)^d and radial \exp(-\gamma\|x-x'\|^2) kernels and the radial kernel’s locality; hinge vs logistic loss, hard label vs probabilities; one-vs-one / one-vs-all multiclass; the Heart (\gamma overfitting) and Khan gene-expression (p\gg n, linearly separable) examples.

Appendix · the mathematics

The soft-margin optimisation. Maximise the margin M subject to correct-side constraints with slack: \max_{\beta,\,\epsilon}\ M \quad\text{s.t.}\quad \|\beta\|=1,\;\; y_i(\beta_0+\beta^\top x_i)\ge M(1-\epsilon_i),\;\; \epsilon_i\ge0,\;\; \sum_i\epsilon_i\le C. Its dual depends on the data only through inner products \langle x_i,x_{i'}\rangle, and the solution f(x)=\beta_0+\sum_{i\in\mathcal S}\alpha_i\langle x,x_i\rangle sums over the support set \mathcal S (the \alpha_i are nonzero only there). Replacing \langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle with K(\cdot,\cdot) gives the kernel SVM, f(x)=\beta_0+\sum_{i\in\mathcal S}\alpha_i K(x,x_i) — non-linear, but still a weighted vote of similarities to the support vectors.

Hinge loss = SVM. The classifier minimises \sum_i \max[0,\,1-y_i f(x_i)] + \lambda\|\beta\|^2, a loss + ridge penalty. The hinge is exactly zero once y_i f(x_i)\ge1 (correct and confident), which is why only margin-violating points — the support vectors — enter the solution, and why the SVM gives a hard label rather than a probability.

Why RBF is local and infinite-dimensional. \exp(-\gamma\|x-x'\|^2) is the inner product of an infinite Taylor-feature expansion, yet computable in closed form. Its decay means K(x,x_i)\to0 for distant x_i, so the prediction at x is dominated by nearby support vectors — large \gamma ⇒ very local ⇒ wiggly ⇒ overfitting (the Heart lesson).

Connections

  • Builds on: classification (the task and KNN’s locality), logistic regression (the loss comparison), bias–variance (C, \gamma), and regularization (the ridge penalty in the hinge form).
  • Leads to: neural networks (another route to non-linear boundaries, via learned features rather than fixed kernels).
  • Bridges back: an SVM is a pure predictive classifier — a boundary, not an explanation; its weights are not effects.

  [bias–variance](../04-predictive/bias-variance-tradeoff.qmd) lesson. [@islr]
- **Khan gene expression** ($p=2{,}308$ genes $\gg n$). The classes were **perfectly linearly
  separable** — because with so many features a separating hyperplane almost always exists — so a
  **linear kernel sufficed**; added kernel flexibility would only overfit. [@islr] In high dimensions,
  keep it linear.
:::

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Using the hard maximal margin.** It demands perfect separability and is hypersensitive; use the
  soft-margin classifier and tune $C$. [@islr]

- **Not tuning $C$ and $\gamma$ by CV.** Both control flexibility; the Heart example shows large $\gamma$
  overfits badly. [@islr]

- **Forgetting to scale features.** Kernels measure distance, so unstandardised predictors let the
  largest-scale variable dominate — the same
  [scaling rule as KNN](../04-predictive/classification-and-knn.qmd). [@islr]

- **Reaching for a fancy kernel in high dimensions.** When $p\gg n$ data are often already separable; a
  **linear** kernel is usually best. [@islr]

- **Wanting probabilities from a raw SVM.** It outputs a hard label; use logistic regression (or
  post-hoc calibration) when you need risk. [@islr]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The soft-margin optimisation.** Maximise the margin $M$ subject to correct-side constraints with slack:
$$
\max_{\beta,\,\epsilon}\ M \quad\text{s.t.}\quad
\|\beta\|=1,\;\; y_i(\beta_0+\beta^\top x_i)\ge M(1-\epsilon_i),\;\; \epsilon_i\ge0,\;\; \sum_i\epsilon_i\le C.
$$
Its dual depends on the data **only through inner products** $\langle x_i,x_{i'}\rangle$, and the solution
$f(x)=\beta_0+\sum_{i\in\mathcal S}\alpha_i\langle x,x_i\rangle$ sums over the **support set** $\mathcal S$
(the $\alpha_i$ are nonzero only there). Replacing $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ with $K(\cdot,\cdot)$ gives
the kernel SVM, $f(x)=\beta_0+\sum_{i\in\mathcal S}\alpha_i K(x,x_i)$ — non-linear, but still a weighted
vote of similarities to the support vectors.

**Hinge loss = SVM.** The classifier minimises
$\sum_i \max[0,\,1-y_i f(x_i)] + \lambda\|\beta\|^2$, a **loss + ridge penalty**. The hinge is exactly
zero once $y_i f(x_i)\ge1$ (correct and confident), which is *why* only margin-violating points — the
support vectors — enter the solution, and why the SVM gives a hard label rather than a probability.

**Why RBF is local and infinite-dimensional.** $\exp(-\gamma\|x-x'\|^2)$ is the inner product of an
*infinite* Taylor-feature expansion, yet computable in closed form. Its decay means $K(x,x_i)\to0$ for
distant $x_i$, so the prediction at $x$ is dominated by **nearby** support vectors — large $\gamma$ ⇒
very local ⇒ wiggly ⇒ overfitting (the Heart lesson).

:::
