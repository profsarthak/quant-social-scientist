Model Selection & Dimension Reduction

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When you have many predictors, fitting them all is a recipe for variance — so choose a subset, or replace them with a few well-chosen combinations.

Regularization was one answer to “too many predictors”: shrink them. This entry covers the other two. Subset selection keeps a discrete handful and drops the rest. Dimension reduction replaces the predictors with a few linear combinations that capture most of what matters. All three are the bias–variance trade-off in another costume — trading a little bias for a large cut in variance — and all become essential once predictors outnumber observations.

Why it matters

Ordinary least squares with many predictors predicts poorly and reads opaquely: variance explodes and no coefficient is trustworthy. (James et al. 2021) Before the flexible methods of this track, you need the classical toolkit for taming dimensionality — the discrete (subset selection), the continuous (shrinkage), and the derived (dimension reduction). It is also where you meet the p>n world head-on, the regime that makes regularization not a refinement but a necessity. (James et al. 2021)

Subset selection

The direct idea: keep only the predictors most related to Y and run OLS on those. (James et al. 2021)

  • Best-subset selection fits OLS for every combination of predictors and, for each size k, keeps the lowest-RSS model. Thorough but 2^p models — infeasible past ~30 predictors. (James et al. 2021)
  • Forward stepwise starts from the null model and adds the single best predictor at each step.
  • Backward stepwise starts from the full model and drops the least useful — but cannot run when p>n (there’s no full model to start from). (James et al. 2021)

You can’t choose on training error

Critically, training RSS and R^2 cannot pick the final model — they improve monotonically with every added predictor, so they always point to the biggest model and straight into overfitting. (James et al. 2021) You need a criterion that penalises size, estimating test error: (James et al. 2021)

C_p=\tfrac1n\big(\text{RSS}+2d\hat\sigma^2\big),\quad \text{BIC}=\tfrac1n\big(\text{RSS}+\log(n)\,d\hat\sigma^2\big),\quad \bar R^2=1-\frac{\text{RSS}/(n-d-1)}{\text{TSS}/(n-1)} .

\text{AIC} is proportional to C_p for least squares; BIC’s \log n penalty is heavier than C_p’s factor of 2 (whenever n>7), so BIC favours smaller models. (James et al. 2021) Higher adjusted R^2 is better; lower C_p/BIC is better. Or skip the formulas entirely and estimate test error directly with a validation set or k-fold CV — fewer assumptions, and now the default. (James et al. 2021) The figure shows the core lesson: training error slides down forever while held-out error is U-shaped.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
n, p, k_true = 120, 14, 5
X = rng.normal(0, 1, (n, p))
beta = np.zeros(p); beta[:k_true] = rng.uniform(1.0, 2.0, k_true)
y = X @ beta + rng.normal(0, 1.5, n)
# train/validation split
tr, va = slice(0, 80), slice(80, n)

def ols_fit_predict(cols, Xtr, ytr, Xva):
    A = np.c_[np.ones(Xtr.shape[0]), Xtr[:, cols]]
    coef, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(A, ytr, rcond=None)
    yhat_tr = A @ coef
    Av = np.c_[np.ones(Xva.shape[0]), Xva[:, cols]]
    return yhat_tr, Av @ coef

chosen, remaining = [], list(range(p))
train_r2, val_mse, bic = [], [], []
ytr, yva = y[tr], y[va]
for step in range(1, p + 1):
    best = None
    for c in remaining:                       # forward step: add the predictor that best cuts train RSS
        cols = chosen + [c]
        yhat_tr, _ = ols_fit_predict(cols, X[tr], ytr, X[va])
        rss = np.sum((ytr - yhat_tr) ** 2)
        if best is None or rss < best[0]:
            best = (rss, c)
    chosen.append(best[1]); remaining.remove(best[1])
    yhat_tr, yhat_va = ols_fit_predict(chosen, X[tr], ytr, X[va])
    rss = np.sum((ytr - yhat_tr) ** 2)
    train_r2.append(1 - rss / np.sum((ytr - ytr.mean()) ** 2))
    val_mse.append(np.mean((yva - yhat_va) ** 2))
    d = len(chosen); sigma2 = rss / (len(ytr) - d - 1)
    bic.append((rss + np.log(len(ytr)) * d * sigma2) / len(ytr))

sizes = np.arange(1, p + 1)
val_min = sizes[int(np.argmin(val_mse))]; bic_min = sizes[int(np.argmin(bic))]

fig, ax1 = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax2 = ax1.twinx()
ax1.plot(sizes, train_r2, "o-", color="#9aa0a6", lw=2, label="training R² (left)")
ax2.plot(sizes, val_mse, "o-", color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.2, label="validation MSE (right)")
ax2.axvline(val_min, color="#1f8a4c", ls=":", lw=1.6)
ax2.axvline(bic_min, color="#d98a29", ls="--", lw=1.4)
ax1.axvline(k_true, color="#c0392b", lw=1.0, alpha=0.5)
ax1.text(k_true + 0.1, 0.2, f"true size = {k_true}", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
ax2.text(val_min + 0.15, max(val_mse) * 0.9, f"val min = {val_min}", color="#1f8a4c", fontsize=8)
ax1.set_xlabel("number of predictors in model")
ax1.set_ylabel("training R²", color="#6b6f76"); ax2.set_ylabel("validation MSE", color="#1f3a5f")
ax1.set_title("Training error always falls; held-out error is U-shaped")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Three philosophies, one trade-off

It helps to see subset selection beside the shrinkage it complements: (James et al. 2021)

  • Subset selection — a discrete choice: a predictor is in or out. Low bias on the kept set, but the discreteness makes it high-variance (a small data change flips which subset wins).
  • Ridge (\ell_2) — shrinks all coefficients toward zero, none exactly to zero: keeps every predictor but tames variance. (James et al. 2021)
  • Lasso (\ell_1) — shrinks and zeroes out, performing selection continuously; the diamond constraint’s corners are what set coefficients exactly to zero. (James et al. 2021)

All three buy a large variance reduction for a little bias — the universal regularization bargain. (James et al. 2021)

Dimension reduction: PCR and PLS

A different idea: don’t select among the p predictors — replace them with M<p linear combinations and regress on those. (James et al. 2021)

  • Principal-components regression (PCR). Take the first M principal components — the directions of maximal variance in X — and use them as predictors. PCR is unsupervised: it finds those directions without looking at Y, betting that high-variance directions in X are also the ones that predict Y. (James et al. 2021)
  • Partial least squares (PLS). The supervised cousin: it builds directions that explain X and correlate with Y — the first PLS component weights each predictor by its correlation with Y. (James et al. 2021) PLS can do better when the predictive directions aren’t the highest-variance ones, though in practice it often matches PCR.

For both, standardise the predictors first (mean 0, SD 1) — otherwise a variable’s measurement scale, not its information, drives the directions. (James et al. 2021) (PCA itself returns in the unsupervised entry; here it’s a regression pre-processor.)

NoteISL’s two datasets
  • Credit (n=400, p=11): the size-penalising criteria disagree — BIC picks 4 variables, C_p/AIC pick 6, adjusted R^2 picks 7 — a clean illustration that BIC is the most parsimonious. (James et al. 2021)
  • Hitters (n=263, p=19, predicting baseball salary): cross-validated Lasso selects a 7-variable model with test MSE (\approx 100,743) comparable to ridge’s — but far more interpretable for being sparse. (James et al. 2021)

The high-dimensional world (p>n)

Modern data routinely has more predictors than observations — genomics, text, behavioural logs. (James et al. 2021) Here ordinary least squares breaks: (James et al. 2021)

  • No unique solution, and coefficient variance becomes infinite.
  • It fits the training data perfectly (\text{RSS}=0) even when every predictor is pure noise — so training R^2=1 is meaningless, evidence of nothing.
  • The curse of dimensionality: test error tends to rise with each added feature unless it’s genuinely associated with Y; and extreme multicollinearity makes it impossible to say which variable is truly predictive (any one is a combination of the others). (James et al. 2021)

In this regime the less-flexible methods — Lasso, PCR, ridge — stop being optional and become the only way to get generalisable predictions. (James et al. 2021) And the hard truth: with p>n you can predict, but you usually cannot identify which predictors matter — a humbling boundary that the causal cautions of this track underline.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Selecting on training RSS/R^2. They fall monotonically and always pick the biggest model; use C_p/BIC/adjusted R^2 or cross-validation. (James et al. 2021)

  • Doing selection outside the CV loop. Choosing predictors on the full data then cross-validating leaks information and flatters the estimate — selection is a choice inside the loop. (James et al. 2021)

  • PCR/PLS without standardising. Unscaled predictors let the largest-scale variable dominate the components. (James et al. 2021)

  • Trusting PCR when variance ≠ signal. High-variance directions need not predict Y; PLS or supervised methods may be needed. (James et al. 2021)

  • Reading R^2=1 in high dimensions as success. With p>n a perfect fit is automatic and worthless. (James et al. 2021)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • James, Witten, Hastie & Tibshirani, ISL (Ch 6) — best-subset and forward/backward stepwise selection (backward needs n>p); why training RSS/R^2 can’t choose; C_p, AIC, BIC, adjusted R^2 and CV; subset vs Ridge/Lasso shrinkage and the bias–variance rationale; PCR (unsupervised, max-variance directions) vs PLS (supervised), and standardising; the p>n failure of OLS, the curse of dimensionality, multicollinearity, and meaningless R^2; the Credit (BIC 4 / C_p 6 / adj-R^2 7) and Hitters (Lasso 7-var, MSE \approx 100,743) examples.

Appendix · the mathematics

Why C_p estimates test error. Training RSS underestimates test error by roughly 2d\hat\sigma^2/n (one \hat\sigma^2 of optimism per fitted parameter). C_p=\tfrac1n(\text{RSS}+2d\hat\sigma^2) adds it back, making C_p an (approximately) unbiased estimate of test MSE. BIC replaces the 2 with \log n, derived from a Bayesian model-evidence approximation, hence its stronger preference for small models and its consistency (it picks the true model as n\to\infty).

PCR as constrained ridge. Let X=UDV^\top (SVD). PCR keeps the first M right-singular directions v_1,\dots,v_M and discards the rest entirely; ridge instead down-weights direction j by d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda) — shrinking low-variance directions smoothly rather than truncating. PCR is the hard-threshold limit of ridge’s soft down-weighting along the principal axes — which is why both protect against the high-variance, low-eigenvalue directions that wreck OLS.

Why OLS dies at p>n. X^\top X is p\times p but has rank \le n<p, so it is singular and (X^\top X)^{-1} does not exist — infinitely many \hat\beta give \text{RSS}=0. Regularization restores a unique solution by adding a full-rank penalty (\lambda I for ridge) or restricting to M\le n derived directions (PCR/PLS).

Connections

  • Builds on: regularization (the shrinkage sibling), bias–variance (the shared rationale), cross-validation (how to choose), and multicollinearity/VIF.
  • Leads to: beyond linearity (flexible forms, once you’ve chosen the features) and unsupervised learning (PCA in its own right).
  • Bridges back: prediction with p>n can succeed while identification of which predictors matter fails — a caution sharpened in ML for social science.

- It fits the training data **perfectly** ($\text{RSS}=0$) **even when every predictor is pure noise** —
  so training $R^2=1$ is **meaningless**, evidence of nothing.
- The **curse of dimensionality**: test error tends to *rise* with each added feature *unless* it's
  genuinely associated with $Y$; and **extreme multicollinearity** makes it impossible to say which
  variable is truly predictive (any one is a combination of the others). [@islr]

In this regime the less-flexible methods — **Lasso, PCR, ridge** — stop being optional and become the
*only* way to get generalisable predictions. [@islr] And the hard truth: with $p>n$ you can predict, but
you usually **cannot identify** *which* predictors matter — a humbling boundary that the
[causal cautions](ml-for-social-science.qmd) of this track underline.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Selecting on training RSS/$R^2$.** They fall monotonically and always pick the biggest model; use
  $C_p$/BIC/adjusted $R^2$ or [cross-validation](../04-predictive/cross-validation.qmd). [@islr]

- **Doing selection *outside* the CV loop.** Choosing predictors on the full data then cross-validating
  leaks information and flatters the estimate — selection is a *choice inside the loop*. [@islr]

- **PCR/PLS without standardising.** Unscaled predictors let the largest-scale variable dominate the
  components. [@islr]

- **Trusting PCR when variance ≠ signal.** High-variance directions need not predict $Y$; PLS or
  supervised methods may be needed. [@islr]

- **Reading $R^2=1$ in high dimensions as success.** With $p>n$ a perfect fit is automatic and
  worthless. [@islr]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why $C_p$ estimates test error.** Training RSS underestimates test error by roughly $2d\hat\sigma^2/n$
(one $\hat\sigma^2$ of optimism per fitted parameter). $C_p=\tfrac1n(\text{RSS}+2d\hat\sigma^2)$ adds it
back, making $C_p$ an (approximately) unbiased estimate of test MSE. **BIC** replaces the $2$ with
$\log n$, derived from a Bayesian model-evidence approximation, hence its stronger preference for small
models and its **consistency** (it picks the true model as $n\to\infty$).

**PCR as constrained ridge.** Let $X=UDV^\top$ (SVD). PCR keeps the first $M$ right-singular directions
$v_1,\dots,v_M$ and discards the rest entirely; **ridge** instead *down-weights* direction $j$ by
$d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$ — shrinking low-variance directions smoothly rather than truncating. PCR is the
hard-threshold limit of ridge's soft down-weighting along the principal axes — which is why both protect
against the high-variance, low-eigenvalue directions that wreck OLS.

**Why OLS dies at $p>n$.** $X^\top X$ is $p\times p$ but has rank $\le n<p$, so it is singular and
$(X^\top X)^{-1}$ does not exist — infinitely many $\hat\beta$ give $\text{RSS}=0$. Regularization
restores a unique solution by adding a full-rank penalty ($\lambda I$ for ridge) or restricting to $M\le
n$ derived directions (PCR/PLS).

:::
