What Makes Research Credible — and How It Fails

Credibility is designed in, not patched on — and a great deal of published research doesn’t survive a second look.

Good quantitative research is consistent (reliable), accurate (valid), and reproducible (replicable). When those slip — through selective analysis, hidden choices, and unpublished null results — you get the replication crisis: confident findings that vanish when someone reruns them. This entry is the manual’s conscience, and the reason the chapters ahead are so insistent about rigour.

Why it matters

It is sobering, and clarifying, to learn up front that many celebrated findings in social science don’t replicate. That fact reframes everything that follows: the point of learning statistics rigorously is not to produce significant results, but to produce trustworthy ones. Knowing how research fails is half of knowing how to do it well.

The criteria for credible research

Three standards, building on the measurement and design ideas you’ve already met (Bryman 2016):

  • Reliability — consistency. A reliable measure gives the same result on repeated trials: stability (steady over time), internal reliability (a scale’s indicators cohere), and inter-rater reliability (coders agree).
  • Validity — accuracy (the most important criterion):
    • Measurement validity — the indicator really reflects the concept (does an IQ test measure intelligence?).
    • Internal validity — the causal claim is genuine (X truly caused Y, not an unobserved confounder); randomised experiments are the benchmark.
    • External validity — the result generalises beyond the study’s context.
  • Replicability vs replication — a distinction worth keeping sharp:
    • Replicability = the procedures are reported clearly enough that someone could repeat the study.
    • Replication = someone actually reruns it and checks the findings hold across new samples or times.

The replication crisis — and what causes it

The replication crisis is the discovery that many widely accepted findings fail to reproduce when rerun (Bryman 2016). Bryman names the mechanisms — and each is a preview of a battle fought later in this manual:

  • p-hacking — selectively analysing data until something crosses significance. (Made rigorous and exposed in Part II’s hypothesis-testing material.)
  • The “garden of forking paths” — many individually defensible analytic choices (which cases to drop, which controls to include) lead to different results; flexibility masquerades as discovery.
  • Publication bias / the “file drawer” — null results go unpublished, so the literature over-represents flukes that happened to reach significance.
WarningThe uncomfortable implication

A single significant result, from a single study, with undisclosed analytic choices, is weak evidence — even when the statistics are flawless. Credibility comes from transparency and repetition, not from a small p-value. Hold this thought; the rest of the manual earns it.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Mistaking statistical significance for truth. Significance is a filter that flukes also pass. Replication is the real test.
  • Reporting too little to be replicable. If no one can rerun your analysis from your description, it isn’t science yet — it’s an anecdote with numbers.
  • Letting forking paths stay hidden. Every “we tried it this way” you don’t disclose inflates your apparent certainty.
  • Treating null results as failures. Unpublished nulls are how the literature lies by omission.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Bryman, Social Research Methods — reliability (stability/internal/inter-rater); validity (measurement/internal/external); the replicability-vs-replication distinction; the replication crisis and its causes (p-hacking, the garden of forking paths, publication bias / the file drawer).

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Pre-register when you can. Committing to your analysis before seeing the data is the cheapest defence against p-hacking and forking paths.
  • Write for the replicator. Document every decision so a stranger could reproduce your result — that discipline also catches your own errors.
  • Report the nulls. Your “it didn’t work” is someone else’s saved year.
  • Distrust single studies, including your own. Treat a lone significant finding as a hypothesis, not a conclusion. :::
