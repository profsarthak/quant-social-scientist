Concepts & Variables: The Problem of Measurement

Turning something you can argue about into something you can compute with — the step where studies are won or lost.

You cannot measure “trust,” “depression,” or “social class” directly. You measure a stand-in you’ve defined — and the whole study inherits the quality of that choice. Measurement is the hinge between an idea and a number: get it right and the statistics mean something; get it wrong and you compute precise answers about the wrong thing.

Why it matters

Every technique later in this manual — averages, regressions, causal estimates — operates on variables, not on concepts. If the variable is a poor stand-in for the concept you care about, no amount of sophisticated analysis downstream can rescue it. This is why experienced researchers spend disproportionate effort before any data is collected, on the unglamorous question: how, exactly, will I measure this?

From concept to variable: operationalisation

Concepts are the building blocks of theory — the categories (“job satisfaction,” “prejudice,” “political participation”) around which a study is built (Bryman 2016). To investigate one, you must translate it into something researchable and quantifiable: a variable. That translation is operationalisation (Bryman 2016).

  • An operational definition is the precise recipe for measuring the variable in this study — “the specification of the activities or operations necessary to measure it” (Martin and Bridgmon 2012). Example: depressive symptomatology operationally defined as a score on the Beck Depression Inventory.
  • When a concept isn’t directly quantifiable, you devise indicators to represent it — drawn from survey questions, structured observation, or official statistics (Bryman 2016). Bryman stresses that multiple indicators (e.g. a 5-item Likert scale) usually give more precision than a single question — one item can catch an idiosyncrasy of wording; several average it out.
TipThe hidden choice

Operationalisation is a decision, not a discovery. “Depression = Beck score” and “depression = a clinician’s diagnosis” are different operational definitions that can disagree. Naming your operational definition out loud — and its limits — is the first act of honest quantitative research.

Levels of measurement: what your numbers are allowed to do

Once you have a variable, its level of measurement dictates which operations and statistics are even meaningful (Albers 2017; Martin and Bridgmon 2012). Using the wrong ones is a silent, common error.

Level Property Examples Permissible operations
Nominal Unordered categories; numbers are arbitrary tags Gender, religion, city Counts, frequencies, mode. Means are meaningless.
Ordinal Ordered categories, but unequal/unknown gaps Likert agreement, race finishing position Order comparisons, median
Interval Equal spacing, no true zero IQ, °Celsius Add/subtract; differences are meaningful
Ratio Equal spacing and a true zero Weight, height, time, count of children All arithmetic; ratios (“twice as heavy”) are valid
WarningThe Likert trap

Albers warns explicitly: averaging a single Likert item is invalid, because the distance from “Agree” to “Neutral” is not guaranteed equal to “Neutral” to “Disagree” (Albers 2017). The number looks computable, but the scale doesn’t earn the mean. (A multi-item Likert scale is treated more leniently — but that’s a defended choice, not a default.)

Reliability vs validity: consistency vs accuracy

These two words are used interchangeably in everyday English but are sharply distinct here — and the relationship between them is one-directional: validity presumes reliability. A measure that is inconsistent cannot be accurate (Bryman 2016).

Reliability — does the measure give the same result on repeated trials? (Consistency.)

  • Stability (test–retest): the same measure given twice to the same group shouldn’t swing wildly.
  • Internal reliability: the multiple indicators in a scale should hang together (measure the same thing).
  • Inter-rater reliability: independent coders should agree — Albers cites Cohen’s Kappa as the standard statistic (Albers 2017).

Validity — does the measure capture the concept you intended? (Accuracy.) The sources distinguish several kinds (Bryman 2016):

  • Face validity — at minimum, it looks like it measures the concept (to experts).
  • Criterion validity — it tracks an external benchmark: concurrent (a present criterion like absenteeism) or predictive (a future one).
  • Construct validity — it behaves as theory says it should; convergent validity checks it against measures of the same thing built by different methods.
  • Internal validity — confidence the independent variable truly drives the dependent one (causality). External validity — whether results generalise beyond the study; ecological validity — whether they hold in everyday, natural settings.
TipThe dartboard

The classic picture: reliable but not valid = tight cluster of darts, all in the wrong spot (consistent, biased). Valid but not reliable is impossible to sustain — scattered darts can’t be reliably on target. You want tight and centred.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Computing means on ordinal/nominal data. Averaging ranks or category codes produces a number with no meaning. Match the statistic to the measurement level.
  • Mistaking reliability for validity. A bathroom scale that’s 5 kg heavy is perfectly reliable and perfectly wrong. Consistency is necessary, not sufficient.
  • Single-indicator fragility. One survey item can be derailed by wording, mood, or translation; multiple indicators are insurance.
  • Treating operationalisation as obvious. “Just measure income” hides a dozen choices (pre/post tax? household/individual? annual/monthly?) that change the findings.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Bryman, Social Research Methods — concepts as theory’s building blocks; operationalisation; indicators and multiple indicators; the reliability types (stability, internal, inter-rater) and validity types (face, criterion, construct, convergent, internal, external, ecological); validity presumes reliability.
  • Martin & Bridgmon, Quantitative and Statistical Research Methods — operational definitions as specified operations (Beck Depression Inventory example); active vs attribute variables.
  • Albers, Quantitative Data Analysis — levels of measurement and their permissible operations; the Likert-averaging warning; Cohen’s Kappa for inter-rater reliability.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Decide the measurement level deliberately — it silently constrains every statistic you can later use. Collecting age as bands (ordinal) instead of years (ratio) throws away power you can never recover.
  • Write the operational definition before you collect anything. If you can’t write it precisely, you don’t yet know what you’re studying.
  • Check reliability before you trust a result. A finding built on an unreliable measure is noise wearing a number.
  • Prefer multiple indicators, then verify they cohere (internal reliability) before combining them.
