What Quantitative Social Science Actually Does

Measuring what matters about people — and then letting the data argue back.

Quantitative social science is the disciplined attempt to test ideas about human behaviour using numbers: you turn a fuzzy concept into something you can measure, collect data on it, and let the evidence confirm or overturn what you believed. The numbers are not the point — deciding what to count, and what the count is allowed to prove, is the point.

Why it matters

Every technique in this manual — probability, regression, causal inference — is a tool. Before tools, you need the job: what are we actually trying to do when we “do quantitative research”? Get this wrong and you produce numbers that look authoritative and mean nothing. Get it right and even a simple average can settle a real question. This entry is the frame that gives all the later machinery a purpose.

Intuition: research as a strategy, not a calculation

Quantitative research is best understood not as “doing maths on data” but as a systematic strategy that prioritises quantification in order to test theories (Bryman 2016). Bryman characterises it through three orientations that travel together:

  • Deductive — theory comes first; you test it. (More on this below.)
  • Positivist — you study social reality using the methods of the natural sciences.
  • Objectivist — you treat social facts as real, external things that exist independently of the observer.

Martin & Bridgmon frame the same enterprise more operationally, as the discipline of managing variance — their “MaxMinCon” principle: Maximise the variance due to the thing you’re studying, Minimise error variance, and Control the extraneous variance from everything else (Martin and Bridgmon 2012). That single idea — make the signal big, the noise small, and the confounds held still — is the hidden agenda behind nearly every method later in this book.

Quantitative vs qualitative: a question of stance

Quantitative isn’t “better” than qualitative research; it answers different questions from a different stance. Bryman contrasts them along several dimensions (Bryman 2016):

Quantitative Qualitative
Medium Numbers, measurement Words, images
Whose view counts The researcher’s (you decide what to measure) The participants’ (seen through their eyes)
Relationship to subjects Distant, sometimes no contact at all Close involvement
Picture of social life A static snapshot of relationships An unfolding process
Scale Large-scale trends (macro) Small-scale interactions (micro)

The trade is real: quantification buys you scale, comparability, and a way to quantify uncertainty, at the cost of closeness and context. Knowing which you need is the first research decision you ever make.

Deductive and inductive: how theory and data talk to each other

The engine of research is the relationship between theory and data, and it runs in two directions.

  • Deductive (theory → data). You start from what’s known and relevant theory, deduce a hypothesis, and test it against evidence. Theory drives what you collect (Bryman 2016). Martin & Bridgmon call this the hypothetico-deductive model: general theory → specific observations → confirmation or rejection (Martin and Bridgmon 2012).
  • Inductive (data → theory). Theory is the outcome: you observe, then draw generalisable inferences that become new theoretical ideas (Bryman 2016).
TipThe honest version: it’s a spiral, not a line

Both sources stress these are tendencies, not boxes. Deductive studies contain “inductive loops” where theory is revised after the findings come in; Martin & Bridgmon call the real process the inductive–hypothetico–deductive spiral (Martin and Bridgmon 2012). In practice you loop: theory shapes data, data reshapes theory, repeat.

Worked examples: the two directions in real studies

Deductive — do immigrants’ gender attitudes converge on their host country’s? Bryman uses Röder & Mühlau (2014) (Röder and Mühlau 2014). They began from established theory (national gender attitudes differ; migrants move between cultures), deduced five hypotheses predicting that second-generation immigrants adopt host-country gender values, and confirmed them using European Social Survey data — evidence of an “intergenerational acculturation process.” Theory first, data as the test.

Inductive — why is front-line customer service so poor? Bryman uses O’Reilly et al. (2012) (O’Reilly et al. 2012). The researcher started with open-ended observation and interviews of retail employees — no hypothesis. The concept of “service silos” (management structures that quietly constrained employees) emerged from the data as the theory that explained what she saw. Data first, theory as the product.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Treating deductive vs inductive as a rigid choice. Real research spirals between them; pretending you “only test hypotheses” hides the inductive judgement you actually used.
  • Forgetting the positivist/objectivist assumptions. Treating a measured construct as if it were an objective external fact — when it’s a contestable operationalisation — is the original sin that later resurfaces as the tyranny of metrics.
  • Forcing numbers onto a qualitative question. If the real question is about meaning, context, or process, quantification can produce precise answers to the wrong question.
  • Skipping “MaxMinCon” thinking. A study that doesn’t deliberately maximise signal, minimise error, and control confounds is usually measuring noise.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Bryman, Social Research Methods — quantitative research as a deductive/positivist/ objectivist strategy; the quant-vs-qual dimensions; deductive and inductive logic; the Röder & Mühlau (deductive) and O’Reilly (inductive) examples.
  • Martin & Bridgmon, Quantitative and Statistical Research Methods — the MaxMinCon variance framing; the hypothetico-deductive model and the inductive–hypothetico–deductive spiral.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Name your direction out loud. Before designing anything, say whether you’re testing a theory or building one. It changes everything downstream — sampling, measurement, how you read a surprising result.
  • Write the conclusion you’re afraid of. State, in advance, the finding that would prove you wrong. If your design can’t produce it, you’re not testing — you’re decorating.
  • “MaxMinCon” is a design checklist. For any study ask: what’s my signal, what’s my error, what are my confounds — and what in the design handles each?
