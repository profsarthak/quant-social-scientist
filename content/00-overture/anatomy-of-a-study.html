The Anatomy of a Study

From a question to a conclusion — the skeleton every method hangs on.

A quantitative study is a pipeline: theory → question → hypothesis → design → measurement → data → analysis → conclusion. Every technique in this manual is a tool for one of these steps. Learn the skeleton first, and each later chapter becomes “ah, that’s the part that does step 5.”

Why it matters

People drown in methods because they learn them as a disconnected toolbox. But methods aren’t a pile — they’re stations on an assembly line. Once you can see the whole line, you always know where you are and what a given technique is for. This entry is that map; the rest of the book fills in each station.

Intuition: research is a sequence, and (almost) everyone agrees on it

Two of our sources lay out the sequence explicitly, and they agree far more than they differ.

Bryman’s process of quantitative research — the canonical 12 steps (Bryman 2016):

  1. Theory — the starting point.
  2. Research question(s) — what, precisely, you want to know.
  3. Hypothesis — deduced from theory (sometimes skipped in exploratory work).
  4. Research design — experiment, cross-section, longitudinal, comparative…
  5. Devise measures of concepts — operationalisation.
  6. Select research site(s).
  7. Select respondents/subjects — your sample.
  8. Collect the data — administer the instruments.
  9. Process the data — turn responses into numbers.
  10. Analyse the data — the statistics.
  11. Findings / conclusions.
  12. Write up.

Martin & Bridgmon’s structure — the same arc, tilted toward the statistics (Martin and Bridgmon 2012):

  1. State the problem; identify questions/hypotheses.
  2. Identify, classify, and operationally define the variables.
  3. Choose the research design.
  4. A priori power analysis — work out the sample size you need before collecting data.
  5. Choose the appropriate statistic.
  6. Collect and analyse the data set.
  7. Data screening and analysis (e.g. in SPSS).
  8. Interpret the statistics.
  9. Write the results.
TipRead the two lists together

Bryman emphasises the social-science front end (theory, sites, respondents); Martin & Bridgmon emphasise the statistical spine (operational definitions, power analysis, choosing the right test). The honest model is the union: a study earns its credibility by doing both well. Note step 4 in Martin & Bridgmon — deciding sample size before you collect — is the single most-skipped step in real research, and a recurring villain later in the manual.

A worked study: does therapy reduce adolescent depression?

Martin & Bridgmon walk through a study of 145 adolescents that runs the entire pipeline end to end (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) — a perfect first sight of the cycle:

  1. Problem & question. Which is more effective at reducing depressive symptoms — Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Interpersonal Therapy (IPT), or no treatment (control)?
  2. Hypotheses. A null hypothesis H_0: the three groups don’t differ. An alternative: they do.
  3. Design. Three groups, an outcome measured the same way in each.
  4. Power / sample size. Enough adolescents (145) to give the test a fair chance of detecting a real difference.
  5. Choose the statistic. A one-way ANOVA (the right tool for comparing three group means).
  6. Collect & analyse. Run the data through the test.
  7. Decide. The result was p = .003. Because that is below the chosen threshold \alpha = .05, they reject the null and conclude the therapies have genuinely different effects.

That final move — comparing a p-value to a threshold to decide — is the heartbeat of classical inference. You’ll meet it properly in Part II; here, just notice where in the pipeline it sits: step 10 of Bryman, step 8 of Martin & Bridgmon. The statistic is one station, not the whole journey.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Mistaking the list for reality. The steps look linear; real studies loop (you revise the question after a pilot, re-operationalise after bad data). The line is a plan, not a promise.
  • Treating analysis as the whole study. Beginners obsess over step 10 (the statistics) and neglect steps 1–5, where studies are actually won or lost. A perfect ANOVA on a badly operationalised concept is a precise wrong answer.
  • Skipping the a priori power analysis. Deciding sample size after seeing the data — or not at all — is how underpowered studies and false findings are born.
  • Letting “write up” hide the messy middle. The clean published pipeline rarely shows the inductive loops that actually happened. Honesty about them is part of rigour.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Bryman, Social Research Methods — the 12-step process of quantitative research.
  • Martin & Bridgmon, Quantitative and Statistical Research Methods — the 9-step structure (with the a priori power-analysis step); the 145-adolescent CBT/IPT depression study run end to end, including the p = .003 vs \alpha = .05 decision.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Locate yourself on the pipeline, always. When stuck, ask “which step am I on?” Confusion usually means you’ve smuggled a later step’s question into an earlier step.
  • Do step 5 before step 7. Operationalise your concepts before you choose your sample; you can’t size a study to measure something you haven’t defined.
  • Run the power analysis as a design check, not a formality. If the honest sample size is unaffordable, you’ve learned something now — before wasting the data collection.
  • Keep a “loop log.” Note every time reality made you revise an earlier step. It’s the difference between a reproducible study and a story.
