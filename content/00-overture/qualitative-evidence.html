A Field Guide to Qualitative Evidence

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

You don’t have to become an ethnographer — but a quantitative researcher who can’t read, weigh, and use qualitative evidence is working with one eye closed.

A number is an answer; it rarely tells you whether you asked the right question. Qualitative evidence — what people said in their own words, what a researcher watched unfold, what a document reveals about its author — is where research questions are born, where surprising mechanisms are spotted, and where a clean coefficient gets a meaning. This entry is the literacy a quantitative researcher needs to read that evidence fairly, judge its quality, and use it — without pretending to be a qualitative researcher.

Why it matters

You will meet qualitative work constantly: in the literature you review, in the collaborators you work with, and in the exploratory phase of your own studies — before you know what to measure. Dismissing it as “anecdote” is a beginner’s error that quietly narrows what you can discover; swallowing it whole is the opposite error. The skill is calibrated reading: knowing what each method can deliver, by what standards its quality is judged, and where its weaknesses lie. That calibration is also what lets you combine the two traditions — the subject of the next entry.

The four workhorse methods

Qualitative research prioritises meaning, context, and the participant’s point of view over measurement and frequency (Bryman 2016). Four families of method do most of the work.

Ethnography / participant observation

The researcher immerses in a social setting for an extended period — observing behaviour, listening to conversation, asking questions — to understand a culture firsthand, in its natural setting (Bryman 2016).

  • Access may be to open/public settings (a park, a protest) or closed/non-public ones (a firm, a school) — the latter usually requiring a gatekeeper.
  • Overt vs covert. Overt research discloses the researcher’s status; covert research hides it to reduce reactivity and reach hard-to-access groups — but is ethically fraught (no informed consent) (Bryman 2016).
  • Field notes come in grades: mental notes (when writing would be intrusive), jotted/scratch notes (quick reminders), and full field notes (detailed accounts written as soon as possible).
  • A role continuum runs from covert full member (totally immersed, identity hidden) → overt full member → participating observer (present but not a full member) → non-participating observer.
  • Yields / costs: unmatched ecological validity and deep context; but it is slow, hard to replicate, and risks the researcher “going native” — over-identifying with the group until critical distance is lost (Bryman 2016).

Qualitative interviewing

Interviews that prioritise the interviewee’s perspective and seek rich, detailed answers (Bryman 2016).

  • Unstructured — a brief set of prompts, conversation-like, letting the participant range freely.
  • Semi-structured — an interview guide of topics/questions, but the interviewer may vary the order and ask unplanned follow-ups. A good guide has order yet flexibility, uses the participant’s own language, and avoids leading questions.
  • Probing — “Can you say more about that?” — draws out the points that matter without putting words in the participant’s mouth.

Focus groups

A moderated group discussion (usually ≥ 4 people) on a defined topic — where the interaction itself is the data (Bryman 2016). Unlike a mere group interview, the focus group studies how people collectively construct meaning, reach consensus, or surface disagreement. More naturalistic than one-on-one interviews and good at letting participants challenge each other — but the moderator has less control, and the overlapping talk is hard to transcribe.

Qualitative content / document analysis

Searching texts — diaries, letters, media, official records — for latent themes (Bryman 2016). The contrast with its quantitative cousin is the crux:

Quantitative content analysis Qualitative content analysis
Target Manifest content (what’s literally there) Latent themes (underlying meaning)
Categories Predetermined, fixed before coding Emergent, arising from the data
Process Count into categories, replicably Iterative, via constant comparison

Document work also asks four quality questions — authenticity, credibility, representativeness, and meaning — and treats a document as a constructed “documentary reality,” written by someone, for an audience, with a purpose (Bryman 2016).

How theory is built: grounded theory and coding

The dominant analytic engine is grounded theory — theory derived from data that is systematically gathered and analysed, rather than imposed in advance (Bryman 2016). Its coding ladder (Strauss & Corbin):

  1. Open coding — break the data down and label it into concepts.
  2. Axial coding — reassemble: draw connections between categories.
  3. Selective coding — choose a core category and relate the others to it, forming the theory.

Two ideas drive the cycle: constant comparison (every new datum is checked against existing codes, forcing a tight data–category fit) and theoretical sampling — you keep sampling new cases until they stop teaching you anything new. That stopping point is theoretical saturation.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

K = 30          # latent themes present in the setting
p = 0.16        # chance a single interview surfaces a given theme
n = np.arange(0, 31)

expected_unique = K * (1 - (1 - p) ** n)          # cumulative distinct themes
marginal = np.diff(expected_unique, prepend=0.0)  # new themes per interview
sat = int(np.argmax(marginal < 1.0))              # first interview adding < 1 new theme

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.bar(n, marginal, width=0.8, color="#cdd6df", label="new themes this interview")
ax.plot(n, expected_unique, color="#1f4e79", lw=2.4, marker="o", ms=3.5,
        label="cumulative themes discovered")
ax.axvline(sat, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.6)
ax.text(sat + 0.3, 4, f"saturation\n≈ interview {sat}", color="#c0392b",
        fontsize=9, fontweight="bold", va="bottom")
ax.axhline(K, color="#3f8f6f", ls=":", lw=1.3)
ax.text(0.3, K - 1.6, f"all {K} themes", color="#3f8f6f", fontsize=8.5)
ax.set_xlabel("interviews conducted"); ax.set_ylabel("themes")
ax.set_title("Why qualitative sampling stops: diminishing new information")
ax.legend(loc="center right", frameon=False, fontsize=8.5)
ax.set_xlim(-0.5, 30.5); ax.set_ylim(0, K + 2)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Judging quality: trustworthiness, not reliability

Because qualitative work doesn’t rest on statistical measurement, many of its practitioners reject reliability and validity as too measurement-centric, adopting instead Lincoln & Guba’s trustworthiness and authenticity (Bryman 2016). The parallels are worth memorising — they let you map a qualitative quality claim onto the quantitative vocabulary you already know:

Trustworthiness criterion The question Quantitative parallel Established by
Credibility Are the findings believable? Internal validity Triangulation; respondent validation
Transferability Do they travel to other contexts? External validity Thick description (so others can judge)
Dependability Would they hold at another time? Reliability An audit trail of records
Confirmability Did the researcher act in good faith? Objectivity Showing values didn’t overtly sway the study

Beyond these, authenticity asks a more political question — does the research represent different viewpoints fairly, and does it empower participants to change their situation (catalytic authenticity)? (Bryman 2016)

Reading it as a quantitative skeptic — fairly

From a measurement-first standpoint, qualitative research draws four standard critiques. Knowing both the charge and the standard reply is what separates a fair critic from a dismissive one (Bryman 2016):

  • Subjectivity. Findings can lean on the researcher’s unsystematic sense of what matters and on close personal relationships with participants. (Reply: reflexivity and audit trails make the lens explicit rather than hidden.)
  • Generalisation. Small, purposive (non-probability) samples can’t generalise to a population. (Reply: qualitative work aims at theoretical / analytic generalisation — to concepts and mechanisms — not statistical representativeness.)
  • Replication. The researcher is the instrument and a social setting can’t be “frozen,” so studies are nearly impossible to repeat. (Reply: dependability via the audit trail is the proposed substitute.)
  • Transparency. Reports are sometimes vague about how participants were chosen or how analysis proceeded. (This one is often a fair hit — and a standard you should hold your own work to.)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reading a small-n quote as a frequency claim. “Participants felt X” is evidence of existence and meaning, not of prevalence. Don’t silently convert it into a percentage.
  • Demanding statistical generalisation from a case study. It generalises to theory; judging it by external-validity rules it never claimed is a category error.
  • Mistaking vividness for validity. A compelling quote is data, not proof — check it against the trustworthiness criteria (was there triangulation? an audit trail?).
  • Treating “qualitative” as “unrigorous.” Saturation, constant comparison, and audit trails are rigour — just a different rigour than a standard error.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Use qualitative work to choose what to measure. Before fielding a survey, a handful of interviews often reveals the categories — and the wording — your instrument should use.
  • When you cite a qualitative finding, cite its mechanism, not a rate. “Interviews suggested that fear of stigma drove non-uptake” is the right use; “30% said stigma” usually isn’t.
  • Ask for the audit trail. When refereeing or reading qualitative work, the presence of explicit sampling logic and a coding record is your single best quality signal.
  • Match the verb to the evidence. Qualitative data suggests, illuminates, contextualises; it rarely estimates or proves.

::: {.callout-note collapse="true" title="Who says what"}
- **Bryman, *Social Research Methods*** — the four method families (ethnography/participant observation,
  qualitative interviewing, focus groups, qualitative content/document analysis); overt vs covert and the
  field-note grades; the role continuum and "going native"; the interview guide and probing; interaction
  as the focus-group's data; latent vs manifest content and the authenticity/credibility/representativeness/
  meaning tests for documents; **grounded theory** (open/axial/selective coding, constant comparison,
  theoretical sampling & saturation); **Lincoln & Guba's trustworthiness** (credibility, transferability,
  dependability, confirmability) and **authenticity**; and the four quantitative critiques with the
  qualitative replies.
:::

## Practitioner's notes — tips & tricks {.unnumbered}

- **Use qualitative work to choose what to measure.** Before fielding a survey, a handful of interviews
  often reveals the categories — and the wording — your instrument should use.
- **When you cite a qualitative finding, cite its mechanism, not a rate.** "Interviews suggested that
  *fear of stigma* drove non-uptake" is the right use; "30% said stigma" usually isn't.
- **Ask for the audit trail.** When refereeing or reading qualitative work, the presence of explicit
  sampling logic and a coding record is your single best quality signal.
- **Match the verb to the evidence.** Qualitative data *suggests, illuminates, contextualises*; it rarely
  *estimates* or *proves*.
