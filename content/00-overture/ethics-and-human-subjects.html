Ethics and the Human-Subjects Frame

We study people. That carries obligations — settled before the first datum, not after.

Quantitative social science measures and sometimes intervenes in real human lives, so it is bound by commitments that precede any technique: don’t harm, get informed consent, protect privacy, justify any deception. And when you run experiments in the field, a second layer of dilemmas appears — because now your study is changing the world it observes.

Why it matters

Ethics is not a form you sign at the end; it is a design constraint that shapes the study from the start. A finding obtained unethically is not a triumph with an asterisk — it is a failure. Establishing the human-subjects frame here, in the Overture, means every design choice in the chapters ahead carries it by default.

The core principles

Ethical practice governs the relationship between researchers and the people they study, to prevent exploitation and harm (Bryman 2016):

  • Informed consent. Participants get enough information — purpose, risks, and the voluntary nature of taking part — to decide freely, usually documented with an information sheet and consent form.
  • Avoidance of harm. No physical, psychological, or social harm: protect participants from stress, loss of self-esteem, or being induced to do something reprehensible.
  • Privacy and confidentiality. Keep records secure and ensure individuals are not identifiable in anything published.
  • Deception. Representing the research as something it isn’t is sometimes used to elicit natural responses, but it is highly controversial, demands strong justification, and requires debriefing afterward.

The role of ethics review (IRB). Most institutions require an Institutional Review Board to evaluate proposed research: it weighs risks, checks confidentiality safeguards, and ensures participants can withdraw. A common yardstick is the minimal-risk threshold — is the anticipated harm no greater than that of ordinary daily life? (Bryman 2016)

The extra dilemmas of field experiments

Field experiments raise problems lab studies don’t, because they intervene in real settings where people may not know they’re in a study (Gerber and Green 2012). Gerber & Green work through several:

  • Protecting the integrity of random assignment. When you collaborate with agencies (police, schools), well-meaning practitioners may try to “help” the neediest by slipping them into the treatment group — subverting randomisation and destroying the study’s logic.
  • The “no-harm” design principle: encourage, don’t deny. Induce what you believe to be helpful treatments. Studying SAT-prep benefits? Randomly encourage people to take the class — don’t randomly prevent willing people from taking it.
  • Stepped-wedge designs to protect the control group. When a treatment is expected to help, give it to everyone eventually but randomise the timing; those still waiting serve as the controls. No one is permanently denied a beneficial treatment.
  • Informed-consent waivers. Where informing people would destroy the very “naturalness” being studied, an IRB may waive consent — if risk is minimal and the research couldn’t otherwise be done.
  • Third-party responses. Other actors can react to your experiment. If an NGO notices you’ve placed female leaders in certain villages and redirects its aid to male-led ones, it both contaminates the study and raises a fairness question about who gets help.
  • Tangible vs intangible harm. Judging harm is hard when the outcome is beliefs or opinions (e.g. nudging someone’s political or religious views) rather than physical or financial loss.
TipEthics as design, not paperwork

Notice that every field-experiment safeguard is a design move — encourage-don’t-deny, stepped-wedge timing, protecting randomisation. Ethics and good design are the same craft seen from two angles: a study that treats people well is usually a study that yields cleaner inferences.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Consent as a checkbox. A signed form without genuine understanding isn’t informed consent.
  • Deception without debriefing. If you must mislead, you must explain afterward — skipping that is indefensible.
  • Letting practitioners “fix” the assignment. Compassionate tampering with randomisation is still tampering; it destroys the causal claim and the fairness rationale.
  • Ignoring third-party contamination. Outside actors reacting to your treatment can wreck both ethics and validity at once.
  • Denying a beneficial treatment to a control group when a stepped-wedge design would have given it to everyone eventually.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Design the ethics in from day one. Ask “who could this harm, and how would I know?” while choosing the design, not after.

  • Prefer encouragement designs. Randomly offering a likely-beneficial treatment is almost always more defensible than randomly withholding one.

  • Reach for the stepped wedge whenever the treatment is expected to help — it dissolves the “control group is denied benefit” objection.
