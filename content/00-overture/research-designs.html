A Field Guide to Research Designs

The design decides what you’re allowed to conclude — and no technique can rescue the wrong one.

A research design is the skeleton of a study: when you collect data, from whom, and with what comparison. It sets a hard ceiling on the strength of any conclusion you can draw. Choose it before you analyse anything, because the most sophisticated statistics in the world cannot buy back a causal claim a cross-sectional design was never able to make.

Why it matters

Beginners think credibility comes from the analysis. It comes from the design. The design determines whether you can say “X causes Y” or only “X and Y go together.” This entry is the map of the main designs and what each can — and cannot — license. It’s also the bridge to the summit of the manual: causal inference is, at heart, the art of designing (or reconstructing) a fair comparison.

The designs, and what each buys you

A research design reflects the priority you place on causal connection, generalisation, and understanding change over time (Bryman 2016).

Experimental

Manipulate an independent variable and use random assignment to make the treatment and control groups comparable.

  • Strengths: the benchmark for internal validity — it rules out alternative causal explanations. Field experiments, run in real settings, add ecological validity.
  • Limits: many social variables (gender, ethnicity, class) can’t be manipulated for ethical or practical reasons; lab experiments can suffer reactive effects when people know they’re studied, hurting generalisability.

Cross-sectional / survey

Data on many cases at a single point in time, to find patterns of association.

  • Strengths: ideal for non-manipulable variables (age, background); with random probability sampling, strong external validity.
  • Limits: weak internal validity — everything is measured at once, so the direction of causation is ambiguous. It shows relationships, not causes.

Longitudinal / panel

The same sample studied on ≥2 occasions (panel = same people; cohort = people sharing a trait, e.g. birth week).

  • Strengths: establishes temporal order, enabling stronger causal inference than cross-sectional designs; essential for charting social change.
  • Limits: expensive and slow; suffers attrition as participants drop out, which can make the survivors unrepresentative.

Comparative

Two or more contrasting cases (e.g. nations) studied with near-identical methods.

  • Strengths: reveals how a phenomenon varies across socio-cultural settings, guarding against findings that are culturally specific.
  • Limits: hard to ensure data are truly comparable across contexts (translation of instruments is a classic trap).

Case study

Intensive analysis of a single case — a community, organisation, or person.

  • Strengths: deep, idiographic insight; supports analytic generalisation (to theory, not population).
  • Limits: weak external validity — one case rarely represents a wider population.

The two rulers: internal vs external validity

Every design is judged on two different axes, and they often trade off:

  • Internal validity — confidence the IV genuinely caused the change in the DV (Bryman 2016). Experiments win (a control group neutralises threats like history and testing effects); cross-sectional designs are weak (association ≠ sequence).
  • External validity — whether results generalise beyond the study (Bryman 2016). Large random-sample surveys win; lab experiments often lose (artificial settings, unrepresentative participants — frequently students). Field experiments sit in the sweet spot: real participants and contexts give them stronger external validity than the lab while keeping randomisation’s internal validity.
TipThere’s usually no free lunch

The lab maximises internal validity at the cost of external; the survey does the reverse. Much of research craft is choosing which validity your question can’t live without, and paying the other’s price knowingly. Field experiments are prized precisely because they compromise least.

Why the randomised experiment is the benchmark

When you can run one, randomisation is a near-magical tool — it is the standard every other design is measured against (Gerber and Green 2012). It works because random assignment makes treatment statistically independent of everything about the subjects:

  • Comparable groups / a fair test. No systematic advantage to treatment or control; with a large enough sample, individual differences wash out (Gerber and Green 2012).
  • Eliminates confounders — even unobserved ones. Randomisation severs the link between treatment and every variable that predicts the outcome, solving the lurking-variable problem that plagues observational research (Gerber and Green 2012). This is the part no statistical “control” can match: you can only control for confounders you thought of and measured; randomisation handles the ones you didn’t.
  • Unbiased estimation. Replicated infinitely, the average estimate equals the true treatment effect (Gerber and Green 2012).

Concrete example — women heading Indian village councils. Because seats were randomly reserved for female leaders (by law), any difference in, say, sanitation spending between those councils and male-led ones could be attributed to the leader’s gender rather than to pre-existing village differences (Gerber and Green 2012). Randomisation turned an unanswerable observational question into a credible causal one. (You’ll meet this study’s statistics again in sampling distributions.)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Causal claims from cross-sectional data. “X correlates with Y at one time point” is not “X causes Y.” This is the most common over-reach in applied social science.
  • Ignoring attrition in panels. If drop-out is related to the outcome, your surviving sample silently biases the result.
  • Generalising a case study to a population. Case studies generalise to theory, not to people-in-general.
  • Trusting a lab result in the wild. High internal validity doesn’t imply the effect survives outside the artificial setting.
  • Assuming you can always randomise. Often you can’t (ethics, law, practicality) — which is exactly why Part V exists: to approximate the experiment you couldn’t run.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Bryman, Social Research Methods — the five designs and their strengths/limits; internal vs external (and ecological) validity; how each design fares on each.
  • Gerber & Green, Field Experiments — randomisation as the benchmark for causal claims; statistical independence, comparable groups, elimination of observed and unobserved confounders, unbiased estimation; the Indian village-council example.

Practitioner’s notes — tips & tricks

  • Start from the conclusion you need, then pick the design. If you need “X causes Y,” a survey won’t do — design for the claim, don’t retrofit it.
  • State your validity priority explicitly. Write down whether internal or external validity matters more for this decision; it resolves most design arguments.
  • When you can’t randomise, say so — and switch to causal-inference tools. The honest move is to name the missing experiment and adopt a design (Part V) that approximates it.
  • Budget for attrition up front in any longitudinal study; plan retention like you plan sampling.
