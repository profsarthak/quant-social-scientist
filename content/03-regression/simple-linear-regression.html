Simple Linear Regression: The Line of Best Fit

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

From a cloud of points to an equation — and from correlation to a number you can interpret.

Correlation tells you whether two things move together; regression tells you by how much, with an equation. Fit a line through a scatter so that it misses the points by as little as possible, and you have the single most-used tool in empirical social science — and, it turns out, an estimate of the conditional expectation you met in Part I.

Why it matters

Almost every empirical claim in the social sciences is, under the hood, a regression coefficient: the return to a year of schooling, the effect of class size on test scores, the gender wage gap. Where correlation gives a single unitless number, regression gives a formula — \hat y = \hat\beta_0 + \hat\beta_1 x — whose slope you can read in real units and whose machinery extends, in the next entries, to holding other things constant. (Wooldridge 2019) This entry builds the one-predictor case completely: what the line means, how it’s chosen, and how to tell whether it fits.

The model

The simple linear regression model describes a relationship in the population: (Wooldridge 2019)

y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u.

  • y is the dependent (outcome, explained) variable; x is the independent (regressor, explanatory) variable. (Wooldridge 2019)
  • \beta_0 is the intercept — the expected y when x=0.
  • \beta_1 is the slope, the parameter of interest: the ceteris paribus effect of x on y.
  • u is the error term — everything else that affects y but isn’t x (all the unobserved factors). (Wooldridge 2019)

Interpreting the slope is the whole game: a one-unit increase in x is associated with a \beta_1 change in y, on average — formally, holding the unobservables fixed (\Delta u = 0), \Delta y = \beta_1 \Delta x. (Wooldridge 2019)

TipRegression is the conditional expectation function

This connects straight back to conditional expectation. Under the zero conditional mean assumption — the unobservables average out to zero at every value of x, E[u\mid x]=0 — the regression line is the CEF: (Wooldridge 2019)

E[y \mid x] = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x.

So the regression line threads the average value of y for every slice of x. That is why regression is the best predictor machinery made practical — and why E[u\mid x]=0 (which we scrutinise in R3) is the assumption that does all the work.

Ordinary least squares

We never see the population line; we estimate it from a sample of n points. Ordinary least squares (OLS) picks the line that makes the total squared miss as small as possible — it chooses \hat\beta_0, \hat\beta_1 to minimise the sum of squared residuals: (Wooldridge 2019)

\min_{\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1} \sum_{i=1}^n \big(y_i - \hat\beta_0 - \hat\beta_1 x_i\big)^2.

Why squared? Squaring makes every miss positive (so they can’t cancel), and penalises big misses disproportionately. Solving this minimisation (calculus in the appendix) gives two beautifully interpretable formulas: (Wooldridge 2019)

\hat\beta_1 = \frac{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i (x_i-\bar x)^2} = \frac{\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(x,y)}{\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(x)}, \qquad \hat\beta_0 = \bar y - \hat\beta_1 \bar x.

The slope is just the covariance of x and y divided by the variance of x — co-movement, rescaled by how much x itself varies. The intercept then sits wherever it must for the line to pass through the average point (\bar x, \bar y).

Least squares, made visible

The picture below is the method: OLS slides and tilts a line until the sum of the squared vertical gaps (the residuals, in red) is as small as possible. No other line through this cloud makes that total smaller.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
n = 70
educ = rng.integers(6, 19, n).astype(float)            # years of schooling
wage = -0.90 + 0.54 * educ + rng.normal(0, 1.6, n)     # WAGE1-style relationship

# OLS by the covariance/variance formula
b1 = np.cov(educ, wage, bias=True)[0, 1] / np.var(educ)
b0 = wage.mean() - b1 * educ.mean()
fit = b0 + b1 * educ

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.2))
ax.vlines(educ, np.minimum(wage, fit), np.maximum(wage, fit),
          color="#c0392b", lw=0.9, alpha=0.7)          # residuals
ax.scatter(educ, wage, s=22, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.8, zorder=3)
xs = np.linspace(6, 18, 50)
ax.plot(xs, b0 + b1 * xs, color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.4,
        label=f"OLS:  wage = {b0:.2f} + {b1:.2f}·educ")
ax.set_xlabel("years of education"); ax.set_ylabel("hourly wage ($)")
ax.set_title("OLS minimises the total squared residual (red)")
ax.legend(fontsize=9, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Fitted values, residuals, and OLS’s built-in properties

For each observation the line gives a fitted value \hat y_i = \hat\beta_0 + \hat\beta_1 x_i (the prediction) and a residual \hat u_i = y_i - \hat y_i (the miss). (Wooldridge 2019) By construction, OLS guarantees three algebraic facts that hold for any dataset: (Wooldridge 2019)

  • The residuals sum to zero, \sum_i \hat u_i = 0 — positive and negative misses cancel exactly.
  • The residuals are uncorrelated with x — the line has wrung all the linear information out of x; whatever is left is, by definition, unrelated to it.
  • The line passes through (\bar x, \bar y) — plug in the average x and you get the average y.

These aren’t assumptions to check; they’re identities baked into the least-squares solution.

Goodness of fit: R² and the standard error

How much of y’s variation did the line actually capture? Decompose the total variation exactly as ANOVA does — the same between/within split: (Wooldridge 2019)

\underbrace{\sum_i (y_i-\bar y)^2}_{\text{SST (total)}} = \underbrace{\sum_i (\hat y_i-\bar y)^2}_{\text{SSE (explained)}} + \underbrace{\sum_i \hat u_i^2}_{\text{SSR (residual)}}.

The coefficient of determination is the explained share: (Wooldridge 2019)

R^2 = \frac{\text{SSE}}{\text{SST}} = 1 - \frac{\text{SSR}}{\text{SST}} \in [0,1].

An R^2 of 0.60 means x explains 60% of the sample variation in y. (Wooldridge 2019) Two facts worth carrying: in simple regression R^2 is exactly the square of the correlation coefficient, R^2 = r_{xy}^2 — regression and correlation measuring the same association two ways; and the standard error of the regression, \text{SER} = \sqrt{\text{SSR}/(n-2)}, estimates the typical vertical distance of points from the line, in the units of y. (Wooldridge 2019) (Low R^2 is not failure: in social data, individual behaviour is noisy, and a small-but-precise slope can still be the answer you want.)

A fully worked example: wage and education

Wooldridge’s canonical case, estimated on n = 526 workers (the WAGE1 data): (Wooldridge 2019)

\widehat{\text{wage}} = -0.90 + 0.54\,\cdot\,\text{educ}, \qquad R^2 = 0.171.

  • Slope 0.54: each additional year of schooling predicts about 54 cents more per hour. Put comparatively (the honest framing): comparing two groups one year of schooling apart, the more-schooled group earns about $0.54/hour more on average. (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Intercept -0.90: literally, zero education predicts a negative wage — nonsensical, a reminder that the intercept is often just a mathematical anchor, not a meaningful x=0 value. (Wooldridge 2019)
  • R^2 = 0.171: education explains about 17% of the variation in wages. The other 83% lives in u — ability, experience, luck, industry — which is exactly why we move to multiple regression next. (Wooldridge 2019)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reading the slope as causal. \hat\beta_1 is an association unless E[u\mid x]=0 holds — and with one regressor it rarely does (ability is in u and correlates with education). The causal reading waits on much more. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Over-interpreting the intercept. \hat\beta_0 is the fitted value at x=0, often outside the data’s range and meaningless (the -0.90 wage). Don’t force a story onto it. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Worshipping R^2. A high R^2 doesn’t make a model correct or causal, and a low R^2 doesn’t make a slope wrong. Social-behaviour models routinely have small R^2 and useful coefficients. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Extrapolating beyond the data. The line is fitted where the points are; predictions far outside the observed x range are unsupported.

  • Forgetting the squared-miss objective. OLS minimises squared residuals, so it is sensitive to outliers — one extreme point can swing the line.

Appendix · the mathematics

Deriving the OLS estimators. Minimise S(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1)=\sum_i(y_i-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 x_i)^2. The first-order conditions (set partial derivatives to zero) are the normal equations:

\sum_i (y_i-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 x_i)=0, \qquad \sum_i x_i(y_i-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 x_i)=0.

The first gives \hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x (the line through the means). Substituting into the second and simplifying gives \hat\beta_1=\dfrac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}. The two normal equations are the algebraic properties “residuals sum to zero” and “residuals uncorrelated with x.”

Why R^2=r_{xy}^2 in simple regression. With \hat y_i-\bar y=\hat\beta_1(x_i-\bar x), \text{SSE}=\hat\beta_1^2\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2. Insert \hat\beta_1=S_{xy}/S_{xx}: \text{SSE}=S_{xy}^2/S_{xx}, so R^2=\text{SSE}/\text{SST}=S_{xy}^2/(S_{xx}S_{yy})=r_{xy}^2.

The SER and degrees of freedom. \text{SER}=\sqrt{\text{SSR}/(n-2)}; the n-2 is the two degrees of freedom spent estimating \hat\beta_0 and \hat\beta_1, making \text{SER}^2 an unbiased estimate of \operatorname{Var}(u).

Connections

  • Builds on: conditional expectation (the regression line as the CEF / best predictor), covariance & correlation (the slope is Cov/Var; R^2=r^2), and estimation (OLS is an estimator).
  • Leads to: multiple regression (adding regressors to hold things constant and shrink u), and inference for coefficients (standard errors and tests on \hat\beta_1).
  • Mirrors: the ANOVA decomposition (SST = SSE + SSR is the between/within split) and the law of total variance.

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Reading the slope as causal.** $\hat\beta_1$ is an *association* unless $E[u\mid x]=0$ holds — and
  with one regressor it rarely does (ability is in $u$ and correlates with education). The
  [causal reading waits](regression-and-causality.qmd) on much more. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Over-interpreting the intercept.** $\hat\beta_0$ is the fitted value at $x=0$, often outside the
  data's range and meaningless (the $-0.90$ wage). Don't force a story onto it. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Worshipping $R^2$.** A high $R^2$ doesn't make a model correct or causal, and a low $R^2$ doesn't
  make a slope wrong. Social-behaviour models routinely have small $R^2$ and useful coefficients.
  [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Extrapolating beyond the data.** The line is fitted where the points are; predictions far outside
  the observed $x$ range are unsupported.

- **Forgetting the squared-miss objective.** OLS minimises *squared* residuals, so it is sensitive to
  [outliers](../01-foundations/messy-real-data.qmd) — one extreme point can swing the line.

:::

  minimising the SSR; the slope $=\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(x,y)/\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(x)$

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Deriving the OLS estimators.** Minimise $S(\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1)=\sum_i(y_i-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 x_i)^2$.
The first-order conditions (set partial derivatives to zero) are the **normal equations**:

$$
\sum_i (y_i-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 x_i)=0, \qquad \sum_i x_i(y_i-\hat\beta_0-\hat\beta_1 x_i)=0.
$$

The first gives $\hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x$ (the line through the means). Substituting into
the second and simplifying gives
$\hat\beta_1=\dfrac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}$. The two normal equations
*are* the algebraic properties "residuals sum to zero" and "residuals uncorrelated with $x$."

**Why $R^2=r_{xy}^2$ in simple regression.** With $\hat y_i-\bar y=\hat\beta_1(x_i-\bar x)$,
$\text{SSE}=\hat\beta_1^2\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2$. Insert $\hat\beta_1=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$:
$\text{SSE}=S_{xy}^2/S_{xx}$, so
$R^2=\text{SSE}/\text{SST}=S_{xy}^2/(S_{xx}S_{yy})=r_{xy}^2$.

**The SER and degrees of freedom.** $\text{SER}=\sqrt{\text{SSR}/(n-2)}$; the $n-2$ is the two
[degrees of freedom](../02-inference/estimation.qmd) spent estimating $\hat\beta_0$ and $\hat\beta_1$,
making $\text{SER}^2$ an unbiased estimate of $\operatorname{Var}(u)$.

:::
