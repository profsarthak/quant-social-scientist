Functional Form: Logs, Polynomials, Dummies & Interactions

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A straight line is a choice. Bend it, branch it, and shift it — and learn to read the coefficients you get.

“Linear regression” is linear in the parameters, not the variables — which means you can fit curves, percentage effects, group differences, and effects-that-depend-on-other-effects, all with OLS. The catch is interpretation: a coefficient on \log x, on x^2, on a 0/1 dummy, or on an interaction each means something different. This entry is the dictionary.

Why it matters

Real relationships are rarely straight lines through raw variables. Income is skewed and its effects are percentage effects; experience pays off with diminishing returns; the wage gap is a difference between groups; the return to education might differ by gender. Each is captured by a change of functional form — and each changes how you read \hat\beta. (Wooldridge 2019) Getting this wrong is one of the most common errors in applied work: reporting a log coefficient as a dollar effect, or a quadratic’s first coefficient as “the” effect. The forms below cover the vast majority of applied specifications.

Logarithms: percentages and elasticities

Taking logs does three jobs at once: it tames skew in positive variables, it encodes constant-percentage effects, and (in log-log form) it yields elasticities. (Wooldridge 2019) The interpretation depends on what is logged:

Model y x Interpretation of \beta_1
Level–level y x \Delta y = \beta_1 \Delta x (a unit change)
Level–log y \log x a 1% rise in x changes y by \beta_1/100
Log–level \log y x a one-unit rise in x changes y by 100\beta_1 percent
Log–log \log y \log x a 1% rise in x changes y by \beta_1 percent — an elasticity

The “100\beta_1 percent” reading is an approximation (the semi-elasticity) that’s excellent for small coefficients; for large ones, use the exact percentage change 100\,[\exp(\hat\beta_1)-1]. (Wooldridge 2019)

NoteTwo Wooldridge workhorses

Returns to education (log–level). \widehat{\log(\text{wage})} = 0.584 + 0.083\,\text{educ} (n=526, R^2=0.186): each extra year of schooling raises wages by about 8.3% — a constant percentage return, which is why log-wage is the standard outcome. (Wooldridge 2019)

CEO pay (log–log). \widehat{\log(\text{salary})} = 4.822 + 0.257\,\log(\text{sales}): the estimated elasticity is 0.257 — a 1% increase in firm sales is associated with a 0.257% increase in CEO salary. (Wooldridge 2019)

Quadratics: diminishing returns and turning points

To let an effect change as x grows, add a squared term: (Wooldridge 2019)

y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 x^2 + u.

Now the effect of x is not constant — it’s the derivative, \Delta y \approx (\beta_1 + 2\beta_2 x)\,\Delta x, which depends on where you start. (Wooldridge 2019) When \beta_1 and \beta_2 have opposite signs the curve has a turning point — a peak or trough — at

x^* = \left|\frac{\beta_1}{2\beta_2}\right|.

NoteWage and experience

Wooldridge’s WAGE1 estimate: \widehat{\text{wage}} = 3.73 + 0.298\,\text{exper} - 0.0061\,\text{exper}^2. The first year of experience adds about 30 cents, but the negative squared term means each further year adds less. The peak is at x^* = 0.298/[2(0.0061)] \approx \mathbf{24.4} years — after which predicted wages actually decline. Reporting only the +0.298 would badly mislead. (Wooldridge 2019)

Dummy variables: differences between groups

A dummy (indicator) variable is 1 if a condition holds, 0 otherwise, and it shifts the intercept between groups. (Wooldridge 2019) Its coefficient is the average difference between that group and the omitted base category, holding the other regressors fixed. (Wooldridge 2019)

NoteThe gender wage gap

\widehat{\text{wage}} = -1.57 - 1.81\,\text{female} + 0.572\,\text{educ} + \cdots — holding education and other factors constant, women earn about $1.81 less per hour than men, the base group. (Wooldridge 2019)

One trap: with an overall intercept and a full set of mutually exclusive categories, you must omit one as the reference — including all of them plus the intercept creates perfect collinearity (the dummy-variable trap, an MLR.3 violation). (Wooldridge 2019)

Interactions: when one effect depends on another

Sometimes the effect of x_1 isn’t fixed — it depends on x_2. Capture that with their product: (Wooldridge 2019)

y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \beta_3 (x_1\cdot x_2) + u.

Now the partial effect of x_2 is \beta_2 + \beta_3 x_1 — it moves with x_1. (Wooldridge 2019) The interaction coefficient \beta_3 answers “does the slope of one variable change as the other changes?”

NoteDoes the return to education differ by gender?

\log(\text{wage}) = \beta_0 + \delta_0\,\text{female} + \beta_1\,\text{educ} + \delta_1(\text{female}\cdot\text{educ}) + u. Here \beta_1 is the return to education for men, \beta_1 + \delta_1 the return for women, and \delta_1 is the difference in returns. A significant \delta_1 means the wage–education profiles are not parallel — education pays differently across groups. (Wooldridge 2019)

Seeing curves and branches

Two functional forms that change the shape of the fit: a quadratic (an effect that peaks) and an interaction (slopes that differ by group).

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.2, 3.8))

# Quadratic: wage = 3.73 + 0.298 exper - 0.0061 exper^2
exper = np.linspace(0, 45, 200)
wage = 3.73 + 0.298 * exper - 0.0061 * exper ** 2
xstar = 0.298 / (2 * 0.0061)
axL.plot(exper, wage, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.4)
axL.axvline(xstar, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.4)
axL.text(xstar + 0.6, wage.min() + 0.3, f"turning point\n≈ {xstar:.1f} yrs",
         color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axL.set_title("Quadratic: diminishing returns to experience")
axL.set_xlabel("years of experience"); axL.set_ylabel("predicted wage ($)")

# Interaction: steeper return to education for one group
educ = np.linspace(8, 20, 50)
axR.plot(educ, 1.0 + 0.40 * educ, color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.4, label="Group A (steeper return)")
axR.plot(educ, 1.0 + 0.25 * educ, color="#d98a29", lw=2.4, label="Group B (flatter return)")
axR.set_title("Interaction: non-parallel slopes")
axR.set_xlabel("years of education"); axR.set_ylabel("predicted log wage")
axR.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reading a log coefficient as a level effect. \hat\beta = 0.083 in a log-wage model is 8.3%, not 8.3 cents. Match the interpretation to the form. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Reporting a quadratic’s linear term as ‘the effect.’ With x^2 in the model the effect is \beta_1+2\beta_2 x — it varies with x. Quote the marginal effect at meaningful values and the turning point. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Falling into the dummy-variable trap. Omit one category as the base; never include a full set of dummies and an intercept. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Interpreting a main effect alone when an interaction is present. With x_1\cdot x_2 in the model, \beta_1 is the effect of x_1 only when x_2=0. The full effect is \beta_1+\beta_3 x_2. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Adding curves to chase R². Higher-order terms can overfit; include them for substantive reasons or when diagnostics (RESET) flag misspecification, not reflexively.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Wooldridge, Introductory Econometrics — the four log forms and their interpretations (semi-elasticity 100\beta vs the exact 100[\exp(\beta)-1]; log-wage 8.3%/year; log-log CEO-pay elasticity 0.257); quadratics, the marginal effect \beta_1+2\beta_2x and turning point |\beta_1/2\beta_2| (wage–experience peak at 24.4 years); dummy variables, the base category, the gender-gap example (−$1.81), and the dummy-variable trap; interaction terms, the partial effect \beta_2+\beta_3x_1, and the education-by-gender example with non-parallel slopes.

Appendix · the mathematics

Why log-level is a percentage. Differentiate \log y = \beta_0+\beta_1 x: d(\log y) = \beta_1\,dx, and d(\log y)=dy/y is the fractional change in y. So \%\Delta y \approx 100\beta_1\,\Delta x. For a discrete change the exact figure is 100[\exp(\beta_1\Delta x)-1], which exceeds 100\beta_1\Delta x as the coefficient grows (the gap is the curvature of \exp).

Why log-log is an elasticity. With \log y=\beta_0+\beta_1\log x, \dfrac{dy/y}{dx/x}=\beta_1 — the ratio of percentage changes, i.e. the elasticity, constant at every x.

The turning point. For \hat y=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1x+\hat\beta_2x^2, set the derivative \hat\beta_1+2\hat\beta_2 x=0 to get x^*=-\hat\beta_1/(2\hat\beta_2); it is a maximum when \hat\beta_2<0, a minimum when \hat\beta_2>0.

Interaction = two-line model. Writing the female-interaction model by group: for men (\text{female}=0) the slope on educ is \beta_1; for women (\text{female}=1) it is \beta_1+\delta_1, and the intercept shifts by \delta_0. The single regression with an interaction is two regression lines estimated jointly.

Connections

  • Builds on: transformations of random variables (logs and the log-normal), inference for coefficients (testing \delta_1, \beta_2), and multiple regression (these are all multiple regressions with constructed regressors).
  • Connects to: comparing groups — a regression on a set of group dummies reproduces the ANOVA F-test, so dummies are how regression absorbs ANOVA.
  • Leads to: diagnostics (RESET catches the wrong functional form) and limited dependent variables (a different nonlinearity — in the outcome).

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why log-level is a percentage.** Differentiate $\log y = \beta_0+\beta_1 x$:
$d(\log y) = \beta_1\,dx$, and $d(\log y)=dy/y$ is the *fractional* change in $y$. So
$\%\Delta y \approx 100\beta_1\,\Delta x$. For a discrete change the exact figure is
$100[\exp(\beta_1\Delta x)-1]$, which exceeds $100\beta_1\Delta x$ as the coefficient grows (the gap is
the curvature of $\exp$).

**Why log-log is an elasticity.** With $\log y=\beta_0+\beta_1\log x$,
$\dfrac{dy/y}{dx/x}=\beta_1$ — the ratio of percentage changes, i.e. the elasticity, constant at every
$x$.

**The turning point.** For $\hat y=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1x+\hat\beta_2x^2$, set the derivative
$\hat\beta_1+2\hat\beta_2 x=0$ to get $x^*=-\hat\beta_1/(2\hat\beta_2)$; it is a maximum when
$\hat\beta_2<0$, a minimum when $\hat\beta_2>0$.

**Interaction = two-line model.** Writing the female-interaction model by group: for men ($\text{female}=0$)
the slope on educ is $\beta_1$; for women ($\text{female}=1$) it is $\beta_1+\delta_1$, and the
intercept shifts by $\delta_0$. The single regression with an interaction *is* two regression lines
estimated jointly.

:::
