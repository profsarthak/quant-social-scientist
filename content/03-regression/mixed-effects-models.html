Mixed-Effects & Multilevel Models

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When your data is nested — students in schools, measures within people — ordinary regression quietly lies about its precision. Model the grouping itself, and let small groups borrow strength from the whole.

A huge share of social data is nested: pupils within classrooms within schools, patients within hospitals, repeated measures within a person. Observations in the same group share unmeasured influences, so they are not independent — and ordinary regression, blind to this, reports standard errors that are far too small. Mixed-effects (multilevel, hierarchical) models fix it by splitting effects into a fixed part shared by all and a random part that varies by group — and, as a bonus, they let data-poor groups borrow strength from data-rich ones.

Why it matters

OLS assumes independent observations. (Li 2023) Clustered data breaks that assumption: students in the same class share a teacher and environment, making them more alike than students in other classes. (Jacobucci et al. 2023) The consequence is precise but subtle — OLS stays unbiased, but it is inefficient and its standard errors are wrong, usually badly underestimated, because your effective sample size is closer to the number of clusters than the number of individuals. (Li 2023) Report those SEs and you will claim significance you haven’t earned. Mixed models are the principled response — and the natural model whenever the clustering that diagnostics only patched is something you’d rather understand.

Fixed and random effects

A mixed model literally mixes two kinds of coefficient: (Li 2023)

  • Fixed effects are the same for every unit — the average return to education across the whole population.
  • Random effects are allowed to vary across clusters, drawn from a distribution the model estimates.

Two flavours cover most needs: (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Random intercepts — each group gets its own baseline. Graphically: parallel lines, one per school, shifted up or down. (Some schools start higher.)
  • Random slopes — the effect of a predictor varies by group: the return to education might differ for every person, so each group’s line gets its own tilt.

y_{ij} = \underbrace{\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{ij}}_{\text{fixed}} + \underbrace{u_{j}}_{\text{random intercept}} + \varepsilon_{ij}, \qquad u_j \sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma_u^2),\ \ \varepsilon_{ij}\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma_e^2).

Here i indexes individuals, j their group; u_j is the group’s random departure from the common intercept.

Partial pooling: the big idea

The conceptual heart, and what makes multilevel models more than “regression with cluster dummies.” There’s a spectrum of how to treat groups: (Lambert 2018)

  • Complete pooling — ignore groups, fit one global mean. Simple, but “too simplistic,” and it reports “insufficient uncertainty.” (Lambert 2018)
  • No pooling — a separate estimate per group (a dummy per school). It overfits, especially for small groups, where a handful of noisy observations produce a wild estimate. (Lambert 2018)
  • Partial pooling (the multilevel model) — assume the group parameters share a common distribution, u_j \sim \mathcal N(\mu, \sigma_u^2), and estimate that distribution and the groups together. (Lambert 2018)

The payoff of partial pooling is shrinkage: each group’s estimate is pulled toward the overall mean, and small or extreme groups shrink most — they have the least information of their own, so they lean hardest on the crowd. (Lambert 2018) This is how data-poor groups “borrow statistical strength” from data-rich ones, (Jacobucci et al. 2023) trading a little bias for a large cut in variance — exactly the bias–variance bargain of regularization, here arising naturally from the grouping.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
grand = 50.0          # true global mean
tau   = 8.0           # between-group SD (true spread of group means)
sigma = 12.0          # within-group SD (noise per observation)

n_groups = 30
sizes = rng.integers(2, 60, n_groups)                 # group sizes vary a lot
true_means = grand + rng.normal(0, tau, n_groups)
raw_means = true_means + rng.normal(0, sigma / np.sqrt(sizes))   # noisy group means

# Partial-pooling (shrinkage) weight: reliability of each group's own mean
#   w_j = tau^2 / (tau^2 + sigma^2/n_j)  -> small groups get small w (shrink more)
w = tau**2 / (tau**2 + sigma**2 / sizes)
pooled = w * raw_means + (1 - w) * grand

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.4, 4.2))
ax.axhline(grand, color="#9a6a2f", ls="--", lw=1.4, label="complete pooling (global mean)")
for s, r, p in zip(sizes, raw_means, pooled):
    ax.annotate("", xy=(s, p), xytext=(s, r),
                arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#9aa0a6", lw=1.0))
ax.scatter(sizes, raw_means, s=22, color="#c0392b", label="raw group mean (no pooling)", zorder=3)
ax.scatter(sizes, pooled, s=22, color="#1f3a5f", label="partial-pooled estimate", zorder=3)
ax.set_xlabel("group size $n_j$"); ax.set_ylabel("estimated group mean")
ax.set_title("Small, noisy groups shrink toward the global mean")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Variance components and the ICC

Because the model separates group-level from individual-level randomness, it decomposes the total variance into a between-group part \sigma_u^2 and a within-group part \sigma_e^2. (Li 2023) Their ratio is the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC): \rho = \frac{\sigma_u^2}{\sigma_u^2 + \sigma_e^2}, the proportion of total variation attributable to the grouping. (Li 2023) A high \rho means group members behave very similarly — so sampling more individuals from the same group adds little new information (and ignoring the clustering would most badly mislead your SEs). (Li 2023) The ICC is both a substantive finding (“how much is about the school?”) and a diagnostic for how much clustering matters.

Random effects vs fixed effects: the Hausman choice

Econometrics frames the same structure as a choice of estimator, and it comes with a famous test: (Li 2023)

  • The random-effects (RE) estimator is more efficient — it estimates the variance of the group intercepts rather than N-1 separate dummy coefficients. But it is consistent only if the group effect is uncorrelated with the regressors. (Li 2023)
  • The fixed-effects (FE) estimator (the panel workhorse) makes no such assumption — it sweeps the group effect away by within-group differencing — at the cost of efficiency and of dropping anything time-invariant.

The Hausman test decides between them: it compares the FE and RE estimates, and if their difference is systematic (significant), the RE exogeneity assumption has failed and you should trust the FE within-group variation instead. (Li 2023) In causal language: RE is a modelling convenience; FE is the one that defends against a confounded grouping.

The Bayesian view: hierarchy all the way up

Mixed models are most natural in the Bayesian framework, because a hierarchy is just a prior extended to multiple levels. (Lambert 2018) Instead of fixing the group distribution’s parameters, you give them their own hyperpriors and infer them from the data — the data tell you how much groups vary, and therefore how much to shrink. (Lambert 2018) The reward is honest uncertainty: the hierarchical posterior reflects the true uncertainty at every level — individual, classroom, school — rather than a single point estimate that pretends the group means were known. (Lambert 2018) Partial pooling, from this angle, isn’t a trick; it’s what Bayes does when you admit the groups are exchangeable draws from a population.

The social-science bridge

NoteMixed models in the wild
  • Education. Pupils nested in classes nested in schools — the canonical case; school quality and state policy induce exactly the dependence multilevel models are built for (SAT/test-score studies). (Lambert 2018; Jacobucci et al. 2023)
  • Tennessee STAR. The class-size experiment is the standard cautionary tale: randomisation was at the classroom level, so honest inference must account for clustering there. (Li 2023)
  • Labour economics. Wage equations where unobserved ability is a person-level effect u_i — the same object panel fixed effects sweep away. (Wooldridge 2019; Li 2023)
  • Repeated measures / growth. Rat growth curves, spinal bone-mineral density across ages and ethnicities — measures within an individual, modelled with random effects per subject. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Ignoring clustering entirely. OLS SEs on nested data are “usually significantly underestimated” — the effective n is the number of clusters. Cluster, or model the levels. (Li 2023)

  • No pooling on small groups. A dummy per group overfits tiny groups into noise; partial pooling shrinks them sensibly. (Lambert 2018)

  • Using RE without checking exogeneity. RE is inconsistent if the group effect correlates with a regressor; run the Hausman test and fall back to FE when it rejects. (Li 2023)

  • Forgetting random slopes. If an effect plausibly varies by group, a random-intercept-only model understates uncertainty in that effect. (Jacobucci et al. 2023)

  • Reading shrinkage as a bug. A shrunken small-group estimate is more accurate, not censored — it’s the bias–variance trade working for you. (Lambert 2018)

Appendix · the mathematics

The variance-components model. With y_{ij}=\mathbf x_{ij}^\top\beta + u_j + \varepsilon_{ij}, u_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma_u^2), \varepsilon_{ij}\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma_e^2), two observations in the same group j have covariance \operatorname{Cov}(y_{ij},y_{i'j})=\sigma_u^2, so their correlation is \rho=\sigma_u^2/(\sigma_u^2+\sigma_e^2) — the ICC. OLS ignores this nonzero off-diagonal covariance, which is precisely why its SEs are wrong; GLS/REML weights by it.

Shrinkage, exactly. The partial-pooled estimate of group j’s mean is the precision-weighted average of its own mean \bar y_j and the grand mean \mu: \hat\theta_j = w_j\,\bar y_j + (1-w_j)\,\mu,\qquad w_j=\frac{\sigma_u^2}{\sigma_u^2+\sigma_e^2/n_j}. w_j\to 1 for large n_j (trust the group) and w_j\to 0 for small n_j (trust the crowd) — the algebra of “small groups shrink most,” and the curve drawn in the figure.

RE vs FE. FE estimates \beta from within-group deviations y_{ij}-\bar y_j, eliminating u_j and so needing no assumption about \operatorname{Cov}(u_j,\mathbf x). RE (FGLS) uses both within- and between-group variation, gaining efficiency but requiring \operatorname{Cov}(u_j,\mathbf x)=0. Hausman compares \hat\beta_{FE}-\hat\beta_{RE} to its variance; a large gap rejects RE.

## The Bayesian view: hierarchy all the way up

Mixed models are **most natural in the Bayesian framework**, because a hierarchy is just a
[prior](../02-inference/bayesian-machinery.qmd) **extended to multiple levels.** [@lambert_bayes] Instead of
fixing the group distribution's parameters, you give *them* their own **hyperpriors** and infer them from the
data — the data tell you how much groups vary, and therefore how much to shrink. [@lambert_bayes] The reward
is honest uncertainty: the hierarchical posterior reflects the *true* uncertainty at **every level** —
individual, classroom, school — rather than a single point estimate that pretends the group means were known.
[@lambert_bayes] Partial pooling, from this angle, isn't a trick; it's what Bayes *does* when you admit the
groups are exchangeable draws from a population.

## The social-science bridge

::: {.callout-note title="Mixed models in the wild"}
- **Education.** Pupils nested in classes nested in schools — the canonical case; school quality and state
  policy induce exactly the dependence multilevel models are built for (SAT/test-score studies).
  [@lambert_bayes; @jacobucci_ml]
- **Tennessee STAR.** The class-size experiment is the standard cautionary tale: randomisation was at the
  **classroom** level, so honest inference *must* account for clustering there. [@dongli_panel]
- **Labour economics.** Wage equations where unobserved **ability** is a person-level effect $u_i$ — the same
  object panel [fixed effects](../05-causal-inference/panel-and-fixed-effects.qmd) sweep away. [@wooldridge_intro; @dongli_panel]
- **Repeated measures / growth.** Rat growth curves, spinal bone-mineral density across ages and ethnicities —
  measures within an individual, modelled with random effects per subject. [@jacobucci_ml]
:::

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Ignoring clustering entirely.** OLS SEs on nested data are *"usually significantly underestimated"* — the
  effective $n$ is the number of clusters. Cluster, or model the levels. [@dongli_panel]

- **No pooling on small groups.** A dummy per group overfits tiny groups into noise; partial pooling shrinks
  them sensibly. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Using RE without checking exogeneity.** RE is inconsistent if the group effect correlates with a
  regressor; run the **Hausman test** and fall back to FE when it rejects. [@dongli_panel]

- **Forgetting random *slopes*.** If an effect plausibly varies by group, a random-intercept-only model
  understates uncertainty in that effect. [@jacobucci_ml]

- **Reading shrinkage as a bug.** A shrunken small-group estimate is *more accurate*, not censored — it's the
  bias–variance trade working for you. [@lambert_bayes]

:::

## From the sources {.unnumbered}

::: {.callout-note collapse="true" title="Who says what"}
- **Dong Li, *Panel Data Econometrics*** — clustered data breaks OLS independence (unbiased but inefficient,
  **underestimated SEs**, effective $n$ ≈ number of clusters); **variance components** and the **ICC**
  $\rho=\sigma_u^2/(\sigma_u^2+\sigma_e^2)$; the **RE estimator** (efficient; consistent only under
  exogeneity) and the **Hausman test** (FE vs RE); ability as a person-level effect; Tennessee STAR
  clustering.
- **Lambert, *A Student's Guide to Bayesian Statistics*** — **complete / no / partial pooling**; **shrinkage**
  toward the mean; hierarchical models as priors extended over levels with **hyperpriors**; honest
  multi-level uncertainty.
- **Jacobucci, Grimm & Zhang, *ML for Social and Behavioral Research*** — multilevel structure (students in
  classes/schools), **random intercepts/slopes**, borrowing statistical strength; repeated-measures/growth
  examples.
- **Wooldridge, *Introductory Econometrics*** — unobserved individual effects in wage equations; the link to
  fixed-effects panel estimation.
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The variance-components model.** With $y_{ij}=\mathbf x_{ij}^\top\beta + u_j + \varepsilon_{ij}$,
$u_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma_u^2)$, $\varepsilon_{ij}\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma_e^2)$, two observations in the
same group $j$ have covariance $\operatorname{Cov}(y_{ij},y_{i'j})=\sigma_u^2$, so their **correlation** is
$\rho=\sigma_u^2/(\sigma_u^2+\sigma_e^2)$ — the ICC. OLS ignores this nonzero off-diagonal covariance, which
is precisely why its SEs are wrong; GLS/REML weights by it.

**Shrinkage, exactly.** The partial-pooled estimate of group $j$'s mean is the precision-weighted average of
its own mean $\bar y_j$ and the grand mean $\mu$:
$$
\hat\theta_j = w_j\,\bar y_j + (1-w_j)\,\mu,\qquad
w_j=\frac{\sigma_u^2}{\sigma_u^2+\sigma_e^2/n_j}.
$$
$w_j\to 1$ for large $n_j$ (trust the group) and $w_j\to 0$ for small $n_j$ (trust the crowd) — the algebra of
"small groups shrink most," and the curve drawn in the figure.

**RE vs FE.** FE estimates $\beta$ from within-group deviations $y_{ij}-\bar y_j$, eliminating $u_j$ and so
needing **no** assumption about $\operatorname{Cov}(u_j,\mathbf x)$. RE (FGLS) uses both within- and
between-group variation, gaining efficiency but requiring $\operatorname{Cov}(u_j,\mathbf x)=0$. Hausman
compares $\hat\beta_{FE}-\hat\beta_{RE}$ to its variance; a large gap rejects RE.

:::
