Multiple Regression & “Holding Constant”

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The central trick of empirical social science: comparing like with like when you can’t run an experiment.

You can’t randomize the world. Richer districts have both smaller classes and better labs, so a raw comparison of class sizes confounds the two. Multiple regression is how observational researchers approximate “all else equal” — adding control variables to hold confounders constant and isolate the one relationship they care about. It is the workhorse, and its central idea is simpler than its reputation.

Why it matters

The simple regression slope folds every other influence into the error term u — and if any of those influences correlates with your x, the slope is contaminated. (Wooldridge 2019) Multiple regression pulls the big confounders out of u and into the model, so each coefficient can be read ceteris paribus — holding the others fixed. This is the move that lets you ask “the effect of education at the same level of ability,” or “spending at the same level of poverty.” (Angrist and Pischke 2015) Master it and most applied empirical work becomes legible; misunderstand it and you will read confounded slopes as effects.

The model and the ceteris paribus reading

The multiple linear regression model adds regressors: (Wooldridge 2019)

y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + u.

The whole payoff is in the interpretation of each slope:

\beta_j is the change in y for a one-unit increase in x_j, holding all the other regressors fixed. (Wooldridge 2019)

That “holding fixed” is the ceteris paribus clause, and it is why regression matters in the social sciences. (Wooldridge 2019) You cannot literally freeze the world to test class size, because the schools with small classes differ in a dozen other ways. (Angrist and Pischke 2015) But you can statistically compare observations identical on every included dimension — schools with the same budget, students with the same prior scores — and that comparison is what each coefficient reports. This is how researchers make “other things equal” claims from data that was never an experiment. (Angrist and Pischke 2015)

How regression “holds constant”: partialling out

What does the machinery actually do to hold things fixed? The Frisch–Waugh–Lovell result — “regression anatomy” — gives the exact, beautiful answer. Any multiple-regression coefficient \hat\beta_1 can be reconstructed in three steps: (Wooldridge 2019; Angrist and Pischke 2015)

  1. Cleanse the regressor. Regress x_1 on all the other controls and keep the residuals \tilde x_1 — the part of x_1 that the controls can’t explain.
  2. Cleanse the outcome. Regress y on the same controls and keep its residuals \tilde y.
  3. Link the leftovers. Regress \tilde y on \tilde x_1. That slope is exactly \hat\beta_1.

The lesson is profound: a multiple-regression coefficient uses only the variation in x_1 that is orthogonal to the other controls. (Wooldridge 2019) It throws away any part of x_1 that moves together with the controls — that’s precisely what “holding them constant” means.

See partialling-out at work

Below, school spending and test scores both depend on a confounder (district poverty): poorer districts spend less and score lower. The naive scatter (left) shows a steep slope — but much of it is poverty masquerading as a spending effect. The partial-regression plot (right), after residualising both variables on poverty, reveals the flatter true slope — spending’s effect among districts with the same poverty level.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(8)
n = 200
poverty = rng.uniform(0, 1, n)                       # the confounder Z
spend   = 8 - 3 * poverty + rng.normal(0, 0.6, n)    # poorer districts spend less
true_beta = 2.0
scores  = 40 + true_beta * spend - 25 * poverty + rng.normal(0, 2.5, n)

def ols_slope(x, y):
    return np.cov(x, y, bias=True)[0, 1] / np.var(x)
def resid_on(z, v):                                  # residual of v after regressing on z
    b = ols_slope(z, v); a = v.mean() - b * z.mean()
    return v - (a + b * z)

naive = ols_slope(spend, scores)
sx, sy = resid_on(poverty, spend), resid_on(poverty, scores)
partial = ols_slope(sx, sy)

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.2, 4.0))
axL.scatter(spend, scores, s=14, color="#b7b6a8", alpha=0.6)
xs = np.linspace(spend.min(), spend.max(), 40)
axL.plot(xs, scores.mean() + naive * (xs - spend.mean()), color="#e8531d", lw=2.4)
axL.set_title(f"Raw: slope ≈ {naive:.1f}  (confounded)")
axL.set_xlabel("school spending"); axL.set_ylabel("test score")

axR.scatter(sx, sy, s=14, color="#161613", alpha=0.6)
xr = np.linspace(sx.min(), sx.max(), 40)
axR.plot(xr, sy.mean() + partial * (xr - sx.mean()), color="#5e5e54", lw=2.4)
axR.set_title(f"Poverty held constant: slope ≈ {partial:.1f}  (true ≈ {true_beta})")
axR.set_xlabel("spending (poverty partialled out)")
axR.set_ylabel("score (poverty partialled out)")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Adjustment: accounting for variation

There is a second, complementary way to say what “holding constant” does — Daniel Kaplan’s framing in Lessons in Statistical Thinking, and a useful one because it carries straight into the causal chapters. Kaplan calls this move adjustment, and sets it inside a bigger idea: accounting for variation. (Kaplan 2024) Variation is the raw material of statistics — “without variation there would be no need for statistical thinking” — and a model’s job is to split it into a signal (the part a model function explains) and noise (the leftover). (Kaplan 2024)

In those terms, a confounder is variation in the wrong column. District poverty makes spending and scores move together; left in the error term, that shared variation gets misread as a spending effect. To adjust for poverty is to move its variation out of the noise and into the explained part of the model — so the spending coefficient is computed only from the variation poverty can’t account for. This is the same residual-variation story as partialling out above, and it is exactly why adding a relevant covariate raises R^2 (just below): R^2 is the fraction of variation the model now accounts for. (Kaplan 2024)

Hold onto the word adjustment: it is the hinge of the DAG chapter. Adjusting for the right covariate blocks a confounding path (good control); adjusting for the wrong one — a mediator or a collider — opens a spurious one (the bad controls below). “Accounting for variation” and “closing a backdoor” are one act seen from two sides. (Kaplan 2024)

Omitted-variable bias

The flip side of “holding constant” is what happens when you fail to. Leave out a variable that (a) affects y and (b) correlates with an included regressor, and its influence gets forced into that regressor’s coefficient — omitted-variable bias. (Wooldridge 2019) The formula is worth memorising because it also tells you the direction: (Angrist and Pischke 2015)

\underbrace{\beta_{\text{short}}}_{\text{omit the control}} = \underbrace{\beta_{\text{long}}}_{\text{true effect}} + \;\pi\,\gamma,

where \gamma is the omitted variable’s effect on y (in the full model) and \pi is the slope from regressing the omitted variable on the included one. So:

\text{sign of bias} = \text{sign}(\gamma)\times\text{sign}(\pi).

NoteAbility bias in the returns to schooling

Regress wage on education but omit ability. Ability raises wages (\gamma > 0) and is positively correlated with education (\pi > 0). So the bias is (+)\times(+) = (+): the simple-regression education coefficient is biased upward — it overstates the true return to schooling, because some of what looks like a payoff to education is really a payoff to the ability that tends to come with it. (Angrist and Pischke 2015) This single example is why “the return to education” was argued about for decades.

Good controls, bad controls

Adding a control changes another coefficient whenever the new variable correlates with both that regressor and the outcome. (Angrist and Pischke 2015) But not all controls are good:

  • Good controls are predetermined — fixed before the treatment (demographics, pre-treatment test scores, baseline characteristics). They remove confounding and, as a bonus, soak up error variance and sharpen your estimate. (Angrist and Pischke 2015)
  • Bad controls are themselves outcomes of the treatment. In a wage regression, controlling for occupation is a trap: education helps determine your occupation, so holding occupation fixed closes off one of the very channels through which education raises wages — you’ve thrown away part of the effect you wanted to measure. (Angrist and Pischke 2015) “Control for everything” is not the rule; control for the right things.

A fully worked example: school spending and test scores

Wooldridge’s MEAP93 data, 408 Michigan high schools, math pass rate on spending. (Wooldridge 2019) First the simple regression:

\widehat{\text{math10}} = -69.34 + 11.16\,\log(\text{expend}).

Taken at face value, a 10% spending increase buys about a 1.12-point rise in pass rates. But high-spending schools sit in wealthier districts — classic omitted-variable territory. Add poverty (free-lunch eligibility, lnchprg) and enrollment: (Wooldridge 2019)

\widehat{\text{math10}} = -23.14 + 7.75\,\log(\text{expend}) - 0.324\,\text{lnchprg} - 1.26\,\log(\text{enroll}).

Holding poverty and enrollment constant, the spending effect drops from 11.16 to 7.75. The OVB logic explains the direction exactly: poverty is negatively correlated with spending (\pi < 0) and negatively affects scores (\gamma < 0), and (-)\times(-) = (+) — so the simple regression was biased upward. Money mattered less than the naive number implied, once you compared schools at the same poverty level. (Wooldridge 2019)

Goodness of fit with many regressors: adjusted R²

A trap waits in the R^2 once you have several regressors: adding any regressor — even a useless, random one — never lowers R^2, and almost always raises it. (Wooldridge 2019) OLS can always exploit a new variable’s chance correlation with the residuals to nudge the fit up, so R^2 rewards kitchen-sink models and is useless for deciding whether a variable belongs. The adjusted R^2 (\bar R^2) fixes this by penalising extra regressors, dividing each sum of squares by its degrees of freedom: (Wooldridge 2019)

\bar R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{SSR}/(n-k-1)}{\text{SST}/(n-1)} = 1 - (1 - R^2)\,\frac{n-1}{n-k-1}.

Adding a regressor lowers SSR (good for \bar R^2) but also spends a degree of freedom (bad). So \bar R^2 rises only if the new variable improves the fit by more than chance would predict — it can and does fall when you add junk. This makes \bar R^2 a rough tool for comparing models with different numbers of regressors, where raw R^2 would always favour the bigger one. Two cautions: a higher \bar R^2 is not a licence to believe a model is correctly specified or causal (the whole next several entries), and it should only be compared across models with the same dependent variable. (Wooldridge 2019)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • “Control for everything.” Bad controls (post-treatment variables) introduce bias rather than remove it. Adding regressors is not automatically safer — it can close the channel you’re studying. (Angrist and Pischke 2015)

  • Reading a controlled coefficient as definitively causal. Holding observed confounders fixed does nothing about unobserved ones (ability is still in u). Selection-on-observables is an assumption, not a guarantee — the causal entry presses this. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Forgetting OVB has a direction. You can often sign the bias before collecting more data (\text{sign}(\gamma)\,\text{sign}(\pi)) — a powerful sanity check on whether your estimate is too high or too low. (Angrist and Pischke 2015)

  • Confusing “holding constant” with “the data has constant values.” Partialling-out uses only the residual variation; if a regressor barely varies once controls are in, its coefficient is imprecise — the multicollinearity issue of diagnostics.

  • Interpreting each coefficient in isolation from its set. A coefficient’s meaning is “holding these specific controls fixed.” Change the controls and you change what the number means.

Appendix · the mathematics

The partialling-out formula. For y = \beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\beta_2 x_2+u, the FWL theorem gives

\hat\beta_1 = \frac{\sum_i \tilde x_{1i}\,y_i}{\sum_i \tilde x_{1i}^2},

where \tilde x_{1i} are the residuals from regressing x_1 on the other regressors. Only the variation in x_1 orthogonal to the controls enters — the algebraic meaning of “holding the others fixed.”

Omitted-variable bias, derived. True model y=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2+u; you instead run the short regression of y on x_1 alone. Then

E[\tilde\beta_1] = \beta_1 + \beta_2\,\delta_1, \qquad \delta_1 = \frac{\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(x_1,x_2)}{\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(x_1)},

with \delta_1 the slope of x_2 on x_1. In the notation above \gamma=\beta_2, \pi=\delta_1, so \text{bias}=\pi\gamma and its sign is \text{sign}(\beta_2)\,\text{sign}(\delta_1).

Bad controls, sketched. If x_2 is itself caused by x_1 (post-treatment), then conditioning on x_2 blocks the indirect path x_1\to x_2\to y, so \hat\beta_1 no longer captures the total effect of x_1 — and can even pick up collider bias. Predetermined controls don’t have this problem because x_1 cannot have caused them.

```{python}
    return np.cov(x, y, bias=True)[0, 1] / np.var(x)
def resid_on(z, v):                                  # residual of v after regressing on z
    b = ols_slope(z, v); a = v.mean() - b * z.mean()
    return v - (a + b * z)

naive = ols_slope(spend, scores)
sx, sy = resid_on(poverty, spend), resid_on(poverty, scores)
partial = ols_slope(sx, sy)

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.2, 4.0))
axL.scatter(spend, scores, s=14, color="#b7b6a8", alpha=0.6)
xs = np.linspace(spend.min(), spend.max(), 40)
axL.plot(xs, scores.mean() + naive * (xs - spend.mean()), color="#e8531d", lw=2.4)
axL.set_title(f"Raw: slope ≈ {naive:.1f}  (confounded)")
axL.set_xlabel("school spending"); axL.set_ylabel("test score")

axR.scatter(sx, sy, s=14, color="#161613", alpha=0.6)
xr = np.linspace(sx.min(), sx.max(), 40)
axR.plot(xr, sy.mean() + partial * (xr - sx.mean()), color="#5e5e54", lw=2.4)
axR.set_title(f"Poverty held constant: slope ≈ {partial:.1f}  (true ≈ {true_beta})")
axR.set_xlabel("spending (poverty partialled out)")
axR.set_ylabel("score (poverty partialled out)")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Adjustment: accounting for variation

There is a second, complementary way to say what "holding constant" does — Daniel Kaplan's framing in
*Lessons in Statistical Thinking*, and a useful one because it carries straight into the causal chapters.
Kaplan calls this move **adjustment**, and sets it inside a bigger idea: **accounting for variation.**
[@kaplan_lessons] Variation is the raw material of statistics — *"without variation there would be no need
for statistical thinking"* — and a model's job is to split it into a **signal** (the part a *model
function* explains) and **noise** (the leftover). [@kaplan_lessons]

In those terms, a confounder is variation in the wrong column. District poverty makes spending and scores
move together; left in the error term, that shared variation gets **misread as a spending effect.** To
**adjust for** poverty is to move its variation *out of the noise* and *into the explained part* of the
model — so the spending coefficient is computed only from the variation poverty can't account for. This is
the same residual-variation story as [partialling out](#how-regression-holds-constant-partialling-out)
above, and it is exactly why adding a relevant covariate raises $R^2$ (just below): $R^2$ *is* the
fraction of variation the model now accounts for. [@kaplan_lessons]

Hold onto the word **adjustment**: it is the hinge of the
[DAG chapter](../05-causal-inference/directed-acyclic-graphs.qmd). Adjusting for the *right* covariate
blocks a confounding path (good control); adjusting for the *wrong* one — a mediator or a collider — opens
a spurious one (the bad controls below). "Accounting for variation" and "closing a backdoor" are one act
seen from two sides. [@kaplan_lessons]

## Omitted-variable bias

The flip side of "holding constant" is what happens when you *fail* to. Leave out a variable that
(a) affects $y$ and (b) correlates with an included regressor, and its influence gets **forced into**
that regressor's coefficient — **omitted-variable bias**. [@wooldridge_intro] The formula is worth
memorising because it also tells you the *direction*: [@angrist_mastering]

$$
\underbrace{\beta_{\text{short}}}_{\text{omit the control}} =
\underbrace{\beta_{\text{long}}}_{\text{true effect}} + \;\pi\,\gamma,
$$

where $\gamma$ is the omitted variable's effect on $y$ (in the full model) and $\pi$ is the slope from
regressing the *omitted* variable on the *included* one. So:

$$
\text{sign of bias} = \text{sign}(\gamma)\times\text{sign}(\pi).
$$

::: {.callout-note title="Ability bias in the returns to schooling"}
Regress wage on education but omit **ability**. Ability raises wages ($\gamma > 0$) and is positively
correlated with education ($\pi > 0$). So the bias is $(+)\times(+) = (+)$: the simple-regression
education coefficient is biased **upward** — it overstates the true return to schooling, because some
of what looks like a payoff to education is really a payoff to the ability that tends to come with it.
[@angrist_mastering] This single example is why "the return to education" was argued about for
decades.
:::

## Good controls, bad controls

Adding a control changes another coefficient whenever the new variable correlates with both that
regressor and the outcome. [@angrist_mastering] But not all controls are good:

- **Good controls** are **predetermined** — fixed *before* the treatment (demographics, pre-treatment
  test scores, baseline characteristics). They remove confounding and, as a bonus, soak up error
  variance and *sharpen* your estimate. [@angrist_mastering]
- **Bad controls** are themselves **outcomes of the treatment**. In a wage regression, controlling for
  *occupation* is a trap: education helps determine your occupation, so holding occupation fixed
  **closes off one of the very channels** through which education raises wages — you've thrown away
  part of the effect you wanted to measure. [@angrist_mastering] "Control for everything" is *not*
  the rule; control for the right things.

## A fully worked example: school spending and test scores

Wooldridge's MEAP93 data, 408 Michigan high schools, math pass rate on spending. [@wooldridge_intro]
First the simple regression:

$$
\widehat{\text{math10}} = -69.34 + 11.16\,\log(\text{expend}).
$$

Taken at face value, a 10% spending increase buys about a 1.12-point rise in pass rates. But
high-spending schools sit in wealthier districts — classic omitted-variable territory. Add poverty
(free-lunch eligibility, `lnchprg`) and enrollment: [@wooldridge_intro]

$$
\widehat{\text{math10}} = -23.14 + 7.75\,\log(\text{expend}) - 0.324\,\text{lnchprg} - 1.26\,\log(\text{enroll}).
$$

Holding poverty and enrollment constant, the spending effect **drops from 11.16 to 7.75**. The OVB
logic explains the direction exactly: poverty is *negatively* correlated with spending ($\pi < 0$) and
*negatively* affects scores ($\gamma < 0$), and $(-)\times(-) = (+)$ — so the simple regression was
biased **upward**. Money mattered less than the naive number implied, once you compared schools at the
*same* poverty level. [@wooldridge_intro]

## Goodness of fit with many regressors: adjusted R²

A trap waits in the [$R^2$](simple-linear-regression.qmd) once you have several regressors:
**adding any regressor — even a useless, random one — never lowers $R^2$, and almost always raises it.**
[@wooldridge_intro] OLS can always exploit a new variable's chance correlation with the residuals to
nudge the fit up, so $R^2$ rewards *kitchen-sink* models and is useless for deciding *whether a variable
belongs*. The **adjusted $R^2$** ($\bar R^2$) fixes this by **penalising extra regressors**, dividing
each sum of squares by its degrees of freedom: [@wooldridge_intro]

$$
\bar R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{SSR}/(n-k-1)}{\text{SST}/(n-1)} = 1 - (1 - R^2)\,\frac{n-1}{n-k-1}.
$$

Adding a regressor lowers SSR (good for $\bar R^2$) but also spends a degree of freedom (bad). So
$\bar R^2$ rises only if the new variable improves the fit by *more than chance would predict* — it can
and does **fall** when you add junk. This makes $\bar R^2$ a rough tool for **comparing models** with
different numbers of regressors, where raw $R^2$ would always favour the bigger one. Two cautions: a
higher $\bar R^2$ is *not* a licence to believe a model is correctly specified or causal (the whole
[next several entries](regression-and-causality.qmd)), and it should only be compared across models with
the *same dependent variable*. [@wooldridge_intro]

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **"Control for everything."** Bad controls (post-treatment variables) introduce bias rather than
  remove it. Adding regressors is not automatically safer — it can close the channel you're studying.
  [@angrist_mastering]

- **Reading a controlled coefficient as definitively causal.** Holding *observed* confounders fixed
  does nothing about *unobserved* ones (ability is still in $u$). Selection-on-observables is an
  assumption, not a guarantee — the [causal entry](regression-and-causality.qmd) presses this.
  [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Forgetting OVB has a direction.** You can often *sign* the bias before collecting more data
  ($\text{sign}(\gamma)\,\text{sign}(\pi)$) — a powerful sanity check on whether your estimate is too
  high or too low. [@angrist_mastering]

- **Confusing "holding constant" with "the data has constant values."** Partialling-out uses only the
  residual variation; if a regressor barely varies once controls are in, its coefficient is
  imprecise — the multicollinearity issue of [diagnostics](regression-diagnostics.qmd).

- **Interpreting each coefficient in isolation from its set.** A coefficient's meaning is "holding
  *these specific* controls fixed." Change the controls and you change what the number means.

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The partialling-out formula.** For $y = \beta_0+\beta_1 x_1+\beta_2 x_2+u$, the FWL theorem gives

$$
\hat\beta_1 = \frac{\sum_i \tilde x_{1i}\,y_i}{\sum_i \tilde x_{1i}^2},
$$

where $\tilde x_{1i}$ are the residuals from regressing $x_1$ on the other regressors. Only the
variation in $x_1$ *orthogonal* to the controls enters — the algebraic meaning of "holding the others
fixed."

**Omitted-variable bias, derived.** True model $y=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2+u$; you instead run the
short regression of $y$ on $x_1$ alone. Then

$$
E[\tilde\beta_1] = \beta_1 + \beta_2\,\delta_1,
\qquad \delta_1 = \frac{\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(x_1,x_2)}{\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(x_1)},
$$

with $\delta_1$ the slope of $x_2$ on $x_1$. In the notation above $\gamma=\beta_2$, $\pi=\delta_1$,
so $\text{bias}=\pi\gamma$ and its sign is $\text{sign}(\beta_2)\,\text{sign}(\delta_1)$.

**Bad controls, sketched.** If $x_2$ is itself caused by $x_1$ (post-treatment), then conditioning on
$x_2$ blocks the indirect path $x_1\to x_2\to y$, so $\hat\beta_1$ no longer captures the *total*
effect of $x_1$ — and can even pick up [collider](regression-and-causality.qmd) bias. Predetermined
controls don't have this problem because $x_1$ cannot have caused them.

:::
