The Gauss–Markov Assumptions & BLUE

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The five conditions that turn a fitted line into a trustworthy estimate — and the one that does all the causal work.

OLS always produces a line. Whether that line tells the truth depends on a short list of assumptions. Four of them make OLS unbiased; a fifth makes it efficient; together they make it BLUE — the best linear unbiased estimator. One assumption, exogeneity, carries the entire causal burden, and it is the one that fails most often.

Why it matters

You have seen OLS fit a line and read off coefficients. But “the computer printed 0.54” is not the same as “0.54 is the truth.” Between them sit the Gauss–Markov assumptions — the conditions under which the OLS estimator is unbiased and as precise as any linear unbiased rule can be. (Wooldridge 2019) Knowing them is not pedantry: it is how you diagnose when to believe a regression and, just as importantly, why a coefficient might be wrong. The assumptions also name the threats — omitted variables, simultaneity, measurement error — that the rest of econometrics and all of causal inference exist to address.

The five assumptions

Wooldridge’s assumptions for multiple regression on cross-sectional data: (Wooldridge 2019)

Assumption What it says
MLR.1 Linear in parameters The population model is y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + u.
MLR.2 Random sampling The n observations are a random sample from that population.
MLR.3 No perfect collinearity No regressor is constant, and none is an exact linear combination of the others.
MLR.4 Zero conditional mean (exogeneity) E[u \mid x_1,\dots,x_k] = 0 — the unobservables average to zero at every combination of the regressors.
MLR.5 Homoskedasticity \operatorname{Var}(u \mid x_1,\dots,x_k) = \sigma^2 — the error variance is constant across the regressors.

A word on each of the load-bearing two. MLR.3 doesn’t forbid correlated regressors — only perfectly collinear ones (you can’t separate the effects of “years of education” and “months of education”). MLR.4 is the heart of everything: it says x carries no information about the unobserved u, so the part of y that moves with x is genuinely x’s doing and not some lurking factor’s. (Wooldridge 2019)

What each assumption buys

The division of labour is clean and worth memorising: (Wooldridge 2019)

  • MLR.1–MLR.4 give unbiasedness: E[\hat\beta_j] = \beta_j. Across many random samples, the OLS estimate averages to the true parameter. Note that homoskedasticity is not needed for unbiasedness — a heteroskedastic model still has unbiased coefficients.
  • MLR.5 buys the usual variance formula: homoskedasticity is what makes the standard-error formulas your software prints (and the tests built on them) valid. Without it the coefficients are still unbiased but the standard errors are wrong — the problem robust standard errors fix.

The Gauss–Markov theorem: OLS is BLUE

Put MLR.1–MLR.5 together and you get the central justification for OLS. (Wooldridge 2019)

Gauss–Markov theorem. Under MLR.1–MLR.5, OLS is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE).

Unpack the acronym: (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Linear — the estimator is a linear function of the outcomes y.
  • Unbiased — it’s right on average, E[\hat\beta_j] = \beta_j.
  • Best — among all linear unbiased estimators, OLS has the smallest variance. No other linear unbiased rule is more precise.

So OLS is not an arbitrary choice; it is provably the most efficient option in its class. The picture makes “Best” concrete: a rival linear-unbiased estimator (here, a simple grouping estimator) is also centred on the truth, but it scatters far more than OLS.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
true_slope, n, reps = 1.0, 40, 5000
ols, grouping = np.empty(reps), np.empty(reps)

for r in range(reps):
    x = rng.uniform(0, 10, n)
    y = 2 + true_slope * x + rng.normal(0, 3, n)
    # OLS slope
    ols[r] = np.cov(x, y, bias=True)[0, 1] / np.var(x)
    # rival: split at median x, slope through the two group means (linear & unbiased)
    hi = x >= np.median(x)
    grouping[r] = (y[hi].mean() - y[~hi].mean()) / (x[hi].mean() - x[~hi].mean())

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.7))
bins = np.linspace(-0.5, 2.5, 70)
ax.hist(grouping, bins=bins, density=True, color="#d98a29", alpha=0.7,
        label=f"Grouping estimator (SD {grouping.std():.2f})")
ax.hist(ols, bins=bins, density=True, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.8,
        label=f"OLS (SD {ols.std():.2f})")
ax.axvline(true_slope, color="#b5446e", ls="--", lw=1.8, label="true slope = 1")
ax.set_yticks([]); ax.set_xlabel("estimated slope")
ax.set_title("Both unbiased — OLS is the minimum-variance (Best) one")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Exogeneity: the assumption that does the causal work

If you remember one thing, remember this: MLR.4 (exogeneity) is the assumption that licenses a causal reading, and it is the most fragile. (Wooldridge 2019) When E[u\mid x]\neq 0, the regressor is endogenous: it carries information about the unobservables, so the slope mixes x’s true effect with whatever lurks in u. Three classic ways it fails: (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Omitted variables. A relevant factor left out of the model and correlated with a regressor — ability in a wage-on-education regression. Its effect contaminates the education coefficient (the OVB of the last entry). (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Simultaneity. x causes y and y causes x — price and quantity in a market, where supply and demand determine each other. (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Measurement error. The recorded x is a noisy proxy for the true one, which (as the next-but-one entry shows) typically attenuates the coefficient toward zero. (Wooldridge 2019)

Crucially, no amount of data fixes an exogeneity failure — a bigger sample just pins down the wrong number more precisely. This is exactly why Part V builds an entire arsenal (instruments, experiments, discontinuities) around defending or engineering exogeneity.

What inflates a coefficient’s variance

Even when OLS is BLUE, “best” can still be imprecise. The variance of an OLS slope is (Wooldridge 2019)

\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\text{SST}_j\,(1 - R_j^2)},

and reading off the three knobs tells you what hurts precision:

  • Error variance \sigma^2 (numerator): more unexplained noise → fuzzier estimates. Good controls reduce it.
  • Regressor variation \text{SST}_j: little spread in x_j → hard to see its effect. You can’t estimate the slope of a line from points that barely move horizontally.
  • Multicollinearity R_j^2: how well the other regressors explain x_j. As R_j^2 \to 1 the denominator \to 0 and the variance explodes — when two regressors move together, OLS struggles to apportion credit between them (diagnostics).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Believing a regression because it ran. OLS always produces numbers; they’re only trustworthy if the assumptions — above all exogeneity — hold. The software cannot check MLR.4 for you. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Thinking more data cures bias. Endogeneity bias does not shrink with n. Precision improves; accuracy doesn’t. Only a better design or a credible identification argument helps. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Treating heteroskedasticity as a threat to unbiasedness. It isn’t — MLR.5 affects standard errors, not the coefficients. Use robust SEs, don’t panic about bias. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Confusing ‘no perfect collinearity’ with ‘no correlation.’ Correlated regressors are fine and normal; only exact linear dependence is forbidden. High (not perfect) correlation just inflates variance. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Forgetting BLUE is conditional. “Best” holds within linear unbiased estimators and under the assumptions. Drop exogeneity and OLS isn’t even unbiased; allow nonlinear or biased estimators and something else (e.g. ridge) can beat it on MSE.

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Wooldridge, Introductory Econometrics — the five MLR assumptions (linearity, random sampling, no perfect collinearity, zero conditional mean / exogeneity, homoskedasticity); MLR.1–4 ⟹ unbiasedness while MLR.5 underwrites the usual variance formula; the Gauss–Markov theorem and the meaning of BLUE (linear, unbiased, minimum-variance); exogeneity as the causal workhorse and its three failure modes (omitted variables, simultaneity, measurement error); the slope-variance formula \sigma^2/[\text{SST}_j(1-R_j^2)] and its three drivers.

Appendix · the mathematics

Unbiasedness from exogeneity. Write \hat\beta_1 = \beta_1 + \sum_i w_i u_i where w_i = \tilde x_{1i}/\sum_j \tilde x_{1j}^2 (the partialling-out weights). Taking the conditional expectation and using MLR.4, E[u_i\mid X]=0, gives E[\hat\beta_1\mid X]=\beta_1 — unbiased, regardless of the variance structure. Homoskedasticity (MLR.5) is never used here.

The variance formula. Under MLR.1–MLR.5, \operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_j\mid X) = \sigma^2/\sum_i \tilde x_{ji}^2. Since \sum_i \tilde x_{ji}^2 = \text{SST}_j(1-R_j^2) — total variation in x_j times the fraction not explained by the other regressors — this is the printed formula. Heteroskedasticity breaks it: the correct variance becomes \sum_i w_i^2\sigma_i^2, which is what the White/robust estimator estimates directly.

Gauss–Markov, sketched. Any linear estimator is \sum_i a_i y_i; unbiasedness imposes linear constraints on the a_i. Minimising \operatorname{Var}=\sigma^2\sum_i a_i^2 subject to those constraints (Lagrange) yields exactly the OLS weights a_i=w_i. Hence OLS attains the minimum variance among linear unbiased estimators — the content of “Best.”

Connections

  • Builds on: multiple regression (the model these assumptions govern), estimation (bias, variance, efficiency, BLUE — first met there), and conditional expectation (exogeneity makes the regression line the CEF).
  • Leads to: inference for coefficients (which adds normality, MLR.6, for exact tests), diagnostics (detecting and fixing MLR.5 and collinearity), and regression and causality (exogeneity as the threat).
  • Motivates: the causal-inference summit, whose every method is a strategy for making exogeneity credible.

- **Wooldridge, *Introductory Econometrics*** — the five MLR assumptions (linearity, random sampling,
  no perfect collinearity, zero conditional mean / exogeneity, homoskedasticity); MLR.1–4 ⟹
  unbiasedness while MLR.5 underwrites the usual variance formula; the Gauss–Markov theorem and the
  meaning of BLUE (linear, unbiased, minimum-variance); exogeneity as the causal workhorse and its
  three failure modes (omitted variables, simultaneity, measurement error); the slope-variance formula
  $\sigma^2/[\text{SST}_j(1-R_j^2)]$ and its three drivers.
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Unbiasedness from exogeneity.** Write $\hat\beta_1 = \beta_1 + \sum_i w_i u_i$ where
$w_i = \tilde x_{1i}/\sum_j \tilde x_{1j}^2$ (the partialling-out weights). Taking the conditional
expectation and using MLR.4, $E[u_i\mid X]=0$, gives $E[\hat\beta_1\mid X]=\beta_1$ — unbiased,
*regardless* of the variance structure. Homoskedasticity (MLR.5) is never used here.

**The variance formula.** Under MLR.1–MLR.5,
$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\beta_j\mid X) = \sigma^2/\sum_i \tilde x_{ji}^2$. Since
$\sum_i \tilde x_{ji}^2 = \text{SST}_j(1-R_j^2)$ — total variation in $x_j$ times the fraction *not*
explained by the other regressors — this is the printed formula. Heteroskedasticity breaks it: the
correct variance becomes $\sum_i w_i^2\sigma_i^2$, which is what the
[White/robust estimator](regression-diagnostics.qmd) estimates directly.

**Gauss–Markov, sketched.** Any linear estimator is $\sum_i a_i y_i$; unbiasedness imposes linear
constraints on the $a_i$. Minimising $\operatorname{Var}=\sigma^2\sum_i a_i^2$ subject to those
constraints (Lagrange) yields exactly the OLS weights $a_i=w_i$. Hence OLS attains the minimum
variance among linear unbiased estimators — the content of "Best."

:::
