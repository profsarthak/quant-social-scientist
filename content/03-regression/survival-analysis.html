Survival & Event-History Analysis

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When the outcome is how long until something happens — and some of it hasn’t happened yet. Regression for time-to-event data, where censoring breaks ordinary least squares.

Many social-science outcomes are durations: time until re-arrest, until a job ends, until a degree is finished, until a marriage dissolves. Two things make these special. The clock can’t run backward (time is non-negative), and — the defining complication — when the study ends, some events haven’t happened yet. That’s censoring, and it quietly wrecks ordinary regression. Survival analysis is the regression family built to handle it: the Kaplan–Meier curve describes it, and Cox proportional hazards models the effect of covariates on the rate at which events occur.

Why it matters

Logistic regression extended OLS to binary outcomes; survival analysis extends it to time-to-event outcomes — and it’s indispensable because the alternative fails badly. The reason is censoring, a feature of almost every longitudinal study, and the methods here are the standard tools of event-history / duration analysis across sociology, political science, criminology, and economics. (Wooldridge 2019; James et al. 2021) If you study how long something takes, this is your toolkit.

The problem: censoring

The outcome is a duration T — but for some units the event simply hasn’t occurred by the time the study ends. This is right-censoring: you don’t know T, only that T>t (the parolee wasn’t re-arrested during the study, the employee was still employed at the last follow-up). (James et al. 2021)

Why not just run OLS on the durations? Both naive fixes are biased: (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Drop the censored cases → the remaining sample is non-random (you keep only the fast events), biasing everything.
  • Treat the censoring time as the event time → you record durations that are too short, shrinking coefficient estimates toward zero. (Wooldridge 2019)

Censored observations carry real information — this unit lasted at least this long — and survival methods are built to use that information correctly rather than discard or distort it.

Two functions: survival and hazard

Survival analysis describes a duration distribution through two interrelated functions: (James et al. 2021)

  • The survival function S(t)=\Pr(T>t) — the probability of “surviving” (no event yet) past time t. It starts at 1 and steps downward.
  • The hazard function h(t) — the instantaneous rate of the event at time t, given survival up to t: among those still at risk at t, how fast are events happening right now?

They are two views of the same distribution, linked by h(t)=\frac{f(t)}{S(t)}=\frac{f(t)}{1-F(t)}, the event density divided by the share still at risk. (Wooldridge 2019) The hazard is the natural object for modelling covariate effects — it asks how a predictor changes the moment-to-moment risk of the event.

Kaplan–Meier: describing survival with censoring

The Kaplan–Meier (KM) estimator is the nonparametric, assumption-light estimate of S(t) that handles censoring correctly. (James et al. 2021) It builds S(t) as a step function, taking the product, over each observed event time, of the conditional probability of surviving that instant: at event time t_j with d_j events among n_j units still at risk, \hat S(t)=\prod_{t_j\le t}\Big(1-\frac{d_j}{n_j}\Big). The trick is the risk set n_j: a censored unit stays counted as “at risk” until the moment it is censored, then drops out — so its partial information is used without pretending its event was observed. (James et al. 2021) To compare groups (treated vs control, high vs low skill), the log-rank test asks whether two KM curves differ by more than chance. (James et al. 2021) The figure shows the canonical picture.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(4)

def make_group(rate, cens_rate, n):
    t_event = rng.exponential(1 / rate, n)
    t_cens = rng.exponential(1 / cens_rate, n)
    obs = np.minimum(t_event, t_cens)
    event = (t_event <= t_cens).astype(int)     # 1 = event observed, 0 = censored
    return obs, event

def kaplan_meier(obs, event):
    order = np.argsort(obs); obs, event = obs[order], event[order]
    times = np.unique(obs)
    n = len(obs); surv = 1.0
    xs, ys = [0.0], [1.0]
    for t in times:
        at_risk = np.sum(obs >= t)
        d = np.sum((obs == t) & (event == 1))
        if d > 0:
            surv *= (1 - d / at_risk)
        xs += [t, t]; ys += [ys[-1], surv]       # step down at event time
    return np.array(xs), np.array(ys)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
for rate, cr, color, name in [(0.7, 0.25, "#e8531d", "Group A (higher hazard)"),
                              (0.35, 0.25, "#161613", "Group B (lower hazard)")]:
    obs, event = make_group(rate, cr, 80)
    xs, ys = kaplan_meier(obs, event)
    ax.step(xs, ys, where="post", color=color, lw=2.2, label=name)
    cens = obs[event == 0]
    # ticks at censoring times, placed on the curve height
    for c in cens:
        h = ys[np.searchsorted(xs, c, side="right") - 1]
        ax.plot(c, h, marker="|", color=color, ms=7, mew=1.4)

ax.set_xlabel("time t"); ax.set_ylabel("Ŝ(t) = P(survive past t)")
ax.set_ylim(0, 1.02); ax.set_xlim(0, 8)
ax.set_title("Kaplan–Meier survival curves (ticks = censored)")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Cox proportional hazards regression

KM describes; the Cox proportional-hazards model explains — the workhorse regression for survival data. (James et al. 2021) It models the hazard for unit i as a shared baseline hazard times an exponential function of covariates: h_i(t\mid X)=h_0(t)\,\exp(\beta_1 X_{i1}+\dots+\beta_p X_{ip}). Its genius is being semiparametric: the baseline hazard h_0(t) is left completely unspecified — you make no assumption about the shape of time-dependence — yet you can still estimate the \beta’s (via a partial likelihood that cancels h_0). (James et al. 2021)

Coefficients are read as hazard ratios, \text{HR}=\exp(\beta): (Wooldridge 2019)

  • \beta>0 \Rightarrow \text{HR}>1: the covariate raises the hazard — the event comes sooner (shorter duration).
  • \beta<0 \Rightarrow \text{HR}<1: it lowers the hazard — the event comes later (longer survival).

An HR of 0.91 means each unit of the predictor cuts the moment-to-moment risk by about 9%.

The proportional-hazards assumption

The “PH” is a real assumption: a covariate multiplies the hazard by the same constant factor at every time — the HR is constant over time. (Wooldridge 2019) If a treatment helps early but fades (non-proportional), the model is misspecified. Check it (e.g. Schoenfeld residuals / cox.zph); if it fails, stratify on the offending variable or interact it with time. (Wooldridge 2019)

The social-science bridge, and the caution

These are the duration / event-history models of applied social science. (Wooldridge 2019)

NoteDuration models in the wild
  • Recidivism. In Wooldridge’s study of North Carolina inmates (time until re-arrest), more prior convictions significantly shortens the duration until the next arrest — a higher hazard. (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Unemployment & worker’s comp. Duration models are standard for unemployment spells and policy effects: a Kentucky benefit increase lengthened the time high-earners stayed on workers’ compensation by roughly 19%. (Wooldridge 2019)

And the discipline this Part has hammered applies here too: a hazard ratio is associational. Unless the covariate was randomly assigned, interpret it with exactly the caution owed any OLS coefficient — unobserved confounding and selection bias don’t vanish because the outcome is a duration. (Wooldridge 2019) Cox regression is a superb description of risk over time; turning that into a cause still needs a design.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Ignoring censoring. Dropping censored units biases via selection; coding them as events biases toward zero. Use survival methods that count them at risk until censoring. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Reading the proportional-hazards model without checking PH. If a covariate’s effect changes over time, the constant-HR model is wrong; test it and stratify/interact if needed. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Misreading hazard ratios. \text{HR}>1 means the event comes sooner (shorter survival), not “more of a good thing”; mind the direction. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Treating a hazard ratio as causal. It’s an association unless a design supports causation — the same rule as every other coefficient in this Part. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Informative censoring. KM and Cox assume censoring is unrelated to the event risk; if people drop out because they’re about to fail, the estimates are biased. (James et al. 2021)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Wooldridge, Introductory Econometrics — duration/event-history models; why OLS fails on censored data (selection from dropping; attenuation from coding censored as events); the hazard h(t)=f(t)/[1-F(t)]; Cox hazard ratios and the proportional-hazards assumption (and fixes); the recidivism and unemployment / Kentucky workers’-comp (~19%) examples; hazard ratios as associational.

Appendix · the mathematics

Survival, hazard, and their identity. With density f, CDF F, and S=1-F: h(t)=\frac{f(t)}{S(t)}=-\frac{d}{dt}\log S(t)\;\Longrightarrow\; S(t)=\exp\!\Big(-\!\int_0^t h(u)\,du\Big). The integral \int_0^t h is the cumulative hazard H(t); survival decays as e^{-H(t)}. A constant hazard h(t)=\lambda gives the exponential duration S(t)=e^{-\lambda t} — the memoryless baseline.

Kaplan–Meier as a product-limit estimator. Order the distinct event times t_1<t_2<\cdots. At t_j, with d_j events and n_j at risk, the conditional survival of that instant is estimated by 1-d_j/n_j, and survival is the running product \hat S(t)=\prod_{t_j\le t}(1-d_j/n_j). Censored units inflate n_j at every time up to their censoring, then leave — using their partial information without bias. It is the nonparametric MLE of S under non-informative censoring.

Cox partial likelihood. At each event time, the probability that the unit who failed was the one who failed, given the risk set \mathcal R(t), is \frac{\exp(\beta^\top X_{i})}{\sum_{j\in\mathcal R(t)}\exp(\beta^\top X_{j})}. The baseline hazard h_0(t) cancels from numerator and denominator, so maximising the product of these terms estimates \beta without ever specifying h_0 — the semiparametric magic. The proportional-hazards assumption is exactly that h_i(t)/h_j(t)=\exp(\beta^\top(X_i-X_j)) is constant in t; Schoenfeld residuals test whether it drifts with time.

## Cox proportional hazards regression

KM describes; the **Cox proportional-hazards model** *explains* — the workhorse regression for survival
data. [@islr] It models the hazard for unit $i$ as a shared **baseline hazard** times an exponential
function of covariates:
$$
h_i(t\mid X)=h_0(t)\,\exp(\beta_1 X_{i1}+\dots+\beta_p X_{ip}).
$$
Its genius is being **semiparametric**: the **baseline hazard $h_0(t)$ is left completely unspecified**
— you make *no assumption* about the shape of time-dependence — yet you can still estimate the
$\beta$'s (via a [partial likelihood](#appendix-the-mathematics) that cancels $h_0$). [@islr]

Coefficients are read as **hazard ratios**, $\text{HR}=\exp(\beta)$: [@wooldridge_intro]

- $\beta>0 \Rightarrow \text{HR}>1$: the covariate **raises the hazard** — the event comes **sooner**
  (shorter duration).
- $\beta<0 \Rightarrow \text{HR}<1$: it **lowers the hazard** — the event comes **later** (longer
  survival).

An HR of $0.91$ means each unit of the predictor cuts the moment-to-moment risk by about **9%**.

### The proportional-hazards assumption

The "PH" is a real assumption: a covariate **multiplies the hazard by the same constant factor at every
time** — the HR is **constant over time.** [@wooldridge_intro] If a treatment helps early but fades
(non-proportional), the model is misspecified. Check it (e.g. **Schoenfeld residuals** / `cox.zph`); if
it fails, **stratify** on the offending variable or **interact it with time.** [@wooldridge_intro]

## The social-science bridge, and the caution

These are the **duration / event-history models** of applied social science. [@wooldridge_intro]

::: {.callout-note title="Duration models in the wild"}
- **Recidivism.** In Wooldridge's study of North Carolina inmates (time until re-arrest), more **prior
  convictions** significantly **shortens** the duration until the next arrest — a higher hazard.
  [@wooldridge_intro]
- **Unemployment & worker's comp.** Duration models are standard for **unemployment spells** and policy
  effects: a Kentucky benefit increase **lengthened** the time high-earners stayed on workers'
  compensation by roughly **19%.** [@wooldridge_intro]
:::

And the discipline this Part has hammered applies here too: **a hazard ratio is associational.** Unless
the covariate was [randomly assigned](../05-causal-inference/randomized-experiments.qmd), interpret it
with exactly the [caution owed any OLS coefficient](regression-and-causality.qmd) — unobserved
confounding and selection bias don't vanish because the outcome is a duration. [@wooldridge_intro] Cox
regression is a superb *description of risk over time*; turning that into a *cause* still needs a
[design](../05-causal-inference/index.qmd).

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Ignoring censoring.** Dropping censored units biases via selection; coding them as events biases
  toward zero. Use survival methods that count them at risk until censoring. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Reading the proportional-hazards model without checking PH.** If a covariate's effect changes over
  time, the constant-HR model is wrong; test it and stratify/interact if needed. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Misreading hazard ratios.** $\text{HR}>1$ means the event comes *sooner* (shorter survival), not
  "more of a good thing"; mind the direction. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Treating a hazard ratio as causal.** It's an association unless a design supports causation — the
  same rule as every other coefficient in this Part. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Informative censoring.** KM and Cox assume censoring is *unrelated* to the event risk; if people drop
  out *because* they're about to fail, the estimates are biased. [@islr]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Survival, hazard, and their identity.** With density $f$, CDF $F$, and $S=1-F$:
$$
h(t)=\frac{f(t)}{S(t)}=-\frac{d}{dt}\log S(t)\;\Longrightarrow\; S(t)=\exp\!\Big(-\!\int_0^t h(u)\,du\Big).
$$
The integral $\int_0^t h$ is the **cumulative hazard** $H(t)$; survival decays as $e^{-H(t)}$. A constant
hazard $h(t)=\lambda$ gives the **exponential** duration $S(t)=e^{-\lambda t}$ — the memoryless baseline.

**Kaplan–Meier as a product-limit estimator.** Order the distinct event times $t_1<t_2<\cdots$. At $t_j$,
with $d_j$ events and $n_j$ at risk, the conditional survival of that instant is estimated by
$1-d_j/n_j$, and survival is the running product $\hat S(t)=\prod_{t_j\le t}(1-d_j/n_j)$. Censored units
inflate $n_j$ at every time up to their censoring, then leave — using their partial information without
bias. It is the nonparametric MLE of $S$ under non-informative censoring.

**Cox partial likelihood.** At each event time, the probability that the unit who failed was *the one who
failed*, given the risk set $\mathcal R(t)$, is
$$
\frac{\exp(\beta^\top X_{i})}{\sum_{j\in\mathcal R(t)}\exp(\beta^\top X_{j})}.
$$
The **baseline hazard $h_0(t)$ cancels** from numerator and denominator, so maximising the product of
these terms estimates $\beta$ **without ever specifying $h_0$** — the semiparametric magic. The
proportional-hazards assumption is exactly that $h_i(t)/h_j(t)=\exp(\beta^\top(X_i-X_j))$ is **constant
in $t$**; Schoenfeld residuals test whether it drifts with time.

:::
