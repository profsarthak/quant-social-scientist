Quantile Regression

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

OLS fits the average. But the average can hide everything that matters — whether a policy lifts the bottom or stretches the top. Quantile regression models the whole distribution.

OLS estimates the conditional mean. Yet the question is often distributional: does schooling raise the wages of the lowest earners or the highest? Does a training programme cut inequality or just lift the average? Quantile regression answers these by fitting a line to any percentile of the outcome — the median, the 10th, the 90th — revealing effects that differ across the distribution and that the mean conceals entirely.

Why it matters

Modelling only the mean can hide changes in inequality (Angrist and Pischke 2009). A program might leave average earnings unchanged while compressing or stretching the distribution — and only a tool that looks at the tails can see it. Quantile regression is also robust to outliers and uniquely suited to censored (top-coded) data, making it a staple of labour economics and any field where the shape of an outcome, not just its centre, is the point.

Beyond the conditional mean

OLS is the “foundation” of econometrics but models only the conditional mean (Angrist and Pischke 2009; Wooldridge 2019). The distributional questions it can’t answer:

  • How does education affect the 10th percentile (lowest earners) versus the 90th percentile (highest)? (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Does a policy change earnings inequality as well as average earnings? (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

Median regression (LAD) and robustness

The most famous special case is median regression at \tau=0.5, also called least absolute deviations (LAD) — it estimates the conditional median rather than the mean (Wooldridge 2019). Its great virtue is robustness: OLS squares each residual, giving “increasing importance to large residuals,” so a single outlier can yank the fit; LAD’s objective is linear on either side of zero, so it is far more resilient to outlying observations (Wooldridge 2019).

The general \tau-th quantile and the check function

Quantile regression generalises LAD to any \tau\in(0,1) by minimising an asymmetric absolute loss — the check (or pinball) function (Angrist and Pischke 2009):

\hat\beta_\tau = \arg\min_b \ \sum_i \rho_\tau\!\big(y_i - x_i'b\big), \qquad \rho_\tau(u) = \big(\tau - \mathbf 1\{u\le 0\}\big)\,u .

So \rho_\tau weights positive residuals by \tau and negative residuals by 1-\tau. That asymmetry is exactly what “picks out conditional quantiles” — to target the 90th percentile you penalise under-prediction nine times as heavily as over-prediction, pushing the fit up until 90% of points lie below it (Angrist and Pischke 2009). A quantile coefficient describes an effect on the distribution, not on an individual: if a training program raises the 0.10 quantile of earnings, “those who are poor in the regime with training are less poor than the poor would be without it” (Angrist and Pischke 2009).

Effects that change across the distribution

The payoff is seeing how the slope itself varies with \tau — the conditional quantile function (CQF), the quantile analogue of the conditional expectation function (Angrist and Pischke 2009):

  • Under a pure location shift (homoskedastic errors), the slopes are identical across all quantiles — quantile regression tells you nothing new (Angrist and Pischke 2009).
  • Under heteroskedasticity — e.g. wage variance growing with education — the quantile coefficients “fan out” (Angrist and Pischke 2009). Angrist & Pischke’s census example: in 2000 the returns to schooling were asymmetric, rising sharply in the upper tail relative to the lower (Angrist and Pischke 2009).
Show the code (IRLS quantile regression by hand) 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(4)
n = 500
x = rng.uniform(0, 10, n)
y = 2.0 + 1.0*x + (0.4 + 0.5*x) * rng.normal(size=n)   # spread grows with x
X = np.column_stack([np.ones(n), x])

def qreg(X, y, tau, iters=200):
    """tau-th quantile regression by iteratively reweighted least squares."""
    beta = np.linalg.lstsq(X, y, rcond=None)[0]
    for _ in range(iters):
        r = y - X @ beta
        w = np.where(r >= 0, tau, 1 - tau) / np.maximum(np.abs(r), 1e-4)
        XtW = X.T * w
        beta = np.linalg.solve(XtW @ X, XtW @ y)
    return beta

ols = np.linalg.lstsq(X, y, rcond=None)[0]
xs = np.linspace(0, 10, 50); Xs = np.column_stack([np.ones(50), xs])
taus = [0.1, 0.5, 0.9]; colors = ["#c0392b", "#1f8a4c", "#1f4e79"]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.4, 4.3))
ax.scatter(x, y, s=8, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.4)
for tau, c in zip(taus, colors):
    b = qreg(X, y, tau)
    ax.plot(xs, Xs @ b, color=c, lw=2.2, label=f"τ = {tau}  (slope {b[1]:.2f})")
ax.plot(xs, Xs @ ols, color="black", lw=2.0, ls="--", label=f"OLS mean (slope {ols[1]:.2f})")
ax.set_xlabel("x"); ax.set_ylabel("y")
ax.set_title("Quantile lines fan out: the effect is bigger at the top")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Inference and cautions

  • Inference via the bootstrap. Analytic standard errors are awkward — they depend on the density of the residual at zero — so practitioners routinely use the bootstrap for standard errors and confidence intervals (Angrist and Pischke 2009).
  • Conditional vs unconditional. It is “surprisingly difficult” to go from conditional quantile effects (for units with given X) to marginal population-quantile effects; linear conditional quantiles do not imply a simple linear population quantile (Angrist and Pischke 2009).
  • Quantile crossing. Linear quantile fits are an MMSE approximation to the CQF, so estimated lines for different \tau can cross — a predicted lower quantile exceeding a higher one — for some X (Angrist and Pischke 2009).
  • Censoring — a superpower. Because the median is unaffected by changes above it, quantile regression can recover the true relationship even when the upper tail is top-coded/hidden (Angrist and Pischke 2009).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Reading a quantile effect as an individual effect. It describes the distribution (the poor are less poor), not a fixed person moving up. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Using OLS SEs. Quantile inference needs the bootstrap (or kernel density of residuals); plug-in OLS errors are wrong. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Ignoring quantile crossing. If lines cross in your X range, the linear specification is straining; consider non-crossing methods or rethink the model. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Expecting insight under homoskedasticity. If errors are a pure location shift, every quantile slope is the same as OLS — quantile regression adds nothing. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Confusing conditional and unconditional quantiles. A policy’s effect on the conditional 10th percentile is not its effect on the population 10th percentile. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

Appendix · the mathematics

Why the check function targets the \tau-quantile. Minimising \mathbb{E}[\rho_\tau(Y-q)] over a scalar q has first-order condition \tau - \Pr(Y\le q) = 0, i.e. \Pr(Y\le q)=\tau — exactly the definition of the \tau-quantile. The regression version replaces the constant q with x'\beta_\tau, so x'\hat\beta_\tau estimates the conditional \tau-quantile.

IRLS (the code). The check loss \sum_i \rho_\tau(r_i) equals \sum_i w_i r_i^2 with w_i = \rho_\tau(r_i)/r_i^2 = \big[\tau\mathbf 1\{r_i>0\}+(1-\tau)\mathbf 1\{r_i\le0\}\big]/|r_i|. Iterating weighted least squares with these weights (clipping |r_i| away from 0) converges to \hat\beta_\tau — a by-hand solver standing in for the linear-programming routines production code uses.

Robustness of LAD. The influence of a point on an OLS fit grows linearly with its residual (from the squared loss derivative 2r); for LAD the derivative is \operatorname{sign}(r), bounded — so an outlier far from the line exerts no more pull than one just past it. That bounded influence is the formal meaning of “resilient to outliers.”
