Limited Dependent Variables: LPM, Logit & Probit

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When the outcome is yes/no, a straight line strains — so we bend it into an S.

Half the outcomes social scientists care about are binary: employed or not, approved or denied, voted or stayed home. You can run OLS on a 0/1 variable — and sometimes should — but a line that keeps climbing will eventually predict probabilities above 1 and below 0. Logit and probit fix that by fitting an S-shaped curve trapped between 0 and 1, at the cost of coefficients you must work harder to interpret.

Why it matters

A great many dependent variables are limited — they can only take certain values, the simplest case being binary. (Wooldridge 2019) Labour-force participation, loan approval, turnout, purchase. The regression machinery you’ve built still applies, but the binary outcome forces three choices — the easy-but-flawed linear model, or the bounded-but-nonlinear logit/probit — and a new interpretive discipline (the coefficients aren’t what you think). This is also the gateway to the classification models of Part IV: logistic regression is the workhorse of predictive modelling, and it’s born here.

The linear probability model

The linear probability model (LPM) is just OLS with a 0/1 outcome. (Wooldridge 2019) Its interpretation is unusually clean: since E[y\mid x] = P(y=1\mid x) for a binary y, each coefficient is the change in the probability of “success” for a one-unit change in x_j:

P(y=1\mid x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k, \qquad \Delta P(y=1\mid x) = \beta_j\,\Delta x_j.

That directness is the appeal — coefficients are marginal effects, no transformation needed, and it estimates in one OLS call. (Wooldridge 2019) But it has three problems: (Wooldridge 2019)

  1. Nonsensical predictions. A line doesn’t stop at 0 or 1, so for extreme x it predicts probabilities below 0 or above 1 — impossible.
  2. Built-in heteroskedasticity. For a binary outcome, \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x) = P(y=1\mid x)\,[1 - P(y=1\mid x)] — the variance depends on x, so MLR.5 fails by construction and you must use robust standard errors.
  3. Unrealistic constant effects. The LPM says x_j moves the probability by the same amount everywhere. In a labour-force model it would claim the first child and the fourth child each cut the probability of working by the same 26.2 points — intuitively wrong, since the effect should shrink as the probability nears the floor. (Wooldridge 2019)

Logit and probit: bending the line into an S

To respect the [0, 1] boundary, logit and probit pass the linear index through an S-shaped (sigmoid) function G: (Wooldridge 2019)

P(y=1\mid x) = G(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k), \qquad 0 < G < 1.

  • Logit uses the logistic CDF, G(z) = \dfrac{e^z}{1 + e^z}.
  • Probit uses the standard Normal CDF, G(z) = \Phi(z).

A compelling way to motivate both is the latent-variable story: imagine an unobserved propensity y^* = \beta_0 + x\beta + e (the underlying tendency to “do it”), and you observe y = 1 only when that propensity crosses a threshold, y^* > 0. (Wooldridge 2019; Moffatt 2016) The S-curve falls out, and it has exactly the right shape: steepest in the middle (near P=0.5) and flattening toward the 0 and 1 boundaries — encoding the realism the LPM lacked, that a push matters most for the undecided and barely moves the near-certain. (Wooldridge 2019)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
n = 200
x = rng.uniform(-4, 4, n)
p = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-(0.2 + 1.1 * x)))     # true logistic probabilities
y = (rng.uniform(size=n) < p).astype(float)

# LPM by OLS
b1 = np.cov(x, y, bias=True)[0, 1] / np.var(x)
b0 = y.mean() - b1 * x.mean()
xs = np.linspace(-5.5, 5.5, 200)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.0))
ax.scatter(x, y, s=14, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.5, zorder=2)
ax.plot(xs, b0 + b1 * xs, color="#c0392b", lw=2.2, label="Linear probability model")
ax.plot(xs, 1 / (1 + np.exp(-(0.2 + 1.1 * xs))), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.6, label="Logit (S-curve)")
ax.axhline(0, color="#999", lw=0.8, ls=":"); ax.axhline(1, color="#999", lw=0.8, ls=":")
ax.fill_between(xs, 1, 1.25, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.06)
ax.fill_between(xs, -0.25, 0, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.06)
ax.set_ylim(-0.25, 1.25)
ax.set_xlabel("predictor x"); ax.set_ylabel("P(y = 1)")
ax.set_title("LPM escapes [0,1]; the logistic S-curve respects it")
ax.legend(fontsize=9, frameon=False, loc="center right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Odds and odds ratios (logit)

Logit has a special gift: while the probability is nonlinear in x, the log-odds is perfectly linear. (Wooldridge 2019) The odds are the ratio of success to failure, and their log is the linear index:

\frac{P(y=1)}{P(y=0)} = e^{\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots}, \qquad \ln\!\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k.

So e^{\beta_j} is the odds ratio — the multiplicative factor by which the odds change per one-unit rise in x_j. (Wooldridge 2019) If e^{\beta_j} = 2.57, a one-unit increase multiplies the odds by 2.57 — a 157% rise in the odds. (Odds, note, not probability — a distinction that trips up many readers.)

Marginal effects: the coefficient isn’t the effect

The crucial interpretive trap: in logit and probit, the coefficients are not the marginal effects on probability. (Wooldridge 2019) They give the sign and significance, but the actual change in probability depends on where you sit on the S-curve — big in the steep middle, tiny in the flat tails. The partial effect of a continuous x_j is g(\beta_0 + x\beta)\,\beta_j, where g is the density (the slope of the S). (Wooldridge 2019) Two standard summaries: (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Marginal effect at the mean (MEM): evaluate the formula at the average person, \bar x.
  • Average marginal effect (AME): compute each person’s marginal effect and average them — usually preferred, since it reflects the whole sample’s diversity rather than a possibly-atypical “average” person.

This is why logit/probit output is reported as marginal effects, not raw coefficients.

Estimation: maximum likelihood, not OLS

Because logit and probit are nonlinear in the parameters, OLS doesn’t apply; they are fit by maximum likelihood instead. (Wooldridge 2019) MLE chooses the parameters that make the observed pattern of 0s and 1s most probable under the assumed (logistic or Normal) model — the same “what parameter best explains my data?” logic from estimation, now doing the heavy lifting. It yields estimates that are consistent (homing in on the truth as n grows), asymptotically Normal (their sampling distribution approaches a Normal in large samples, by the CLT — so the usual t- and z-style inference applies), and efficient — and it automatically handles the binary heteroskedasticity. (Wooldridge 2019)

Which to use, and how they compare

In practice all three usually tell the same story about signs and significance. (Wooldridge 2019) Guidance:

  • LPM is a fine starting point and quick approximation, especially when fitted probabilities sit near 0.5 (away from the boundaries). (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Logit/probit when probabilities approach 0 or 1, or when you need predictions guaranteed inside [0, 1]. (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Rough conversion: multiply probit coefficients by ~1.6 to compare with logit; divide logit by ~4 (probit by ~2.5) to compare with LPM marginal effects. (Wooldridge 2019)
NoteMarried women’s labour-force participation (Mroz, n = 753)

The marginal effect of a year of education on the probability of working is remarkably stable across methods: LPM 0.038, logit ≈0.040, probit ≈0.039 — about a 3.8–4.0 percentage-point increase per year. Where they diverge is at the extremes: a child under six lowers the probability of working by 26.2 points in the LPM but 33.4 points for an average woman under probit — the nonlinear “penalty” the S-curve captures and the straight line flattens. (Wooldridge 2019)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reading logit/probit coefficients as probability effects. They aren’t — compute marginal effects (AME preferred). Only LPM coefficients are direct probability changes. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Confusing odds ratios with probability changes. e^{\beta}=2.57 means the odds multiply by 2.57, not the probability. At p=0.5 that’s a big move; near p=0.95 it’s small. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Forgetting robust SEs in the LPM. Binary outcomes are inherently heteroskedastic (p(1-p)), so the LPM always needs robust standard errors. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Over-trusting LPM predictions near the boundary. When fitted probabilities approach 0 or 1, the LPM can predict impossibilities; switch to logit/probit. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Treating a binary-outcome coefficient as causal. Everything from the causal entry still applies — a precise logit coefficient on an endogenous regressor estimates a biased effect.

Appendix · the mathematics

LPM heteroskedasticity. For y\in\{0,1\} with p=P(y=1\mid x), y is Bernoulli, so \operatorname{Var}(y\mid x)=p(1-p)=(\beta_0+x\beta)(1-\beta_0-x\beta) — a function of x. Hence the homoskedasticity assumption fails identically, and robust SEs are mandatory.

Latent variable → S-curve. With y^*=x\beta+e and y=\mathbf 1\{y^*>0\}, P(y=1\mid x)=P(e>-x\beta)=1-F(-x\beta)=F(x\beta) for a symmetric error CDF F. Take F=\Phi for probit (Normal e) or the logistic CDF for logit. The threshold-crossing is the S-curve.

Marginal effect. Differentiate P(y=1\mid x)=G(x\beta): \partial P/\partial x_j = g(x\beta)\,\beta_j, where g=G' is the density. Because g peaks at the centre and vanishes in the tails, the same \beta_j produces a large probability effect near P=0.5 and a negligible one near 0 or 1.

Log-likelihood (logit). \ell(\beta)=\sum_i\big[y_i\log G(x_i\beta)+(1-y_i)\log(1-G(x_i\beta))\big]; maximising it (no closed form — solved numerically) gives the MLE, the binary-data analogue of the coin-bias MLE.

Connections

  • Builds on: multiple regression (the linear index inside G), the common distributions (the logistic and Normal CDFs), maximum likelihood (the estimator), and diagnostics (the built-in heteroskedasticity).
  • Leads to: logistic regression as classification in Part IV — the same model, judged by out-of-sample prediction rather than coefficient interpretation.
  • Inherits: every caveat of regression and causality — a binary outcome doesn’t make a coefficient causal.

- **Moffatt, *Experimetrics*** — the latent-variable / index-function formulation $y^*=x\beta+e$,
  $y=\mathbf 1\{y^*>0\}$, for binary choice.
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**LPM heteroskedasticity.** For $y\in\{0,1\}$ with $p=P(y=1\mid x)$, $y$ is Bernoulli, so
$\operatorname{Var}(y\mid x)=p(1-p)=(\beta_0+x\beta)(1-\beta_0-x\beta)$ — a function of $x$. Hence the
[homoskedasticity assumption](gauss-markov-assumptions.qmd) fails identically, and robust SEs are
mandatory.

**Latent variable → S-curve.** With $y^*=x\beta+e$ and $y=\mathbf 1\{y^*>0\}$,
$P(y=1\mid x)=P(e>-x\beta)=1-F(-x\beta)=F(x\beta)$ for a symmetric error CDF $F$. Take $F=\Phi$ for
probit (Normal $e$) or the logistic CDF for logit. The threshold-crossing *is* the S-curve.

**Marginal effect.** Differentiate $P(y=1\mid x)=G(x\beta)$:
$\partial P/\partial x_j = g(x\beta)\,\beta_j$, where $g=G'$ is the density. Because $g$ peaks at the
centre and vanishes in the tails, the same $\beta_j$ produces a large probability effect near
$P=0.5$ and a negligible one near 0 or 1.

**Log-likelihood (logit).** $\ell(\beta)=\sum_i\big[y_i\log G(x_i\beta)+(1-y_i)\log(1-G(x_i\beta))\big]$;
maximising it (no closed form — solved numerically) gives the MLE, the binary-data analogue of the
coin-bias [MLE](../02-inference/estimation.qmd).

:::
