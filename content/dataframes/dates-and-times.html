Dates & Times

The hardest ‘simple’ data type there is — because the calendar is a tangle of astronomy and politics. The lubridate toolkit that makes it bearable.

Dates look easy and are not. They have to reconcile two physical facts — the Earth spinning and orbiting — with “a whole raft of geopolitical phenomena”: months of unequal length, time zones, leap years, daylight saving. (Wickham et al. 2023) Get them wrong and your “events per week” is silently off by an hour or a day. The lubridate package gives dates a sane, consistent toolkit: parse them, pull them apart, round them, and do arithmetic that respects the calendar’s weirdness.

Why dates are hard

The book is unusually candid: dates and times are difficult because they must square the Earth’s rotation and orbit with human inventions — months, time zones, and daylight saving time. (Wickham et al. 2023) Every hard case (a “day” that is 23 or 25 hours long under DST, a February that is 28 or 29) comes from that collision. Treat a date as “just a number” and the calendar will eventually betray you.

Three types

R distinguishes three things, by precision: (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Date — a calendar day, printed in a tibble as <date>.
  • Time — a time within a day (handled by the hms package).
  • Date-time — a date and a time that pins down a single instant (POSIXct), printed as <dttm>.

Reach for the simplest type that captures what you need; a date-time carries time-zone baggage a plain date does not.

Parsing: from text to dates

Real dates arrive as strings in a dozen formats. lubridate’s helpers parse them once you name the order of the components — ymd(), mdy(), dmy() — and you append _hms/_hm for date-times: (Wickham et al. 2023)

library(tidyverse)

ymd("2024-01-31")        # year-month-day
mdy("January 31, 2024")  # month-day-year  (same instant, messier string)
dmy("31/1/24")           # day-month-year
ymd_hms("2024-01-31 14:05:00")   # a full date-time

When the pieces live in separate columns (year, month, day, hour…), make_datetime() assembles them — the book’s flights example builds a departure date-time from five columns. (Wickham et al. 2023)

Getting and setting components

Accessor functions pull a piece out of a date-time: year(), month(), mday() (day of month), yday() (day of year), wday() (day of week), hour(). (Wickham et al. 2023) The same functions set components (year(d) <- 2020), or use update() to change several at once. And rounding — floor_date(), round_date(), ceiling_date() — snaps a date to a unit, which is exactly what you need to count “flights per week” before plotting. (Wickham et al. 2023)

flights |>
  mutate(dep = make_datetime(year, month, day, hour, minute)) |>
  count(week = floor_date(dep, "week")) |>     # bucket into weeks, then
  ggplot(aes(week, n)) + geom_line()           # plot the weekly series

Time spans: durations, periods, intervals

Arithmetic on dates needs three different notions of “how long,” and choosing wrong is a classic bug: (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Durations — an exact number of seconds. ddays(1) is always 86,400 seconds, even across a DST boundary. Use them for physical elapsed time.
  • Periods — human units (weeks, months) with no fixed length in seconds; they “just work” across DST and leap years. Adding months(1) lands you on the same day next month, however many days that took.
  • Intervals — a duration with a start point, so it is precise even for human units (how many days in this specific month).

The DST trap: add a duration of one day to a date crossing the spring-forward boundary and you land an hour off; add a period of one day and you land on the right calendar day. Match the type to the question. (Wickham et al. 2023)

Time zones

R uses the IANA names of the form "<continent>/<city>" (e.g. "America/New_York") — not the ambiguous “EST”. (Wickham et al. 2023) Two operations you must not confuse: with_tz() changes how an instant is displayed (same moment, different clock), while force_tz() relabels an instant whose zone was recorded wrong (different moment). (Wickham et al. 2023)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Durations vs periods across DST. ddays(1) (exactly 86,400s) and days(1) (one calendar day) differ on DST days. Use periods for human “a day later,” durations for elapsed physical time. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Parsing with the wrong component order. 01/02/03 is three different dates depending on locale; pick ymd/mdy/dmy deliberately. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Confusing with_tz() and force_tz(). One re-displays the same instant; the other changes which instant you mean. Mixing them corrupts timestamps. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Using a date-time when a date will do. Time-zone handling adds bugs you don’t need if the time of day is irrelevant. (Wickham et al. 2023)
