Exploratory Visualization: Variation & Covariation

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Exploratory data analysis is not a checklist — it’s a state of mind. Two questions, asked of every dataset, and the plots that answer them.

Before any model, you interrogate the data. R for Data Science frames exploratory data analysis (EDA) not as a fixed procedure but as an iterative cycle and a “state of mind”: ask a question, answer it with a plot or a summary, let the answer breed the next question. (Wickham et al. 2023) Two questions are always worth asking — about variation within a variable and covariation between variables — and a handful of plots answer them.

Why it matters

This is the visualize loop of the workflow, the step that can surprise you. The authors are explicit that EDA is “fundamentally a creative process”: at the start you don’t know what’s hidden, so the goal is to “generate a large quantity of questions” and investigate “every idea that occurs to you” before honing in. (Wickham et al. 2023) It is the dataframe-native version of Part I’s exploratory data analysis — and, as Tukey said (quoted in the book), “the simple graph has brought more information to the data analyst’s mind than any other device.” (Wickham et al. 2023)

The EDA cycle

A three-step loop you spin many times: (Wickham et al. 2023)

  1. Generate questions about your data.
  2. Search for answers by visualizing, transforming, and modelling.
  3. Refine the questions, or spawn new ones, from what you learn.

There are no rules for which questions — but two types are “always useful”: (Wickham et al. 2023)

What type of variation occurs within my variables? What type of covariation occurs between my variables?

Variation: looking within one variable

Variation is the tendency of a variable’s values to change from measurement to measurement; you see it by plotting the distribution. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Categorical → a bar chart (geom_bar()): bar height is the count per category.
  • Continuous → a histogram (geom_histogram()): counts within bins.

What to look for: (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Typical values — the tall bars (common) vs short bars (rare). Why is each common or rare?
  • Clusters / subgroups — separated humps suggest distinct subpopulations. How are within-cluster cases alike, and how do clusters differ?
  • Outliers — points that don’t fit. In big data they hide; the authors zoom the y-axis with coord_cartesian(ylim = c(0, 50)) to expose the rare bins.
Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(11)
# Two subgroups + a few outliers — the kind of structure a histogram reveals
a = rng.normal(20, 3, 600)
b = rng.normal(38, 4, 400)
outliers = np.array([70, 72, 75, 78, 80])
x = np.concatenate([a, b, outliers])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.0))
ax.hist(x, bins=40, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85, edgecolor="white")
ax.annotate("two clusters →\nlikely two subgroups", xy=(29, 40), fontsize=8.5, color="#3f8f6f")
ax.annotate("outliers", xy=(74, 8), xytext=(60, 35), fontsize=8.5, color="#c0392b",
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#c0392b"))
ax.set_xlabel("value"); ax.set_ylabel("count")
ax.set_title("What does the distribution look like?")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Handling unusual values: the authors don’t recommend silently dropping outlier rows. Instead, replace the suspect value with NA and keep the row: mutate(y = ifelse(y < 3 | y > 20, NA, y)). (Wickham et al. 2023) That preserves the rest of the observation and makes the decision explicit (see missing values).

Covariation: looking between variables

Covariation is the tendency of two variables to move together. (Wickham et al. 2023) The right plot depends on the pair of types:

  • Categorical × continuous. Overlaid frequency polygons (geom_freqpoly()), or — to compare groups of very different sizes — map y = after_stat(density) so shapes are comparable. A boxplot (geom_boxplot()) gives a compact median/spread summary; if the categories have no natural order, reorder() them by the value you’re plotting (aes(x = reorder(class, hwy, FUN = median), y = hwy)) to reveal the trend. (Wickham et al. 2023)
  • Two categorical. geom_count() (circle size = count per combination), or count() then geom_tile() with a fill aesthetic — a heatmap of the cross-tabulation. (Wickham et al. 2023)
  • Two continuous. The scatterplot (geom_point()). When points pile up (overplotting), bin into geom_bin2d() (rectangles) or geom_hex() (hexagons), or slice one variable with cut_width() and boxplot each slice. (Wickham et al. 2023)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Treating EDA as a checklist. It’s a creative, iterative search; mechanical plotting misses the surprising question. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Dropping outliers reflexively. Investigate first; if you must remove, set to NA (keep the row) and say so. A dropped outlier can be the most interesting finding. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Comparing raw counts across unequal groups. Use density so shape, not size, drives the comparison. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Overplotting hiding the pattern. Thousands of overlapping points look like a blob; bin (geom_bin2d/geom_hex) to recover the density. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Leaving categories in alphabetical order. reorder() (or fct_reorder) turns a jumbled boxplot into a readable ranking. (Wickham et al. 2023)

A histogram exposes what a mean and SD hide: here, two clusters (a bimodal distribution — evidence of two subgroups) plus a handful of outliers far to the right. ## Covariation: looking between variables

**Covariation** is the tendency of two variables to move together. [@wickham_r4ds] The right plot depends on
the pair of types:

- **Categorical × continuous.** Overlaid **frequency polygons** (`geom_freqpoly()`), or — to compare groups
  of very different sizes — map `y = after_stat(density)` so shapes are comparable. A **boxplot**
  (`geom_boxplot()`) gives a compact median/spread summary; if the categories have no natural order,
  **`reorder()`** them by the value you're plotting (`aes(x = reorder(class, hwy, FUN = median), y = hwy)`) to
  reveal the trend. [@wickham_r4ds]
- **Two categorical.** **`geom_count()`** (circle size = count per combination), or `count()` then
  **`geom_tile()`** with a `fill` aesthetic — a **heatmap** of the cross-tabulation. [@wickham_r4ds]
- **Two continuous.** The **scatterplot** (`geom_point()`). When points pile up (**overplotting**), bin into
  **`geom_bin2d()`** (rectangles) or **`geom_hex()`** (hexagons), or slice one variable with
  `cut_width()` and boxplot each slice. [@wickham_r4ds]
