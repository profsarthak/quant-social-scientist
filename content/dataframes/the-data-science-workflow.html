The Data-Science Workflow: The Whole Game

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

One picture of the entire process — import, tidy, transform, visualize, model, communicate — and the thinking each step demands.

Before any single technique, R for Data Science hands you a map of the whole game: a repeatable pipeline that turns raw data into “understanding, insight, and knowledge.” Every method elsewhere in this book lives at one station on that map. Knowing the map keeps you oriented — you always know which step you’re on, which you’ve skipped, and why the analysis feels stuck.

Why it matters

Beginners imagine data science as “run the model.” Practitioners know the model is a small, late step sitting on a mountain of unglamorous work that decides whether it means anything. Wickham & Grolemund’s contribution is to make that mountain legible — to give the whole process a shape you can hold in your head and return to. (Wickham et al. 2023) Internalise it and you stop thrashing: a confusing result becomes “I’m stuck at tidy,” not “data science is hard.”

The map

The book organises everything around a single diagram of the process. (Wickham et al. 2023)

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

The six steps, and the thinking each demands

Import. Get the data out of a file, database, or API and into R. Trivial-sounding, occasionally maddening, and strictly first: as the authors put it, “if you can’t get your data into R, you can’t do data science on it!” (Wickham et al. 2023)

Tidy. Store the data “in a consistent form that matches the semantics of the dataset with the way it is stored.” (Wickham et al. 2023) One row per observation, one column per variable — the subject of the next entry. Tidying pays off because a consistent structure lets you spend your energy on the question, not the format.

Transform. Narrow to the observations of interest, create new variables from existing ones, and compute summaries. (Wickham et al. 2023) This is the grammar of manipulation — the verbs you apply to a tidy table.

Visualize. A “fundamentally human activity.” A good plot “will show you things that you did not expect, or raise new questions” — and sometimes hint that “you’re asking the wrong question.” (Wickham et al. 2023) Visualisation is how the data surprises you.

Model. Mathematical and computational tools, complementary to visualisation: they scale far better than a human brain. But the authors issue a sharp caution — a model “cannot fundamentally surprise you,” because it cannot question its own assumptions. (Wickham et al. 2023) Models answer the question you posed; only you (often via a plot) can notice it was the wrong question.

Communicate. “Absolutely critical.” (Wickham et al. 2023) An insight no one understands changes nothing; the analysis isn’t finished until someone else can act on it — the concern of Part VI.

NoteWrangling, and the understanding loop

Two groupings are worth naming. Tidy + Transform is “wrangling” — so named because getting data into a workable form “often feels like a fight!” (Wickham et al. 2023) And Transform ⇄ Visualize ⇄ Model is the understanding loop: you rarely run it once. You transform, plot, fit, notice something off, and circle back — many times — before anything is ready to communicate. (Wickham et al. 2023) Underneath every box sits programming, the tool that runs the whole cycle.

What the map is not

It is not a one-way conveyor belt. The arrows into the understanding loop go both ways for a reason: a plot sends you back to wrangle a column you thought was clean; a model sends you back to engineer a feature. (Wickham et al. 2023) The discipline is knowing which station you’re at, so that “the analysis is broken” sharpens into a locatable problem — bad import, untidy shape, wrong transform, misleading plot, misspecified model, or unclear write-up.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Jumping to model before tidy. A model fit on an untidy or unclean table estimates something precise about the wrong numbers. The mountain comes before the summit. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Skipping visualize. If you never plot, you forfeit the one step that can surprise you — and you let a model’s silent assumptions stand unchecked. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Treating wrangling as menial. It is the majority of the work and the part most determinative of whether the result is trustworthy; it deserves the same care as the analysis. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Forgetting communicate. A correct analysis nobody can follow has zero effect on the world. The last step is not optional. (Wickham et al. 2023)

From the sources

NoteWho says what

## The six steps, and the thinking each demands

**Import.** Get the data out of a file, database, or API and into R. Trivial-sounding, occasionally

**Tidy.** Store the data *"in a consistent form that matches the semantics of the dataset with the way it
is stored."* [@wickham_r4ds] One row per observation, one column per variable — the subject of the
[next entry](tidy-data.qmd). Tidying pays off because a consistent structure lets you spend your energy on
the question, not the format.

**Transform.** Narrow to the observations of interest, create new variables from existing ones, and compute
summaries. [@wickham_r4ds] This is the [grammar of manipulation](grammar-of-data-manipulation.qmd) — the
verbs you apply to a tidy table.

**Visualize.** A *"fundamentally human activity."* A good plot *"will show you things that you did not
expect, or raise new questions"* — and sometimes hint that *"you're asking the wrong question."*
[@wickham_r4ds] Visualisation is how the data surprises you.

**Model.** Mathematical and computational tools, *complementary* to visualisation: they scale far better
than a human brain. But the authors issue a sharp caution — a model *"cannot fundamentally surprise you,"*
because it cannot question its own assumptions. [@wickham_r4ds] Models answer the question you posed; only
*you* (often via a plot) can notice it was the wrong question.

**Communicate.** *"Absolutely critical."* [@wickham_r4ds] An insight no one understands changes nothing;
the analysis isn't finished until someone else can act on it — the concern of
[Part VI](../06-field-realities/communicating-quantitative-findings.qmd).

## What the map is *not*
