Factors: Categorical Data

Survey data is mostly categories — party, religion, response option. Factors give those categories an order you choose and a guard against typos, and they make plots tell the truth.

Social-science data is overwhelmingly categorical: party ID, religion, Likert responses, “Refused.” Stored as plain strings, categories sort alphabetically (so “Apr” comes before “Jan”) and silently absorb typos. A factor fixes both — it pins a variable to a known set of levels in an order you control. The forcats package then lets you reorder and recode those levels, which is most of what makes a categorical plot or table readable.

Why factors exist

A factor is R’s structure for a variable with “a fixed and known set of possible values.” (Wickham et al. 2023) Recording categories as raw strings has two failure modes the book names directly: strings “don’t sort in a useful way” (month names land alphabetically — Apr, Aug, Dec…), and there is “no protection against typos” — "Jam" for "Jan" slips through unnoticed. (Wickham et al. 2023) A factor solves both by declaring the levels up front.

library(tidyverse)

month_levels <- c("Jan","Feb","Mar","Apr","May","Jun",
                  "Jul","Aug","Sep","Oct","Nov","Dec")
x <- c("Dec","Apr","Jam","Mar")
factor(x, levels = month_levels)
#> [1] Dec  Apr  <NA> Mar      <- "Jam" wasn't a valid level, so it became NA (the typo is caught)
#> Levels: Jan Feb Mar ... Dec  <- and they now sort in calendar order, not alphabetically

factor() maps the data onto the declared levels; any value not in the set becomes NA (so typos surface as missing rather than passing silently). (Wickham et al. 2023) Omit the levels and R falls back to alphabetical order — rarely what you want. (Wickham et al. 2023)

The forcats verbs

The point of forcats (an anagram of factors) is to change the order or the values of levels, which is where most categorical-data work actually lives: (Wickham et al. 2023)

Verb What it does
fct_reorder() reorder levels by another (numeric) variable — sort religions by mean TV hours to reveal the pattern
fct_relevel() hand-move specific levels to the front (keep a principled order, but pull "Not applicable" first)
fct_infreq() / fct_rev() order by frequency (and reverse it) — the standard tidy-up for bar charts
fct_recode() rename levels by hand for clearer, consistent labels
fct_collapse() bundle many old levels into a few new ones (various party labels → rep, dem, ind)
fct_lump() automatically lump the smallest groups into "Other" to de-clutter a plot or table

The highest-value one is fct_reorder(): a categorical axis sorted by the value you’re plotting turns a jumbled bar chart into an instantly readable ranking. (Wickham et al. 2023)

# Reorder 'relig' by the mean of tvhours so the chart reads top-to-bottom by magnitude
gss_cat |>
  group_by(relig) |>
  summarize(tvhours = mean(tvhours, na.rm = TRUE)) |>
  ggplot(aes(x = tvhours, y = fct_reorder(relig, tvhours))) +
  geom_point()
NoteThe gss_cat example

The book uses the General Social Survey sample (gss_cat) — real survey data, with the real headaches: thousands of categories, and non-substantive levels like “Refused” and “No answer” that you must decide how to handle. (Wickham et al. 2023) This is exactly the categorical mess a quantitative social scientist faces, which is why factors are a survey-research staple, not a programming nicety.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Letting categories sort alphabetically. Months, Likert scales, and income brackets have a meaningful order; declare it in the levels or your tables and plots mislead. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Not catching invalid levels. Values outside the declared set become NA — useful, but check, or a miscoded category vanishes silently. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Plotting unordered categories. A bar chart in alphabetical order hides the pattern; fct_reorder() / fct_infreq() make it visible. (Wickham et al. 2023)

  • Carrying dozens of tiny categories. They clutter and overfit visuals; fct_lump() into “Other” keeps the signal. (Wickham et al. 2023)

**alphabetical order** — rarely what you want. [@wickham_r4ds]
