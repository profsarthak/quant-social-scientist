Instrumental Variables

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When you can’t close the backdoor, find a lever that moves the treatment for reasons unrelated to the outcome — and ride only that variation.

Matching fails the moment an unobserved confounder drives both treatment and outcome — exactly the case the bridge warned is untestable. Instrumental variables sidesteps it entirely. Find a variable that nudges the treatment but has no other path to the outcome, and the slice of treatment variation it creates is as-good-as-random. IV throws away all the contaminated variation and keeps only that clean slice — buying identification at the price of estimating an effect for a particular subpopulation.

Why it matters

Every selection-on-observables method assumes you measured the confounders. When you didn’t — ability in the wage equation, health in the income equation — those methods stay biased, with no test to warn you. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) IV is the first design in this Part that defeats unobserved confounding, and it does so with an idea that recurs everywhere after: isolate a source of variation in the treatment that is exogenous by argument, and use only that. (Cunningham 2021) It is the engine beneath noncompliance, fuzzy RD, and many natural experiments — learn it once, recognise it for the rest of the summit.

The endogeneity problem

A regressor D is endogenous when it is correlated with the error term, \operatorname{Cov}(D,u)\ne 0 — from omitted variables, measurement error, or simultaneity. Then OLS is biased and inconsistent: it cannot separate D’s effect on Y from the influence of the unobserved u that moves both. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) IV’s job is to recover the causal effect despite that correlation.

What makes an instrument valid

An instrument Z is a variable that perturbs D for reasons unrelated to u. Validity rests on two conditions (plus an independence premise): (Angrist and Pischke 2009; Cunningham 2021)

  • Relevance (the first stage). Z actually moves the treatment: \operatorname{Cov}(Z,D)\ne0. This is testable — regress D on Z and look. A strong first stage is non-negotiable.
  • Exclusion restriction. Z affects Y only through D — no direct path, no other channel: \operatorname{Cov}(Z,u)=0. This is not testable; it is an argument about the world.
  • Independence / as-good-as-random. Z is unrelated to the unobserved confounders, as if randomly assigned.

The whole design is one DAG: \;Z\to D\to Y\; with an unobserved u creating D\leftarrow u\to Y — and no arrow Z\to Y. (Cunningham 2021) That missing arrow is the exclusion restriction; it is a collider-free assumption you draw, not test, and the entire credibility of an IV study lives or dies on defending it.

How IV estimates: Wald and 2SLS

With a single binary instrument and no covariates, the estimator is the Wald ratio — the reduced form (effect of Z on Y) divided by the first stage (effect of Z on D): \rho_{\text{Wald}}=\frac{\mathbb E[Y\mid Z=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid Z=0]}{\mathbb E[D\mid Z=1]-\mathbb E[D\mid Z=0]}. The intuition is a unit-conversion: the instrument shifts the outcome by the numerator, but only because it shifted treatment by the denominator; dividing rescales “effect of the nudge” into “effect of the treatment.” (Angrist and Pischke 2009) (This is exactly the LATE formula \text{ITT}_Y/\text{ITT}_D — random assignment is the perfect instrument.)

Two-stage least squares (2SLS) generalises it to covariates and multiple instruments: (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  1. First stage: regress D on Z (and controls X); keep the fitted values \hat D — the part of treatment driven by the instrument, purged of the variation correlated with u.
  2. Second stage: regress Y on \hat D (and X). The coefficient on \hat D is the IV estimate.

The slogan: 2SLS uses only the treatment variation the instrument explains, discarding the endogenous remainder. The figure shows the payoff — OLS is dragged off by the confounder; IV, riding only the instrument’s clean variation, recovers the truth.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(8)
n = 4000
beta = 1.0                                   # true causal effect of D on Y
Z = rng.normal(0, 1, n)                      # the instrument
u = rng.normal(0, 1, n)                      # unobserved confounder
D = 0.8 * Z + 1.2 * u + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)   # treatment: moved by Z AND by u
Y = beta * D - 1.5 * u + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)  # u also pushes Y (so OLS is biased)

ols = np.cov(D, Y, bias=True)[0, 1] / np.var(D)
wald = (np.cov(Z, Y, bias=True)[0, 1]) / (np.cov(Z, D, bias=True)[0, 1])  # reduced form / first stage

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(7.8, 3.8))
# scatter with OLS vs true line
idx = rng.choice(n, 500, replace=False)
axL.scatter(D[idx], Y[idx], s=8, color="#c9ccd1", alpha=0.6)
xs = np.linspace(D.min(), D.max(), 20)
axL.plot(xs, ols * xs, color="#d98a29", lw=2.4, label=f"OLS slope = {ols:.2f}")
axL.plot(xs, beta * xs, color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.2, ls="--", label=f"true effect = {beta:.2f}")
axL.set_xlabel("treatment D"); axL.set_ylabel("outcome Y")
axL.set_title("OLS is pulled off by the confounder"); axL.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)

labels = ["true", "OLS", "IV (Wald)"]
vals = [beta, ols, wald]
colors = ["#1f8a4c", "#d98a29", "#1f3a5f"]
axR.bar(labels, vals, color=colors)
for i, v in enumerate(vals):
    axR.text(i, v + 0.03, f"{v:.2f}", ha="center", fontsize=9)
axR.axhline(beta, color="#1f8a4c", ls="--", lw=1.3)
axR.set_ylabel("estimated effect"); axR.set_title("IV lands on the truth")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

What IV actually estimates: LATE and compliers

Here is IV’s subtle, essential catch. When the effect varies across people, IV does not deliver the population ATE. It identifies the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE) — the average effect for compliers, the people whose treatment status the instrument actually moved. (Angrist and Pischke 2009; Cunningham 2021) Sort the population by how they respond to Z:

  • Compliers — take treatment iff the instrument says so (D_1=1, D_0=0). These, and only these, IV speaks for.
  • Always-takers (D_1=D_0=1) and never-takers (D_1=D_0=0) — the instrument doesn’t move them, so they contribute nothing to the estimate.
  • Defiers (D_1=0, D_0=1) — do the opposite of the nudge.

LATE requires monotonicity: the instrument pushes everyone it affects the same direction — no defiers. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) The consequence is interpretive, not technical: a draft-lottery estimate is the effect of service on men whom the lottery induced to serve, not on volunteers or the unfit. Different instruments for the same treatment estimate different LATEs, on different complier groups — which is why two valid IV studies can honestly disagree.

Weak instruments

Relevance isn’t binary — instruments can be weak, barely correlated with D. The damage is double: 2SLS standard errors balloon, and the estimator suffers finite-sample bias back toward OLS, even in large samples — so a weak instrument quietly reintroduces the very bias IV was meant to remove. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) The standard guard is the first-stage F-statistic on the excluded instruments, with the rule of thumb F>10 for the instrument to be considered strong enough. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) A weak first stage is the most common way an IV paper fails.

NoteThree instruments that made the method famous
  • The Vietnam draft lottery (Angrist 1990). To estimate the effect of military service on earnings, use random draft-eligibility lottery numbers as an instrument for veteran status — random by construction (independence), and plausibly affecting earnings only through service (exclusion). For white men born in 1950 the first stage was 0.159 (eligibility raised service by ~16 points), and the IV estimate was an earnings loss of about $2,400. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Quarter of birth (Angrist & Krueger 1991). Compulsory-schooling laws let those born later in the year leave school with slightly less education. Using quarter-of-birth dummies as instruments for schooling, the IV return to education was 8.9%, against an OLS 7.1%. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Distance to college (Card 1995). Growing up near a four-year college instruments years of schooling: it raised education by 0.327 years (first-stage F=15.8), and the IV return was 13.2%, well above OLS’s 7.5%. (Cunningham 2021)

That IV often exceeds OLS is itself informative — consistent with attenuation from measurement error, or with LATE weighting toward high-return compliers.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • A shaky exclusion restriction. If Z affects Y through any channel besides D, IV is biased — and you can’t test it. Most IV critiques are exclusion critiques. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  • Weak instruments. Low first-stage F inflates variance and biases 2SLS toward OLS. Report the first stage and its F; treat F<10 as a red flag. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  • Reading IV as the ATE. Under heterogeneity it’s the LATE for compliers — a local effect on whoever the instrument moved, not the population. State the complier group. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Forgetting monotonicity. Defiers break the complier interpretation; argue the instrument pushes one way for everyone. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  • Instrument-shopping. Trying many instruments until one “works” mines for a strong-but-invalid first stage; the exclusion restriction must be defended on substance, not chosen by fit. (Cunningham 2021)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Angrist & Pischke, Mastering ’Metrics / Mostly Harmless — endogeneity and OLS inconsistency; relevance (first stage) and the exclusion restriction + independence; the Wald estimator (reduced form ÷ first stage) and 2SLS (fitted \hat D); LATE and the complier/always-taker/never-taker/defier taxonomy with monotonicity; weak instruments and the first-stage F>10 rule; the draft-lottery (first stage .159, \approx-\$2{,}400) and quarter-of-birth (.089 vs OLS .071) studies.
  • Cunningham, The Mixtape — the IV DAG Z\to D\to Y with D\leftarrow u\to Y and no Z\to Y; the exclusion restriction as the missing arrow; Card’s distance-to-college (+0.327 yrs, F=15.8, IV return 13.2\% vs OLS 7.5\%).

Appendix · the mathematics

Why the IV estimand is unbiased under exclusion. With structural equation Y=\beta D+u, take the covariance with Z: \operatorname{Cov}(Z,Y)=\beta\operatorname{Cov}(Z,D)+\operatorname{Cov}(Z,u). Exclusion sets the last term to zero, so \beta=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(Z,Y)}{\operatorname{Cov}(Z,D)}, the population Wald/2SLS estimand. The denominator is relevance — if \operatorname{Cov}(Z,D)\to0 (weak instrument) the ratio explodes, which is why weak instruments wreck precision.

2SLS as a projection. \hat D=P_Z D where P_Z=Z(Z'Z)^{-1}Z' projects D onto the instrument space; the second stage gives \hat\beta_{2SLS}=(\hat D'\hat D)^{-1}\hat D'Y. 2SLS keeps only the component of D in the column space of Z — the exogenous slice — and discards the rest.

LATE theorem (Imbens–Angrist). Under independence, exclusion, relevance, and monotonicity, \frac{\mathbb E[Y\mid Z=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid Z=0]}{\mathbb E[D\mid Z=1]-\mathbb E[D\mid Z=0]} =\mathbb E[\,Y(1)-Y(0)\mid \text{complier}\,]. The denominator equals the share of compliers, so the ratio reweights the reduced form onto exactly the subpopulation the instrument moves — the formal reason IV is local.

Connections

  • Builds on: randomized experiments (LATE/compliers from noncompliance; assignment as the ideal instrument), DAGs (the exclusion restriction is a missing arrow), and endogeneity (the problem IV solves).
  • Leads to: fuzzy regression discontinuity (the cutoff as an instrument) and the credibility-revolution natural experiments.
  • Reappears in: causal machine learning, where double/debiased ML orthogonalises treatment and instrument with flexible first stages.

@cunningham_mixtape] Sort the population by how they respond to $Z$:

- **Compliers** — take treatment iff the instrument says so ($D_1=1, D_0=0$). *These, and only these,
  IV speaks for.*
- **Always-takers** ($D_1=D_0=1$) and **never-takers** ($D_1=D_0=0$) — the instrument doesn't move
  them, so they contribute nothing to the estimate.
- **Defiers** ($D_1=0, D_0=1$) — do the opposite of the nudge.

LATE requires **monotonicity**: the instrument pushes *everyone it affects the same direction* — **no
defiers**. [@angrist_mostly_harmless] The consequence is interpretive, not technical: a draft-lottery
estimate is the effect of service *on men whom the lottery induced to serve*, not on volunteers or the
unfit. Different instruments for the same treatment estimate different LATEs, on different complier
groups — which is why two valid IV studies can honestly disagree.

## Weak instruments

Relevance isn't binary — instruments can be **weak**, barely correlated with $D$. The damage is double:
2SLS standard errors **balloon**, and the estimator suffers **finite-sample bias back toward OLS**,
even in large samples — so a weak instrument quietly reintroduces the very bias IV was meant to remove.
[@angrist_mostly_harmless] The standard guard is the **first-stage $F$-statistic** on the excluded
instruments, with the rule of thumb **$F>10$** for the instrument to be considered strong enough.
[@angrist_mostly_harmless] A weak first stage is the most common way an IV paper fails.

::: {.callout-note title="Three instruments that made the method famous"}
- **The Vietnam draft lottery (Angrist 1990).** To estimate the effect of military service on earnings,
  use random **draft-eligibility** lottery numbers as an instrument for veteran status — random by
  construction (independence), and plausibly affecting earnings only through service (exclusion). For
  white men born in 1950 the **first stage** was $0.159$ (eligibility raised service by ~16 points), and
  the IV estimate was an earnings **loss of about \$2,400**. [@angrist_mostly_harmless]
- **Quarter of birth (Angrist & Krueger 1991).** Compulsory-schooling laws let those born later in the
  year leave school with slightly less education. Using **quarter-of-birth** dummies as instruments for
  schooling, the IV return to education was **8.9%**, against an OLS **7.1%**. [@angrist_mostly_harmless]
- **Distance to college (Card 1995).** Growing up **near a four-year college** instruments years of
  schooling: it raised education by **0.327 years** (first-stage **$F=15.8$**), and the IV return was
  **13.2%**, well above OLS's **7.5%**. [@cunningham_mixtape]

That IV often *exceeds* OLS is itself informative — consistent with attenuation from measurement error,
or with LATE weighting toward high-return compliers.
:::

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **A shaky exclusion restriction.** If $Z$ affects $Y$ through *any* channel besides $D$, IV is
  biased — and you can't test it. Most IV critiques are exclusion critiques. [@angrist_mostly_harmless]

- **Weak instruments.** Low first-stage $F$ inflates variance and biases 2SLS toward OLS. Report the
  first stage and its $F$; treat $F<10$ as a red flag. [@angrist_mostly_harmless]

- **Reading IV as the ATE.** Under heterogeneity it's the **LATE for compliers** — a local effect on
  whoever the instrument moved, not the population. State the complier group. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Forgetting monotonicity.** Defiers break the complier interpretation; argue the instrument pushes
  one way for everyone. [@angrist_mostly_harmless]

- **Instrument-shopping.** Trying many instruments until one "works" mines for a strong-but-invalid
  first stage; the exclusion restriction must be defended on substance, not chosen by fit.
  [@cunningham_mixtape]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why the IV estimand is unbiased under exclusion.** With structural equation $Y=\beta D+u$, take the
covariance with $Z$: $\operatorname{Cov}(Z,Y)=\beta\operatorname{Cov}(Z,D)+\operatorname{Cov}(Z,u)$.
Exclusion sets the last term to zero, so
$$
\beta=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(Z,Y)}{\operatorname{Cov}(Z,D)},
$$
the population Wald/2SLS estimand. The denominator is relevance — if $\operatorname{Cov}(Z,D)\to0$
(weak instrument) the ratio explodes, which is why weak instruments wreck precision.

**2SLS as a projection.** $\hat D=P_Z D$ where $P_Z=Z(Z'Z)^{-1}Z'$ projects $D$ onto the instrument
space; the second stage gives $\hat\beta_{2SLS}=(\hat D'\hat D)^{-1}\hat D'Y$. 2SLS keeps only the
component of $D$ in the column space of $Z$ — the exogenous slice — and discards the rest.

**LATE theorem (Imbens–Angrist).** Under independence, exclusion, relevance, and **monotonicity**,
$$
\frac{\mathbb E[Y\mid Z=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid Z=0]}{\mathbb E[D\mid Z=1]-\mathbb E[D\mid Z=0]}
=\mathbb E[\,Y(1)-Y(0)\mid \text{complier}\,].
$$
The denominator equals the **share of compliers**, so the ratio reweights the reduced form onto exactly
the subpopulation the instrument moves — the formal reason IV is *local*.

:::
