Selection on Observables: Matching & Propensity Scores

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When ‘control for the right things’ is actually enough — and how to do it without trusting a regression’s functional form.

Sometimes treatment isn’t random, but it is as-good-as-random once you condition on what you observe. That bet — the conditional independence assumption — is the backdoor criterion cashed out as an estimator. Matching and propensity scores build the missing counterfactual by pairing the treated with comparable untreated units, approximating the experiment you couldn’t run. The catch never goes away: they balance only what you measured, so the assumption is an argument, never a proof.

Why it matters

The DAG told you which variables close the backdoors; this entry is how you actually adjust for them to recover an effect. (Cunningham 2021) It matters because “selection on observables” is the workhorse of applied social science — the strategy behind most “we controlled for X, Y, Z” papers — and doing it as matching rather than a single regression makes its assumptions, and its weaknesses, impossible to hide. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) The reward is a transparent approximation of an experiment; the price is honesty about the one assumption that can’t be tested.

The two conditions

Everything rests on two requirements: (Cunningham 2021)

  • Conditional Independence Assumption (CIA) — also unconfoundedness or ignorability: \big(Y_i(1),Y_i(0)\big)\ \perp\ D_i \ \big|\ X_i. Within cells of the observed covariates X, treatment is as good as randomly assigned — independent of the potential outcomes. It is randomization’s independence, but only conditional on X. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)
  • Common support / overlap — for every covariate value, some chance of either arm: 0 < \Pr(D_i=1\mid X_i) < 1. If a kind of person is always treated (or never), they have no counterpart in the other group and no comparison is possible for them. (Cunningham 2021)

CIA is the identifying assumption; overlap is what makes it operational. Both must hold.

Subclassification: the idea in its purest form

The most transparent estimator simply stratifies. Partition the sample into cells (strata) of X; within a cell, units are alike on X, so under CIA the treated–control difference is causal; then average those within-cell differences, weighting by each stratum’s share of the population. That weighted average is the ATE — the backdoor adjustment formula made arithmetic. (Cunningham 2021)

NoteCunningham’s Titanic

Did riding first class raise your chance of surviving the Titanic? The naive survival gap (the simple difference in outcomes) is 35.4 percentage points. But first-class passengers were disproportionately women and the better-off — and “women and children first” plus age drove survival directly. Stratify on age and gender, take the within-stratum first-class effect, and reweight: the estimate falls to 16.1 points. (Cunningham 2021) Less than half the naive gap was the cabin; the rest was who was in it — selection bias, removed by conditioning on the confounders the DAG flagged.

Matching, and why many covariates break it

Matching generalises the idea: impute each unit’s missing counterfactual from the closest units in the other arm. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Exact matching pairs a treated unit with controls having identical X — perfect, when feasible.
  • Nearest-neighbour matching pairs with the closest available X when exact matches run out.

The wall it hits is the curse of dimensionality. With many covariates, the space is vast and the data sparse: cells empty out, exact matches vanish, and common support fails — you simply can’t find a treated and a control unit in most cells. (Cunningham 2021) Matching on more than a few covariates at once is infeasible in finite samples. This is the problem the propensity score was invented to dissolve.

The propensity score: one number to balance them all

The propensity score is the probability of treatment given covariates, e(X) = \Pr(D=1\mid X). The Rosenbaum–Rubin (1983) theorem is the magic: if CIA holds given the full vector X, then it also holds given the single scalar e(X): (Cunningham 2021) \big(Y(1),Y(0)\big)\perp D \ \big|\ e(X). The score is a balancing score — among units with the same propensity, the entire distribution of X is identical across treated and control. So you can condition on one number instead of a high-dimensional vector, sidestepping the curse of dimensionality entirely. (Cunningham 2021) You estimate e(X) (usually a logit), then match, stratify, or weight on it.

The figure shows what overlap looks like — and where it fails.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
n = 4000
X = rng.normal(0, 1, n)
# True propensity rises with X; treatment assigned accordingly (confounding by X).
e = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-1.3 * X))
D = rng.uniform(size=n) < e

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.0))
bins = np.linspace(0, 1, 41)
ax.hist(e[D],  bins=bins, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.6, label="treated", density=True)
ax.hist(e[~D], bins=bins, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.6, label="control", density=True)
ax.axvspan(0.0, 0.12, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.08)
ax.axvspan(0.88, 1.0, color="#c0392b", alpha=0.08)
ax.text(0.02, ax.get_ylim()[1]*0.85, "few treated\n(no controls' match here?)", fontsize=7.5, color="#b9791f")
ax.text(0.62, ax.get_ylim()[1]*0.92, "common support\n(matching works)", fontsize=8, color="#1f3a5f")
ax.set_xlabel("estimated propensity score  e(X) = P(D=1 | X)")
ax.set_ylabel("density")
ax.set_title("Overlap is what makes matching possible")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper center")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Inverse probability weighting

Instead of matching, you can reweight. Inverse probability weighting (IPW) builds a synthetic sample in which treatment is independent of X by up-weighting the rare cases — a treated unit with a low propensity stands in for the many similar units who went untreated. (Cunningham 2021) The ATE estimator weights each outcome by treatment status over (one minus) the propensity: \widehat{\text{ATE}} = \frac1N\sum_i Y_i\cdot\frac{D_i-\hat e(X_i)}{\hat e(X_i)\,(1-\hat e(X_i))}. Weighting and matching are two ways to spend the same propensity score; both lean entirely on getting e(X) — and CIA — right. (Note the danger lurking in the denominator: propensities near 0 or 1 produce explosive weights, which is the overlap problem returning in a new guise.)

Regression is matching in disguise

Where does ordinary regression fit? Angrist & Pischke call it an “automated matchmaker.” (Angrist and Pischke 2009) Regression with controls is also a selection-on-observables estimator built on CIA, and it can be written as a weighted average of within-cell treatment–control contrasts — i.e. a matching estimator. The difference is the weights: matching for the ATT weights cells by the covariate distribution among the treated, while regression weights them by the variance of treatment within each cell, \,\Pr(X)(1-\Pr(X)) — giving most weight to cells where treatment is a coin flip (near 0.5), because those are the most precise. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) Same identifying assumption, different weighting — which is why matching and regression usually agree, and why, when they disagree, the culprit is the weighting or the functional form.

The limitation you can never escape

Here is the line that separates this whole family from randomization: matching and propensity scores balance only the observables. (Cunningham 2021) An experiment balances everything, seen and unseen; selection-on-observables balances X and prays the rest goes with it. CIA is fundamentally untestable — it concerns the missing counterfactuals — so if unobserved ability or motivation drives both treatment and outcome, every method here stays biased, no matter how clean the balance table. (Cunningham 2021) Matching is not a machine for removing bias; it is a machine for removing bias due to observed confounders, conditional on an assumption you must defend on substance.

NoteLaLonde, and when it works (NSW / Dehejia–Wahba)

The famous test: the National Supported Work job-training program had a true experimental benchmark of about +$1,794 in earnings. LaLonde (1986) discarded the experimental controls and substituted observational comparison groups (from the CPS and PSID) — the naive gap swung to −$8,498, catastrophic negative selection (trainees were far more disadvantaged to begin with). (Cunningham 2021) Dehejia & Wahba (1999) then applied propensity-score matching with trimming to enforce common support, conditioning on demographics and two years of pre-treatment earnings; their screened estimate, +$1,362, landed close to the experimental truth. (Cunningham 2021) The moral cuts both ways: matching can recover the experiment — if the selection really is on observables you have (here, rich earnings histories) — and fails badly when it isn’t.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Treating CIA as established. It’s untestable. “We controlled for a lot” is an argument about unobservables, not a proof; a hidden confounder leaves the estimate biased. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Ignoring common support. Comparing units with no counterpart in the other arm extrapolates off a cliff; check overlap and trim non-overlapping units. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Matching on many covariates directly. The curse of dimensionality empties the cells — use the propensity score to collapse X to one number. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Extreme IPW weights. Propensities near 0 or 1 blow up the weights and the variance; it’s the overlap problem in disguise. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Reading balance on X as balance overall. A perfect balance table says nothing about the unobservables — the very things that make this weaker than an experiment. (Cunningham 2021)

Appendix · the mathematics

Identification under CIA. Under (Y(1),Y(0))\perp D\mid X and overlap, \mathbb E[Y\mid D=1,X]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0,X]=\mathbb E[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid X], and averaging over the distribution of X gives the ATE — the backdoor adjustment. Subclassification estimates the inner contrast cell by cell and averages with weights \Pr(X=x).

The balancing-score theorem (sketch). Rosenbaum & Rubin show D\perp X\mid e(X): by construction \Pr(D=1\mid X,e(X))=e(X) depends on X only through e(X), so within a level of e(X) the covariate distribution is identical across arms. Combined with CIA-given-X, this yields CIA-given-e(X) — so conditioning on the scalar e(X) suffices. The high-dimensional X is compressed to one number with no loss for identification.

Why regression weights by treatment variance. With saturated controls, OLS recovers \sum_x w(x)\,\big(\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1,x]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0,x]\big) with w(x)\propto \Pr(X=x)\,\operatorname{Var}(D\mid X=x)=\Pr(X=x)\,e(x)(1-e(x)). The factor e(x)(1-e(x)) peaks at e=1/2, so cells with the most treatment variation — the most informative comparisons — dominate the OLS estimand. This is why the regression number can differ from a matching ATT even under the same CIA.

Connections

  • Builds on: DAGs (the backdoor criterion this estimates), potential outcomes (CIA is conditional independence of D and the Y(d)), and logistic regression (how e(X) is fit).
  • Leads to: the designs for when CIA fails — instrumental variables, regression discontinuity, and difference-in-differences find leverage beyond the observables.
  • Reappears in: causal machine learning, where flexible ML estimates e(X) and the outcome model and double/debiased ML combines them to be robust to errors in each.

  overlap problem in disguise. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Reading balance on $X$ as balance overall.** A perfect balance table says nothing about the
  unobservables — the very things that make this weaker than an experiment. [@cunningham_mixtape]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Identification under CIA.** Under $(Y(1),Y(0))\perp D\mid X$ and overlap,
$$
\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1,X]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0,X]=\mathbb E[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid X],
$$
and averaging over the distribution of $X$ gives the ATE — the
[backdoor adjustment](directed-acyclic-graphs.qmd). Subclassification estimates the inner contrast
cell by cell and averages with weights $\Pr(X=x)$.

**The balancing-score theorem (sketch).** Rosenbaum & Rubin show $D\perp X\mid e(X)$: by construction
$\Pr(D=1\mid X,e(X))=e(X)$ depends on $X$ only through $e(X)$, so within a level of $e(X)$ the covariate
distribution is identical across arms. Combined with CIA-given-$X$, this yields CIA-given-$e(X)$ — so
conditioning on the scalar $e(X)$ suffices. The high-dimensional $X$ is compressed to one number with
no loss for identification.

**Why regression weights by treatment variance.** With saturated controls, OLS recovers
$\sum_x w(x)\,\big(\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1,x]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0,x]\big)$ with
$w(x)\propto \Pr(X=x)\,\operatorname{Var}(D\mid X=x)=\Pr(X=x)\,e(x)(1-e(x))$. The factor $e(x)(1-e(x))$
peaks at $e=1/2$, so cells with the most treatment variation — the most informative comparisons —
dominate the OLS estimand. This is why the regression number can differ from a matching ATT even under
the *same* CIA.

:::
