Difference-in-Differences

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Let each group be its own control through time — subtract away the fixed gaps and the common shocks, and what’s left is the policy.

When a policy hits one group and not another, you can’t just compare the two — they differed to begin with. And you can’t just track the treated group over time — everything else moved too. Difference-in- differences does both subtractions at once: the treated group’s change minus the control group’s change. The first difference erases fixed group gaps; the second erases shocks common to all. What survives is the treatment effect — if the groups would have moved in parallel anyway.

Why it matters

RD needs a sharp cutoff; IV needs a clever instrument. DiD needs only a policy change, a comparison group, and data before and after — which is why it is the most widely used quasi-experimental design in economics and policy. (Cunningham 2021) It exploits a different kind of as-good-as-random variation: the timing of a treatment that some units get and others don’t. And it is the design where the field has most recently discovered it was doing things wrong — making this entry both a how-to and a cautionary tale. (Baker et al. 2025)

The canonical 2×2

The simplest DiD has two groups — treated (D=1) and control (D=0) — and two periods, before and after. The estimator double-differences four means: (Baker et al. 2025) \hat\delta_{DD}=\big(\bar Y_{\text{treat,post}}-\bar Y_{\text{treat,pre}}\big) -\big(\bar Y_{\text{ctrl,post}}-\bar Y_{\text{ctrl,pre}}\big). Read the two subtractions: (Baker et al. 2025)

  • The first difference (post − pre, within each group) wipes out every time-invariant group trait — the treated group can be richer, sicker, bigger; if it doesn’t change, it cancels.
  • The second difference (treated change − control change) wipes out any shock common to both groups in that period — a recession, a seasonal swing, a nationwide trend.

Two confounding layers removed by arithmetic, no controls required. What remains is attributed to the treatment.

NoteCard & Krueger and the minimum wage

The study that made DiD famous — and overturned a textbook prediction. In 1992 New Jersey raised its minimum wage from $4.25 to $5.05; neighbouring Pennsylvania held at $4.25. Card & Krueger surveyed ~400 fast-food restaurants before and after, measuring full-time-equivalent employment: (Cunningham 2021)

Pre Post Change
New Jersey (treated) 20.44 21.03 +0.59
Pennsylvania (control) 23.33 21.17 −2.16

DiD = (+0.59)-(-2.16) = \mathbf{+2.76}. Employment fell in Pennsylvania (a common regional shock) but rose relative to it in New Jersey — the opposite of the competitive prediction that a higher minimum wage destroys jobs. Note the logic: NJ’s raw +0.59 alone would be misleading; only by netting out PA’s -2.16 regional slump does the policy’s relative effect appear. (Cunningham 2021)

The one assumption: parallel trends

DiD’s entire credibility rests on parallel trends: absent the treatment, the treated and control groups would have moved in parallel. (Baker et al. 2025) The control group’s change is used as the treated group’s missing counterfactual change — valid only if their paths would have stayed parallel. Two things to internalise: (Baker et al. 2025)

  • It is fundamentally untestable. It concerns the treated group’s unobserved no-treatment path after the intervention — a counterfactual we never see.
  • Pre-trends are the evidence. If the groups tracked each other before treatment, it’s more plausible they’d have continued. So researchers plot the pre-period and check for parallel paths — necessary support, not proof. (Levels may differ wildly; it is the slopes that must match.)
Show the figure code 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = [0, 1]                       # pre, post
nj = [20.44, 21.03]             # treated
pa = [23.33, 21.17]             # control
nj_cf = [20.44, 20.44 + (pa[1] - pa[0])]   # counterfactual: NJ + PA's change
did = (nj[1] - nj[0]) - (pa[1] - pa[0])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.0, 4.2))
ax.plot(t, nj, "o-", color="#1f8a4c", lw=2.4, label="New Jersey (treated)")
ax.plot(t, pa, "o-", color="#9aa0a6", lw=2.4, label="Pennsylvania (control)")
ax.plot(t, nj_cf, "o--", color="#c0392b", lw=2.0, label="NJ counterfactual (parallel)")
ax.annotate("", xy=(1, nj[1]), xytext=(1, nj_cf[1]),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="<->", color="#c0392b", lw=2.0))
ax.text(1.02, (nj[1] + nj_cf[1]) / 2, f"DiD = {did:+.2f}", color="#c0392b", fontsize=10, va="center")
ax.set_xticks(t); ax.set_xticklabels(["pre (Feb 1992)", "post (Nov 1992)"])
ax.set_ylabel("FTE employment per restaurant")
ax.set_title("Each group its own control: subtract the common shock")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower left")
ax.set_xlim(-0.15, 1.35)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

TWFE and the event study

In practice DiD is run as a two-way fixed effects (TWFE) regression, which generalises the four means to many units and periods: (Baker et al. 2025) Y_{it}=\eta_i+\theta_t+\beta D_{it}+\epsilon_{it}. The unit fixed effects \eta_i absorb all time-invariant differences between units (the first difference); the time fixed effects \theta_t absorb shocks common to all units in a period (the second difference); \beta is the DiD effect. (Baker et al. 2025) It is the panel/fixed-effects machinery of the next entry — DiD is a fixed-effects design.

The event study enriches this by replacing the single D_{it} with dummies for periods relative to treatment: (Baker et al. 2025) Y_{it}=\eta_i+\theta_t+\sum_{\tau=-q}^{m}\delta_\tau D_{i,\tau}+\epsilon_{it}. The leads (\tau<0) should be near zero — a visual parallel-trends check; the lags (\tau\ge0) trace how the effect grows, fades, or plateaus after treatment. (Baker et al. 2025) An event study is the single most persuasive DiD graphic: flat before, a clear break after.

The DiD revolution: when TWFE lies

Here is the cautionary tale, and why this entry cites a 2020s practitioner’s guide. When units are treated at different times (staggered adoption), the trusty TWFE regression can become biased — even sign-flipped. (Baker et al. 2025)

  • Forbidden comparisons. With staggering, TWFE implicitly uses already-treated units as controls for later-treated ones. If effects change over time (dynamic effects), those already-treated “controls” are themselves still moving because of their own treatment, contaminating the comparison. (Baker et al. 2025)
  • Goodman-Bacon decomposition. TWFE’s \beta is a weighted average of all possible 2×2 DiDs — and in staggered designs some weights can be negative. The estimate can come out the wrong sign even if every unit’s true effect is positive. (Baker et al. 2025)
  • New estimators. “Forward-engineered” methods fix it by using only clean controls: Callaway–Sant’Anna (compare each treated cohort only to never-treated or not-yet-treated units), Sun–Abraham (a saturated cohort-by-time interaction), and de Chaisemartin–D’Haultfœuille (robust when treatment switches on and off). (Baker et al. 2025)

The lesson is humbling and current: a method everyone trusted for decades was quietly mis-estimating staggered rollouts, and the corpus’s most recent source exists to set it right.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Asserting parallel trends from a good story. It’s untestable; show pre-trends / event-study leads near zero, and reason about confounders that could break parallelism. (Baker et al. 2025)

  • A control group on its own trajectory. If the control was already diverging (a differential trend), the second difference doesn’t cancel it and DiD is biased. (Baker et al. 2025)

  • Default TWFE under staggered adoption. Negative weights and forbidden comparisons can flip the sign; use Callaway–Sant’Anna / Sun–Abraham / dCDH and report a Goodman-Bacon decomposition. (Baker et al. 2025)

  • A shock that hits only one group. Parallel trends fails if something else changed for the treated group at the same time (a concurrent policy). (Cunningham 2021)

  • Anticipation. If units change behaviour before treatment (expecting it), pre-periods are contaminated and leads aren’t a clean test. (Baker et al. 2025)

Appendix · the mathematics

Why double-differencing identifies the effect. Write potential outcomes with additive group and time components: Y_{it}(0)=\gamma_i+\lambda_t+\varepsilon_{it}, and Y_{it}(1)=Y_{it}(0)+\delta. Parallel trends is the statement that \lambda_t is common across groups (the no-treatment trend is the same). Taking each group’s post−pre difference removes \gamma_i; subtracting the control’s difference removes the common \lambda_{\text{post}}-\lambda_{\text{pre}}, leaving exactly \delta. The assumption is precisely “the \lambda_t path is shared,” which is why differing levels \gamma_i are harmless but differing trends are fatal.

TWFE recovers the 2×2. In the saturated two-period, two-group case, the OLS coefficient on D_{it} in Y_{it}=\eta_i+\theta_t+\beta D_{it}+\epsilon_{it} equals \hat\delta_{DD} exactly — the unit dummies are the first difference, the time dummies the second.

Goodman-Bacon weights. With staggered timing, \hat\beta_{TWFE}=\sum_k s_k\,\hat\beta_k over all 2×2 sub-experiments k, with weights s_k proportional to subsample size and treatment variance. Comparisons that use already-treated units as controls can receive negative s_k when those controls’ effects are still evolving — so a positive-everywhere effect can aggregate to a negative \hat\beta_{TWFE}. The new estimators restrict attention to clean (never- / not-yet-treated) controls, forcing non-negative weighting.

  trend), the second difference doesn't cancel it and DiD is biased. [@did_guide]

- **Default TWFE under staggered adoption.** Negative weights and forbidden comparisons can flip the
  sign; use Callaway–Sant'Anna / Sun–Abraham / dCDH and report a Goodman-Bacon decomposition.
  [@did_guide]

- **A shock that hits only one group.** Parallel trends fails if something *else* changed for the
  treated group at the same time (a concurrent policy). [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Anticipation.** If units change behaviour *before* treatment (expecting it), pre-periods are
  contaminated and leads aren't a clean test. [@did_guide]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why double-differencing identifies the effect.** Write potential outcomes with additive group and
time components: $Y_{it}(0)=\gamma_i+\lambda_t+\varepsilon_{it}$, and $Y_{it}(1)=Y_{it}(0)+\delta$.
**Parallel trends** is the statement that $\lambda_t$ is *common* across groups (the no-treatment trend
is the same). Taking each group's post−pre difference removes $\gamma_i$; subtracting the control's
difference removes the common $\lambda_{\text{post}}-\lambda_{\text{pre}}$, leaving exactly $\delta$.
The assumption is precisely "the $\lambda_t$ path is shared," which is why differing *levels*
$\gamma_i$ are harmless but differing *trends* are fatal.

**TWFE recovers the 2×2.** In the saturated two-period, two-group case, the OLS coefficient on
$D_{it}$ in $Y_{it}=\eta_i+\theta_t+\beta D_{it}+\epsilon_{it}$ equals $\hat\delta_{DD}$ exactly — the
unit dummies are the first difference, the time dummies the second.

**Goodman-Bacon weights.** With staggered timing, $\hat\beta_{TWFE}=\sum_k s_k\,\hat\beta_k$ over all
2×2 sub-experiments $k$, with weights $s_k$ proportional to subsample size **and treatment variance**.
Comparisons that use **already-treated** units as controls can receive **negative** $s_k$ when those
controls' effects are still evolving — so a positive-everywhere effect can aggregate to a negative
$\hat\beta_{TWFE}$. The new estimators restrict attention to clean (never- / not-yet-treated) controls,
forcing non-negative weighting.

:::
