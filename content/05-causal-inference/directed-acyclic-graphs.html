Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs)

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A picture of your causal assumptions — and an algebra that tells you exactly what to control for, and what controlling would wreck.

A DAG is a diagram of what causes what, drawn before you touch the data. Its power is that it turns the murky question “which variables should I control for?” into a mechanical rule. Confounders open backdoor paths that must be closed; colliders are dams that you must not open. The same graph that justifies a control exposes the controls that would manufacture bias — Part III’s good-vs-bad controls, now derivable rather than memorised.

Why it matters

Randomization is the gold standard, but most questions can’t be randomized — you can’t assign people a gender, a recession, or a war. Then you must reason about confounding explicitly, and a directed acyclic graph is the tool for it: a transparent statement of your causal assumptions that makes the consequences for estimation derivable. (Cunningham 2021) DAGs are how Cunningham bridges theory and econometrics — and they settle, with a rule instead of an argument, the question that ended Part III: which controls help, and which ones hurt?

The intuitive picture: causal influence and adjustment

Before the formal machinery, the plain-language version — which is how Daniel Kaplan introduces DAGs in Lessons in Statistical Thinking. A DAG is a network of causal influence: each arrow carries influence from cause to effect, and “acyclic” keeps that influence flowing one way, so the reasoning is a clean chain of deduction rather than a circular argument. (Kaplan 2024) A confounder is then easy to see — it is a variable that sends influence down two arrows at once, manufacturing a non-causal association between your explanatory variable and your response and so disguising the true effect size. (Kaplan 2024)

The cure is adjustment — and it is literally the same move as holding a confounder constant in regression. To adjust for a covariate is to account for the variation it injects, blocking the non-causal flow so that what remains is the signal you want, separated from the noise. (Kaplan 2024) That is the bridge worth holding onto: “accounting for variation” in Part III and “closing a backdoor” here are one act seen from two sides. What the rest of this entry adds is the precision Kaplan’s intuition can’t supply on its own — exactly which covariates to adjust for, and the cases where adjusting creates bias instead of removing it.

Reading the graph

A DAG has three ingredients: (Cunningham 2021)

  • Nodes are random variables.
  • Directed edges (arrows) are direct causal effects: D\to Y means D causes Y. The absence of an arrow is the strongest claim of all — a firm theoretical commitment that no direct effect exists.
  • Acyclic means no feedback loops: causation flows one way. Reverse causality isn’t drawn as a cycle but by splitting the variable across time (Y_1, Y_2), so the arrows still point forward.

Behind the picture sits a structural causal model — a set of equations in which each variable is generated by its parents (the nodes with arrows into it) plus a random shock. (Cunningham 2021) The graph is the qualitative skeleton of that data-generating process; drawing it forces you to make your assumptions visible and falsifiable.

Three structures, and how association flows

Every DAG is built from three elementary configurations, and association (statistical correlation, causal or not) flows through them differently: (Cunningham 2021)

  • The chain / mediator D\to M\to Y. D affects Y only through M. Association flows along the chain — that flow is the causal effect.
  • The fork / confounder D\leftarrow Z\to Y. A common cause Z drives both. This creates a non-causal association between D and Y: they move together because they share an origin, not because one causes the other.
  • The collider D\to C\leftarrow Y. Two arrows collide into a common effect. Here D and Y are independent — until you condition on C.

Paths, backdoors, and the dam

A path is any sequence of edges connecting D and Y, ignoring arrow direction. The one you want is the direct/causal path D\to Y. The troublemakers are backdoor paths — any path that leaves D through an incoming arrow, like D\leftarrow Z\to Y. (Cunningham 2021) A backdoor path is a pipe through which confounding flows, manufacturing correlation that isn’t cause.

Whether a path transmits association depends on what’s on it — the intuition of d-separation: (Cunningham 2021)

  • A path through a confounder (a fork) is open: association flows. Conditioning on that confounder blocks it.
  • A path through a collider is naturally blocked. The collider is a dam: it stops the flow — unless you condition on it (or its descendant), which opens the dam and lets spurious association through.

This is the crucial asymmetry. Conditioning closes forks but opens colliders. Controlling is not free; it changes which pipes are open.

The backdoor criterion: the rule for controls

Putting it together gives the backdoor criterion, the formal rule for what to condition on: a set of variables identifies the causal effect of D on Y if it (Cunningham 2021)

  1. closes every backdoor path (block each one by conditioning on a non-collider along it), and
  2. opens no new path (never condition on a collider or a descendant of one).

Satisfy it and the leftover association between D and Y is purely causal. This is selection-on-observables stated as graph surgery: the “controls” you need are exactly the ones that shut the backdoors, and not one variable more.

Good vs bad controls, now derivable

The graph delivers Part III’s control rules as theorems: (Cunningham 2021)

  • Conditioning on a mediator M in D\to M\to Y blocks the very causal flow you want, stripping out the indirect effect and under-stating the total impact. This is over-control — the occupation-in-the-wage-regression mistake, now visibly a blocked chain.
  • Conditioning on a collider C opens a closed path and invents association between variables that were independent. This is selection/collider bias — the gravest control error.

Mediation: when the mechanism is the question

Sometimes you want the path through M — you ask not just whether D affects Y but why and how much through which channel. That is mediation analysis, which decomposes the total effect into a direct effect (D\to Y, not through M) and an indirect effect (the part flowing D\to M\to Y). (Gerber and Green 2012) The DAG makes the stakes precise, and they cut two ways:

  • To estimate the total effect, never condition on the mediator — that is the over-control above, blocking the indirect channel and biasing the total toward zero. (Cunningham 2021)
  • To estimate the direct/indirect split, you must reason about M — but naively “controlling for M” does not cleanly identify the direct effect, because M is a post-treatment variable and is typically a collider linking D to the unobserved causes of Y. Conditioning on it reopens a backdoor and manufactures bias.

This is why Gerber & Green warn that mediation is one of the most misused techniques in social science: the intuitive “regress Y on D and M” (Baron–Kenny-style) silently assumes the mediator is itself unconfounded with the outcome — an assumption as strong as, and separate from, the one that justified the experiment on D. (Gerber and Green 2012) Credible mechanism work needs experimental manipulation of the mediator itself, or explicit, defended assumptions about M’s confounders — not a third variable bolted into the regression. (Gerber and Green 2012) The graph turns a tempting shortcut into a visible warning.

NoteTwo colliders worth memorising (Cunningham)

Google and the gender pay gap. Google argued that once you control for occupation (plus location, tenure, role), the gender gap disappears. But occupation is a collider: discrimination affects which jobs women get (\text{gender}\to\text{occupation}) and so does unobserved ability (\text{ability}\to\text{occupation}). Conditioning on occupation opens a path between gender and ability and can mask real discrimination. The “control” that made the gap vanish is exactly the one the backdoor criterion forbids. (Cunningham 2021)

The movie-star collider (Cunningham, crediting Gabriel Rossman). Why do film stars seem to be either gorgeous or talented, rarely both? In the population, beauty and talent are independent. But stardom is a collider: \text{beauty}\to\text{star}\leftarrow\text{talent}. Look only at stars (condition on the collider) and a spurious negative correlation appears — an untalented star must be beautiful, a plain star must be talented. Pure selection bias, manufactured by conditioning. (Cunningham 2021)

Intervention and the front door: Pearl’s do-operator

Cunningham’s backdoor language and Judea Pearl’s are two dialects of one theory; Pearl’s Primer supplies the piece that makes “cause” a verb. To intervene is written with the do-operator, do(X=x), and it is sharply different from merely observing X=x. Conditioning on a value “narrows our focus” to the cases where X happened to equal x — it changes your perception. Intervening “changes the system”: you reach in and set X, overriding whatever normally produces it. (Pearl et al. 2016) Formally, do(x) is graph surgery — erase every arrow pointing into X, which “emancipates it from all other influences,” so any remaining association with Y is genuinely causal. (Pearl et al. 2016) The backdoor criterion is precisely the condition under which the surgical \Pr(Y\mid do(x)) can be rewritten as ordinary, estimable conditional probabilities — seeing used to compute doing. (Pearl et al. 2016)

When every backdoor is blocked for you: the front-door

Sometimes there is an incurable unobserved confounder U between X and Y — no backdoor set exists, because the variable you’d need to adjust for is unmeasured. Pearl’s front-door criterion is the escape: if the effect of X on Y runs entirely through an observed mediator Z that is itself shielded from the confounder, you can still identify the effect — without any data on U. (Pearl et al. 2016)

His example is the 1950s smoking debate. The tobacco industry posited a “smoking gene” U that caused both the craving to smoke (X) and cancer (Y), which would make the observed link spurious. Pearl’s move: measure tar deposits Z, on the assumptions that smoking causes cancer only through tar (X\to Z\to Y) and the gene does not affect tar. Then estimate X\to Z and Z\to Y (adjusting for X), and chain them together. (Pearl et al. 2016) You have deconfounded U using only observed variables — what Pearl calls an “utter miracle.” (Pearl et al. 2016) The front door is rarer in practice than the backdoor (its “effect flows only through Z” assumption is demanding), but it is the proof that a credible mechanism can rescue identification where adjustment alone is helpless.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • “Control for everything you have.” Each control can close a backdoor or open a collider. Conditioning is surgery, not insurance. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Conditioning on a collider (or its descendant). It creates association from nothing — the subtlest bias in applied work, and invisible to R^2 or significance. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Controlling for a mediator when you want the total effect. You block the channel you meant to measure and under-state the effect. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Drawing the DAG to fit the result. The graph encodes assumptions; its missing arrows are claims. Draw it from theory, before estimation, and defend the absences. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Believing a DAG removes unobserved confounding. It only tells you what to condition on given the assumed structure; an omitted common cause you didn’t draw is still a backdoor left open. (Cunningham 2021)

Appendix · the mathematics

The backdoor adjustment formula. If a set Z satisfies the backdoor criterion for D\to Y, the causal effect is identified by conditioning then averaging over Z: \Pr(Y\mid \operatorname{do}(D=d)) = \sum_{z}\Pr(Y\mid D=d, Z=z)\,\Pr(Z=z), where \operatorname{do}(D=d) denotes setting D by intervention (not merely observing it). In means, \text{ATE}=\mathbb E_Z\big[\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1,Z]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0,Z]\big] — exactly the subclassification estimator of the next entry. The criterion is what licenses writing the causal \operatorname{do}-probability as ordinary conditional probabilities you can estimate.

Why a collider induces dependence (algebra). Let D\perp Y marginally and C=\alpha D+\beta Y+\varepsilon. Then \operatorname{Cov}(D,Y)=0, but conditional on C=c we have \beta Y \approx c-\alpha D-\varepsilon, so within a stratum of C a higher D forces a lower Y — a nonzero conditional covariance created purely by conditioning. This is the same algebra as the collider figure in Part III, now located on the graph.

Acyclicity and solvability. Acyclicity guarantees the structural equations can be ordered topologically and solved recursively (each variable from its parents), so the joint distribution factorises as \prod_i \Pr(X_i\mid \text{parents}(X_i)) — the Markov factorisation that makes d-separation read off the graph correspond to real conditional independence in the data.

## Reading the graph

A DAG has three ingredients: [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Nodes** are random variables.
- **Directed edges (arrows)** are direct causal effects: $D\to Y$ means $D$ causes $Y$. The **absence**
  of an arrow is the strongest claim of all — a firm theoretical commitment that *no* direct effect
  exists.
- **Acyclic** means no feedback loops: causation flows one way. Reverse causality isn't drawn as a
  cycle but by **splitting the variable across time** ($Y_1, Y_2$), so the arrows still point forward.

Behind the picture sits a **structural causal model** — a set of equations in which each variable is
generated by its **parents** (the nodes with arrows into it) plus a random shock. [@cunningham_mixtape]

your assumptions visible and falsifiable.

## Three structures, and how association flows

Every DAG is built from three elementary configurations, and *association* (statistical correlation,
causal or not) flows through them differently: [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **The chain / mediator** $D\to M\to Y$. $D$ affects $Y$ *only through* $M$. Association flows along
  the chain — that flow **is** the causal effect.
- **The fork / confounder** $D\leftarrow Z\to Y$. A common cause $Z$ drives both. This creates a
  **non-causal** association between $D$ and $Y$: they move together because they share an origin, not
  because one causes the other.
- **The collider** $D\to C\leftarrow Y$. Two arrows *collide* into a common effect. Here $D$ and $Y$
  are **independent** — until you condition on $C$.

## Paths, backdoors, and the dam

A **path** is any sequence of edges connecting $D$ and $Y$, ignoring arrow direction. The one you want
is the **direct/causal path** $D\to Y$. The troublemakers are **backdoor paths** — any path that
leaves $D$ through an *incoming* arrow, like $D\leftarrow Z\to Y$. [@cunningham_mixtape] A backdoor path
is a pipe through which confounding flows, manufacturing correlation that isn't cause.

Whether a path transmits association depends on what's on it — the intuition of **d-separation**:
[@cunningham_mixtape]

- A path through a **confounder** (a fork) is **open**: association flows. Conditioning on that
  confounder **blocks** it.
- A path through a **collider** is **naturally blocked**. The collider is a **dam**: it stops the flow
  — *unless* you condition on it (or its descendant), which **opens** the dam and lets spurious
  association through.

This is the crucial asymmetry. **Conditioning closes forks but opens colliders.** Controlling is not
free; it changes which pipes are open.

## The backdoor criterion: the rule for controls

Putting it together gives the **backdoor criterion**, the formal rule for *what to condition on*: a set
of variables identifies the causal effect of $D$ on $Y$ if it [@cunningham_mixtape]

1. **closes every backdoor path** (block each one by conditioning on a non-collider along it), and
2. **opens no new path** (never condition on a collider or a descendant of one).

Satisfy it and the leftover association between $D$ and $Y$ is **purely causal**. This is
selection-on-observables stated as graph surgery: the "controls" you need are exactly the ones that
shut the backdoors, and not one variable more.

```{python}
#| label: fig-dag
#| fig-cap: "A DAG with both a confounder and a collider. The causal effect of interest is D → Y (green). Z is a confounder opening the backdoor path D ← Z → Y (red), which you must close by conditioning on Z. C is a collider (D → C ← Y): it sits on no backdoor path and is naturally blocked, so conditioning on it would OPEN a spurious path and manufacture bias. The backdoor criterion here: control for Z, never for C."
#| code-fold: true
```

## Good vs bad controls, now derivable

The graph delivers Part III's [control rules](../03-regression/regression-and-causality.qmd) as
*theorems*: [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Conditioning on a mediator** $M$ in $D\to M\to Y$ **blocks the very causal flow you want**,
  stripping out the indirect effect and *under*-stating the total impact. This is **over-control** —
  the occupation-in-the-wage-regression mistake, now visibly a blocked chain.
- **Conditioning on a collider** $C$ **opens** a closed path and **invents** association between
  variables that were independent. This is selection/collider bias — the gravest control error.

### Mediation: when the mechanism *is* the question

Sometimes you *want* the path through $M$ — you ask not just *whether* $D$ affects $Y$ but **why** and
**how much through which channel**. That is **mediation analysis**, which decomposes the **total
effect** into a **direct effect** ($D\to Y$, not through $M$) and an **indirect effect** (the part
flowing $D\to M\to Y$). [@gerber_green] The DAG makes the stakes precise, and they cut two ways:

- To estimate the **total** effect, **never condition on the mediator** — that is the over-control
  above, blocking the indirect channel and biasing the total toward zero. [@cunningham_mixtape]
- To estimate the **direct/indirect split**, you *must* reason about $M$ — but naively "controlling for
  $M$" does **not** cleanly identify the direct effect, because $M$ is a [post-treatment
  variable](../03-regression/regression-and-causality.qmd) and is typically a **collider** linking $D$
  to the unobserved causes of $Y$. Conditioning on it reopens a backdoor and **manufactures** bias.

This is why Gerber & Green warn that mediation is **one of the most misused techniques** in social
science: the intuitive "regress $Y$ on $D$ and $M$" (Baron–Kenny-style) silently assumes the mediator
is itself unconfounded with the outcome — an assumption as strong as, and *separate from*, the one that
justified the experiment on $D$. [@gerber_green] Credible mechanism work needs **experimental
manipulation of the mediator itself**, or explicit, defended assumptions about $M$'s confounders — not
a third variable bolted into the regression. [@gerber_green] The graph turns a tempting shortcut into a
visible warning.

::: {.callout-note title="Two colliders worth memorising (Cunningham)"}
**Google and the gender pay gap.** Google argued that once you control for *occupation* (plus location,
tenure, role), the gender gap disappears. But occupation is a **collider**: discrimination affects
which jobs women get ($\text{gender}\to\text{occupation}$) and so does unobserved ability
($\text{ability}\to\text{occupation}$). Conditioning on occupation opens a path between gender and
ability and can **mask real discrimination**. The "control" that made the gap vanish is exactly the one
the backdoor criterion forbids. [@cunningham_mixtape]

**The movie-star collider** (Cunningham, crediting Gabriel Rossman). Why do film stars seem to be
*either* gorgeous *or* talented, rarely both? In the population, beauty and talent are independent. But
stardom is a collider: $\text{beauty}\to\text{star}\leftarrow\text{talent}$. Look only at stars
(condition on the collider) and a **spurious negative** correlation appears — an untalented star must
be beautiful, a plain star must be talented. Pure selection bias, manufactured by conditioning.
[@cunningham_mixtape]
:::

## Intervention and the front door: Pearl's do-operator

Cunningham's backdoor language and Judea Pearl's are two dialects of one theory; Pearl's *Primer* supplies
the piece that makes "cause" a *verb*. To **intervene** is written with the **do-operator**, $do(X=x)$, and
it is sharply different from merely **observing** $X=x$. Conditioning on a value *"narrows our focus"* to the
cases where $X$ happened to equal $x$ — it changes your *perception*. Intervening *"changes the system"*: you
reach in and set $X$, overriding whatever normally produces it. [@pearl_primer] Formally, $do(x)$ is **graph
surgery** — *erase every arrow pointing into $X$*, which *"emancipates it from all other influences,"* so any
remaining association with $Y$ is genuinely causal. [@pearl_primer] The backdoor criterion is precisely the
condition under which the surgical $\Pr(Y\mid do(x))$ can be rewritten as ordinary, estimable conditional
probabilities — *seeing* used to compute *doing*. [@pearl_primer]

### When every backdoor is blocked *for you*: the front-door

Sometimes there is an **incurable unobserved confounder** $U$ between $X$ and $Y$ — no backdoor set exists,
because the variable you'd need to adjust for is unmeasured. Pearl's **front-door criterion** is the escape:
if the effect of $X$ on $Y$ runs entirely through an observed **mediator** $Z$ that is itself *shielded* from
the confounder, you can still identify the effect — *without any data on $U$.* [@pearl_primer]

His example is the 1950s smoking debate. The tobacco industry posited a **"smoking gene"** $U$ that caused
both the craving to smoke ($X$) *and* cancer ($Y$), which would make the observed link spurious. Pearl's
move: measure **tar deposits** $Z$, on the assumptions that smoking causes cancer *only through* tar
($X\to Z\to Y$) and the gene does *not* affect tar. Then estimate $X\to Z$ and $Z\to Y$ (adjusting for $X$),
and **chain them together**. [@pearl_primer] You have deconfounded $U$ using only observed variables — what
Pearl calls an *"utter miracle."* [@pearl_primer] The front door is rarer in practice than the backdoor (its
"effect flows *only* through $Z$" assumption is demanding), but it is the proof that a credible **mechanism**
can rescue identification where adjustment alone is helpless.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **"Control for everything you have."** Each control can *close* a backdoor *or open a collider*.
  Conditioning is surgery, not insurance. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Conditioning on a collider (or its descendant).** It creates association from nothing — the
  subtlest bias in applied work, and invisible to $R^2$ or significance. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Controlling for a mediator when you want the total effect.** You block the channel you meant to
  measure and under-state the effect. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Drawing the DAG to fit the result.** The graph encodes *assumptions*; its missing arrows are
  claims. Draw it from theory, before estimation, and defend the absences. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Believing a DAG removes unobserved confounding.** It only tells you what to condition on *given*
  the assumed structure; an omitted common cause you didn't draw is still a backdoor left open.
  [@cunningham_mixtape]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The backdoor adjustment formula.** If a set $Z$ satisfies the backdoor criterion for $D\to Y$, the
causal effect is identified by *conditioning then averaging over* $Z$:
$$
\Pr(Y\mid \operatorname{do}(D=d)) = \sum_{z}\Pr(Y\mid D=d, Z=z)\,\Pr(Z=z),
$$
where $\operatorname{do}(D=d)$ denotes *setting* $D$ by intervention (not merely observing it). In
means, $\text{ATE}=\mathbb E_Z\big[\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1,Z]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0,Z]\big]$ — exactly the
[subclassification estimator](matching-and-propensity-scores.qmd) of the next entry. The criterion is
what *licenses* writing the causal $\operatorname{do}$-probability as ordinary conditional
probabilities you can estimate.

**Why a collider induces dependence (algebra).** Let $D\perp Y$ marginally and $C=\alpha D+\beta
Y+\varepsilon$. Then $\operatorname{Cov}(D,Y)=0$, but conditional on $C=c$ we have
$\beta Y \approx c-\alpha D-\varepsilon$, so within a stratum of $C$ a higher $D$ forces a lower $Y$ —
a **nonzero conditional covariance** created purely by conditioning. This is the same algebra as the
[collider figure](../03-regression/regression-and-causality.qmd) in Part III, now located on the graph.

**Acyclicity and solvability.** Acyclicity guarantees the structural equations can be ordered
topologically and solved recursively (each variable from its parents), so the joint distribution
**factorises** as $\prod_i \Pr(X_i\mid \text{parents}(X_i))$ — the Markov factorisation that makes
d-separation read off the graph correspond to real conditional independence in the data.

:::
