Regression Discontinuity

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

An arbitrary cutoff turns a bureaucratic rule into a natural experiment — comparison so local it is almost a coin flip.

Wherever a sharp rule assigns a treatment by a threshold — pass at 80, eligible at 65, win at 50% — the people just on either side are, by luck of a few decimal points, interchangeable. The jump in the outcome right at the cutoff can only be the treatment, because nothing else changes discontinuously there. RD is the observational design closest to an experiment, with the most defensible identifying assumption in this Part — and the narrowest reach.

Why it matters

IV needed an exclusion restriction you can only argue for. RD needs something far easier to believe: that the world is smooth except where the rule cuts. (Cunningham 2021) That makes it one of the most credible strategies in observational social science — its key assumption is nearly visible in a scatterplot. (Cunningham 2021) Rules with thresholds are everywhere in administrative data — test scores, age, income, vote share — so RD turns the bureaucracy’s arbitrariness into identification.

The idea: a running variable and a cutoff

RD applies when treatment D is assigned by whether a continuous running variable (a.k.a. forcing or score variable) X crosses a known cutoff c. (Cunningham 2021) The engine is comparability at the boundary: a student scoring 79.9 and one scoring 80.1 are effectively identical in preparedness — one just had marginally better luck on exam day — yet only the second gets the scholarship. (Cunningham 2021) So any jump in the outcome exactly at c is caused by the treatment, not by the running variable, which varies smoothly across it. The cutoff manufactures as-good-as-random assignment in a narrow window — a local experiment.

Sharp vs fuzzy

Two flavours, by how strictly the rule binds: (Cunningham 2021)

  • Sharp RD. Treatment is a deterministic step of the running variable: D_i=\mathbf 1[X_i\ge c] — probability jumps cleanly from 0 to 1 at c. The RD estimate is simply the jump in the outcome at the cutoff.
  • Fuzzy RD. Crossing the cutoff changes the probability of treatment, not treatment itself (some above stay untreated; some below get treated). Now the threshold is an instrument: the effect is the jump in the outcome divided by the jump in treatment probability at c — a LATE for compliers (those the rule actually moves). (Cunningham 2021) Fuzzy RD is IV with the cutoff as Z.

The identifying assumption: continuity

RD rests on continuity of potential outcomes at the cutoff: \mathbb E[Y(0)\mid X] and \mathbb E[Y(1)\mid X] are smooth functions of X through c. (Cunningham 2021) In words: absent the treatment, nothing about the outcome would jump at c — the cutoff is special only because it triggers treatment. Then the observed discontinuity in Y is the causal effect, full stop. Continuity is weaker and more credible than IV’s exclusion or matching’s CIA: it allows the running variable to be related to the outcome in any smooth way; it forbids only a coincidental jump at the exact threshold.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(2)
n = 600
X = rng.uniform(-1, 1, n)              # running variable, cutoff at 0
tau = 2.0                              # true jump (causal effect) at the cutoff
Y = 1.0 + 1.5 * X + 0.8 * X**2 + tau * (X >= 0) + rng.normal(0, 0.5, n)

def local_fit(mask):
    b = np.polyfit(X[mask], Y[mask], 1)
    return b
bl = local_fit(X < 0); br = local_fit(X >= 0)
xl = np.linspace(-1, 0, 50); xr = np.linspace(0, 1, 50)
jump = np.polyval(br, 0) - np.polyval(bl, 0)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.scatter(X[X < 0], Y[X < 0], s=10, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.5, label="untreated (X < c)")
ax.scatter(X[X >= 0], Y[X >= 0], s=10, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.5, label="treated (X ≥ c)")
ax.plot(xl, np.polyval(bl, xl), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.4)
ax.plot(xr, np.polyval(br, xr), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.4)
ax.axvline(0, color="#c0392b", ls=":", lw=1.4)
ax.annotate("", xy=(0, np.polyval(br, 0)), xytext=(0, np.polyval(bl, 0)),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="<->", color="#c0392b", lw=2.2))
ax.text(0.04, (np.polyval(br,0)+np.polyval(bl,0))/2, f"jump = {jump:.2f}\n(true {tau})",
        color="#c0392b", fontsize=9, va="center")
ax.set_xlabel("running variable  X   (cutoff c = 0)"); ax.set_ylabel("outcome Y")
ax.set_title("The treatment effect is the jump at the cutoff")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Estimation: stay local, stay simple

Since few units sit exactly at c, you estimate the outcome’s limits from each side and take their difference. The modern default is local linear regression: fit a separate line to the data within a bandwidth h on each side of the cutoff. (Cunningham 2021) Two choices matter:

  • Bandwidth h is a bias–variance dial in disguise. Narrow h uses only the most comparable units (low bias) but little data (high variance); wide h adds precision but risks bias if the smooth trend curves. (Cunningham 2021)
  • Keep the polynomial low. High-order global polynomials (X^3, X^4) were once standard but are now discouraged: they overfit noise and can manufacture illusory jumps at the boundary. Prefer linear or quadratic local fits. (Cunningham 2021)

The threat: manipulation

RD has one characteristic failure: sorting. If units can precisely manipulate their position relative to the cutoff, those just below are no longer comparable to those just above — think people misreporting income to drop just under a benefits threshold. (Cunningham 2021) The diagnostic is the McCrary density test: examine the distribution of the running variable for a discontinuous jump in density at c. Bunching just on the favourable side is the fingerprint of manipulation and invalidates the design. (Cunningham 2021) (Continuity of the density is something you can look at — a rare testable implication in this Part.)

What you get: local, but clean

RD’s bargain is sharp. Internal validity near the cutoff is exceptional — close to experimental. But external validity is local: the estimate is the effect for units at the threshold and need not generalise. (Cunningham 2021) A scholarship’s effect estimated at a score of 80 says nothing about how it would help A-students or C-students. You trade reach for credibility — the recurring price of a clean natural experiment.

NoteFour RD classics
  • Close elections & incumbency (Lee 2008). The 50% vote-share threshold is a sharp RD: winning a close race is as-good-as-random. Incumbency raises a party’s reelection probability by ~40 percentage points. (Cunningham 2021)
  • The drinking age (Carpenter & Dobkin 2009). Age is the running variable, 21 the cutoff. Mortality from external causes (car crashes, etc.) jumps at 21 — roughly +7.7 deaths per 100,000. (Cunningham 2021)
  • The original RD (Thistlethwaite & Campbell 1960). Compared National Merit award winners just above and below the qualifying score — the design’s debut. (Cunningham 2021)
  • Class size, Maimonides’ rule (Angrist & Lavy 1999). Israeli law caps classes at 40, so a 41st pupil forces a split into two ~20-pupil classes — a fuzzy RD. Smaller classes raised achievement, a Wald estimate of about −0.27 SD (per added pupil) for fourth-graders’ scores. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Manipulable running variables. Precise sorting around c breaks comparability. Run the McCrary density test; look for bunching. (Cunningham 2021)

  • High-order polynomials. They overfit and fabricate boundary jumps. Use local linear/quadratic and show robustness to bandwidth. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Over-claiming generality. RD identifies a local effect at the cutoff; don’t extrapolate to units far away. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Another rule at the same cutoff. If a different program also switches at c (e.g. multiple benefits at the same income line), continuity fails and the jump conflates them. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Ignoring the fuzzy denominator. In fuzzy RD you must scale by the jump in treatment probability; the raw outcome jump understates the effect. (Cunningham 2021)

Appendix · the mathematics

The sharp RD estimand. Under continuity, the effect at the cutoff is the difference of one-sided limits, \tau_{\text{SRD}}=\lim_{x\downarrow c}\mathbb E[Y\mid X=x]-\lim_{x\uparrow c}\mathbb E[Y\mid X=x] =\mathbb E[Y(1)-Y(0)\mid X=c]. Continuity is what lets the two limits estimate the treated and untreated potential-outcome means at the same point c.

Fuzzy RD as a ratio (Wald at the cutoff). \tau_{\text{FRD}}=\frac{\lim_{x\downarrow c}\mathbb E[Y\mid x]-\lim_{x\uparrow c}\mathbb E[Y\mid x]} {\lim_{x\downarrow c}\mathbb E[D\mid x]-\lim_{x\uparrow c}\mathbb E[D\mid x]}, the outcome discontinuity over the treatment-probability discontinuity — the Wald/IV estimator with the threshold-crossing indicator as the instrument, identifying the LATE for cutoff compliers.

Bandwidth and the bias–variance tradeoff. Local linear estimation has bias O(h^2) (from curvature of the conditional mean) and variance O(1/(nh)). Minimising MSE \propto h^4 + 1/(nh) gives an optimal h^\star\propto n^{-1/5} — formalising “narrow enough to be comparable, wide enough to be precise,” the same logic as kernel/CV bandwidth selection.
