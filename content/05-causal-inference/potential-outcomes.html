Potential Outcomes & the Fundamental Problem

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Causation, defined precisely: the gap between what happened and what would have happened — and why that gap is never directly observed.

A causal effect is a comparison between two worlds: the outcome with the treatment and the outcome without it, for the same unit at the same moment. Exactly one of those worlds is ever realised — the other is a counterfactual we can never see. That single missing number is the fundamental problem of causal inference, and every method in this Part is a strategy for filling it in credibly. Master this language and the whole summit becomes one idea applied many ways.

Why it matters

The bridge from regression left us with a verdict: a coefficient is causal only under an assumption data can’t verify, and you usually need a design. To state those assumptions precisely we need a language for causation itself — one that defines an effect before any estimator, so we can say exactly what we are trying to learn and exactly what could go wrong. That language is the potential outcomes framework (the Rubin Causal Model), shared by Cunningham, Gerber & Green, and Angrist & Pischke alike. (Cunningham 2021; Gerber and Green 2012) It reframes causal inference as a missing-data problem — which is the most clarifying sentence in this entire book.

Two potential outcomes, one effect

For each unit i and a binary treatment, imagine two outcomes: (Cunningham 2021)

  • Y_i(1) — what i’s outcome would be if treated,
  • Y_i(0) — what i’s outcome would be if untreated (the control state).

The individual treatment effect is their difference, the gap between the two worlds for that one person: \tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0).

These are defined for everyone, treated or not — they are properties of the unit, not of what happened to it. (Cunningham 2021) A small intuition pump from the corpus: to know the effect of an article on your mood, compare your mood had you read it (Y(1)=3) with your mood had you not (Y(0)=5); the effect is \tau=-2. (Cunningham 2021) Nothing statistical yet — just a careful definition of what “effect” means.

The fundamental problem of causal inference

Here is the catch that defines the field. For any unit you observe only one potential outcome — the one corresponding to the treatment it actually received. The other is a counterfactual, and it is missing. (Cunningham 2021) This is the fundamental problem of causal inference: the individual effect \tau_i is never observable, because it requires two numbers and reality hands you one. (Cunningham 2021; Gerber and Green 2012)

The link between the unobservable potential outcomes and the single number you do see is the switching equation (or observation equation), where D_i\in\{0,1\} indicates treatment: Y_i = D_i\,Y_i(1) + (1-D_i)\,Y_i(0). Set D_i=1 and you observe Y_i(1), with Y_i(0) forever hidden; set D_i=0 and the reverse. (Cunningham 2021) The treatment indicator is a switch that reveals one world and conceals the other. Causal inference is the science of reasoning about the concealed one.

What we can hope to learn: the estimands

Since individual effects are out of reach, we retreat to averages over groups — the population quantities we actually try to estimate (the estimands): (Cunningham 2021)

  • Average Treatment Effect (ATE) — the average effect across the whole population: \;\text{ATE} = \mathbb E[Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)].
  • Average Treatment effect on the Treated (ATT) — the average among those who actually got treated: \;\text{ATT} = \mathbb E[Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)\mid D_i=1].
  • Average Treatment effect on the Untreated (ATU) — the average among the untreated: \;\text{ATU} = \mathbb E[Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)\mid D_i=0].

These can differ sharply. A job-training program may help those who enrol (ATT) far more than it would help those who stayed away (ATU) — which average you want depends on the policy question (expand the program to everyone? then ATE; evaluate the current program? then ATT). (Cunningham 2021) Being explicit about the estimand is half of thinking clearly about causation.

Why a naive comparison fails: selection bias

The tempting estimator is the naive difference in group means: average outcome of the treated minus average outcome of the untreated, \mathbb E[Y_i\mid D_i=1]-\mathbb E[Y_i\mid D_i=0]. It is usually biased, because the groups were not comparable to begin with. (Cunningham 2021) Using the switching equation, the naive comparison decomposes exactly: (Cunningham 2021) \underbrace{\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0]}_{\text{naive comparison}} = \underbrace{\text{ATT}}_{\text{the effect we want}} + \underbrace{\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]}_{\textbf{selection bias}}.

The second term is selection bias: the difference between the groups in their untreated state. It asks — how would the treated and control groups have compared even if no one had been treated? If sicker people check into hospital, comparing the hospitalised to the healthy makes hospitals look lethal, because the hospitalised “would have been sicker even if they’d stayed home.” (Cunningham 2021) The bias has nothing to do with the treatment’s effect; it is baked into who ends up where.

NoteCunningham’s surgery-vs-chemo reversal

The starkest illustration in the Mixtape: a surgeon assigns patients to surgery when it would extend their life and to chemotherapy otherwise — optimal medicine, ruinous statistics. Across the patients the true ATE is +0.6 years (surgery genuinely helps on average), yet the naive difference in means is -0.4 — surgery looks worse. The culprit is a selection bias of -4.8: the surgery group was naturally far sicker, so their good-outcome-under-no-surgery baseline was much lower. Selection bias doesn’t just shrink the estimate — here it flips its sign. (Cunningham 2021)

The figure makes the mechanism visible: when assignment depends on the untreated baseline, the observed gap measures who got sorted where as much as what the treatment did.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
n = 400
severity = rng.uniform(0, 1, n)            # 1 = sickest
y0 = 8.0 - 6.0 * severity + rng.normal(0, 0.4, n)   # survival w/o surgery: sicker -> lower
tau = 0.6 + 0.0 * severity                  # surgery adds ~0.6 yr on average (true ATE>0)
y1 = y0 + tau + rng.normal(0, 0.1, n)
# Sicker patients are routed to surgery (selection on the untreated baseline).
D = (severity + rng.normal(0, 0.15, n)) > 0.5

ate = np.mean(y1 - y0)
obs_treated = y1[D].mean()
obs_control = y0[~D].mean()
naive = obs_treated - obs_control
sel_bias = y0[D].mean() - y0[~D].mean()     # difference in untreated baselines

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.scatter(severity[~D], y0[~D], s=10, color="#c9ccd1", alpha=0.6, label="control: observed Y(0)")
ax.scatter(severity[D], y1[D], s=10, color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.6, label="treated: observed Y(1)")
ax.axhline(obs_control, color="#9aa0a6", lw=1.6, ls="--")
ax.axhline(obs_treated, color="#1f8a4c", lw=1.6, ls="--")
# selection-bias bracket: treated group's hidden Y(0) baseline vs control's
ax.axhline(y0[D].mean(), color="#c0392b", lw=1.4, ls=":")
ax.annotate("", xy=(0.04, y0[D].mean()), xytext=(0.04, obs_control),
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="<->", color="#c0392b", lw=1.8))
ax.text(0.06, (y0[D].mean()+obs_control)/2, f"selection bias\n= {sel_bias:+.1f}",
        color="#c0392b", fontsize=8, va="center")
ax.text(0.62, obs_treated+0.3, f"treated observed mean = {obs_treated:.1f}", color="#1f8a4c", fontsize=8)
ax.text(0.62, obs_control+0.3, f"control observed mean = {obs_control:.1f}", color="#6b6f76", fontsize=8)
ax.set_title(f"True ATE = {ate:+.1f}, but naive comparison = {naive:+.1f}")
ax.set_xlabel("severity  (sicker →)"); ax.set_ylabel("survival (years)")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The lesson the whole Part builds on: identification is about making the selection-bias term vanish. A randomized experiment forces it to zero by design; every observational method is an argument that, conditional on something, it is as if zero.

SUTVA: the assumption that makes outcomes well-defined

The framework quietly assumes the potential outcomes even exist as stable, single numbers. That is the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA), and it has two parts: (Cunningham 2021)

  • No interference (no spillovers). Unit i’s potential outcomes depend only on i’s own treatment, not on whether others are treated. (Cunningham 2021) Tutoring a student who then helps a control-group friend violates it — the friend’s Y(0) was altered by someone else’s treatment.
  • No hidden variations of treatment. There is one well-defined “version” of treatment, so Y_i(1) is unambiguous. A vague or inconsistently delivered treatment has many versions and no single potential outcome. (Cunningham 2021)

Classic violations to watch for: spillovers/contagion between units; resource displacement (moving equipment from control to treatment classrooms changes the controls’ baseline); and general-equilibrium effects (a job-training program so large it shifts the market wage, changing the “no-treatment” world itself). (Cunningham 2021) SUTVA is why causal claims need a clearly localised, non-interfering treatment — and why scaling a program up can break the very estimate that justified it.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reading the naive difference as the effect. It equals ATT plus selection bias; unless the groups are comparable in their untreated state, it can shrink, inflate, or even reverse the truth. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Confusing the estimands. ATE, ATT, and ATU answer different policy questions and can differ greatly under effect heterogeneity. State which one you mean before estimating. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Forgetting the effect is unit-level and unobservable. \tau_i exists for everyone but is never seen; all we ever estimate are averages, and only under assumptions. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Ignoring SUTVA. Spillovers, multiple treatment versions, and general-equilibrium effects quietly invalidate the potential outcomes themselves — no estimator fixes a mis-defined estimand. (Cunningham 2021)

Appendix · the mathematics

Deriving the selection-bias decomposition. The naive comparison uses observed outcomes, which the switching equation ties to potential outcomes: \mathbb E[Y\mid D=1]=\mathbb E[Y(1)\mid D=1] and \mathbb E[Y\mid D=0]=\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]. Subtract, then add and subtract \mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1] (the treated group’s missing untreated baseline): \mathbb E[Y\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0] = \underbrace{\big(\mathbb E[Y(1)\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]\big)}_{\text{ATT}} + \underbrace{\big(\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]\big)}_{\text{selection bias}}.

The fuller ATE form. Let \pi=\Pr(D=1). Writing the naive comparison in terms of the ATE, Cunningham shows (Cunningham 2021) \mathbb E[Y\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0] = \text{ATE} + \underbrace{\big(\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]\big)}_{\text{selection bias}} + \underbrace{(1-\pi)\,(\text{ATT}-\text{ATU})}_{\text{heterogeneous-effect bias}}. Two distinct biases, then: the groups differ in their baselines (selection bias) and the treatment may affect the treated differently from the untreated (heterogeneity). Random assignment kills both at once, because it makes D independent of both potential outcomes: (Y(1),Y(0))\perp D forces \mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]=\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0] and \text{ATT}=\text{ATU}=\text{ATE} — the subject of the next entry.

argument that, conditional on something, it is *as if* zero.

## SUTVA: the assumption that makes outcomes well-defined

The framework quietly assumes the potential outcomes even *exist* as stable, single numbers. That is
the **Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA)**, and it has two parts: [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **No interference (no spillovers).** Unit $i$'s potential outcomes depend only on $i$'s *own*
  treatment, not on whether others are treated. [@cunningham_mixtape] Tutoring a student who then
  helps a control-group friend violates it — the friend's $Y(0)$ was altered by *someone else's*
  treatment.
- **No hidden variations of treatment.** There is one well-defined "version" of treatment, so
  $Y_i(1)$ is unambiguous. A vague or inconsistently delivered treatment has many versions and no
  single potential outcome. [@cunningham_mixtape]

Classic violations to watch for: **spillovers/contagion** between units; **resource displacement**
(moving equipment from control to treatment classrooms changes the controls' baseline); and
**general-equilibrium effects** (a job-training program so large it shifts the market wage, changing
the "no-treatment" world itself). [@cunningham_mixtape] SUTVA is why causal claims need a clearly
*localised*, *non-interfering* treatment — and why scaling a program up can break the very estimate
that justified it.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Reading the naive difference as the effect.** It equals ATT *plus selection bias*; unless the
  groups are comparable in their untreated state, it can shrink, inflate, or even **reverse** the
  truth. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Confusing the estimands.** ATE, ATT, and ATU answer different policy questions and can differ
  greatly under effect heterogeneity. State which one you mean *before* estimating. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Forgetting the effect is unit-level and unobservable.** $\tau_i$ exists for everyone but is never
  seen; all we ever estimate are *averages*, and only under assumptions. [@cunningham_mixtape]

- **Ignoring SUTVA.** Spillovers, multiple treatment versions, and general-equilibrium effects quietly
  invalidate the potential outcomes themselves — no estimator fixes a mis-defined estimand.
  [@cunningham_mixtape]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Deriving the selection-bias decomposition.** The naive comparison uses observed outcomes, which the
switching equation ties to potential outcomes: $\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1]=\mathbb E[Y(1)\mid D=1]$ and
$\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0]=\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]$. Subtract, then **add and subtract**
$\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]$ (the treated group's *missing* untreated baseline):
$$
\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0]
= \underbrace{\big(\mathbb E[Y(1)\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]\big)}_{\text{ATT}}
+ \underbrace{\big(\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]\big)}_{\text{selection bias}}.
$$

**The fuller ATE form.** Let $\pi=\Pr(D=1)$. Writing the naive comparison in terms of the ATE,
Cunningham shows [@cunningham_mixtape]
$$
\mathbb E[Y\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y\mid D=0]
= \text{ATE} + \underbrace{\big(\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]-\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]\big)}_{\text{selection bias}}
+ \underbrace{(1-\pi)\,(\text{ATT}-\text{ATU})}_{\text{heterogeneous-effect bias}}.
$$
Two distinct biases, then: the groups differ in their **baselines** (selection bias) *and* the
treatment may affect the treated differently from the untreated (heterogeneity). **Random assignment
kills both at once**, because it makes $D$ independent of *both* potential outcomes:
$(Y(1),Y(0))\perp D$ forces $\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=1]=\mathbb E[Y(0)\mid D=0]$ and
$\text{ATT}=\text{ATU}=\text{ATE}$ — the subject of the [next entry](randomized-experiments.qmd).

:::
