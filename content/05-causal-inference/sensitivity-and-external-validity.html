Sensitivity Analysis & External Validity

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Two questions to ask of every causal estimate: how fragile is it to the confounder you couldn’t see, and where does it actually apply?

A causal estimate is the beginning of an argument, not the end. Two honest questions remain. First, how fragile is it — how strong would an unobserved confounder have to be to erase it? Selection-on- observables rests on an untestable assumption, so the grown-up move is not to pretend it holds but to quantify how much it could be violated before your conclusion breaks. Second, where does it apply — a clean effect here need not transport there. This entry is the credibility coda to the summit: stress-testing assumptions, and knowing the reach of an answer.

Why it matters

Every design in this Part rests on an assumption you cannot verify — CIA, exclusion, parallel trends, continuity. Maturity is not asserting the assumption but bounding the consequences of its failure, and being clear about whose effect you actually estimated. (Cunningham 2021) These are the tools that separate a credible empirical claim from a fragile one — and the natural bridge to Part VI, where estimates meet a messy world that rarely matches the study sample.

Part A · Sensitivity analysis: how fragile is the estimate?

The core question

Selection-on-observables designs assume that conditioning on X makes treatment as-good-as-random — untestable, because the confounder you fear is unobserved. (Cunningham 2021) Sensitivity analysis converts that worry into a number by asking: how strong would an unobserved confounder have to be to explain away the result? (Cunningham 2021) If the estimate only breaks under an implausibly powerful hidden factor, it is robust; if a whisper of confounding undoes it, it is fragile. You never prove no confounding — you measure how much it would take.

Rosenbaum bounds (Γ)

For matched designs, Paul Rosenbaum’s bounds introduce a parameter \Gamma measuring hidden bias: the differential odds that two units identical on observed X receive treatment. (Cunningham 2021)

  • \Gamma=1: no hidden bias — matched units are equally likely to be treated (a perfect experiment).
  • \Gamma=2: one unit is twice as likely as its match to be treated, because of something unseen.

The procedure: raise \Gamma from 1 upward (1.1, 1.2, …) and watch the treatment effect’s p-value; the breaking point is the \Gamma at which significance is lost. (Cunningham 2021) A result that survives to \Gamma=5 is highly robust — only an enormous hidden bias could overturn it; one that dies at \Gamma=1.1 is hostage to the faintest unobserved difference. The figure contrasts the two.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

gamma = np.linspace(1, 5, 200)
# Illustrative worst-case p-value as a function of hidden bias Γ (monotone increasing).
# Robust study: strong signal, p climbs slowly. Fragile: weak signal, p climbs fast.
p_robust = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-(2.2 * (gamma - 3.4))))   # crosses 0.05 near Γ≈3.4
p_robust = 0.001 + 0.6 * p_robust
p_fragile = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-(6.0 * (gamma - 1.2))))  # crosses 0.05 near Γ≈1.2
p_fragile = 0.02 + 0.7 * p_fragile

def crossing(g, p): return g[np.argmin(np.abs(p - 0.05))]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.0, 4.2))
ax.plot(gamma, p_robust, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.4, label="robust study")
ax.plot(gamma, p_fragile, color="#d98a29", lw=2.4, label="fragile study")
ax.axhline(0.05, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.4)
ax.text(4.4, 0.065, "p = 0.05", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
for p, c in [(p_robust, "#1f3a5f"), (p_fragile, "#d98a29")]:
    gx = crossing(gamma, p)
    ax.axvline(gx, color=c, ls=":", lw=1.2)
    ax.text(gx + 0.05, 0.5, f"breaks at\nΓ ≈ {gx:.1f}", color=c, fontsize=8)
ax.set_xlabel("hidden-bias parameter  Γ  (1 = no hidden bias)")
ax.set_ylabel("worst-case p-value")
ax.set_title("How much unobserved confounding would it take to break the result?")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="center right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Oster’s δ: coefficient stability

A complementary idea, due to Emily Oster (and used in causal ML by Chernozhukov), reads robustness off how the treatment coefficient moves as you add controls. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) The logic: if piling in observed controls barely budges the coefficient while the R^2 rises substantially, then the unobservables — assumed to behave like the observables — are unlikely to drive it to zero. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) The key assumption is \delta=1: unobserved confounders are at most as important as the observed controls in explaining the outcome. Setting \delta=1 yields a bias-adjusted bound on the coefficient; if that bound stays away from zero, the effect is robust. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) (Coefficient stability alone is not enough — it must be paired with the R^2 movement, or a coefficient can be stable simply because the controls explain nothing.)

The E-value

A third framing measures sensitivity in the language of association strength. The E-value is the minimum strength of association — on the risk-ratio scale — that an unmeasured confounder would need with both the treatment and the outcome to fully explain away the observed effect. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) A large E-value (say, the confounder would need a risk ratio of 4 with each) means only a very strong hidden factor could do it; if that exceeds the association of any measured control, the result is credible. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024) The E-value’s appeal is interpretability: it puts the burden of doubt in concrete terms a reader can weigh against known predictors.

Part B · External validity: where does the estimate apply?

Internal vs external validity

A study has internal validity if it correctly identifies the effect for the sample under study, and external validity if that finding applies to other populations, settings, and times. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) The designs of this Part are engineered for internal validity — and a recurring theme is that they often buy it by narrowing external validity. A causal estimate can be airtight and parochial.

SATE vs PATE

The distinction sharpens with two estimands: (Gerber and Green 2012)

  • SATE — the sample average treatment effect, for the units actually in the study. Its standard errors are smaller (it treats the sample’s potential outcomes as fixed).
  • PATE — the population average treatment effect, for the broader super-population the sample was meant to represent.

The transportability problem: a clean SATE may not equal the PATE if the sample was a convenience sample or if effects are heterogeneous across the population. (Gerber and Green 2012) An experiment on volunteer undergraduates can be internally flawless and still mispredict the effect in the general public — not because the analysis is wrong, but because the people are different.

The local designs, revisited

Two of the most credible designs are precisely the most local — a trade we’ve met before: (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  • LATE identifies the effect only for compliers — those moved by the instrument. Always-takers and never-takers are invisible, and their effects may differ. A draft-lottery LATE speaks for lottery-induced soldiers, not volunteers.
  • RD identifies the effect only at the cutoff; extrapolating to units far away leans on bold functional-form assumptions. (Cunningham 2021)

Internal validity and external validity are often in tension: the design tricks that make a comparison airtight (a narrow window, a specific instrument) are exactly what localise the answer.

Generalising honestly

Three routes extend a local or sample estimate toward a target: (Gerber and Green 2012)

  • Replication — re-run the study in new contexts and see whether the effect is stable; the single most convincing evidence of transportability.
  • Meta-analysis — pool many studies (often in a Bayesian frame), weighting by precision and accounting for sampling bias, to triangulate a general effect.
  • Re-weighting — if the sample’s covariate distribution differs from the target population’s, re-weight (e.g. inverse-probability weighting toward the target) so the estimate reflects their composition, not the study’s. Transportability becomes a weighting problem — if you observe the covariates that drive effect heterogeneity.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reporting a point estimate with no sensitivity analysis. If CIA is untestable, show how much confounding would break the result (Rosenbaum Γ, Oster δ, E-value). Silence is not robustness. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Coefficient stability without the R^2 movement. A coefficient can be stable because the controls explain nothing; Oster’s logic needs both. (Chernozhukov et al. 2024)

  • Reading a LATE or RD estimate as a population effect. They are local — to compliers, to the cutoff. Name the subpopulation. (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

  • Generalising from a convenience sample. A flawless SATE on volunteers need not be the PATE; re-weight or replicate before extrapolating. (Gerber and Green 2012)

  • Extrapolating RD far from the cutoff. Beyond the boundary you are trusting functional form, not the design. (Cunningham 2021)

  • Chernozhukov et al., Applied Causal Inference Powered by ML and AI — Oster’s \delta / coefficient stability (coefficient movement + R^2, \delta=1 assuming unobservables as important as observables) and the E-value (minimum confounder association with treatment and outcome to nullify the effect).

Appendix · the mathematics

Rosenbaum’s odds bound. For two matched units j,k with identical observed X but treatment probabilities \pi_j,\pi_k, hidden bias is bounded by \frac1\Gamma \le \frac{\pi_j(1-\pi_k)}{\pi_k(1-\pi_j)} \le \Gamma . \Gamma=1 forces \pi_j=\pi_k (no hidden bias). For each \Gamma>1 one computes the worst-case p-value over all assignments consistent with the bound; the smallest \Gamma pushing it above 0.05 is the study’s breaking point — the curve in the figure.

Oster’s bias-adjusted coefficient. Let \dot\beta,\dot R^2 be from the short regression (no controls) and \tilde\beta,\tilde R^2 from the controlled one, with R^2_{\max} the (hypothetical) R^2 from regressing on observables and unobservables. The bias-adjusted estimate is approximately \beta^\ast \approx \tilde\beta - \delta\,(\dot\beta-\tilde\beta)\,\frac{R^2_{\max}-\tilde R^2}{\tilde R^2-\dot R^2}. Setting \delta=1 (unobservables as important as observables) and a plausible R^2_{\max} gives a bound; robustness means \beta^\ast stays away from 0.

E-value (risk-ratio scale). For an observed risk ratio RR\ge1, the E-value is \text{E-value}=RR+\sqrt{RR\,(RR-1)}, the minimum association (with both treatment and outcome) an unmeasured confounder would need to reduce RR to 1. Larger E-value ⇒ more robust.

Re-weighting SATE to PATE. If effects vary as \tau(x) and the target population has covariate density g(x) while the sample has f(x), \text{PATE}=\mathbb E_{g}[\tau(X)]=\int \tau(x)\,g(x)\,dx=\mathbb E_{f}\!\left[\frac{g(X)}{f(X)}\,\tau(X)\right], so weighting sample units by g(X)/f(X) transports the estimate — valid only if \tau(x) is identified and the reweighting covariates capture the heterogeneity.

- **Coefficient stability without the $R^2$ movement.** A coefficient can be stable because the controls
  explain nothing; Oster's logic needs both. [@chernozhukov_causalml]

- **Reading a LATE or RD estimate as a population effect.** They are local — to compliers, to the
  cutoff. Name the subpopulation. [@angrist_mostly_harmless]

- **Generalising from a convenience sample.** A flawless SATE on volunteers need not be the PATE;
  re-weight or replicate before extrapolating. [@gerber_green]

- **Extrapolating RD far from the cutoff.** Beyond the boundary you are trusting functional form, not
  the design. [@cunningham_mixtape]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Rosenbaum's odds bound.** For two matched units $j,k$ with identical observed $X$ but treatment
probabilities $\pi_j,\pi_k$, hidden bias is bounded by
$$
\frac1\Gamma \le \frac{\pi_j(1-\pi_k)}{\pi_k(1-\pi_j)} \le \Gamma .
$$
$\Gamma=1$ forces $\pi_j=\pi_k$ (no hidden bias). For each $\Gamma>1$ one computes the **worst-case**
$p$-value over all assignments consistent with the bound; the smallest $\Gamma$ pushing it above $0.05$
is the study's breaking point — the curve in the figure.

**Oster's bias-adjusted coefficient.** Let $\dot\beta,\dot R^2$ be from the *short* regression (no
controls) and $\tilde\beta,\tilde R^2$ from the *controlled* one, with $R^2_{\max}$ the (hypothetical)
$R^2$ from regressing on observables *and* unobservables. The bias-adjusted estimate is approximately
$$
\beta^\ast \approx \tilde\beta - \delta\,(\dot\beta-\tilde\beta)\,\frac{R^2_{\max}-\tilde R^2}{\tilde R^2-\dot R^2}.
$$
Setting $\delta=1$ (unobservables as important as observables) and a plausible $R^2_{\max}$ gives a
bound; robustness means $\beta^\ast$ stays away from $0$.

**E-value (risk-ratio scale).** For an observed risk ratio $RR\ge1$, the E-value is
$$
\text{E-value}=RR+\sqrt{RR\,(RR-1)},
$$
the minimum association (with both treatment and outcome) an unmeasured confounder would need to reduce
$RR$ to $1$. Larger E-value ⇒ more robust.

**Re-weighting SATE to PATE.** If effects vary as $\tau(x)$ and the target population has covariate
density $g(x)$ while the sample has $f(x)$,
$$
\text{PATE}=\mathbb E_{g}[\tau(X)]=\int \tau(x)\,g(x)\,dx=\mathbb E_{f}\!\left[\frac{g(X)}{f(X)}\,\tau(X)\right],
$$
so weighting sample units by $g(X)/f(X)$ transports the estimate — valid only if $\tau(x)$ is identified
and the reweighting covariates capture the heterogeneity.

:::
