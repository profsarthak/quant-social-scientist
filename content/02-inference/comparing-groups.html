Comparing Groups: ANOVA, Chi-Square & Proportions

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Past the two-sample t-test — the standard tests for many means, for categories, and for rates.

The t-test compares two group means. Real studies rarely stop at two, and rarely have means at all — they have three or more groups, categorical outcomes, binary conversions. This entry stocks the rest of the toolkit: ANOVA for many means, chi-square for categories, and proportion tests for rates — each answering “are these groups really different, or is it noise?”

Why it matters

A t-test handles exactly two groups of numeric data. But you will constantly face questions it can’t: Do these four web layouts differ in engagement? Are click-throughs independent of which headline a visitor saw? Does price A convert better than price B? Each needs a different test, matched to the number of groups and the type of outcome. Knowing which test when — and why you can’t just paper over the gap with repeated t-tests — is core inferential literacy, and the sources treat all three as essential. (Bruce et al. 2020; Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

First, the trap: why not just run many t-tests?

The tempting move with four groups is to t-test every pair. But four groups means six pairwise tests, and each carries its own 5% false-positive risk. (Bruce et al. 2020) As the replication-crisis entry showed, running many tests makes at least one spurious “hit” nearly inevitable — the multiple-comparisons problem in miniature. The fix is an omnibus test: a single test asking “is there any difference among the groups?” before you go hunting for which pair. (Bruce et al. 2020) That omnibus test is ANOVA.

ANOVA and the F-test

Analysis of variance compares three or more group means with one test, and its engine is a beautiful idea: detect differences in means by comparing two kinds of variance. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Between-group variance — how far the group means scatter around the overall grand mean. This is the “signal”: real differences push group means apart.
  • Within-group variance — how much individual observations scatter inside their own group. This is the “noise”: natural variability that has nothing to do with the groups.

The F-statistic is their ratio: (Bruce et al. 2020; Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

F = \frac{\text{between-group variance}}{\text{within-group variance}} = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{treatment}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{error}}}.

When the groups are truly identical, signal and noise are the same thing and F \approx 1. A large F says the group means differ by more than the within-group noise can explain, and the F-distribution converts it to a p-value. (Bruce et al. 2020) This rests on the ANOVA decomposition — total variation splits cleanly, exactly the law of total variance made into a test: (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

\underbrace{\text{SS}_{\text{total}}}_{\text{all variation}} = \underbrace{\text{SS}_{\text{between}}}_{\text{explained by group}} + \underbrace{\text{SS}_{\text{within}}}_{\text{residual noise}},

with degrees of freedom K-1 between (for K groups) and N-K within. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

Seeing the F-ratio

F is a signal-to-noise ratio, so the same set of group means can be clearly significant or clearly not — it all depends on the within-group scatter. Below, three groups sit at identical means in both panels; only the noise differs.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(2)
means = np.array([168.0, 176.0, 184.0])
n = 30

def f_stat(groups):
    allx = np.concatenate(groups)
    grand = allx.mean()
    k, N = len(groups), len(allx)
    ss_between = sum(len(g) * (g.mean() - grand) ** 2 for g in groups)
    ss_within  = sum(((g - g.mean()) ** 2).sum() for g in groups)
    return (ss_between / (k - 1)) / (ss_within / (N - k))

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 3.8), sharey=True)
for ax, sd, label in zip(axes, [14.0, 3.5], ["large noise", "small noise"]):
    groups = [rng.normal(m, sd, n) for m in means]
    F = f_stat(groups)
    for i, g in enumerate(groups):
        ax.scatter(np.full(n, i) + rng.uniform(-0.08, 0.08, n), g,
                   s=12, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.6)
        ax.plot([i - 0.25, i + 0.25], [g.mean()] * 2, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.5)
    ax.axhline(np.concatenate(groups).mean(), color="#7a7f87", ls="--", lw=1.2)
    ax.set_title(f"{label}:  F = {F:.1f}", fontsize=11)
    ax.set_xticks([0, 1, 2]); ax.set_xticklabels(["Group 1", "Group 2", "Group 3"])
axes[0].set_ylabel("outcome")
fig.suptitle("Same means, different noise — F is signal ÷ noise", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
NoteBruce & Bruce’s web-page stickiness test

A company compared four page layouts by how long visitors stayed (seconds), 5 visitors per page (N=20). The group means were Page 1: 172, Page 2: 185, Page 3: 176, Page 4: 162, around a grand mean of 173.75. ANOVA gave F = 2.74 on (3, 16) degrees of freedom, with p = 0.0776. Since p > 0.05, the differences are not significant — the spread among those four means is within what chance alone would produce. The eye-catching 23-second gap between the best and worst page is, statistically, noise. (Bruce et al. 2020)

If the omnibus F is significant, you follow up with post-hoc tests like Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference to find which pairs differ — and, crucially, only after a significant F, which keeps the multiple-comparison error rate in check. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

The chi-square test: are categories related?

When the data are counts in categories, not means, the workhorse is the chi-square test. It asks whether the observed frequencies in a contingency table depart from what you’d expect if the variables were independent. (Bruce et al. 2020) The logic is observed vs expected: compute the counts you’d see under “no relationship,” then measure the total discrepancy: (Bruce et al. 2020)

\chi^2 = \sum_{\text{cells}} \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}, \qquad \text{df} = (\text{rows}-1)\times(\text{columns}-1).

Each cell contributes its squared surprise, scaled by what was expected; a large total means the data stray far from independence. (Bruce et al. 2020)

NoteBruce & Bruce’s headline test

Three headlines (A, B, C) were each shown to 1,000 visitors; clicks were 14, 8, 12 (total 34). Under the null that all headlines convert equally, the expected rate is 34/3000 \approx 1.13\%, so each headline’s expected clicks are \approx \mathbf{11.33}. The statistic works out to \chi^2 = 1.666 on \text{df} = (3-1)(2-1) = 2. That value sits well inside the range of ordinary chance variation: the headlines do not differ significantly. The 14-vs-8 gap is the kind of wobble random clicks produce. (Bruce et al. 2020)

The same machinery does goodness of fit — testing whether one categorical variable matches a hypothesised distribution (are digits uniform? are arrivals as forecast?). And when counts are tiny (expected cells around 5 or fewer), the chi-square approximation breaks down; switch to Fisher’s exact test, which computes the exact probability of the observed table. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Tests for proportions

The most common metric in applied and data-science work is a binary proportion — converted or not, clicked or not, turned out to vote or not. (Bruce et al. 2020) To compare two such rates, the two-proportion z-test uses the Normal approximation to the binomial. (For two groups this is algebraically equivalent to a 2\times2 chi-square — same question, two dialects.)

NoteBruce & Bruce’s price test

An e-commerce experiment compared two prices. Price A converted 200 of 23,739 (0.842%); Price B 182 of 22,588 (0.806%). Despite 45,000+ visitors, the two-proportion test returned p = 0.3498 — not significant. The lesson is sharp: with very low base rates, statistical power comes from the number of conversions, not the number of exposures. Tens of thousands of visitors can still leave you underpowered if almost none convert. (Bruce et al. 2020) (A direct echo of the power entry.)

Which test when?

Outcome type Two groups Three or more groups
Numeric (means) two-sample t-test ANOVA / F-test (then Tukey HSD)
Categorical (counts) 2\times2 chi-square (or Fisher’s exact if small) chi-square on the full table
Binary (proportion) two-proportion z-test chi-square across groups

Pick by the shape of your outcome and the number of groups; the question — “real difference or noise?” — is always the same.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Running pairwise t-tests instead of ANOVA. Many comparisons inflate the false-positive rate; use the omnibus F first, post-hoc tests only after. (Bruce et al. 2020; Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

  • Mining post-hoc pairs after a non-significant F. If the omnibus test is null, hunting for a “significant pair” is p-hacking. The gate exists for a reason. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

  • Using chi-square with tiny expected counts. Below ~5 per cell the approximation misleads — switch to Fisher’s exact. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Confusing exposures with conversions. A huge sample with a rare outcome can still be underpowered; precision tracks the count of events, not of trials. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Reporting a significant F as the whole story. ANOVA says some group differs, not which or by how much. Follow up with post-hoc tests and effect sizes. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

From the sources

NoteWho says what
  • Bruce & Bruce, Practical Statistics for Data Scientists — the multiplicity problem motivating ANOVA; the F-test as between- over within-group variance; the web-page-stickiness example (means 172/185/176/162; F=2.74, p=0.0776, not significant); the chi-square observed-vs-expected logic and statistic; the headline-click example (14/8/12; expected 11.33; \chi^2=1.666, df 2, not significant); goodness-of-fit and Fisher’s exact for small counts; the two-proportion test and the e-commerce price example (p=0.3498 despite 45,000+ trials).
  • Martin & Bridgmon, Quantitative & Statistical Research Methods — the ANOVA sum-of-squares decomposition and degrees of freedom (K-1, N-K); post-hoc Tukey HSD and the rule to run it only after a significant omnibus F.

Appendix · the mathematics

The one-way ANOVA F-statistic. For K groups of sizes n_j with group means \bar y_j, grand mean \bar y, and total size N:

\text{SS}_{\text{between}} = \sum_{j=1}^K n_j(\bar y_j - \bar y)^2, \qquad \text{SS}_{\text{within}} = \sum_{j=1}^K \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} (y_{ij} - \bar y_j)^2,

F = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{between}}/(K-1)}{\text{SS}_{\text{within}}/(N-K)} = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{between}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{within}}} \;\sim\; F_{K-1,\,N-K}\ \text{under } H_0.

Under the null (all group means equal) both mean squares estimate the same \sigma^2, so F\approx1; real differences inflate only the numerator. This is the total-variance decomposition \operatorname{Var}(Y) = \operatorname{Var}(E[Y\mid g]) + E[\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid g)] turned into a test of the between-group term.

Chi-square expected counts. For a contingency table, the expected count under independence is E_{ij} = (\text{row}_i\,\text{total})(\text{col}_j\,\text{total})/N. The headline test: expected = 1000 \times 34/3000 = 11.33 clicks each, and \chi^2 = \frac{(14-11.33)^2}{11.33} + \frac{(8-11.33)^2}{11.33} + \frac{(12-11.33)^2}{11.33} on the clicks, plus the matching non-click cells, \approx 1.67 — small for df =2.

Two-proportion z-test. With pooled proportion \hat p = (x_1+x_2)/(n_1+n_2),

z = \frac{\hat p_1 - \hat p_2}{\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)\left(\tfrac{1}{n_1}+\tfrac{1}{n_2}\right)}}.

Squaring this z gives exactly the 2\times2 chi-square statistic — the two tests are one.

## The chi-square test: are categories related?

When the data are **counts in categories**, not means, the workhorse is the **chi-square test**. It
asks whether the observed frequencies in a contingency table depart from what you'd expect if the
variables were **independent**. [@bruce_practical] The logic is **observed vs expected**: compute the
counts you'd see under "no relationship," then measure the total discrepancy: [@bruce_practical]

$$
\chi^2 = \sum_{\text{cells}} \frac{(O - E)^2}{E},
\qquad \text{df} = (\text{rows}-1)\times(\text{columns}-1).
$$

Each cell contributes its squared surprise, scaled by what was expected; a large total means the data
stray far from independence. [@bruce_practical]

::: {.callout-note title="Bruce & Bruce's headline test"}
Three headlines (A, B, C) were each shown to **1,000** visitors; clicks were **14, 8, 12** (total
34). Under the null that all headlines convert equally, the expected rate is $34/3000 \approx 1.13\%$,
so each headline's expected clicks are $\approx \mathbf{11.33}$. The statistic works out to
$\chi^2 = 1.666$ on $\text{df} = (3-1)(2-1) = 2$. That value sits well inside the range of ordinary
chance variation: **the headlines do not differ significantly**. The 14-vs-8 gap is the kind of wobble
random clicks produce. [@bruce_practical]
:::

The same machinery does **goodness of fit** — testing whether one categorical variable matches a
hypothesised distribution (are digits uniform? are arrivals as forecast?). And when counts are tiny
(expected cells around **5 or fewer**), the chi-square *approximation* breaks down; switch to
**Fisher's exact test**, which computes the exact probability of the observed table.
[@bruce_practical]

## Tests for proportions

The most common metric in applied and data-science work is a **binary proportion** — converted or
not, clicked or not, turned out to vote or not. [@bruce_practical] To compare two such rates, the
**two-proportion z-test** uses the [Normal approximation](../01-foundations/common-distributions.qmd)
to the binomial. (For two groups this is algebraically equivalent to a $2\times2$ chi-square — same
question, two dialects.)

::: {.callout-note title="Bruce & Bruce's price test"}
An e-commerce experiment compared two prices. Price A converted **200 of 23,739** (0.842%); Price B
**182 of 22,588** (0.806%). Despite **45,000+** visitors, the two-proportion test returned
$p = 0.3498$ — **not significant**. The lesson is sharp: with very low base rates, statistical power
comes from the number of **conversions**, not the number of **exposures**. Tens of thousands of
visitors can still leave you underpowered if almost none convert. [@bruce_practical] (A direct echo
of the [power entry](statistical-power.qmd).)
:::

## Which test when?

| Outcome type | Two groups | Three or more groups |
|---|---|---|
| **Numeric (means)** | [two-sample t-test](hypothesis-testing.qmd) | **ANOVA / F-test** (then Tukey HSD) |
| **Categorical (counts)** | $2\times2$ **chi-square** (or **Fisher's exact** if small) | **chi-square** on the full table |
| **Binary (proportion)** | **two-proportion z-test** | chi-square across groups |

Pick by the shape of your outcome and the number of groups; the question — "real difference or
noise?" — is always the same.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}

- **Running pairwise t-tests instead of ANOVA.** Many comparisons inflate the false-positive rate;
  use the omnibus F first, post-hoc tests only after. [@bruce_practical; @martin_bridgmon]

- **Mining post-hoc pairs after a non-significant F.** If the omnibus test is null, hunting for a
  "significant pair" is p-hacking. The gate exists for a reason. [@martin_bridgmon]

- **Using chi-square with tiny expected counts.** Below ~5 per cell the approximation misleads —
  switch to Fisher's exact. [@bruce_practical]

- **Confusing exposures with conversions.** A huge sample with a rare outcome can still be
  underpowered; precision tracks the count of *events*, not of *trials*. [@bruce_practical]

- **Reporting a significant F as the whole story.** ANOVA says *some* group differs, not *which* or
  *by how much*. Follow up with post-hoc tests and effect sizes. [@martin_bridgmon]

:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The one-way ANOVA F-statistic.** For $K$ groups of sizes $n_j$ with group means $\bar y_j$, grand
mean $\bar y$, and total size $N$:

$$
\text{SS}_{\text{between}} = \sum_{j=1}^K n_j(\bar y_j - \bar y)^2, \qquad
\text{SS}_{\text{within}} = \sum_{j=1}^K \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} (y_{ij} - \bar y_j)^2,
$$

$$
F = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{between}}/(K-1)}{\text{SS}_{\text{within}}/(N-K)}
= \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{between}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{within}}} \;\sim\; F_{K-1,\,N-K}\ \text{under } H_0.
$$

Under the null (all group means equal) both mean squares estimate the same $\sigma^2$, so $F\approx1$;
real differences inflate only the numerator. This is the [total-variance
decomposition](../01-foundations/conditional-expectation.qmd) $\operatorname{Var}(Y) =
\operatorname{Var}(E[Y\mid g]) + E[\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid g)]$ turned into a test of the
between-group term.

**Chi-square expected counts.** For a contingency table, the expected count under independence is
$E_{ij} = (\text{row}_i\,\text{total})(\text{col}_j\,\text{total})/N$. The headline test: expected
$= 1000 \times 34/3000 = 11.33$ clicks each, and
$\chi^2 = \frac{(14-11.33)^2}{11.33} + \frac{(8-11.33)^2}{11.33} + \frac{(12-11.33)^2}{11.33}$ on the
*clicks*, plus the matching non-click cells, $\approx 1.67$ — small for df $=2$.

**Two-proportion z-test.** With pooled proportion $\hat p = (x_1+x_2)/(n_1+n_2)$,

$$
z = \frac{\hat p_1 - \hat p_2}{\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)\left(\tfrac{1}{n_1}+\tfrac{1}{n_2}\right)}}.
$$

Squaring this $z$ gives exactly the $2\times2$ chi-square statistic — the two tests are one.

:::
