Resampling & Distribution-Free Tests

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When the data won’t behave — testing by shuffling, resampling, and ranking instead of assuming a bell.

Every test in the last few entries leaned on an assumption — usually Normality, or a large enough sample for the CLT to rescue you. Real social data is skewed, outlier-ridden, ordinal, and often small. Resampling and rank-based tests throw the assumptions out: they build the null distribution from your own data by shuffling it, or sidestep distribution entirely by working with ranks. Often the most honest p-value is one you simulated.

Why it matters

The t-test, ANOVA, and the Normal-approximation proportion tests all assume something about the shape of the data or the size of the sample. (Bruce et al. 2020) When income is log-skewed, when a few outliers dominate, when responses are 1–5 Likert ratings, or when n is simply too small for the CLT to kick in, those assumptions fail — and the p-values they produce can’t be trusted. (Bruce et al. 2020; Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

Two families fix this. Resampling tests (permutation and bootstrap) manufacture the null distribution directly from the observed data, assuming almost nothing. Rank-based non-parametric tests discard the raw numbers for their ranks, immunising against skew and outliers. Together they are the assumption-light backbone of modern data analysis — and the engine behind every A/B test.

The permutation test

The permutation (or randomization) test is the most intuitive idea in all of inference. (Bruce et al. 2020) Its logic: if the two groups are really the same, the labels are arbitrary. “Page A” versus “Page B” is meaningless under the null, so you can shuffle the labels and the data shouldn’t care. Do that thousands of times, and you see how big a group difference chance alone manufactures — then ask where your real difference falls in that pile. (Bruce et al. 2020)

The procedure: (Bruce et al. 2020)

  1. Combine both groups into one pooled dataset.
  2. Shuffle and deal out new groups of the original sizes.
  3. Recompute the statistic (e.g. difference in means) on the shuffled groups.
  4. Repeat thousands of times to build the permutation distribution — the null, made from your own data.
  5. The p-value is the fraction of shuffles whose difference equals or exceeds the one you actually observed.

Because the null distribution is built from the data, the test needs almost no distributional assumptions — no Normality, no minimum sample size. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
# Two groups of skewed session times (lognormal); B a bit higher than A.
group_a = rng.lognormal(4.7, 0.6, 21)
group_b = rng.lognormal(4.9, 0.6, 15)
obs_diff = group_b.mean() - group_a.mean()

pooled = np.concatenate([group_a, group_b])
nA = len(group_a)
n_perm = 10_000
perm_diffs = np.empty(n_perm)
for i in range(n_perm):
    rng.shuffle(pooled)
    perm_diffs[i] = pooled[nA:].mean() - pooled[:nA].mean()

p_val = np.mean(np.abs(perm_diffs) >= abs(obs_diff))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.7))
ax.hist(perm_diffs, bins=60, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.8)
ax.axvline(obs_diff, color="#c0392b", lw=2.0)
ax.axvline(-abs(obs_diff), color="#c0392b", lw=1.0, ls=":")
ax.text(obs_diff, ax.get_ylim()[1]*0.9, f"  observed = {obs_diff:.1f}s",
        color="#c0392b", fontsize=9)
ax.set_title(f"Permutation null distribution — p ≈ {p_val:.3f}")
ax.set_xlabel("difference in group means under shuffled labels")
ax.set_yticks([])
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
NoteBruce & Bruce’s web-stickiness test

A company asked whether Page A or Page B held visitors longer. Page A: 21 visitors, mean 126.33 seconds; Page B: 15 visitors, mean 162.00 seconds — an observed gap of 35.67 seconds. They pooled all 36 session times, reshuffled them into groups of 21 and 15 1,000 times, and found a difference that large arose by chance about 12.4% of the time (p = 0.124). The verdict: not significant — the 35.67-second lead can’t be distinguished from luck. No Normality was ever assumed. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Full vs randomized. An exhaustive permutation test enumerates every possible relabeling for an exact p-value — feasible only for small samples. For anything larger, a randomized test uses many random shuffles (10,000, say) and lands at a near-identical answer. (Bruce et al. 2020) The figure above is the randomized kind.

A/B testing: the framework underneath

An A/B test is just a randomized experiment in digital dress: group A gets the control, group B the new treatment, subjects are randomly assigned, and you compare a pre-chosen metric. (Bruce et al. 2020) Two disciplines make it trustworthy:

  • Randomization ensures any group difference is due to the treatment or to chance — never to systematic bias in who got what. (Bruce et al. 2020) (The same logic that powers causal inference.)
  • A single, pre-specified test statistic (conversion rate, click rate, session time) chosen before the test — so you can’t fish through metrics for one that’s significant. (Bruce et al. 2020)

And how do you analyse the result? Usually with exactly the permutation and bootstrap machinery of this entry — letting the data gauge chance variation without leaning on a formula. (Bruce et al. 2020)

The bootstrap hypothesis test

The bootstrap you met building confidence intervals can also test. (Bruce et al. 2020) The twist: to test a null, first transform the data so the null is true — e.g. shift the two groups to share a common mean — then resample with replacement and see how often a resampled difference reaches your observed one. (Bruce et al. 2020) The contrast with the CI is worth fixing:

  • A bootstrap confidence interval measures a statistic’s variability to locate the true parameter.
  • A bootstrap hypothesis test computes the probability of your result if the null held.

Same resampling engine, aimed at two different questions. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Rank-based non-parametric tests

The classical distribution-free alternatives predate the computer: replace each value by its rank (1st, 2nd, 3rd…) and test on the ranks. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) Ranks are immune to skew and outliers — the largest value is just “rank n,” however extreme — and they work for inherently ordinal data like Likert scales. Each parametric test has a rank twin:

Parametric test Rank-based (distribution-free) twin Compares
Two-sample t-test Mann–Whitney U / Wilcoxon rank-sum two independent groups
Paired t-test Wilcoxon signed-rank before/after, matched pairs
One-way ANOVA Kruskal–Wallis three or more groups

Reach for them when the data is skewed, outlier-heavy, ordinal (Likert), or so small that Normality can’t be checked. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) They ask a subtly different question — whether one group tends to rank higher than another — but for most purposes they answer “are these groups different?” without the parametric baggage.

Multi-arm bandits: testing that optimises as it goes

A brief look forward. A fixed A/B test runs to completion before you act — exposing many users to the losing version meanwhile. Multi-arm bandit algorithms instead learn in real time: they start by showing variants equally, then shift traffic toward whichever “arm” is winning, while still occasionally exploring the others to confirm the trend. (Bruce et al. 2020) The epsilon-greedy algorithm is the classic. The aim shifts from “cleanly measure the difference” to “maximise payoff during the experiment” — trading some inferential tidiness for fewer users sent to the inferior option. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Defaulting to the t-test on ugly data. Heavy skew, wild outliers, or tiny n break the parametric assumptions; a permutation or rank test is the honest call. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Too few permutations. A handful of shuffles gives a noisy p-value. Use thousands (10,000 is common) so the permutation distribution is smooth. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Forgetting to impose the null in a bootstrap test. Unlike a bootstrap CI, a bootstrap test requires shifting the data so the null holds first — skip it and you’re not testing anything. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Choosing the metric after seeing the data. A/B tests demand a pre-specified statistic; picking the winning metric post-hoc is p-hacking. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Treating a rank test as identical to its parametric twin. Rank tests compare typical ranks, not means; with very different group shapes they answer a slightly different question. Know what you’re claiming. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

Appendix · the mathematics

The permutation p-value. Let t_{\text{obs}} be the observed statistic and t_1,\dots,t_M the values from M random relabelings. The (two-sided) p-value is

\hat p = \frac{1 + \#\{m : |t_m| \ge |t_{\text{obs}}|\}}{1 + M},

the “+1” counting the observed arrangement itself (so \hat p is never exactly 0 and the test is valid). Its justification is exchangeability: under the null, every relabeling of the pooled data is equally likely, so t_{\text{obs}} is just one draw from the permutation distribution.

Why it needs no Normality. The reference distribution is the empirical distribution of t over relabelings, not a theoretical t or F curve. Nothing about the population shape is assumed — only that, under H_0, the labels are exchangeable.

Bootstrap test vs CI. For two groups, recentre each to a common mean (impose H_0: \tilde x_i = x_i - \bar x_g + \bar x_{\text{pool}}), resample each group with replacement B times, and compute the proportion of |\bar x_B^{*1}-\bar x_B^{*2}| \ge |t_{\text{obs}}|. Contrast with the percentile CI, which resamples the un-shifted data and reads off the 2.5th/97.5th percentiles — same engine, null imposed in the test but not in the interval.

Rank-sum sketch. Mann–Whitney pools the two groups, ranks all N values, and sums the ranks W in one group. Under H_0, W has mean n_1(N+1)/2 and a known variance, giving a Normal approximation for large samples and exact tables for small ones — all from ranks, no raw-value distribution required.

the "$+1$" counting the observed arrangement itself (so $\hat p$ is never exactly 0 and the test is
valid). Its justification is **exchangeability**: under the null, every relabeling of the pooled data
is equally likely, so $t_{\text{obs}}$ is just one draw from the permutation distribution.

**Why it needs no Normality.** The reference distribution is the *empirical* distribution of $t$ over
relabelings, not a theoretical $t$ or $F$ curve. Nothing about the population shape is assumed — only
that, under $H_0$, the labels are exchangeable.

**Bootstrap test vs CI.** For two groups, recentre each to a common mean (impose $H_0$:
$\tilde x_i = x_i - \bar x_g + \bar x_{\text{pool}}$), resample each group with replacement $B$ times,
and compute the proportion of $|\bar x_B^{*1}-\bar x_B^{*2}| \ge |t_{\text{obs}}|$. Contrast with the
percentile **CI**, which resamples the *un-shifted* data and reads off the 2.5th/97.5th percentiles —
same engine, null imposed in the test but not in the interval.

**Rank-sum sketch.** Mann–Whitney pools the two groups, ranks all $N$ values, and sums the ranks
$W$ in one group. Under $H_0$, $W$ has mean $n_1(N+1)/2$ and a known variance, giving a Normal
approximation for large samples and exact tables for small ones — all from ranks, no raw-value
distribution required.

:::
