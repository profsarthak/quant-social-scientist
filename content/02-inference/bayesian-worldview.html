The Bayesian Worldview

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Inference as belief updating: probability not as long-run frequency, but as a measured state of knowledge.

Everything in the Classical Toolkit treated the parameter as a fixed unknown and the data as random. Bayesian inference flips that on its head: the data are the fixed thing you actually saw, and your uncertainty about the parameter is what gets a probability distribution. From that single inversion follows a different — and to many, more natural — way of reasoning under uncertainty.

Why it matters

Ask a frequentist “what’s the probability this candidate wins the election?” and they squirm: an election happens once, it can’t be rerun infinitely, so a long-run frequency doesn’t apply. (Lambert 2018) Yet you want to talk about that probability — and so does every forecaster, doctor, and analyst facing a one-off decision. The Bayesian framework exists to make that talk rigorous. It also delivers the thing people wrongly believed a confidence interval gave them: the ability to say “there’s a 95% probability the truth lies in this range.”

This entry rebuilds inference from a different foundation. It is not a rejection of what came before — the two frameworks often agree numerically — but a second language for uncertainty that you should be able to speak fluently. It extends Bayes’ theorem, which we met for events, to its grown-up job: reasoning about parameters.

Two philosophies of probability

The whole split begins with a disagreement about what the word “probability” means. (Lambert 2018)

  • Frequentist (objective). A probability is the long-run frequency of an event over infinitely many identical repetitions. It is a fixed feature of the world, independent of any observer — which is why it struggles with one-off events that can’t be repeated. (Lambert 2018)
  • Bayesian (subjective). A probability is a degree of belief — a number from 0 (certain it won’t happen) to 1 (certain it will) that measures your state of knowledge. Because it describes what you know rather than a law of nature, it can be attached to anything: a coin, an election, a one-time hypothesis. (Lambert 2018)

Neither is “right.” They are different definitions, and which you adopt reshapes every downstream move.

The crucial inversion: what’s fixed, what’s random

The practical heart of the difference is what each school holds fixed and what it lets vary. (Lambert 2018)

The parameter \theta The data
Frequentist Fixed unknown constant Random — your sample is one of many “fictitious samples” you might have drawn
Bayesian Random — described by a probability distribution for your uncertainty Fixed — the data are the one thing you actually witnessed

For the frequentist, the truth \theta is a fixed pin and the data jiggle around it; inference imagines re-running the study. For the Bayesian, the data are given — they already happened — and the only uncertainty left is about \theta, so \theta is what carries a distribution. (Lambert 2018) This is epistemic uncertainty: not randomness in the world, but the honest admission that you don’t know the value, expressed as a probability. Once you make that move, the rest is mechanical.

Bayes’ rule for parameters

Bayesian inference is a machine for inverting a model — turning “this cause would produce that data” into “this data implies that cause.” (Lambert 2018) The engine is Bayes’ theorem, written for a parameter:

\underbrace{p(\theta \mid \text{data})}_{\text{posterior}} \;\propto\; \underbrace{p(\text{data} \mid \theta)}_{\text{likelihood}} \;\times\; \underbrace{p(\theta)}_{\text{prior}} .

Lambert calls this the central dogma of Bayesian statistics, and each piece has a clean meaning: (Lambert 2018)

  • The prior p(\theta) — what you believe before seeing the data, as a full distribution over the parameter’s possible values, drawn from past experience or expert judgement.
  • The likelihood p(\text{data}\mid\theta) — what the data say: how probable your actual sample would be for each candidate value of \theta. Crucially, viewed as a function of \theta (with the data fixed), the likelihood is not a probability distribution — its area doesn’t integrate to 1. (Lambert 2018)
  • The posterior p(\theta\mid\text{data}) — the goal: your updated belief after folding in the data. It contains everything you now know about \theta, and its width is your uncertainty — a narrow posterior means high confidence. (Lambert 2018)

The posterior as a compromise

The most useful intuition Lambert offers: the posterior is a compromise — a weighted average — between the prior and the likelihood, a tug-of-war whose rope settles between the two pulls. (Lambert 2018) The posterior’s peak always lands between the prior’s peak and the likelihood’s peak.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from math import lgamma

def beta_pdf(theta, a, b):
    logB = lgamma(a) + lgamma(b) - lgamma(a + b)
    return np.exp((a - 1) * np.log(theta) + (b - 1) * np.log(1 - theta) - logB)

theta = np.linspace(1e-3, 1 - 1e-3, 500)
prior = beta_pdf(theta, 2, 5)      # skeptical: leans toward low bias, mode 0.2
like  = beta_pdf(theta, 8, 4)      # normalized likelihood for 7 heads in 10, mode 0.7
post  = beta_pdf(theta, 9, 8)      # Beta(2+7, 5+3): posterior, mode 0.53

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.8))
ax.plot(theta, prior, color="#9aa0a6", lw=2.0, label="Prior  Beta(2,5) — skeptical")
ax.plot(theta, like,  color="#d98a29", lw=2.0, ls="--", label="Likelihood — 7 heads in 10")
ax.plot(theta, post,  color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.6, label="Posterior  Beta(9,8)")
for m, c in [(0.2, "#9aa0a6"), (0.7, "#d98a29"), (8/15, "#1f3a5f")]:
    ax.axvline(m, color=c, ls=":", lw=1.0, alpha=0.7)
ax.set_xlabel("θ — the coin's bias  (probability of heads)")
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_title("The posterior is a compromise between prior belief and data")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The dotted lines mark the three peaks: prior at 0.2, likelihood at 0.7, posterior at 0.53 — squarely between. And the power of data is built into the picture: as the sample grows, the likelihood narrows and sharpens, until it swamps the prior and the posterior slides almost entirely onto the data. (Lambert 2018) A profound consequence follows: two researchers who start with different priors will converge on the same posterior once enough data arrives. (Lambert 2018) The prior matters most exactly when data is scarce — which is also when honestly declaring your assumptions matters most.

Sequential updating: today’s posterior is tomorrow’s prior

Because the posterior simply is your updated state of knowledge, it becomes the prior for the next batch of data. (Lambert 2018) Learning is recursive: update, then update again, each posterior feeding forward. Lambert proves a reassuring property — order invariance: you reach the identical final posterior whether you swallow all the data at once or digest it piece by piece. (Lambert 2018) The arithmetic of belief is consistent, however you slice the evidence.

Lambert’s gallery of intuitions

Lambert teaches the worldview through vivid scenes, several of which we already met for events in the Bayes’ theorem entry — now read them as inference about a parameter: (Lambert 2018)

  • The Bayesian Coastguard. A ship is lost in a storm. The search starts with a wide circular prior (the 25 km radio range). A faint signal — the likelihood — reshapes it into an oval posterior. A second tower’s reading (sequential updating) shrinks the search to a tiny high-probability patch, where the crew is found. Inference as a search that concentrates belief.

  • The Murder Trial. A frequentist juror asks only “how likely is this camera footage if the defendant were guilty?” and convicts if that’s small. A Bayesian juror folds in a prior — the defendant’s character, the existence of other suspects — and can rationally conclude only a 1 in 1,000 chance of guilt despite the footage, because the prior was so strongly exculpatory. The base rate is not a distraction; it is evidence.

  • The Penguin Tail. The cleanest contrast of all. A frequentist 95% confidence interval of 1%–5% red-tailed penguins describes a procedure that brackets the truth 95% of the time across infinite fictitious samples. A Bayesian 95% credible interval lets you say the thing you always wanted to: “there’s a 95% probability the true value is in this range.” Same headline number, fundamentally different — and more direct — meaning. (We build credible intervals properly in B3.)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Thinking the prior is “cheating.” A prior is an explicit, auditable assumption — and with enough data it washes out. Frequentist methods make assumptions too (the model, the test); the Bayesian just writes one of them down as a distribution. (Lambert 2018)

  • Treating the likelihood as a probability distribution over \theta. It isn’t — as a function of \theta it need not integrate to 1. It is the weight the data assign to each parameter value, not a belief about them. (Lambert 2018)

  • Reporting a posterior without its width. The point of going Bayesian is the whole distribution. A posterior mean with no spread throws away the uncertainty you worked to quantify. (Lambert 2018)

  • Forgetting that priors dominate when data is thin. With small samples the prior drives the posterior, so a careless prior can mislead. Convergence is a large-sample promise — check prior sensitivity when n is small (the subject of B2). (Lambert 2018)

  • Confusing a credible interval with a confidence interval. They answer different questions. The credible interval is a statement about the parameter; the confidence interval is a statement about the procedure. (Lambert 2018)

Appendix · the full mathematics

Bayes’ theorem for a parameter. With a continuous parameter \theta,

p(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \frac{p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)}{p(\text{data})}, \qquad p(\text{data}) = \int p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,d\theta .

The denominator p(\text{data}) — the evidence — does not depend on \theta; it is just the constant that makes the posterior integrate to 1. That is why we usually write the proportional form p(\theta\mid\text{data}) \propto p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta) and normalise at the end. (Computing that integral is the hard part — see B2 and B3.)

Why the figure works: the Beta–Binomial. For a coin with bias \theta, k heads in n flips has binomial likelihood p(\text{data}\mid\theta) \propto \theta^{k}(1-\theta)^{n-k}. With a \text{Beta}(a,b) prior, p(\theta) \propto \theta^{a-1}(1-\theta)^{b-1}, the posterior is

p(\theta\mid\text{data}) \propto \theta^{a+k-1}(1-\theta)^{b+n-k-1} = \text{Beta}(a+k,\; b+n-k).

In the figure: prior \text{Beta}(2,5), data k=7 heads in n=10, posterior \text{Beta}(2+7,\,5+3)=\text{Beta}(9,8). The modes are (a-1)/(a+b-2): prior 1/5=0.2, likelihood 7/10=0.7, posterior 8/15\approx0.53 — the posterior mode lies between, as the compromise picture requires.

Order invariance. Updating on D_1 then D_2 gives p(\theta\mid D_1,D_2) \propto p(D_2\mid\theta)\,p(D_1\mid\theta)\,p(\theta), which is symmetric in D_1 and D_2 — so the final posterior is independent of the order, and identical to a single batch update on (D_1,D_2).

  *posterior*. A second tower's reading (*sequential updating*) shrinks the search to a tiny
  high-probability patch, where the crew is found. Inference as a search that *concentrates* belief.

- **The Murder Trial.** A frequentist juror asks only "how likely is this camera footage *if* the
  defendant were guilty?" and convicts if that's small. A Bayesian juror folds in a **prior** — the
  defendant's character, the existence of other suspects — and can rationally conclude only a **1 in
  1,000** chance of guilt *despite* the footage, because the prior was so strongly exculpatory. The
  base rate is not a distraction; it is evidence.

- **The Penguin Tail.** The cleanest contrast of all. A frequentist 95% [confidence interval](confidence-intervals.qmd)
  of 1%–5% red-tailed penguins describes a *procedure* that brackets the truth 95% of the time
  across infinite fictitious samples. A Bayesian 95% **credible interval** lets you say the thing you
  always wanted to: *"there's a 95% probability the true value is in this range."* Same headline
  number, fundamentally different — and more direct — meaning. (We build credible intervals properly
  in [B3](credible-intervals-and-computation.qmd).)

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong in practice"}

- **Thinking the prior is "cheating."** A prior is an explicit, auditable assumption — and with
  enough data it washes out. Frequentist methods make assumptions too (the model, the test); the
  Bayesian just writes one of them down as a distribution. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Treating the likelihood as a probability distribution over $\theta$.** It isn't — as a function
  of $\theta$ it need not integrate to 1. It is the *weight the data assign* to each parameter value,
  not a belief about them. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Reporting a posterior without its width.** The point of going Bayesian is the *whole*
  distribution. A posterior mean with no spread throws away the uncertainty you worked to quantify.
  [@lambert_bayes]

- **Forgetting that priors dominate when data is thin.** With small samples the prior drives the
  posterior, so a careless prior can mislead. Convergence is a large-sample promise — check prior
  sensitivity when $n$ is small (the subject of [B2](bayesian-machinery.qmd)). [@lambert_bayes]

- **Confusing a credible interval with a confidence interval.** They answer different questions. The
  credible interval is a statement about the *parameter*; the confidence interval is a statement
  about the *procedure*. [@lambert_bayes]

:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Bayes' theorem for a parameter.** With a continuous parameter $\theta$,

$$
p(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \frac{p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)}{p(\text{data})},
\qquad
p(\text{data}) = \int p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,d\theta .
$$

The denominator $p(\text{data})$ — the **evidence** — does not depend on $\theta$; it is just the
constant that makes the posterior integrate to 1. That is why we usually write the proportional
form $p(\theta\mid\text{data}) \propto p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)$ and normalise at the end.
(Computing that integral is the hard part — see [B2](bayesian-machinery.qmd) and
[B3](credible-intervals-and-computation.qmd).)

**Why the figure works: the Beta–Binomial.** For a coin with bias $\theta$, $k$ heads in $n$ flips
has binomial likelihood $p(\text{data}\mid\theta) \propto \theta^{k}(1-\theta)^{n-k}$. With a
$\text{Beta}(a,b)$ prior, $p(\theta) \propto \theta^{a-1}(1-\theta)^{b-1}$, the posterior is

$$
p(\theta\mid\text{data}) \propto \theta^{a+k-1}(1-\theta)^{b+n-k-1}
= \text{Beta}(a+k,\; b+n-k).
$$

In the figure: prior $\text{Beta}(2,5)$, data $k=7$ heads in $n=10$, posterior
$\text{Beta}(2+7,\,5+3)=\text{Beta}(9,8)$. The modes are $(a-1)/(a+b-2)$: prior $1/5=0.2$, likelihood
$7/10=0.7$, posterior $8/15\approx0.53$ — the posterior mode lies between, as the compromise picture
requires.

**Order invariance.** Updating on $D_1$ then $D_2$ gives
$p(\theta\mid D_1,D_2) \propto p(D_2\mid\theta)\,p(D_1\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)$, which is symmetric in
$D_1$ and $D_2$ — so the final posterior is independent of the order, and identical to a single
batch update on $(D_1,D_2)$.

:::
