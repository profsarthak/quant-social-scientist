The Replication Crisis, Made Rigorous

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Why so much published research is wrong — assembled from the tools of the last six entries, and the reforms that fix it.

The replication crisis is not a story about fraud. It is what happens when honest researchers, pushed by real incentives, use the tools of the previous six entries slightly wrong — flexibly, selectively, repeatedly — and a publication system rewards exactly that. The mathematics of why the literature fills with false positives is the natural capstone of this whole sub-chapter.

Why it matters

Across psychology, medicine, and economics, findings that everyone “knew” to be true have collapsed when independent labs tried to reproduce them. (Reinhart 2015) The unsettling part is the diagnosis: this is driven less by fraud than by the systemic misuse of statistical tools and the misaligned incentives of publishing. (Reinhart 2015) Every mechanism below is something a well-meaning researcher can do without ever feeling dishonest — which is precisely what makes it dangerous, and what makes understanding it a core competence rather than an ethics footnote.

This entry gathers the threads of Part II — p-values, power, confidence intervals, and the base-rate logic of Bayes — into one rigorous account of how science goes wrong at scale, and how it is being fixed.

Researcher degrees of freedom: the garden of forking paths

Hand the same dataset to the same question, and a researcher still faces a thicket of small, individually defensible choices: which outliers to drop, which covariates to control for, how to code a borderline response, where to bin a continuous variable. Gelman and Loken named the result the garden of forking paths. (Reinhart 2015) Each fork looks reasonable in isolation; taken together they grant enormous researcher degrees of freedom — enough latitude to steer an analysis, even unconsciously, toward whatever comes out significant. Simulations show that flexible outlier handling and covariate selection alone can swing an effect size by a factor of two — routinely enough to drag a null result across the p<0.05 line. (Reinhart 2015)

The deep point: you do not need to cheat to exploit the forking paths. You only need to make each choice after glancing at the data and pick, at each fork, the path that looks a little better.

P-hacking: torture the data until it confesses

When that flexibility is turned, deliberately or not, toward chasing significance, it has a name: p-hacking (or data dredging) — running analysis after analysis until a p-value drops below 0.05, then reporting only the winners. (Bruce et al. 2020) The folk wisdom is exact: “torture the data long enough, and it will confess.” (Reinhart 2015)

NoteProving the Beatles make you younger

In a deliberately outlandish demonstration, researchers “showed” that listening to the Beatles’ When I’m Sixty-Four made participants literally younger in chronological age. They got there purely through flexible analysis — trying combinations of covariates and outcome variables until a significant result surfaced. The absurd conclusion was the point: the same freedom can manufacture a plausible-sounding effect just as easily, and you’d never know. (Reinhart 2015)

Optional stopping: the peeking problem

A special, seductive form of p-hacking concerns when you stop collecting data. Optional stopping means watching results arrive and halting the moment significance appears. (Reinhart 2015) It feels rational — why gather more once you’ve “found it”? — but it quietly wrecks the error rate. The \alpha=0.05 guarantee assumes a single test at a fixed sample size; peek after every participant and you take many bites at the apple, so the chance of eventually crossing p<0.05 by pure luck climbs far above 5%. (Reinhart 2015) Combine flexible analyses and flexible stopping and simulations put the false-positive rate above 50% for a single dataset — worse than a coin flip. (Reinhart 2015)

Multiple comparisons: the jelly-bean problem

The cleanest way to see false positives accumulate is to test many things at once. Each test at \alpha=0.05 has a 1-in-20 chance of a false alarm, so testing twenty near-guarantees one.

TipThe green jelly beans

Reinhart’s parable: test whether jelly beans cause acne and find nothing. So you test each colour — 20 of them. Nineteen show nothing; the green one hits p<0.05. Headline: “Green Jelly Beans Linked to Acne!” But you ran 20 tests; finding one false positive at the 5% level is exactly what chance predicts. (Reinhart 2015)

The arithmetic is brutal. For n independent tests, the probability of at least one false positive is

P(\text{at least one false positive}) = 1 - (1 - 0.05)^{n},

which reaches 99% by n = 100. (Reinhart 2015) And n runs into the thousands more often than you’d think — most memorably when researchers slid a dead Atlantic salmon into an fMRI scanner, showed it photographs, and ran thousands of uncorrected per-voxel tests. They found “significant” brain activity. In a dead fish. (Reinhart 2015)

The fixes adjust for the number of tests:

  • Bonferroni correction — divide \alpha by the number of tests (0.05/n). Simple and strict; it controls the chance of any false positive but costs power. (Reinhart 2015)
  • False discovery rate (Benjamini–Hochberg) — controls the proportion of significant results that are false alarms rather than the chance of any, retaining far more power when you expect some real effects among many tests. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Publication bias: the file drawer

Even flawless individual studies produce a broken literature if which studies get published depends on their results. Publication bias is journals’ preference for “exciting,” significant findings; the null results get filed away. (Reinhart 2015) So the published record is a biased sample of all studies run — selection bias, returned from the sampling entry to haunt science itself. If 20 labs run the same true-null study, the one that gets lucky at p<0.05 is the one the world sees; the 19 nulls vanish.

NoteWhen the file drawer was opened

When the suppressed trial data for the antidepressant reboxetine was finally unearthed, the “highly significant” benefit from the published studies evaporated: across all trials, the drug was no better than placebo. The published subset had told a confident, and false, story. (Reinhart 2015)

Why most published findings can be false

Put p-hacking, low power, and publication bias together and you get John Ioannidis’s famous claim, and it is just the base-rate fallacy wearing a lab coat. The chance that a significant finding is true — its positive predictive value (PPV) — depends on three things: the prior probability the hypothesis is true, the study’s power, and \alpha. (Reinhart 2015)

ImportantThe base-rate fallacy, in science

Suppose a field tests 100 hypotheses of which only 10 are true (a 10% base rate). With 80% power and \alpha=0.05:

  • True positives: 0.80 \times 10 = 8.
  • False positives: 0.05 \times 90 \approx 5.

So of the 13 “significant” findings, 5 — nearly 40% — are flukes. (Reinhart 2015) And that’s the optimistic case: drop power, add p-hacking, and the false share climbs past half.

This is the exact structure of the rare-disease Bayes calculation: a test can be individually reliable yet mostly wrong when the thing it screens for is rare. Significant findings are no different.

Watch the predictive value collapse

The PPV is governed by one formula, and plotting it is sobering. As the prior probability of true hypotheses falls — as a field reaches for more surprising, less likely effects — the share of “discoveries” that are real drops, fast, and low power drops it faster.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

alpha = 0.05
prior = np.linspace(0.005, 0.6, 400)

def ppv(power):
    tp = power * prior
    fp = alpha * (1 - prior)
    return tp / (tp + fp)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.8))
ax.plot(prior, ppv(0.80), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.0, label="80% power")
ax.plot(prior, ppv(0.35), color="#d98a29", lw=2.0, label="35% power (underpowered field)")
ax.axhline(0.5, color="#9aa0a6", ls=":", lw=1.2)
ax.text(0.45, 0.52, "coin flip", color="#9aa0a6", fontsize=8)

# Ioannidis's 10% base-rate example at 80% power
p0 = 0.80 * 0.10 / (0.80 * 0.10 + alpha * 0.90)
ax.scatter([0.10], [p0], color="#c0392b", zorder=5)
ax.annotate(f"10% base rate → PPV ≈ {p0:.2f}", xy=(0.10, p0), xytext=(0.16, 0.45),
            fontsize=9, color="#c0392b",
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#c0392b"))

ax.set_xlabel("Base rate: fraction of tested hypotheses that are true")
ax.set_ylabel("P(finding is true | significant)")
ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
ax.set_title("Most 'discoveries' are false when the truth is rare")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The lesson is structural, not moral: a field that prizes surprising results (low base rate) and tolerates underpowered studies (low power) will publish mostly false positives even if every individual researcher is honest. Reform has to change the system, not just scold the scientists.

HARKing: the story told backwards

One more degree of freedom deserves its own name. HARKing — Hypothesizing After the Results are Known — is presenting a hypothesis cooked up after seeing the data as though it had been predicted in advance. (Reinhart 2015) It weaponises a deep human reflex: our talent for spinning a plausible story around any pattern that catches the eye. The fingerprint is exploratory research dressed as confirmatory — and it dissolves the entire logic of the p-value, which only means anything when the hypothesis is fixed before the data.

The fixes: a new rigour

The encouraging half of the story is that the social sciences are responding, and the reforms map directly onto the diseases above: (Reinhart 2015; Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Pre-registration and registered reports — commit to the hypothesis and the full analysis plan before seeing the data; with registered reports the journal accepts the study on its design, before the results exist. This closes the garden of forking paths, p-hacking, optional stopping, and HARKing in one stroke, and defangs publication bias by deciding to publish before the result is known. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Replication — independent teams rerun studies; a finding earns trust by holding up, not by appearing once. (Reinhart 2015)
  • The ASA statement on p-values — the American Statistical Association’s six principles, formally warning that a p-value is not the probability a hypothesis is true and not a measure of an effect’s importance — the misreadings from the testing entry, now official. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Estimation over testing — report effect sizes and confidence intervals instead of bare significance verdicts, so readers see how big and how certain, not just whether. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Believing only fraudsters p-hack. The forking paths are walked by honest people making defensible choices. Good intentions are no protection; only pre-committed procedures are. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Running many tests and celebrating the one that’s significant. With 20 tests, one false positive is expected. Correct for multiplicity (Bonferroni or FDR) or pre-specify your primary outcome. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Trusting a literature of significant results. Publication bias means the published record overstates effects and hides the nulls. A single significant paper is weak evidence; a registered replication is strong. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Forgetting the base rate. A significant result for a surprising hypothesis is more likely a fluke than a discovery — exactly the Bayesian point. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Stopping data collection when it “looks significant.” Fix your sample size in advance (via a power analysis) or use a method designed for sequential peeking. (Reinhart 2015)

Appendix · the full mathematics

Family-wise error from multiple tests. For m independent true nulls each tested at level \alpha, the probability of no false positive is (1-\alpha)^m, so the family-wise error rate is

\text{FWER} = 1 - (1-\alpha)^m .

At \alpha = 0.05: m=10 \Rightarrow 0.40; m=100 \Rightarrow 0.99. Bonferroni tests each at \alpha/m, since 1-(1-\alpha/m)^m \le \alpha keeps the FWER at or below \alpha.

Positive predictive value. Let \pi be the prior probability a tested hypothesis is true, 1-\beta the power, \alpha the level. Among many tests,

\text{PPV} = \Pr(\text{true}\mid \text{significant}) = \frac{(1-\beta)\,\pi}{(1-\beta)\,\pi + \alpha\,(1-\pi)} .

With \pi=0.10, power 0.80, \alpha=0.05: \text{PPV} = \frac{0.08}{0.08 + 0.045} = 0.64 — so 36% of significant findings are false, matching the “5 of 13” count once the 0.05\times90 = 4.5 false positives are rounded. As \pi\to 0 or power falls, \text{PPV}\to 0. This is identically the rare-disease Bayes formula with power playing the role of sensitivity and \alpha the role of the false-positive rate.

False discovery rate (Benjamini–Hochberg). Order the m p-values p_{(1)}\le\dots\le p_{(m)}; find the largest k with p_{(k)} \le \frac{k}{m}q and reject H_{(1)},\dots,H_{(k)}. This bounds the expected proportion of false discoveries at q — controlling a fraction rather than a probability, which is why it keeps more power than Bonferroni.

```{python}
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

alpha = 0.05
prior = np.linspace(0.005, 0.6, 400)

def ppv(power):
    tp = power * prior
    fp = alpha * (1 - prior)
    return tp / (tp + fp)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.8))
ax.plot(prior, ppv(0.80), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.0, label="80% power")
ax.plot(prior, ppv(0.35), color="#d98a29", lw=2.0, label="35% power (underpowered field)")
ax.axhline(0.5, color="#9aa0a6", ls=":", lw=1.2)
ax.text(0.45, 0.52, "coin flip", color="#9aa0a6", fontsize=8)

# Ioannidis's 10% base-rate example at 80% power
p0 = 0.80 * 0.10 / (0.80 * 0.10 + alpha * 0.90)
ax.scatter([0.10], [p0], color="#c0392b", zorder=5)
ax.annotate(f"10% base rate → PPV ≈ {p0:.2f}", xy=(0.10, p0), xytext=(0.16, 0.45),
            fontsize=9, color="#c0392b",
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#c0392b"))

ax.set_xlabel("Base rate: fraction of tested hypotheses that are true")
ax.set_ylabel("P(finding is true | significant)")
ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
ax.set_title("Most 'discoveries' are false when the truth is rare")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

The lesson is structural, not moral: a field that prizes *surprising* results (low base rate) and
tolerates *underpowered* studies (low power) will publish mostly false positives **even if every
individual researcher is honest.** Reform has to change the system, not just scold the scientists.

## HARKing: the story told backwards

One more degree of freedom deserves its own name. **HARKing** — *Hypothesizing After the Results are
Known* — is presenting a hypothesis cooked up *after* seeing the data as though it had been
predicted in advance. [@reinhart_swrong] It weaponises a deep human reflex: our talent for spinning
a plausible story around *any* pattern that catches the eye. The fingerprint is exploratory research
dressed as confirmatory — and it dissolves the entire logic of the p-value, which only means
anything when the hypothesis is fixed *before* the data.

## The fixes: a new rigour

The encouraging half of the story is that the social sciences are responding, and the reforms map
directly onto the diseases above: [@reinhart_swrong; @bruce_practical]

- **Pre-registration and registered reports** — commit to the hypothesis and the full analysis plan
  *before* seeing the data; with registered reports the journal accepts the study on its design,
  before the results exist. This closes the garden of forking paths, p-hacking, optional stopping,
  *and* HARKing in one stroke, and defangs publication bias by deciding to publish before the result
  is known. [@reinhart_swrong]
- **Replication** — independent teams rerun studies; a finding earns trust by holding up, not by
  appearing once. [@reinhart_swrong]
- **The ASA statement on p-values** — the American Statistical Association's six principles,
  formally warning that a p-value is **not** the probability a hypothesis is true and **not** a
  measure of an effect's importance — the misreadings from the [testing entry](hypothesis-testing.qmd),
  now official. [@reinhart_swrong]
- **Estimation over testing** — report **effect sizes and [confidence intervals](confidence-intervals.qmd)**
  instead of bare significance verdicts, so readers see *how big* and *how certain*, not just
  *whether*. [@bruce_practical]

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong in practice"}

- **Believing only fraudsters p-hack.** The forking paths are walked by honest people making
  defensible choices. Good intentions are no protection; only pre-committed procedures are.
  [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Running many tests and celebrating the one that's significant.** With 20 tests, one false
  positive is *expected*. Correct for multiplicity (Bonferroni or FDR) or pre-specify your primary
  outcome. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Trusting a literature of significant results.** Publication bias means the published record
  overstates effects and hides the nulls. A single significant paper is weak evidence; a *registered
  replication* is strong. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Forgetting the base rate.** A significant result for a *surprising* hypothesis is more likely a
  fluke than a discovery — exactly the [Bayesian](../01-foundations/bayes-theorem.qmd) point.
  [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Stopping data collection when it "looks significant."** Fix your sample size in advance (via a
  [power analysis](statistical-power.qmd)) or use a method designed for sequential peeking.
  [@reinhart_swrong]

:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Family-wise error from multiple tests.** For $m$ independent true nulls each tested at level
$\alpha$, the probability of *no* false positive is $(1-\alpha)^m$, so the **family-wise error rate**
is

$$
\text{FWER} = 1 - (1-\alpha)^m .
$$

At $\alpha = 0.05$: $m=10 \Rightarrow 0.40$; $m=100 \Rightarrow 0.99$. **Bonferroni** tests each at
$\alpha/m$, since $1-(1-\alpha/m)^m \le \alpha$ keeps the FWER at or below $\alpha$.

**Positive predictive value.** Let $\pi$ be the prior probability a tested hypothesis is true,
$1-\beta$ the power, $\alpha$ the level. Among many tests,

$$
\text{PPV} = \Pr(\text{true}\mid \text{significant})
= \frac{(1-\beta)\,\pi}{(1-\beta)\,\pi + \alpha\,(1-\pi)} .
$$

With $\pi=0.10$, power $0.80$, $\alpha=0.05$:
$\text{PPV} = \frac{0.08}{0.08 + 0.045} = 0.64$ — so 36% of significant findings are false, matching
the "5 of 13" count once the $0.05\times90 = 4.5$ false positives are rounded. As $\pi\to 0$ or
power falls, $\text{PPV}\to 0$. This is identically the
[rare-disease Bayes formula](../01-foundations/bayes-theorem.qmd) with power playing the role of
sensitivity and $\alpha$ the role of the false-positive rate.

**False discovery rate (Benjamini–Hochberg).** Order the $m$ p-values $p_{(1)}\le\dots\le p_{(m)}$;
find the largest $k$ with $p_{(k)} \le \frac{k}{m}q$ and reject $H_{(1)},\dots,H_{(k)}$. This bounds
the expected *proportion of false discoveries* at $q$ — controlling a fraction rather than a
probability, which is why it keeps more power than Bonferroni.

:::
