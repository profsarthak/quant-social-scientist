Maximum Likelihood Estimation

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

One idea behind half the models in this book: pick the parameter values that make the data you actually saw as likely as possible. The estimation engine under logit, Poisson, and most of modern statistics.

You have data and a model with unknown knobs. Maximum likelihood turns the knobs to whatever setting makes the data you actually observed the most probable. That single principle — “choose the parameters that make the observed sample most likely” — generates the estimators for logistic regression, Poisson regression, mixed models, and far beyond, and it comes with a built-in measure of its own precision. Once you see it, a huge swath of statistics stops being a list of formulas and becomes one move applied again and again.

Why it matters

OLS handles linear models, but the moment the outcome is binary (logit/probit), a count (Poisson), or censored (Tobit), least squares is “infeasible” or “notably inconsistent.” (Wooldridge 2019) Maximum likelihood is the general estimation framework that covers all of them — and most of the models you’ll meet are “fit by maximum likelihood” under the hood. Understanding it once explains the standard errors, the asymptotics, and the link to Bayes everywhere else.

The likelihood: probability, read backwards

Start with the likelihood function, \Pr(\text{data}\mid\theta). A probability distribution fixes the parameters \theta and asks how likely various data are. The likelihood reverses the view: it holds the data fixed (the sample you actually got) and lets the parameters vary, asking — for each candidate \theta — how probable would this data have been? (Lambert 2018) Maximum likelihood then picks the \hat\theta that “maximizes the likelihood of achieving the observed data sample” — the value that makes the sample “most likely.” (Moffatt 2016)

NoteWorked intuition: a biased coin

Draw 10 times and see 7 successes, 3 failures. If the success probability is p, the likelihood of that exact sample is L(p) = p^7(1-p)^3. Take logs, differentiate, set to zero, and the maximiser is \hat p = 0.7 — exactly the observed proportion. (Moffatt 2016) That is maximum likelihood’s sanity check: the parameter that makes the data most probable is the one that matches what you saw. (Lambert 2018)

Why the log-likelihood

In practice you almost always maximise the log-likelihood, \ell(\theta)=\log L(\theta), for three reasons: (Moffatt 2016)

  1. Sums beat products. With independent observations the likelihood is a product of densities; the log turns it into a sum, which is far easier to differentiate. (Wooldridge 2019)
  2. Numerical stability. A product of many small probabilities is a number “extremely close to zero” — a very flat function a computer struggles to maximise. The log “stretches it vertically,” exposing the peak. (Moffatt 2016)
  3. Same answer. Because \log is monotonically increasing, the \theta that maximises \ell is the identical \theta that maximises L. (Moffatt 2016)
TipMLE contains OLS

For a Normal model, the log-likelihood is a constant minus \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_i (y_i-\beta x_i)^2. So maximising the likelihood is exactly minimising the sum of squared residuals — the MLE of a Normal mean is the OLS estimator. (Wooldridge 2019) Least squares is maximum likelihood wearing a Gaussian coat.

The shape of the likelihood is your precision

Maximum likelihood hands you uncertainty for free, through the curvature of the log-likelihood at its peak: (Moffatt 2016)

  • The score is the gradient \partial\ell/\partial\theta — the direction that would raise the likelihood; it is zero at the MLE.
  • The Fisher information is the curvature (negative second derivative) at the peak. The memorable picture: a strongly peaked likelihood means “only a small range of parameters could have generated that data” — high confidence; a gently peaked, flat one means low confidence. (Moffatt 2016)

The standard error follows directly: the variance of the MLE is the inverse of the Fisher information — so peakedness dictates precision. (Moffatt 2016)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

p = np.linspace(0.01, 0.99, 400)

def loglik(n, phat):
    k = phat * n
    ll = k * np.log(p) + (n - k) * np.log(1 - p)
    return ll - ll.max()          # rescale so the peak sits at 0 (compare shapes)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.2))
ax.plot(p, loglik(10, 0.7),  color="#9bb4d4", lw=2.4, label="n = 10  (flat peak → imprecise)")
ax.plot(p, loglik(200, 0.7), color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.6, label="n = 200 (sharp peak → precise)")
ax.axvline(0.7, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.3)
ax.text(0.705, -6.5, r"$\hat p = 0.7$", color="#c0392b", fontsize=10)
ax.set_ylim(-8, 0.5)
ax.set_xlabel("candidate parameter  $p$"); ax.set_ylabel("log-likelihood (peak set to 0)")
ax.set_title("Maximum likelihood: the peak is the estimate, its sharpness the precision")
ax.legend(fontsize=8.5, frameon=False, loc="lower center")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Why statisticians trust it: the asymptotic properties

Maximum likelihood is the default because of its “superior statistical properties” as the sample grows: (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Consistency — as n\to\infty, \hat\theta converges to the true value. Wooldridge calls this the “minimal requirement” for a useful estimator, quoting Clive Granger: “If you can’t get it right as n goes to infinity, you shouldn’t be in this business.” (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Asymptotic normality — even when the data aren’t Normal, the sampling distribution of the MLE becomes approximately Normal in large samples (so the usual z/t machinery applies). (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Asymptotic efficiency — among consistent estimators, the MLE has the smallest possible variance as n\to\infty, attaining the Cramér–Rao lower bound (the theoretical floor for an unbiased estimator’s variance). (Wooldridge 2019)
  • Invariance — if \hat\theta is the MLE of \theta, then g(\hat\theta) is the MLE of g(\theta) for any continuous g — so you can estimate odds ratios, elasticities, or probabilities without re-deriving anything. (Moffatt 2016)

These are large-sample guarantees; in small samples the MLE can be biased (a reason the bootstrap and exact methods still matter).

The Bayesian neighbour

Maximum likelihood and Bayesian estimation share the same likelihood but treat \theta differently. (Lambert 2018) MLE is frequentist: it treats the parameter as a “static, unwavering constant” and reports its single peak. A Bayesian treats \theta as a random variable and uses the likelihood to compute a full posterior — a whole distribution of belief. The bridge is the MAP (maximum a posteriori) estimate, the mode of the posterior — and Lambert’s clean identity: MLE is exactly the MAP estimate under a flat (uniform) prior. (Lambert 2018) So “just maximise the likelihood” is the Bayesian answer when you bring no prior information — which is also why regularization (a penalty) is MAP with an informative prior.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Reading large-sample properties as small-sample truth. Consistency/efficiency are n\to\infty guarantees; small-sample MLEs can be biased. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Maximising the raw likelihood numerically. It underflows to ~0 and is too flat; always work on the log scale. (Moffatt 2016)

  • Forgetting MLE supplies its own SEs. The inverse Fisher information is the variance; a flat peak means a big standard error you must report, not hide. (Moffatt 2016)

  • Using OLS where MLE is required. For binary, count, or censored outcomes, least squares is infeasible or inconsistent — switch to the likelihood. (Wooldridge 2019)

Appendix · the mathematics

The estimator. With independent data, \ell(\theta)=\sum_i \log f(y_i;\theta). The MLE solves the score equation \nabla_\theta \ell(\hat\theta)=0. Under regularity conditions, \sqrt{n}\,(\hat\theta-\theta_0)\ \xrightarrow{d}\ \mathcal N\!\big(0,\ \mathcal I(\theta_0)^{-1}\big), where \mathcal I(\theta)=-\mathbb E\big[\partial^2\ell/\partial\theta\,\partial\theta^\top\big] is the Fisher information — the curvature. The asymptotic variance \mathcal I^{-1} is the Cramér–Rao bound, so the MLE is efficient; in practice the SE is \sqrt{[\hat{\mathcal I}^{-1}]_{jj}}.

Coin, in full. \ell(p)=k\log p+(n-k)\log(1-p) with k successes. Setting \ell'(p)=k/p-(n-k)/(1-p)=0 gives \hat p=k/n; the second derivative gives \mathcal I(p)=n/[p(1-p)], so \operatorname{Var}(\hat p)\approx p(1-p)/n — the familiar binomial standard error, derived from curvature.

MLE ⊂ MAP. The posterior mode maximises \ell(\theta)+\log \pi(\theta). A flat prior makes \log\pi constant, so the MAP equals the MLE; an informative prior adds a penalty — the link to ridge/lasso.

Connections

  • Builds on: estimation (MLE is the workhorse estimator), common distributions (the densities you take logs of), and the likelihood in Bayes.
  • Powers: logit & probit, GLMs, count models, survival/Cox, and mixed models — all fit by maximum likelihood.
  • Contrasts with: the Bayesian posterior (MLE = MAP under a flat prior) and is penalised into regularization.

## Why statisticians trust it: the asymptotic properties

Maximum likelihood is the default because of its *"superior statistical properties"* as the sample grows:
[@wooldridge_intro]

- **Consistency** — as $n\to\infty$, $\hat\theta$ converges to the true value. Wooldridge calls this the
  *"minimal requirement"* for a useful estimator, quoting Clive Granger: *"If you can't get it right as $n$
  goes to infinity, you shouldn't be in this business."* [@wooldridge_intro]
- **Asymptotic normality** — even when the data aren't Normal, the *sampling distribution of the MLE* becomes
  approximately Normal in large samples (so the usual $z$/$t$ machinery applies). [@wooldridge_intro]
- **Asymptotic efficiency** — among consistent estimators, the MLE has the **smallest possible variance** as
  $n\to\infty$, attaining the **Cramér–Rao lower bound** (the theoretical floor for an unbiased estimator's
  variance). [@wooldridge_intro]
- **Invariance** — if $\hat\theta$ is the MLE of $\theta$, then $g(\hat\theta)$ is the MLE of $g(\theta)$ for
  any continuous $g$ — so you can estimate odds ratios, elasticities, or probabilities without re-deriving
  anything. [@moffatt_experimetrics]

These are *large-sample* guarantees; in small samples the MLE can be biased (a reason the
[bootstrap](resampling-and-nonparametric-tests.qmd) and exact methods still matter).

## The Bayesian neighbour

Maximum likelihood and [Bayesian estimation](bayesian-worldview.qmd) share the *same likelihood* but treat
$\theta$ differently. [@lambert_bayes] MLE is **frequentist**: it treats the parameter as a *"static,
unwavering constant"* and reports its single peak. A Bayesian treats $\theta$ as a random variable and uses
the likelihood to compute a **full posterior** — a whole distribution of belief. The bridge is the **MAP**
(maximum a posteriori) estimate, the *mode* of the posterior — and Lambert's clean identity: **MLE is exactly
the MAP estimate under a flat (uniform) prior.** [@lambert_bayes] So "just maximise the likelihood" is the
Bayesian answer when you bring *no* prior information — which is also why [regularization](../04-predictive/regularization.qmd)
(a penalty) is MAP with an *informative* prior.

  stretch, monotonicity); the **score**, **Fisher information** as curvature ("strongly vs gently peaked"),
  and SEs as the inverse information; **invariance**; the experimental-design "chicken-and-egg."
- **Lambert, *A Student's Guide to Bayesian Statistics*** — likelihood as probability of data viewed as a
  function of $\theta$; the **MLE vs MAP vs full posterior** contrast and the identity **MLE = MAP under a
  flat prior**.
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The estimator.** With independent data, $\ell(\theta)=\sum_i \log f(y_i;\theta)$. The MLE solves the
**score equation** $\nabla_\theta \ell(\hat\theta)=0$. Under regularity conditions,
$$
\sqrt{n}\,(\hat\theta-\theta_0)\ \xrightarrow{d}\ \mathcal N\!\big(0,\ \mathcal I(\theta_0)^{-1}\big),
$$
where $\mathcal I(\theta)=-\mathbb E\big[\partial^2\ell/\partial\theta\,\partial\theta^\top\big]$ is the
**Fisher information** — the curvature. The asymptotic variance $\mathcal I^{-1}$ is the **Cramér–Rao bound**,
so the MLE is efficient; in practice the SE is $\sqrt{[\hat{\mathcal I}^{-1}]_{jj}}$.

**Coin, in full.** $\ell(p)=k\log p+(n-k)\log(1-p)$ with $k$ successes. Setting $\ell'(p)=k/p-(n-k)/(1-p)=0$
gives $\hat p=k/n$; the second derivative gives $\mathcal I(p)=n/[p(1-p)]$, so
$\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)\approx p(1-p)/n$ — the familiar binomial standard error, *derived* from curvature.

**MLE ⊂ MAP.** The posterior mode maximises $\ell(\theta)+\log \pi(\theta)$. A flat prior makes $\log\pi$
constant, so the MAP equals the MLE; an informative prior adds a penalty — the link to
[ridge/lasso](../04-predictive/regularization.qmd).

:::
