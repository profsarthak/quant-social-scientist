Sampling & Survey Design

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Inference can only be as honest as the sample it starts from — how to draw one that deserves to be generalised.

Every claim about a population rests on a sample, and every sample rests on how it was drawn. Get the drawing right — give every unit a known, non-zero chance of selection — and a few hundred people can speak for millions. Get it wrong, and ten million ballots can point to the loser.

Why it matters

The Central Limit Theorem told you that the average of a sample behaves predictably — if the sample is a fair draw from the population. That “if” is the whole of survey design. Inference is a machine for turning a sample into a claim about a population, and like any machine it obeys garbage-in, garbage-out: no amount of clever statistics downstream can repair a sample that was biased the moment it was selected.

This is the least mathematical chapter in Part II and the one practitioners most often skip — to their cost. The famous disasters of empirical social science are almost never failures of arithmetic. They are failures of sampling: the right calculation performed on the wrong people. So before estimators, confidence intervals, and tests, we ask the prior question: who is in your data, and how did they get there?

The core trio: population, sample, frame

Three ideas, constantly confused, must be kept apart. (Bryman 2016)

  • The population is the universe of units you want to draw conclusions about. “Units” need not be people — they can be nations, cities, firms, hospital records, even suicide notes. The population is defined by your research question, not by convenience.
  • The sample is the subset you actually study.
  • The sampling frame is the concrete list of population units from which you draw — the registrar’s roster, the phone book, the voter file. The frame is the population as the world actually hands it to you, and the gap between frame and population is where much trouble lives.
NoteA worked picture

Your population is all 10,000 students at a university. Your sampling frame is the registrar’s digital list of enrolled students. You randomly select 450 names from that list — those 450 people are your sample. If the registrar’s list silently omits part-time students, your frame no longer matches your population, and every downstream number inherits that flaw. (Bryman 2016)

Probability sampling: the only road to inference

The dividing line in all of survey design is whether selection is random. In probability sampling, every unit has a known, non-zero probability of being selected. (Bryman 2016) This is not a stylistic preference — it is the precondition that makes the rest of the book legal. Only when selection probabilities are known can you compute a standard error and attach a margin of error to your estimate. (Bruce et al. 2020) Randomness is what lets a sample be, in Bryman’s phrase, a “population in miniature.”

There are four workhorses, in rising order of sophistication.

Simple random sampling (SRS). Every unit has an equal chance; assign the frame numbers 1 to N and draw n of them with a random number generator. It is the conceptual baseline — mechanical, unbiased, assumption-free. Its cost is practical: you need a complete, enumerated list of the population, which for dispersed or hidden populations may not exist. (Bryman 2016)

Systematic sampling. Order the frame and take every k-th unit after a random start. For a frame of N = 9{,}000 and a desired n = 450, the interval is k = 20; pick a random start between 1 and 20 — say 16 — then take units 16, 36, 56, \dots It is easier to execute than SRS and needs no random draw per unit. The one hazard: if the list has a hidden periodicity that lines up with your interval (every 20th apartment is a corner unit; the roster alternates male/female), the regularity quietly biases the sample. (Bryman 2016)

Stratified random sampling. Divide the population into strata — subgroups defined by some characteristic (faculty, gender, region) — and sample randomly within each. With proportional allocation you draw from each stratum in proportion to its size: if Humanities is 20% of a university and you want n = 450, you draw exactly 90 Humanities students. (Bryman 2016) The payoff is real and worth understanding: stratifying guarantees the subgroups are represented in the right proportions instead of leaving it to luck, and in doing so it removes the between-stratum variation from your estimate’s error. A stratified estimate is never less precise than SRS, and is often dramatically more precise — we will see exactly how much below. The price is information: you must know each unit’s stratum before you sample. (Bryman 2016)

Cluster and multistage sampling. Sample whole clusters — naturally occurring groupings — rather than individuals. For a national student survey you might randomly select 10 universities (clusters), then randomly sample 500 students within each. (Bryman 2016) Clustering exists for one reason: cost. Interviewing 5,000 students at 10 sites is enormously cheaper than chasing 5,000 students scattered across 200 campuses. But there is no free lunch: units within a cluster tend to resemble each other (students at one university share a catchment, a culture, a curriculum), so each new unit carries less fresh information than an independent draw would. The result is larger standard errors for a given n — the so-called design effect. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Method Core idea Buys you Costs you
Simple random Equal chance for all Cleanest baseline; no assumptions A complete enumerated frame
Systematic Every k-th after random start Easy execution Fails on hidden periodicity
Stratified Random within subgroups Guaranteed representation; lower variance Must know strata in advance
Cluster / multistage Sample groups, then units Big cost savings on dispersed populations Higher variance (design effect)

See the gain from stratifying

The claim that stratification reduces the variance of your estimate is not hand-waving — it is visible. Below, the same population is sampled two ways for the same total n = 100. The population has three strata with quite different means (think income across three regions). Simple random sampling lets the number you happen to draw from each region vary by luck, and that luck sloshes around in your estimate. Proportional stratification nails the regional mix down, so only the small within-region noise remains.

Show the simulation code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)

# Three strata: weights, means, and a common within-stratum SD.
weights = np.array([0.5, 0.3, 0.2])
mus     = np.array([40.0, 60.0, 80.0])
sd      = 5.0
true_mean = float(weights @ mus)          # 0.5*40 + 0.3*60 + 0.2*80 = 54

n_total = 100
n_reps  = 30_000

# --- Simple random sampling: stratum membership is itself random ---
choice = rng.choice(3, size=(n_reps, n_total), p=weights)
srs_vals = rng.normal(mus[choice], sd)
srs_means = srs_vals.mean(axis=1)

# --- Stratified proportional allocation: fixed 50 / 30 / 20 ---
a = rng.normal(mus[0], sd, size=(n_reps, 50))
b = rng.normal(mus[1], sd, size=(n_reps, 30))
c = rng.normal(mus[2], sd, size=(n_reps, 20))
strat_means = (a.sum(1) + b.sum(1) + c.sum(1)) / n_total

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.6))
bins = np.linspace(48, 60, 70)
ax.hist(srs_means,   bins=bins, density=True, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.75,
        label=f"Simple random  (SE ≈ {srs_means.std():.2f})")
ax.hist(strat_means, bins=bins, density=True, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85,
        label=f"Stratified      (SE ≈ {strat_means.std():.2f})")
ax.axvline(true_mean, color="#b5446e", ls="--", lw=1.8, label="True mean = 54")
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_xlabel("Estimated population mean")
ax.set_title("Same n, same population — stratifying sharpens the estimate")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Both estimators are unbiased — both clouds centre on the truth, 54. But the stratified estimator is roughly three times tighter. That difference is free: it costs nothing but knowing each unit’s region before you draw. This is the single most useful design lesson in sampling — when you have strata that differ, use them.

Non-probability sampling: convenient, and uninferable

The opposite of probability sampling is selection by judgement or availability, where some units have no chance of selection and the probabilities are unknown. The common forms: (Bryman 2016)

  • Convenience sampling — whoever is easiest to reach (the undergraduates in your 9 a.m. lecture, the shoppers who walk past).
  • Snowball sampling — start with a few relevant people and ask them to nominate others; useful for hidden populations (undocumented workers, niche subcultures) where no frame exists.
  • Quota sampling — fill pre-set category targets non-randomly (e.g., “5 Asian women aged 25–34”) until each quota is met.

These designs have legitimate uses — exploratory work, qualitative depth, hard-to-frame populations. But they share a disqualifying flaw for inference: because the probability of selection is unknown, you cannot compute a standard error, and without a standard error you cannot say how far your sample statistic might be from the population truth. (Bruce et al. 2020) A convenience sample can describe itself perfectly and tell you nothing certain about anyone else. Generalisation requires randomness; judgement cannot substitute for it.

Representativeness, and the two kinds of error

A sample is representative when it mirrors the population — the “population in miniature.” The engine of representativeness is random selection, which has no opinion about whom it picks and so introduces no systematic tilt. (Bryman 2016) When a sample is unrepresentative, the findings simply do not generalise beyond the people in it.

It helps enormously to split a survey’s total error into two species, because they have different cures. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Sampling error is the unavoidable discrepancy between a sample and its population that arises purely because you looked at a subset. It is the bounce the CLT describes; it exists even in a flawless random sample, and it shrinks predictably as n grows. This is the “honest” error — quantifiable, and the thing confidence intervals measure.
  • Non-sampling error is everything else: bad question wording, interviewer effects, data-entry mistakes, coverage gaps, non-response. It does not shrink with n — a bigger biased sample is just a more confidently wrong one — and it is invisible to your standard error. This is the dangerous error, because the statistics give you no warning of it.

The whole moral of this chapter lives in that contrast: statistics manages sampling error; only design manages non-sampling error.

The three biases that ruin samples

Non-sampling error usually arrives as one of three biases — systematic, directional errors baked in by the selection or measurement process. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Selection bias — the sample is selected in a way related to the thing you are measuring. In Gerber & Green’s framing, the danger of non-random selection is that receiving the treatment is correlated with the outcome: healthier people self-select into exercise programs, so a naïve comparison credits the program with their pre-existing health. (Gerber and Green 2012)
  • Coverage / frame bias — the frame fails to list part of the target population, so those people had zero chance from the start (the part-time students missing from the registrar’s list). (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • Non-response bias — selected units who decline or can’t be reached differ systematically from those who respond. If wealthier households are likelier to refuse a survey about income, the recorded average is biased downward — and collecting more responses from the same cooperative group does not fix it. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Sample size: why a thousand can speak for millions

Here is the result that surprises every newcomer and underlies the entire polling industry: the precision of an estimate depends on the absolute sample size n, not on the population size N. (Bruce et al. 2020) A well-drawn random sample of about 1,000 people pins down a national proportion to roughly a \pm 3\% margin — and that margin is the same whether the country has 100,000 people or 100 million.

The intuition: each random draw is an independent measurement of the population, and the information you accumulate is governed by how many measurements you take, not by how large the thing you’re measuring is. (The population size matters only when your sample is a sizeable fraction of it — the finite-population correction in the appendix — which for national surveys it essentially never is.) The same draw also reveals the law of diminishing returns: because sampling error falls like 1/\sqrt{n}, each additional thousand respondents buys less precision than the last. Doubling a poll from 1,000 to 2,000 does not halve the margin of error; it shrinks it by only about 30%. (Bruce et al. 2020)

A cautionary tale: the Literary Digest, 1936

No story makes the point more bluntly. In 1936 the magazine Literary Digest mailed straw-poll ballots to ten million people, drawn from telephone directories and automobile registration lists, and got over two million back. On that mountain of data it confidently predicted a landslide for Alf Landon. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Franklin Roosevelt won 46 states.

The frames — phone and car owners in 1936 — skewed sharply toward the affluent, who favoured Landon; that was coverage bias. And the people motivated to mail back an unsolicited ballot differed from those who didn’t; that was non-response bias. Ten million biased responses are still biased. Meanwhile George Gallup, using a scientifically representative sample of around 2,000, called the election correctly. (Bruce et al. 2020)

The lesson is the founding creed of survey design, and it directly contradicts the instinct of the big-data age: how you sample beats how much you sample. A small representative sample crushes a huge biased one.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Believing a bigger sample fixes bias. Size only shrinks sampling error. Bias — selection, coverage, non-response — is untouched by n and is the actual cause of the famous failures. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Confusing the frame with the population. Your inference is only ever valid for the frame you drew from. If the frame omits a group, your conclusions silently omit them too — and no statistic will flag it. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Reporting a margin of error for a non-probability sample. Convenience, quota, and snowball samples have no defined selection probabilities, so a “±3%” attached to them is fiction. The margin of error is a property of random sampling. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Ignoring the design effect in clustered data. Treating a cluster sample as if it were a simple random sample understates the standard errors and overstates your certainty. Clustering buys cost savings and costs precision; both must be booked. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Forgetting that non-response is informative. A 70% response rate is not “70% of a good sample” — it is a complete sample only if the 30% who declined are like the 70% who answered, and they usually aren’t. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Appendix · the full mathematics

Standard error of a proportion. For a simple random sample of size n estimating a population proportion p with \hat p, the sampling distribution has

\operatorname{SE}(\hat p) = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}.

This is maximised at p = 0.5, where \sqrt{p(1-p)} = 0.5. So for a “50/50” question the margin of error at 95% confidence is about 1.96 \times 0.5/\sqrt{n} \approx 0.98/\sqrt{n}. Setting n = 1{,}000 gives \approx 0.031 — the famous \pm 3\%. Nothing in this expression involves the population size N.

Finite-population correction (FPC). When the sample is a non-negligible fraction of the population, the SE is multiplied by

\sqrt{\frac{N-n}{N-1}}.

For n/N small (a national poll: 1{,}000 from 10^8) this factor is essentially 1, which is why population size drops out. It only bites when you sample a large share of a small population (e.g., 80 of 100 employees), where it correctly shrinks the SE toward zero — a census has no sampling error at all.

Why stratification helps. Decompose the population variance into within-stratum and between-stratum parts, \sigma^2 = \sigma^2_{\text{within}} + \sigma^2_{\text{between}}. A simple random sample’s estimate carries both. A stratified sample with proportional allocation fixes the stratum mix, eliminating the between-stratum component, so its estimator variance is

\operatorname{Var}(\bar x_{\text{strat}}) = \sum_h W_h^2 \,\frac{\sigma_h^2}{n_h},

which depends only on the within-stratum variances \sigma_h^2. In the figure above this gives 0.5^2\frac{25}{50} + 0.3^2\frac{25}{30} + 0.2^2\frac{25}{20} = 0.25, an SE of 0.5, versus the SRS SE of \sqrt{269/100} \approx 1.64 — the threefold gain you saw, because the strata means (40, 60, 80) differ a lot while the within-stratum SD (5) is small.

Design effect (clustering). The cost of cluster sampling is summarised by the design effect \text{DEFF} = 1 + (m-1)\rho, where m is the cluster size and \rho the intraclass correlation (how alike units within a cluster are). The effective sample size is n / \text{DEFF}: with \rho = 0.05 and clusters of m = 100, \text{DEFF} \approx 6, so 5,000 clustered interviews carry the precision of only about 830 independent ones — the price of cheap fieldwork.

sampling**, every unit has a *known, non-zero* probability of being selected. [@bryman_social_research]
This is not a stylistic preference — it is the precondition that makes the rest of the book legal.
Only when selection probabilities are known can you compute a standard error and attach a margin
of error to your estimate. [@bruce_practical] Randomness is what lets a sample be, in Bryman's
phrase, a *"population in miniature."*

There are four workhorses, in rising order of sophistication.

**Simple random sampling (SRS).** Every unit has an equal chance; assign the frame numbers
$1$ to $N$ and draw $n$ of them with a random number generator. It is the conceptual baseline —
mechanical, unbiased, assumption-free. Its cost is practical: you need a *complete, enumerated
list* of the population, which for dispersed or hidden populations may not exist.
[@bryman_social_research]

**Systematic sampling.** Order the frame and take every $k$-th unit after a random start. For a
frame of $N = 9{,}000$ and a desired $n = 450$, the interval is $k = 20$; pick a random start
between 1 and 20 — say 16 — then take units $16, 36, 56, \dots$ It is easier to execute than SRS
and needs no random draw per unit. The one hazard: if the list has a hidden **periodicity** that
lines up with your interval (every 20th apartment is a corner unit; the roster alternates
male/female), the regularity quietly biases the sample. [@bryman_social_research]

**Stratified random sampling.** Divide the population into **strata** — subgroups defined by some
characteristic (faculty, gender, region) — and sample randomly *within each*. With **proportional
allocation** you draw from each stratum in proportion to its size: if Humanities is 20% of a
university and you want $n = 450$, you draw exactly 90 Humanities students. [@bryman_social_research]
The payoff is real and worth understanding: stratifying **guarantees** the subgroups are
represented in the right proportions instead of leaving it to luck, and in doing so it *removes the
between-stratum variation from your estimate's error*. A stratified estimate is never less precise
than SRS, and is often dramatically more precise — we will see exactly how much below. The price
is information: you must know each unit's stratum *before* you sample. [@bryman_social_research]

**Cluster and multistage sampling.** Sample whole **clusters** — naturally occurring groupings —
rather than individuals. For a national student survey you might randomly select 10 universities
(clusters), then randomly sample 500 students within each. [@bryman_social_research] Clustering
exists for one reason: **cost**. Interviewing 5,000 students at 10 sites is enormously cheaper than
chasing 5,000 students scattered across 200 campuses. But there is no free lunch: units within a
cluster tend to *resemble each other* (students at one university share a catchment, a culture, a
curriculum), so each new unit carries less fresh information than an independent draw would. The
result is **larger standard errors** for a given $n$ — the so-called design effect.
[@bruce_practical]

| Method | Core idea | Buys you | Costs you |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Simple random** | Equal chance for all | Cleanest baseline; no assumptions | A complete enumerated frame |
| **Systematic** | Every $k$-th after random start | Easy execution | Fails on hidden periodicity |
| **Stratified** | Random *within* subgroups | Guaranteed representation; lower variance | Must know strata in advance |
| **Cluster / multistage** | Sample groups, then units | Big cost savings on dispersed populations | Higher variance (design effect) |

### See the gain from stratifying

The claim that stratification *reduces the variance of your estimate* is not hand-waving — it is
visible. Below, the same population is sampled two ways for the same total $n = 100$. The
population has three strata with quite different means (think income across three regions). Simple
random sampling lets the *number* you happen to draw from each region vary by luck, and that luck
sloshes around in your estimate. Proportional stratification nails the regional mix down, so only
the small within-region noise remains.

```{python}
#| label: fig-srs-vs-stratified
#| fig-cap: "Two ways to estimate one population mean (true value 54, dashed line) with n = 100. Simple random sampling (grey) scatters widely because the stratum mix varies by chance; proportional stratified sampling (blue) is over three times tighter because it removes the between-stratum variation."
#| code-fold: true
#| code-summary: "Show the simulation code"

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7)

# Three strata: weights, means, and a common within-stratum SD.
weights = np.array([0.5, 0.3, 0.2])
mus     = np.array([40.0, 60.0, 80.0])
sd      = 5.0
true_mean = float(weights @ mus)          # 0.5*40 + 0.3*60 + 0.2*80 = 54

n_total = 100
n_reps  = 30_000

# --- Simple random sampling: stratum membership is itself random ---
choice = rng.choice(3, size=(n_reps, n_total), p=weights)
srs_vals = rng.normal(mus[choice], sd)
srs_means = srs_vals.mean(axis=1)

# --- Stratified proportional allocation: fixed 50 / 30 / 20 ---
a = rng.normal(mus[0], sd, size=(n_reps, 50))
b = rng.normal(mus[1], sd, size=(n_reps, 30))
c = rng.normal(mus[2], sd, size=(n_reps, 20))
strat_means = (a.sum(1) + b.sum(1) + c.sum(1)) / n_total

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.6))
bins = np.linspace(48, 60, 70)
ax.hist(srs_means,   bins=bins, density=True, color="#9aa0a6", alpha=0.75,
        label=f"Simple random  (SE ≈ {srs_means.std():.2f})")
ax.hist(strat_means, bins=bins, density=True, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85,
        label=f"Stratified      (SE ≈ {strat_means.std():.2f})")
ax.axvline(true_mean, color="#b5446e", ls="--", lw=1.8, label="True mean = 54")
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_xlabel("Estimated population mean")
ax.set_title("Same n, same population — stratifying sharpens the estimate")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

Both estimators are *unbiased* — both clouds centre on the truth, 54. But the stratified estimator
is roughly three times tighter. That difference is free: it costs nothing but knowing each unit's
region before you draw. This is the single most useful design lesson in sampling — **when you have
strata that differ, use them.**

## Non-probability sampling: convenient, and uninferable

The opposite of probability sampling is selection by judgement or availability, where some units
have *no* chance of selection and the probabilities are unknown. The common forms:
[@bryman_social_research]

- **Convenience sampling** — whoever is easiest to reach (the undergraduates in your 9 a.m.
  lecture, the shoppers who walk past).
- **Snowball sampling** — start with a few relevant people and ask them to nominate others; useful
  for hidden populations (undocumented workers, niche subcultures) where no frame exists.
- **Quota sampling** — fill pre-set category targets non-randomly (e.g., "5 Asian women aged
  25–34") until each quota is met.

These designs have legitimate uses — exploratory work, qualitative depth, hard-to-frame
populations. But they share a disqualifying flaw for inference: because the probability of
selection is unknown, **you cannot compute a standard error**, and without a standard error you
cannot say how far your sample statistic might be from the population truth. [@bruce_practical] A
convenience sample can describe *itself* perfectly and tell you nothing certain about anyone else.
Generalisation requires randomness; judgement cannot substitute for it.

## Representativeness, and the two kinds of error

A sample is **representative** when it mirrors the population — the "population in miniature." The
engine of representativeness is random selection, which has no opinion about whom it picks and so
introduces no *systematic* tilt. [@bryman_social_research] When a sample is unrepresentative, the
findings simply do not generalise beyond the people in it.

It helps enormously to split a survey's total error into two species, because they have different
cures. [@bruce_practical]

- **Sampling error** is the unavoidable discrepancy between a sample and its population that arises
  *purely because you looked at a subset*. It is the bounce the CLT describes; it exists even in a
  flawless random sample, and it **shrinks predictably as $n$ grows**. This is the "honest"
  error — quantifiable, and the thing confidence intervals measure.
- **Non-sampling error** is everything else: bad question wording, interviewer effects, data-entry
  mistakes, coverage gaps, non-response. It does **not** shrink with $n$ — a bigger biased sample
  is just a more confidently wrong one — and it is invisible to your standard error. This is the
  dangerous error, because the statistics give you no warning of it.

The whole moral of this chapter lives in that contrast: **statistics manages sampling error;
only design manages non-sampling error.**

### The three biases that ruin samples

Non-sampling error usually arrives as one of three **biases** — systematic, directional errors
baked in by the selection or measurement process. [@bruce_practical]

- **Selection bias** — the sample is selected in a way related to the thing you are measuring. In
  Gerber & Green's framing, the danger of non-random selection is that *receiving the treatment is
  correlated with the outcome*: healthier people self-select into exercise programs, so a naïve
  comparison credits the program with their pre-existing health. [@gerber_green]
- **Coverage / frame bias** — the frame fails to list part of the target population, so those
  people had zero chance from the start (the part-time students missing from the registrar's list).
  [@bruce_practical]
- **Non-response bias** — selected units who decline or can't be reached differ systematically
  from those who respond. If wealthier households are likelier to refuse a survey about income, the
  recorded average is biased *downward* — and collecting more responses from the same cooperative
  group does not fix it. [@bruce_practical]

## Sample size: why a thousand can speak for millions

Here is the result that surprises every newcomer and underlies the entire polling industry: **the
precision of an estimate depends on the absolute sample size $n$, not on the population size $N$.**
[@bruce_practical] A well-drawn random sample of about 1,000 people pins down a national proportion
to roughly a $\pm 3\%$ margin — and that margin is *the same* whether the country has 100,000
people or 100 million.

The intuition: each random draw is an independent measurement of the population, and the
information you accumulate is governed by *how many measurements you take*, not by how large the
thing you're measuring is. (The population size matters only when your sample is a sizeable
fraction of it — the finite-population correction in the appendix — which for national surveys it
essentially never is.) The same draw also reveals the law of diminishing returns: because
sampling error falls like $1/\sqrt{n}$, each additional thousand respondents buys less precision
than the last. Doubling a poll from 1,000 to 2,000 does not halve the margin of error; it shrinks
it by only about 30%. [@bruce_practical]

## A cautionary tale: the *Literary Digest*, 1936

No story makes the point more bluntly. In 1936 the magazine *Literary Digest* mailed straw-poll
ballots to **ten million** people, drawn from telephone directories and automobile registration
lists, and got over two million back. On that mountain of data it confidently predicted a landslide
for Alf Landon. [@bruce_practical]

Franklin Roosevelt won 46 states.

The frames — phone and car owners in 1936 — skewed sharply toward the affluent, who favoured
Landon; that was **coverage bias**. And the people motivated to mail back an unsolicited ballot
differed from those who didn't; that was **non-response bias**. Ten million biased responses are
still biased. Meanwhile George Gallup, using a *scientifically representative* sample of around
**2,000**, called the election correctly. [@bruce_practical]

The lesson is the founding creed of survey design, and it directly contradicts the instinct of the
big-data age: **how you sample beats how much you sample.** A small representative sample crushes a
huge biased one.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong in practice"}

- **Believing a bigger sample fixes bias.** Size only shrinks *sampling* error. Bias — selection,
  coverage, non-response — is untouched by $n$ and is the actual cause of the famous failures.
  [@bruce_practical]

- **Confusing the frame with the population.** Your inference is only ever valid for the frame you
  drew from. If the frame omits a group, your conclusions silently omit them too — and no statistic
  will flag it. [@bruce_practical]

- **Reporting a margin of error for a non-probability sample.** Convenience, quota, and snowball
  samples have no defined selection probabilities, so a "±3%" attached to them is fiction. The
  margin of error is a property of *random* sampling. [@bruce_practical]

- **Ignoring the design effect in clustered data.** Treating a cluster sample as if it were a
  simple random sample understates the standard errors and overstates your certainty. Clustering
  buys cost savings *and* costs precision; both must be booked. [@bruce_practical]

- **Forgetting that non-response is informative.** A 70% response rate is not "70% of a good
  sample" — it is a complete sample only if the 30% who declined are like the 70% who answered, and
  they usually aren't. [@bruce_practical]

:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Standard error of a proportion.** For a simple random sample of size $n$ estimating a population
proportion $p$ with $\hat p$, the sampling distribution has

$$
\operatorname{SE}(\hat p) = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}.
$$

This is maximised at $p = 0.5$, where $\sqrt{p(1-p)} = 0.5$. So for a "50/50" question the margin
of error at 95% confidence is about $1.96 \times 0.5/\sqrt{n} \approx 0.98/\sqrt{n}$. Setting
$n = 1{,}000$ gives $\approx 0.031$ — the famous $\pm 3\%$. Nothing in this expression involves the
population size $N$.

**Finite-population correction (FPC).** When the sample is a non-negligible fraction of the
population, the SE is multiplied by

$$
\sqrt{\frac{N-n}{N-1}}.
$$

For $n/N$ small (a national poll: $1{,}000$ from $10^8$) this factor is essentially 1, which is
*why* population size drops out. It only bites when you sample a large share of a small population
(e.g., 80 of 100 employees), where it correctly shrinks the SE toward zero — a census has no
sampling error at all.

**Why stratification helps.** Decompose the population variance into within-stratum and
between-stratum parts, $\sigma^2 = \sigma^2_{\text{within}} + \sigma^2_{\text{between}}$. A simple
random sample's estimate carries *both*. A stratified sample with proportional allocation fixes the
stratum mix, eliminating the between-stratum component, so its estimator variance is

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\bar x_{\text{strat}}) = \sum_h W_h^2 \,\frac{\sigma_h^2}{n_h},
$$

which depends only on the *within*-stratum variances $\sigma_h^2$. In the figure above this gives
$0.5^2\frac{25}{50} + 0.3^2\frac{25}{30} + 0.2^2\frac{25}{20} = 0.25$, an SE of $0.5$, versus the
SRS SE of $\sqrt{269/100} \approx 1.64$ — the threefold gain you saw, because the strata means
(40, 60, 80) differ a lot while the within-stratum SD (5) is small.

**Design effect (clustering).** The cost of cluster sampling is summarised by the design effect
$\text{DEFF} = 1 + (m-1)\rho$, where $m$ is the cluster size and $\rho$ the intraclass correlation
(how alike units within a cluster are). The *effective* sample size is $n / \text{DEFF}$: with
$\rho = 0.05$ and clusters of $m = 100$, $\text{DEFF} \approx 6$, so 5,000 clustered interviews
carry the precision of only about 830 independent ones — the price of cheap fieldwork.

:::
