A/B Testing & Multi-Armed Bandits

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The applied capstone of the testing toolkit: how to run a real experiment well — and then how adaptive algorithms rewrite the rules when the goal is not to measure the winner but to profit from it while you learn.

An A/B test is a randomized experiment in digital dress: split users at random between the old version (A) and the new (B), compare one pre-chosen metric, and let the hypothesis test tell you whether the difference is more than chance (Bruce et al. 2020). It is the workhorse of the modern web. But it has a hidden cost: while you run it, half your users keep seeing the losing version — and you only learn about two options. Multi-armed bandits attack that waste head-on, turning the experiment itself into a profit-seeking machine that shifts traffic toward the winner as it learns. This entry covers both: the discipline of a clean test, and the explore–exploit logic that sometimes beats testing entirely.

Why it matters

Almost every product decision at scale — a headline, a price, a button colour, a recommendation — is settled by an online experiment. Done right, A/B testing is the cleanest causal evidence you will ever get cheaply: randomization does all the work the Furious Five strain to approximate. Done wrong — peeking until significant, fishing for a metric, stopping early — it manufactures false discoveries at industrial rates. And in many business settings the classic fixed test is simply the wrong tool: when the aim is to maximise reward, not to publish an effect, a bandit reaches the best option faster and bleeds fewer customers on the way. Knowing which regime you are in is the skill.

The A/B test: a randomized experiment in digital dress

Three disciplines make an A/B test trustworthy (Bruce et al. 2020):

  • A control group. The treatment group sees the change; the control sees the standard version. Without a control there is no assurance that “other things are equal” — the control is exposed to the same time-of-day, seasonality, and traffic mix, so any difference between the groups can’t be blamed on those (Bruce et al. 2020).
  • Random assignment. Subjects are split at random, so any group difference is due to the treatment or to the luck of the draw — never to systematic bias in who got what (Bruce et al. 2020). This is the same logic that powers causal inference.
  • A single, pre-specified metric. Choose the test statistic — conversion rate, revenue per visitor, session length — before the test. Picking the winning metric afterwards is data snooping, and it guarantees you find “significance” somewhere (Bruce et al. 2020).

The analysis is then ordinary inference: a two-proportion test or — increasingly — the permutation and bootstrap machinery that gauges chance variation without leaning on a formula (Bruce et al. 2020). The null hypothesis is the skeptic’s baseline, “the difference is just chance”; a small p-value is evidence against it (Bruce et al. 2020).

Design it before you run it

The single most important decision happens before any data arrives: how big must the test be? Bruce & Bruce frame it as four interlocking quantities — fix any three and the fourth follows (Bruce et al. 2020):

Quantity What it is
Effect size the smallest improvement you care to detect (e.g. a 10% lift in conversions)
Significance α your false-positive threshold (usually 5%) — a Type I error
Power 1-\beta the chance of catching a real effect (convention: 80%); missing it is a Type II error
Sample size n how many users you must collect

The lever that hurts is effect size: the smaller the effect you want to detect, the dramatically larger the sample you need (Bruce et al. 2020). Computing the required n in advance is what stops you from running an underpowered test that was doomed to be inconclusive — a full treatment lives in statistical power & sample size. Fixing n ahead of time also disarms the most common online-experiment sin, below.

WarningThe peeking problem (optional stopping)

The tempting move — watch the dashboard and stop the moment it crosses significance — quietly destroys the test (Bruce et al. 2020). Every peek is another chance for noise to wander past the threshold, so repeated looks inflate the false-positive rate far above the nominal 5% (check a test enough times and a null difference will eventually look “significant”). The fixes are exactly two: commit to a pre-computed sample size and analyse once, or use a sequential method designed to allow continuous monitoring. Eyeballing and stopping is neither. This is optional stopping, the same bug behind the replication crisis and a cousin of the multiple-testing problem.

From testing to optimising: explore vs exploit

A fixed A/B test answers a static question — “is B better than A?” — and to answer it cleanly it keeps sending half the traffic to the loser right up to the end, even once the data strongly hint at a winner (Bruce et al. 2020). In a business that is pure waste, and it scales badly: classic tests handle two arms, but you often have five headlines or ten prices to try at once (Bruce et al. 2020).

Reframe the problem and the waste disappears. Picture a row of slot machines — “one-armed bandits” — each with an unknown payout rate; you want to maximise your total winnings across many pulls (Bruce et al. 2020). Every pull forces the central dilemma:

  • Exploit — play the arm that looks best so far, to cash in now.
  • Explore — try other arms, to learn whether one is secretly better.

Pure exploitation can lock onto a false early leader; pure exploration is just a never-ending A/B test. A good multi-armed bandit algorithm balances the two, steering traffic toward the apparent winner while still sampling the rest enough to stay honest (Bruce et al. 2020).

Two bandit algorithms

Epsilon-greedy — the simplest balance (Bruce et al. 2020):

  1. Draw a random number between 0 and 1.
  2. If it is below ε, explore: pick an arm at random.
  3. Otherwise (≥ ε), exploit: serve the arm with the best response rate so far.

The knob ε tunes the trade-off: ε = 1 is a plain A/B test (always explore), ε = 0 is pure greed (never experiment again, so it can get stuck on a false leader) (Bruce et al. 2020). A small ε (say 0.1) keeps a trickle of exploration alive.

Thompson sampling — the Bayesian bandit, and usually the better one (Bruce et al. 2020). Keep a posterior belief about each arm’s success rate — a Beta distribution, updated with every win and loss. At each step, draw one sample from each arm’s posterior and play whichever sample is highest (Bruce et al. 2020). Arms that are probably good get played often; arms that are merely uncertain still get tried (their wide posteriors occasionally sample high) — so exploration is automatic and self-extinguishing as evidence accumulates. The objective has shifted from cleanly measuring a difference to minimising regret — the cumulative reward lost by not having played the best arm all along (Bruce et al. 2020).

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
p = np.array([0.030, 0.050, 0.045, 0.055, 0.040])   # true conversion rates
K = p.size; best = p.max(); best_arm = int(p.argmax())
T, runs, eps = 1200, 150, 0.10

def argmax_random(v):                # argmax with random tie-break
    m = np.flatnonzero(v == v.max())
    return int(m[0] if m.size == 1 else rng.choice(m))

def simulate(kind):
    regret = np.zeros(T); share = np.zeros(T)
    for _ in range(runs):
        succ = np.zeros(K); fail = np.zeros(K); cum = 0.0
        for t in range(T):
            if kind == "ab":                                   # fixed: equal allocation
                arm = t % K
            elif kind == "eps":                                # epsilon-greedy
                if rng.random() < eps:
                    arm = int(rng.integers(K))
                else:
                    arm = argmax_random((succ + 1) / (succ + fail + 2))   # Laplace-smoothed rate
            else:                                              # Thompson sampling
                arm = int(np.argmax(rng.beta(1 + succ, 1 + fail)))
            reward = 1.0 if rng.random() < p[arm] else 0.0
            if reward: succ[arm] += 1
            else:      fail[arm] += 1
            cum += best - p[arm]
            regret[t] += cum
            share[t]  += (arm == best_arm)
    return regret / runs, share / runs

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.6, 4.3))
styles = [("ab", "fixed A/B (equal split)", "#888888"),
          ("eps", "epsilon-greedy (ε=0.1)", "#d97706"),
          ("ts", "Thompson sampling", "#1f4e79")]
steps = np.arange(1, T + 1)
for kind, label, c in styles:
    reg, sh = simulate(kind)
    axL.plot(steps, reg, color=c, lw=2, label=label)
    axR.plot(steps, sh, color=c, lw=2, label=label)

axL.set_xlabel("users (time)"); axL.set_ylabel("cumulative regret (conversions lost)")
axL.set_title("Regret: the cost of still exploring"); axL.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
axR.axhline(1 / K, color="#888888", ls=":", lw=1)
axR.set_xlabel("users (time)"); axR.set_ylabel("share of traffic to the best arm")
axR.set_title("Where the traffic goes"); axR.set_ylim(0, 1.0)
axR.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Which one should you run?

Fixed A/B test Multi-armed bandit
Goal measure the effect cleanly maximise reward while learning
Allocation equal, fixed in advance adaptive, shifts to the winner
Arms usually 2 scales to many (Bruce et al. 2020)
Best for a decision you must defend; an unbiased effect size; regulated / scientific settings web, marketing, e-commerce; rapid optimisation; short-lived content (Bruce et al. 2020)
The catch wastes traffic on the loser; only two options biased sampling makes a clean, unbiased effect estimate and p-value hard to recover (Bruce et al. 2020)

The honest summary (Bruce et al. 2020): bandits get you to the best option faster and cheaper, but because they deliberately sample toward the winner, they sacrifice clean inference — you can no longer read off an unbiased difference or a textbook p-value. Choose the fixed test when you need to know and defend the effect; choose the bandit when you need to win and move on.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Peeking and early stopping. Repeated looks inflate false positives; fix n in advance or use a proper sequential design. (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • Choosing the metric after the fact. A single statistic, pre-specified — anything else is fishing. (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • No control group. Comparing “after” to “before” without a concurrent control confounds the change with time, seasonality, and traffic shifts. (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • Running underpowered. Too small an n for the effect you care about is a Type II error waiting to happen — money spent on an inconclusive result. (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • Reading a bandit like an A/B test. Its adaptive, biased allocation means the naïve difference in rates is not an unbiased treatment effect; don’t quote it as one. (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • ε = 0 greed. Switching off exploration entirely can lock you onto a false early leader forever. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Appendix · the mathematics

Sample size for two proportions. To detect a lift from p_A to p_B (with \delta=p_B-p_A) at significance \alpha and power 1-\beta, the per-arm sample size is n \;\approx\; \frac{\bigl(z_{1-\alpha/2}+z_{1-\beta}\bigr)^2\,\bigl[p_A(1-p_A)+p_B(1-p_B)\bigr]}{\delta^{2}} . Because n\propto 1/\delta^2, halving the effect you want to detect quadruples the users you need — the quantitative form of Bruce & Bruce’s “smaller effect size → much larger sample.” A handy rule of thumb for \alpha=0.05, 80\% power is n\approx 16\,\bar p(1-\bar p)/\delta^2 per arm.

Regret. With arms of mean reward p_k and best arm p^{*}=\max_k p_k, choosing arm a_t at step t incurs cumulative regret R_T \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T}\bigl(p^{*}-p_{a_t}\bigr)\;=\;T p^{*}-\sum_t p_{a_t}. A fixed equal-allocation test pulls each arm T/K times, so its regret grows linearly, R_T=\tfrac{T}{K}\sum_k (p^{*}-p_k)=\Theta(T) — the straight line in the figure. Good bandits achieve sub-linear regret, R_T=O(\log T): the per-user waste tends to zero, which is why their curves flatten.

Thompson sampling is Beta–Bernoulli. With a \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0,\beta_0) prior on an arm’s success rate, observing s successes and f failures gives the conjugate posterior \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0+s,\ \beta_0+f). At each step you draw \theta_k\sim\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0+s_k,\beta_0+f_k) for every arm and play \arg\max_k\theta_k. Arms with high posteriors are usually picked (exploit); arms with wide posteriors occasionally sample high (explore) — so exploration is automatic and fades exactly as fast as the evidence sharpens.

Connections

  • Builds on: hypothesis testing and comparing groups (the two-proportion test under the hood), statistical power (the sample-size calculation), and resampling (how A/B results are often analysed).
  • Guards against: optional stopping and p-hacking and the multiple-testing inflation that peeking is a special case of.
  • Bayesian engine: Thompson sampling is Bayesian updating with a Beta–Bernoulli conjugate posterior, used for decisions rather than estimation.
  • Causally: an A/B test is a randomized experiment — the cleanest identification strategy there is.

## Two bandit algorithms

**Epsilon-greedy** — the simplest balance [@bruce_practical]:

1. Draw a random number between 0 and 1.
2. If it is below **ε**, **explore**: pick an arm at random.
3. Otherwise (**≥ ε**), **exploit**: serve the arm with the best response rate so far.

The knob ε tunes the trade-off: **ε = 1 is a plain A/B test** (always explore), **ε = 0 is pure greed**
(never experiment again, so it can get stuck on a false leader) [@bruce_practical]. A small ε (say 0.1) keeps
a trickle of exploration alive.

**Thompson sampling** — the Bayesian bandit, and usually the better one [@bruce_practical]. Keep a
[posterior](bayesian-machinery.qmd) belief about each arm's success rate — a **Beta distribution**, updated
with every win and loss. At each step, **draw one sample from each arm's posterior and play whichever sample
is highest** [@bruce_practical]. Arms that are *probably* good get played often; arms that are merely
*uncertain* still get tried (their wide posteriors occasionally sample high) — so exploration is automatic
and self-extinguishing as evidence accumulates. The objective has shifted from *cleanly measuring* a
difference to **minimising regret** — the cumulative reward lost by not having played the best arm all along
[@bruce_practical].

```{python}
#| label: fig-bandit
#| fig-cap: "Why adaptive beats fixed, simulated by hand over many runs. Five 'arms' (think headlines) have true conversion rates 3.0–5.5%; the best is arm 4 at 5.5%. LEFT: cumulative regret — total conversions lost versus an oracle that always played the best arm. A fixed A/B test (equal split throughout) keeps sending 4/5 of traffic to inferior arms, so its regret grows in a straight line forever. Epsilon-greedy (ε=0.1) and Thompson sampling bend sharply: once they identify the winner they stop wasting pulls, and regret nearly flattens. RIGHT: the share of traffic sent to the BEST arm — fixed A/B is pinned at 1/5 = 20% by design, while the bandits climb toward 90–97%. Thompson edges out epsilon-greedy by exploring smarter. The price (see text): the bandits' biased sampling makes a clean, unbiased effect estimate much harder to recover."
#| code-fold: true
#| code-summary: "Show the figure code"

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
p = np.array([0.030, 0.050, 0.045, 0.055, 0.040])   # true conversion rates
K = p.size; best = p.max(); best_arm = int(p.argmax())
T, runs, eps = 1200, 150, 0.10

def argmax_random(v):                # argmax with random tie-break
    m = np.flatnonzero(v == v.max())
    return int(m[0] if m.size == 1 else rng.choice(m))

def simulate(kind):
    regret = np.zeros(T); share = np.zeros(T)
    for _ in range(runs):
        succ = np.zeros(K); fail = np.zeros(K); cum = 0.0
        for t in range(T):
            if kind == "ab":                                   # fixed: equal allocation
                arm = t % K
            elif kind == "eps":                                # epsilon-greedy
                if rng.random() < eps:
                    arm = int(rng.integers(K))
                else:
                    arm = argmax_random((succ + 1) / (succ + fail + 2))   # Laplace-smoothed rate
            else:                                              # Thompson sampling
                arm = int(np.argmax(rng.beta(1 + succ, 1 + fail)))
            reward = 1.0 if rng.random() < p[arm] else 0.0
            if reward: succ[arm] += 1
            else:      fail[arm] += 1
            cum += best - p[arm]
            regret[t] += cum
            share[t]  += (arm == best_arm)
    return regret / runs, share / runs

fig, (axL, axR) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9.6, 4.3))
styles = [("ab", "fixed A/B (equal split)", "#888888"),
          ("eps", "epsilon-greedy (ε=0.1)", "#d97706"),
          ("ts", "Thompson sampling", "#1f4e79")]
steps = np.arange(1, T + 1)
for kind, label, c in styles:
    reg, sh = simulate(kind)
    axL.plot(steps, reg, color=c, lw=2, label=label)
    axR.plot(steps, sh, color=c, lw=2, label=label)

axL.set_xlabel("users (time)"); axL.set_ylabel("cumulative regret (conversions lost)")
axL.set_title("Regret: the cost of still exploring"); axL.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
axR.axhline(1 / K, color="#888888", ls=":", lw=1)
axR.set_xlabel("users (time)"); axR.set_ylabel("share of traffic to the best arm")
axR.set_title("Where the traffic goes"); axR.set_ylim(0, 1.0)
axR.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="lower right")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Which one should you run?

| | **Fixed A/B test** | **Multi-armed bandit** |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | *measure* the effect cleanly | *maximise reward* while learning |
| **Allocation** | equal, fixed in advance | adaptive, shifts to the winner |
| **Arms** | usually 2 | scales to many [@bruce_practical] |
| **Best for** | a decision you must defend; an unbiased effect size; regulated / scientific settings | web, marketing, e-commerce; rapid optimisation; short-lived content [@bruce_practical] |
| **The catch** | wastes traffic on the loser; only two options | **biased sampling** makes a clean, unbiased effect estimate and p-value hard to recover [@bruce_practical] |

The honest summary [@bruce_practical]: bandits get you to the best option **faster and cheaper**, but because
they deliberately sample toward the winner, they **sacrifice clean inference** — you can no longer read off an
unbiased difference or a textbook p-value. Choose the fixed test when you need to *know and defend* the
effect; choose the bandit when you need to *win* and move on.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong"}
- **Peeking and early stopping.** Repeated looks inflate false positives; fix $n$ in advance or use a proper
  sequential design. [@bruce_practical]
- **Choosing the metric after the fact.** A single statistic, pre-specified — anything else is fishing.
  [@bruce_practical]
- **No control group.** Comparing "after" to "before" without a concurrent control confounds the change with
  time, seasonality, and traffic shifts. [@bruce_practical]
- **Running underpowered.** Too small an $n$ for the effect you care about is a Type II error waiting to
  happen — money spent on an inconclusive result. [@bruce_practical]
- **Reading a bandit like an A/B test.** Its adaptive, biased allocation means the naïve difference in rates
  is *not* an unbiased treatment effect; don't quote it as one. [@bruce_practical]
- **ε = 0 greed.** Switching off exploration entirely can lock you onto a false early leader forever.
  [@bruce_practical]
:::

## Appendix · the mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Sample size for two proportions.** To detect a lift from $p_A$ to $p_B$ (with $\delta=p_B-p_A$) at
significance $\alpha$ and power $1-\beta$, the per-arm sample size is
$$
n \;\approx\; \frac{\bigl(z_{1-\alpha/2}+z_{1-\beta}\bigr)^2\,\bigl[p_A(1-p_A)+p_B(1-p_B)\bigr]}{\delta^{2}} .
$$
Because $n\propto 1/\delta^2$, **halving the effect you want to detect quadruples the users you need** — the
quantitative form of Bruce & Bruce's "smaller effect size → much larger sample." A handy rule of thumb for
$\alpha=0.05$, $80\%$ power is $n\approx 16\,\bar p(1-\bar p)/\delta^2$ per arm.

**Regret.** With arms of mean reward $p_k$ and best arm $p^{*}=\max_k p_k$, choosing arm $a_t$ at step $t$ incurs
cumulative regret
$$
R_T \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T}\bigl(p^{*}-p_{a_t}\bigr)\;=\;T p^{*}-\sum_t p_{a_t}.
$$
A fixed equal-allocation test pulls each arm $T/K$ times, so its regret grows **linearly**,
$R_T=\tfrac{T}{K}\sum_k (p^{*}-p_k)=\Theta(T)$ — the straight line in the figure. Good bandits achieve
**sub-linear** regret, $R_T=O(\log T)$: the per-user waste tends to zero, which is why their curves flatten.

**Thompson sampling is Beta–Bernoulli.** With a $\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0,\beta_0)$ prior on an arm's success
rate, observing $s$ successes and $f$ failures gives the [conjugate posterior](bayesian-machinery.qmd)
$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0+s,\ \beta_0+f)$. At each step you draw $\theta_k\sim\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0+s_k,\beta_0+f_k)$
for every arm and play $\arg\max_k\theta_k$. Arms with high posteriors are usually picked (exploit); arms
with *wide* posteriors occasionally sample high (explore) — so exploration is automatic and fades exactly as
fast as the evidence sharpens.

:::
