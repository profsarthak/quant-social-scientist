Estimation: From Sample to Best Guess

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

An estimate is a recipe applied to data, not a number — so judge the recipe, not the meal it cooked this once.

You never get to see the population parameter; you only get an estimate of it, computed from one sample by some rule. The whole craft of estimation is learning to evaluate the rule — how far off it runs on average, how much it wobbles, and how to trade a little of one for a lot of the other — because the rule is the only thing you actually control.

Why it matters

A pollster reports 52% support. A lab reports a mean reaction time of 340 ms. An economist reports a wage gap of $3.10. Every one of these is an estimate: a single number squeezed out of a single sample by some formula. Run the study again with a fresh sample and every number would shift. So the honest question is never “is this number right?” — you can’t know — but “is the procedure that produced it a good one?”

That shift in attention, from the estimate to the estimator, is the conceptual core of inference. It lets us ask precise questions — Is the procedure right on average? How much does it bounce? Does it home in on the truth as data accumulates? — and it gives us a vocabulary (bias, variance, consistency, efficiency) that will reappear, unchanged, attached to every regression coefficient and every causal effect later in the book.

The crucial distinction: estimator vs estimate

Keep two words rigorously apart. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • An estimator is a rule — a formula that turns any possible sample into a number. Because the sample is random, the estimator is itself a random variable: feed it different data and it returns different values. The sample mean \bar X = \frac1n\sum X_i is an estimator.
  • An estimate is the one number the rule spits out for your actual dataset. “52%” is an estimate.
TipThe recipe and the cake

The estimator is a recipe; the estimate is the cake you baked this morning. The recipe is fixed, but this morning’s flour and eggs (your particular sample) give a cake slightly different from tomorrow’s. We write the parameter as \theta and the estimator with a hat, \hat\theta — the hat is a promise that the thing underneath is random. (Wooldridge 2019; Ross 2018)

Because \hat\theta is a random variable, it has a sampling distribution (the idea from the CLT entry), and every property we care about is a property of that distribution. Three of them matter most.

Property 1 — Bias: right on average?

An estimator is unbiased if its expected value equals the parameter it targets: \mathbb{E}[\hat\theta] = \theta. The bias is the gap,

\operatorname{Bias}(\hat\theta) = \mathbb{E}[\hat\theta] - \theta .

Unbiased does not mean “correct.” It means that if you drew infinitely many samples and averaged all their estimates, you would land exactly on the truth — the procedure has no systematic tilt, even though any single estimate misses. (Wooldridge 2019) The headline example: the sample mean \bar X is an unbiased estimator of the population mean \mu whatever the underlying distribution. (Wooldridge 2019)

Property 2 — Variance: how much does it wobble?

An unbiased estimator that swings wildly from sample to sample is still nearly useless. The sampling variance measures that swing — the spread of the estimator’s distribution — and its square root is the standard error, the typical distance an estimate falls from its own average. (Ross 2018) For the sample mean,

\operatorname{Var}(\bar X) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n},

which is the engine behind the whole book: precision rises with n, and the standard error falls like \sigma/\sqrt n. Bias asks where the estimator points; variance asks how steadily.

Property 3 — Mean squared error and the great trade-off

Real choices are rarely “unbiased vs biased.” They are “a clean estimator that wobbles a lot vs a slightly-off one that barely moves.” To choose, you need a yardstick that counts both sins. That yardstick is the mean squared error, and it splits cleanly:

\operatorname{MSE}(\hat\theta) = \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta)}_{\text{wobble}} + \underbrace{\big[\operatorname{Bias}(\hat\theta)\big]^2}_{\text{tilt}} .

This identity — MSE = variance + bias² — is one of the most important in all of statistics, and it is the seed of the bias–variance trade-off that organises the whole of predictive modelling in Part IV. (Wooldridge 2019) It says the best estimator is the one with the smallest total error, and that can mean accepting a little bias to kill a lot of variance.

Two source examples pin down the extremes. Wooldridge’s cautionary one: the “estimator” that always guesses 0 has zero variance but enormous bias — perfectly precise, perfectly useless. (Wooldridge 2019) And Ross’s constructive one: a shrinkage estimator that pulls the sample mean gently toward zero is slightly biased, yet can have a smaller MSE than the unbiased sample mean when the true value is modest relative to the noise. (Ross 2018)

See the trade-off

Below, two estimators chase the same true mean (\mu = 1, marked). One is the ordinary sample mean — dead-centred but wide. The other shrinks it halfway to zero — visibly biased (shifted left) but far tighter. Watch which one ends up closer to the truth more often.

Show the simulation code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(11)
mu, sigma, n = 1.0, 3.0, 10
reps = 50_000

samples = rng.normal(mu, sigma, size=(reps, n))
xbar   = samples.mean(axis=1)      # unbiased estimator
shrink = 0.5 * xbar                # biased toward 0, lower variance

def mse(est):  return np.mean((est - mu) ** 2)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.6))
bins = np.linspace(-3, 5, 80)
ax.hist(xbar,   bins=bins, density=True, color="#b7b6a8", alpha=0.75,
        label=f"Sample mean  X̄   (bias 0,  MSE ≈ {mse(xbar):.2f})")
ax.hist(shrink, bins=bins, density=True, color="#161613", alpha=0.85,
        label=f"Shrinkage  ½·X̄   (bias −0.5, MSE ≈ {mse(shrink):.2f})")
ax.axvline(mu, color="#cf4718", ls="--", lw=1.8, label="True mean μ = 1")
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_xlabel("Estimated value")
ax.set_title("Unbiased isn't always best — total error is what counts")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The shrinkage estimator is wrong on average — its cloud is centred at 0.5, not 1 — yet because it wobbles so much less, its squared misses are smaller on the whole. When the signal is weak relative to the noise, a confident-but-slightly-biased rule beats an honest-but-jittery one. This is not a trick; it is the logic behind ridge regression, James–Stein estimation, and every regularised model in Part IV.

Two more properties for large samples

Consistency is the minimal demand we place on any serious estimator: as the sample grows without bound, the estimator’s whole distribution should collapse onto the true value. (Wooldridge 2019) Where unbiasedness is about averaging over many small samples, consistency is about one sample getting huge. The two can come apart: the sample standard deviation S is a consistent estimator of \sigma yet is biased in small samples — because the expectation of a square root is not the square root of an expectation, taking \sqrt{\,\cdot\,} bends the estimate. (Wooldridge 2019) An estimator that isn’t even consistent is hard to defend at all.

Efficiency ranks the survivors. Among unbiased estimators, the efficient one is the minimum-variance one — it extracts the most precision from the same data. The gold standard is the Minimum-Variance Unbiased Estimator (MVUE). (Ross 2018) In the regression setting this becomes the celebrated Gauss–Markov theorem: under its assumptions, Ordinary Least Squares is BLUE — the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator, “best” meaning smallest variance — a result we cash in fully in Part III. (Wooldridge 2019)

Where estimators come from: two recipes

So far we have judged estimators. Where do they come from? Two general recipes generate most of them.

Method of moments (MoM) — the intuitive one. A population moment (the mean \mu, the variance \sigma^2) is some function of the parameter you want. So replace each population moment with its sample counterpart and solve. The population mean’s sample counterpart is the sample average, which is exactly why \bar X is the method-of-moments estimator of \mu. (Wooldridge 2019) Simple, general, and the historical starting point.

Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) — the powerful one, and the workhorse of modern modelling. Fix a probability model, observe your data, then choose the parameter values that make the data you actually saw as probable as possible. (Ross 2018) Lambert frames the logic as an inversion: from the effects (data) we reason backward to the most likely cause (parameter). (Lambert 2018)

TipMLE in one coin flip

Flip a coin 10 times, see 7 heads. What bias p best explains that? The likelihood of 7 heads is \binom{10}{7}p^7(1-p)^3; maximise it over p and the answer is exactly \hat p = 7/10 = 0.7. The maximum-likelihood estimate is just the sample proportion — the value that makes “7 heads out of 10” the least surprising thing that could have happened. (Ross 2018; Lambert 2018)

Why divide by n-1? Degrees of freedom

The most-asked question in introductory statistics: why is the sample variance

S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar X)^2

divided by n-1 and not n? Because dividing by n systematically underestimates the true variance — it is biased. (Wooldridge 2019) The intuition is degrees of freedom. To measure spread you must first compute the sample mean \bar X from the very same data; that step spends one piece of information and imposes a constraint (the deviations X_i - \bar X must sum to zero). Once n-1 of the deviations are known, the last is forced. More tellingly: each X_i sits closer to its own sample mean \bar X than to the unseen true mean \mu, so the squared deviations come out too small. Dividing by the smaller n-1 inflates them by exactly the right amount to make \mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2. (Wooldridge 2019) (A formal proof is in the appendix.)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Judging the estimate instead of the estimator. A single estimate is one draw from a distribution; it can be unlucky even when the procedure is excellent. Evaluate the rule’s long-run behaviour, not whether this one number “looks right.” (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Worshipping unbiasedness. Unbiased estimators can have ruinous variance. A slightly biased estimator with far lower variance has lower MSE and is genuinely better — the entire premise of regularisation. (Ross 2018; Wooldridge 2019)

  • Confusing standard deviation with standard error. \sigma (and its estimate S) describes the data’s spread; the standard error \sigma/\sqrt n describes the estimator’s spread. Same confusion that haunts the CLT entry; same cost.

  • Assuming consistency implies unbiasedness (or vice versa). They are different limits. S is consistent but biased in small samples; some estimators are unbiased yet inconsistent. Check the one you actually need. (Wooldridge 2019)

  • Forgetting the model behind MLE. Maximum likelihood is only as good as the distribution you assumed. Choose the wrong model and you efficiently estimate the wrong thing. (Ross 2018)

Appendix · the full mathematics

The MSE decomposition. Add and subtract \mathbb{E}[\hat\theta] inside the square:

\operatorname{MSE}(\hat\theta) = \mathbb{E}\big[(\hat\theta - \theta)^2\big] = \mathbb{E}\Big[\big((\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta) + (\mathbb{E}\hat\theta - \theta)\big)^2\Big].

Expand. The cross term is 2(\mathbb{E}\hat\theta - \theta)\,\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta] = 0 because \mathbb{E}[\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta] = 0. What survives is

\operatorname{MSE}(\hat\theta) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\big[(\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta)^2\big]}_{\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta)} + \underbrace{(\mathbb{E}\hat\theta - \theta)^2}_{\operatorname{Bias}^2}.

Why n-1 is unbiased. With X_i i.i.d. (\mathbb{E}X_i=\mu, \operatorname{Var}X_i=\sigma^2), use the algebraic identity \sum_i (X_i-\bar X)^2 = \sum_i (X_i-\mu)^2 - n(\bar X-\mu)^2. Take expectations: \mathbb{E}\sum_i(X_i-\mu)^2 = n\sigma^2, and \mathbb{E}[(\bar X-\mu)^2]=\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\sigma^2/n, so

\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_i (X_i-\bar X)^2\right] = n\sigma^2 - n\cdot\frac{\sigma^2}{n} = (n-1)\sigma^2 .

Dividing by n-1 — not n — therefore gives \mathbb{E}[S^2]=\sigma^2. The “lost” \sigma^2/n is exactly the degree of freedom consumed by estimating \mu with \bar X.

MLE of a coin’s bias. The log-likelihood for k heads in n flips is \ell(p) = k\log p + (n-k)\log(1-p) + \text{const}. Differentiate and set to zero: \ell'(p) = \frac{k}{p} - \frac{n-k}{1-p} = 0 \Rightarrow \hat p = k/n. For k=7, n=10, \hat p = 0.7 — the sample proportion is the maximum-likelihood estimate. The second derivative is negative, confirming a maximum.

```{python}
#| fig-cap: "Two estimators of the same mean μ = 1 (dashed line), over 50,000 samples of n = 10. The sample mean (grey) is unbiased but wide; the shrinkage estimator ½·X̄ (blue) is biased toward 0 yet tighter — and here it wins on total error (MSE), the sum of variance and squared bias."
#| code-fold: true
#| code-summary: "Show the simulation code"

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(11)
mu, sigma, n = 1.0, 3.0, 10
reps = 50_000

samples = rng.normal(mu, sigma, size=(reps, n))
xbar   = samples.mean(axis=1)      # unbiased estimator
shrink = 0.5 * xbar                # biased toward 0, lower variance

def mse(est):  return np.mean((est - mu) ** 2)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.6))
bins = np.linspace(-3, 5, 80)
ax.hist(xbar,   bins=bins, density=True, color="#b7b6a8", alpha=0.75,
        label=f"Sample mean  X̄   (bias 0,  MSE ≈ {mse(xbar):.2f})")
ax.hist(shrink, bins=bins, density=True, color="#161613", alpha=0.85,
        label=f"Shrinkage  ½·X̄   (bias −0.5, MSE ≈ {mse(shrink):.2f})")
ax.axvline(mu, color="#cf4718", ls="--", lw=1.8, label="True mean μ = 1")
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_xlabel("Estimated value")
ax.set_title("Unbiased isn't always best — total error is what counts")
ax.legend(fontsize=8, frameon=False, loc="upper left")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

The shrinkage estimator is *wrong on average* — its cloud is centred at 0.5, not 1 — yet because
it wobbles so much less, its squared misses are *smaller on the whole*. When the signal is weak
relative to the noise, a confident-but-slightly-biased rule beats an honest-but-jittery one. This
is not a trick; it is the logic behind ridge regression, James–Stein estimation, and every
regularised model in Part IV.

## Two more properties for large samples

**Consistency** is the *minimal* demand we place on any serious estimator: as the sample grows
without bound, the estimator's whole distribution should **collapse onto the true value**.
[@wooldridge_intro] Where unbiasedness is about averaging over many *small* samples, consistency is
about one sample getting *huge*. The two can come apart: the sample standard deviation $S$ is a
*consistent* estimator of $\sigma$ yet is *biased* in small samples — because the expectation of a
square root is not the square root of an expectation, taking $\sqrt{\,\cdot\,}$ bends the estimate.
[@wooldridge_intro] An estimator that isn't even consistent is hard to defend at all.

**Efficiency** ranks the survivors. Among *unbiased* estimators, the **efficient** one is the
**minimum-variance** one — it extracts the most precision from the same data. The gold standard is
the **Minimum-Variance Unbiased Estimator (MVUE)**. [@ross_first_course] In the regression setting
this becomes the celebrated **Gauss–Markov theorem**: under its assumptions, Ordinary Least
Squares is **BLUE** — the *Best Linear Unbiased Estimator*, "best" meaning smallest variance — a
result we cash in fully in [Part III](../03-regression/index.qmd). [@wooldridge_intro]

## Where estimators come from: two recipes

So far we have judged estimators. Where do they come from? Two general recipes generate most of
them.

**Method of moments (MoM)** — the intuitive one. A population *moment* (the mean $\mu$, the
variance $\sigma^2$) is some function of the parameter you want. So replace each population moment
with its **sample counterpart** and solve. The population mean's sample counterpart is the sample
average, which is exactly why $\bar X$ is the method-of-moments estimator of $\mu$.
[@wooldridge_intro] Simple, general, and the historical starting point.

**Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE)** — the powerful one, and the workhorse of modern modelling.
Fix a probability model, observe your data, then choose the parameter values that make *the data
you actually saw* as **probable as possible**. [@ross_first_course] Lambert frames the logic as an
inversion: from the *effects* (data) we reason backward to the most likely *cause* (parameter).
[@lambert_bayes]

::: {.callout-tip title="MLE in one coin flip"}
Flip a coin 10 times, see 7 heads. What bias $p$ best explains that? The likelihood of 7 heads is
$\binom{10}{7}p^7(1-p)^3$; maximise it over $p$ and the answer is exactly
$\hat p = 7/10 = 0.7$. The maximum-likelihood estimate is just the sample proportion — the value
that makes "7 heads out of 10" the least surprising thing that could have happened.
[@ross_first_course; @lambert_bayes]
:::

## Why divide by $n-1$? Degrees of freedom

The most-asked question in introductory statistics: why is the sample variance

$$
S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar X)^2
$$

divided by $n-1$ and not $n$? Because dividing by $n$ **systematically underestimates** the true
variance — it is *biased*. [@wooldridge_intro] The intuition is **degrees of freedom**. To measure
spread you must first compute the sample mean $\bar X$ from the very same data; that step spends one
piece of information and imposes a constraint (the deviations $X_i - \bar X$ must sum to zero). Once
$n-1$ of the deviations are known, the last is forced. More tellingly: each $X_i$ sits *closer to
its own sample mean* $\bar X$ than to the unseen true mean $\mu$, so the squared deviations come out
**too small**. Dividing by the smaller $n-1$ inflates them by exactly the right amount to make
$\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$. [@wooldridge_intro] (A formal proof is in the appendix.)

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong in practice"}

- **Judging the estimate instead of the estimator.** A single estimate is one draw from a
  distribution; it can be unlucky even when the procedure is excellent. Evaluate the *rule's*
  long-run behaviour, not whether this one number "looks right." [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Worshipping unbiasedness.** Unbiased estimators can have ruinous variance. A slightly biased
  estimator with far lower variance has lower MSE and is genuinely better — the entire premise of
  regularisation. [@ross_first_course; @wooldridge_intro]

- **Confusing standard deviation with standard error.** $\sigma$ (and its estimate $S$) describes
  the *data's* spread; the standard error $\sigma/\sqrt n$ describes the *estimator's* spread. Same
  confusion that haunts the [CLT entry](sampling-distributions-clt.qmd); same cost.

- **Assuming consistency implies unbiasedness (or vice versa).** They are different limits. $S$ is
  consistent but biased in small samples; some estimators are unbiased yet inconsistent. Check the
  one you actually need. [@wooldridge_intro]

- **Forgetting the model behind MLE.** Maximum likelihood is only as good as the distribution you
  assumed. Choose the wrong model and you efficiently estimate the wrong thing.
  [@ross_first_course]

:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The MSE decomposition.** Add and subtract $\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta]$ inside the square:

$$
\operatorname{MSE}(\hat\theta) = \mathbb{E}\big[(\hat\theta - \theta)^2\big]
= \mathbb{E}\Big[\big((\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta) + (\mathbb{E}\hat\theta - \theta)\big)^2\Big].
$$

Expand. The cross term is $2(\mathbb{E}\hat\theta - \theta)\,\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta] = 0$
because $\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta] = 0$. What survives is

$$
\operatorname{MSE}(\hat\theta) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\big[(\hat\theta - \mathbb{E}\hat\theta)^2\big]}_{\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta)}
+ \underbrace{(\mathbb{E}\hat\theta - \theta)^2}_{\operatorname{Bias}^2}.
$$

**Why $n-1$ is unbiased.** With $X_i$ i.i.d. ($\mathbb{E}X_i=\mu$, $\operatorname{Var}X_i=\sigma^2$),
use the algebraic identity $\sum_i (X_i-\bar X)^2 = \sum_i (X_i-\mu)^2 - n(\bar X-\mu)^2$. Take
expectations: $\mathbb{E}\sum_i(X_i-\mu)^2 = n\sigma^2$, and
$\mathbb{E}[(\bar X-\mu)^2]=\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\sigma^2/n$, so

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_i (X_i-\bar X)^2\right] = n\sigma^2 - n\cdot\frac{\sigma^2}{n} = (n-1)\sigma^2 .
$$

Dividing by $n-1$ — not $n$ — therefore gives $\mathbb{E}[S^2]=\sigma^2$. The "lost" $\sigma^2/n$ is
exactly the degree of freedom consumed by estimating $\mu$ with $\bar X$.

**MLE of a coin's bias.** The log-likelihood for $k$ heads in $n$ flips is
$\ell(p) = k\log p + (n-k)\log(1-p) + \text{const}$. Differentiate and set to zero:
$\ell'(p) = \frac{k}{p} - \frac{n-k}{1-p} = 0 \Rightarrow \hat p = k/n$. For $k=7, n=10$,
$\hat p = 0.7$ — the sample proportion is the maximum-likelihood estimate. The second derivative is
negative, confirming a maximum.

:::
