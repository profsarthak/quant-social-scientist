Statistical Power & Sample Size

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The question asked too late: could this study have seen the effect, even if it were real?

Hypothesis testing guards against crying wolf. Power guards against the opposite failure — running a study so small it couldn’t have spotted the wolf if one were standing in the room. A test with low power is a coin flip dressed in the costume of science, and most of the replication crisis traces back to ignoring it.

Why it matters

A test can fail in two directions. It can raise a false alarm — a Type I error, the thing \alpha controls. Or it can miss a real signal — a Type II error. Significance testing obsesses over the first and routinely ignores the second, and that lopsided attention is enormously costly. (Reinhart 2015) A study with too few subjects is, in Reinhart’s phrase, dead on arrival: it cannot detect the very effect it set out to find, so it wastes the money, the participants, and the years — and, worse, pollutes the literature with misleading results. (Reinhart 2015)

Power analysis is the cure, and it belongs before data collection, not after. It is the single highest-leverage thing a researcher does, because no amount of sophisticated analysis can recover a signal a study was never built to see.

Power, defined

Recall the two error rates from the testing entry: \alpha is the false-positive rate, and \beta is the false-negative rate — the chance of missing a real effect. Statistical power is the complement of \beta: (Reinhart 2015)

\text{Power} = 1 - \beta = \Pr(\text{reject } H_0 \mid H_0 \text{ is false}).

In plain words: if the effect is genuinely there, what’s the probability your study actually catches it (gets p<0.05)? A study with 80% power — the conventional minimum — has an 80% chance of detecting a true effect and a 20% chance of missing it to sampling noise. (Reinhart 2015; Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

The four interlocking quantities

Power never stands alone. It is one corner of a four-way balancing act, and the four are bound together so tightly that fixing any three pins down the fourth. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

Quantity What it is Typical value
\alpha False-positive rate (significance threshold) 0.05
Power (1-\beta) Chance of detecting a real effect 0.80
Effect size How big the real-world effect is the unknown you posit
Sample size n How many observations what you solve for

This is the golden rule of design: pre-commit to \alpha = 0.05 and 80% power, posit a target effect size, and the mathematics dictates exactly how many subjects you must recruit. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) Turn it around and it diagnoses: with \alpha, n, and effect size fixed, you can compute the power a planned study will have — and discover, before spending a cent, whether it’s worth running.

Power is the overlap of two distributions

Here is the whole idea in one picture. There are two sampling distributions in play: the one under the null (no effect, centred at 0) and the one under the truth (a real effect, centred to the right). You reject when your statistic lands past the critical value. Power is the share of the true distribution that falls in the rejection region — and it grows as the true distribution slides rightward, which is exactly what a bigger effect or a larger n does.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statistics import NormalDist

zc = 1.645                       # one-sided 5% critical value
x = np.linspace(-4, 7, 700)
null_pdf = np.array([NormalDist(0, 1).pdf(v) for v in x])

def draw(ax, alt_mean, label):
    alt_pdf = np.array([NormalDist(alt_mean, 1).pdf(v) for v in x])
    power = 1 - NormalDist(alt_mean, 1).cdf(zc)
    ax.plot(x, null_pdf, color="#9aa0a6", lw=1.6)
    ax.plot(x, alt_pdf,  color="#1f3a5f", lw=1.8)
    ax.fill_between(x, alt_pdf, where=(x >= zc), color="#1f8a4c", alpha=0.55)
    ax.axvline(zc, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.2)
    ax.set_title(f"{label}\npower = {power:.0%}", fontsize=10)
    ax.set_yticks([])
    ax.set_xlabel("Test statistic")

fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 3.4), sharey=True)
draw(ax1, 1.5, "Small effect / small n")
draw(ax2, 3.0, "Bigger effect / larger n")
ax1.text(zc + 0.1, 0.38, "reject →", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
fig.suptitle("Power is the slice of the truth that clears the critical value", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The grey curve never moves — the null is fixed. What changes is where the truth sits. When the real effect is small relative to noise (left), most of the true distribution piles up below the threshold, so the study misses more than half the time. Push the truth rightward with a stronger effect or more data (right), and almost all of it clears the bar. That is what “buying power” literally buys.

Effect size: you must guess the target

To run the power calculation you need a target effect size — a posited magnitude for the real effect, measured independently of sample size. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) The standard currencies are Cohen’s d (the difference in means divided by the pooled standard deviation) and R^2 / \eta^2 (the proportion of variance explained). (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

The logic is intuitive and unforgiving: big effects are cheap to detect, subtle ones ruinously expensive. A 50% jump in sales screams through a handful of observations; a 1% shift in an election outcome hides behind a wall of noise that only an enormous sample can see past. (Reinhart 2015) The smaller the needle you hope to detect moving, the more data you must buy.

A priori power analysis

An a priori power analysis runs the calculation before the study, to decide whether it is even worth doing. (Reinhart 2015) You estimate the expected effect size from prior literature or a pilot, fix \alpha and your target power, and solve for n — typically with a tool like G*Power. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

TipThe heart-attack trial

Suppose a treatment cuts the risk of death from a heart attack in half — from 20% to 10%, a genuinely large clinical effect. How many patients do you need for 80% power? Intuition says maybe 50. The a priori analysis says 400 — two hundred per arm. (Reinhart 2015) Even a dramatic, life-saving effect demands far more data than instinct suggests. This is why so many trials are quietly underpowered: researchers budget by gut, and gut is wildly optimistic.

The perils of underpowered research

Underpowered studies don’t just fail individually — they corrode the whole literature. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Wasted resources. A study with 50% power is a coin flip: even when the theory is correct, it finds the effect only half the time, after burning the full cost. (Reinhart 2015)
  • A field-wide problem. This is not hypothetical. Back in 1960, Jacob Cohen found the average social-psychology study had just 48% power to detect a medium effect — worse than a coin flip. Decades on, power in some fields had barely improved or even fallen, helping produce the wave of failed replications we now call a crisis. (Reinhart 2015)

Truth inflation: the winner’s curse

Here is the most insidious consequence, and the one researchers least expect. In an underpowered study, the average outcome doesn’t reach significance — so the only results that cross p<0.05 are the lucky, extreme ones that happened to overshoot. (Reinhart 2015) And those are exactly the ones that get published. The result is truth inflation (a Type M, or magnitude, error): the effect sizes in the literature are systematically exaggerated versions of reality. (Reinhart 2015)

The exaggeration can be staggering. In a small-sample study of gender ratios (Kanazawa), the samples were so thin that any result reaching significance had to overstate the true effect by at least a factor of 20 to clear the bar. (Reinhart 2015) The “discovery” wasn’t just lucky — it was guaranteed to be enormous-or-invisible, with nothing true in between. Low power doesn’t merely hide real effects; it manufactures overstated ones.

“Not significant” is not “no effect”

The mirror-image error closes the loop with the testing entry. A non-significant result (p>0.05) is constantly misread as proof that an effect is absent. It is nothing of the kind. (Reinhart 2015) “Not significant” means only that the data were too uninformative to rule out chance — the study could not distinguish a real effect from noise. (Albers 2017) Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Countless clinical trials announce “no difference in side effects” while having had the power to detect only the most catastrophic reactions. (Reinhart 2015)

The diagnostic tool is the confidence interval: a narrow interval hugging zero genuinely suggests the effect is small; a wide interval spanning zero says the study was simply underpowered and inconclusive. Same p-value, opposite meanings — which is why you report the interval. (Reinhart 2015)

Designing an adequately powered study

The practical levers, in order of bluntness: (Reinhart 2015; Gerber and Green 2012)

  • Aim for ≥ 80% power as the floor for adequacy. (Reinhart 2015)
  • Increase n. The most direct lever — more observations shrink the standard error and slide the truth-distribution rightward. (Subject, always, to the \sqrt n tax.)
  • Strengthen the treatment. If subjects are expensive, a more intense intervention raises the effect size itself — the other way to separate the two bells. (Gerber and Green 2012)
  • Reduce noise. Prognostic covariates, blocking, and matching strip out background variation, making a given effect easier to see — power without extra subjects. (Gerber and Green 2012)
  • Report confidence intervals, always, so readers can tell “precisely zero” from “we couldn’t tell.” (Reinhart 2015)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Skipping power analysis entirely, then being surprised by a null result. Without it you can’t distinguish “no effect” from “no chance of seeing it.” Run it before collecting data. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Budgeting sample size by intuition. Gut feeling drastically underestimates the n needed — the heart-attack trial needs 400, not 50. Use a real calculation. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Trusting effect sizes from small significant studies. Truth inflation guarantees they’re exaggerated; the smaller and noisier the study, the bigger the overstatement. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Post-hoc “observed power.” Computing power from your observed effect after a null result is circular and uninformative — it just re-encodes the p-value. Power analysis is a design tool.

  • Reading non-significance as proof of no effect. Check whether the confidence interval is narrow around zero (real null) or wide (underpowered). (Reinhart 2015; Albers 2017)

Appendix · the full mathematics

Power as a tail probability. For a one-sided test at level \alpha with critical value z_\alpha, write the true standardized effect as \delta (the alternative mean in standard-error units). The test statistic under the truth is \mathcal N(\delta, 1), so

\text{Power} = \Pr(Z > z_\alpha \mid Z \sim \mathcal N(\delta,1)) = 1 - \Phi(z_\alpha - \delta).

Power rises with \delta, and \delta itself grows with both the raw effect size and \sqrt n — which is why both levers work, and why power climbs only as fast as \sqrt n.

Solving for sample size. Inverting the two-error condition for a two-sample mean comparison with effect size d (Cohen’s d) gives the familiar per-group formula

n \;\approx\; \frac{2\,(z_{\alpha/2} + z_{\beta})^2}{d^{2}} .

For \alpha = 0.05 (so z_{\alpha/2}=1.96) and 80% power (z_\beta = 0.84), (1.96+0.84)^2 \approx 7.85, so n \approx 15.7/d^2 per group. A “small” effect d=0.2 needs \approx 393 per group; a “large” d=0.8 needs only \approx 25 — the four-fold-effect, sixteen-fold-sample relationship in one line.

Why truth inflation is forced. Conditioning on significance, the published estimate is drawn from the truncated upper tail of the sampling distribution. When power is low the unconditional mean lies below the threshold, so the conditional mean \mathbb{E}[\hat\theta \mid |\hat\theta| > c] can sit far above the true \theta — the exaggeration (Type M) factor. The lower the power, the heavier the truncation, the larger the inflation.

- **Reinhart, *Statistics Done Wrong*** — power as $1-\beta$ and the "dead on arrival" framing; the
  heart-attack 20%→10% / 400-patient example; Cohen's 1960 finding of 48% average power and its
  decline; truth inflation / Type M error and the Kanazawa factor-of-20; non-significance ≠ no
  effect; the narrow-vs-wide-CI diagnostic; the 80% convention.
- **Martin & Bridgmon, *Quantitative & Statistical Research Methods*** — the four interlocking
  quantities and the "fix three, get the fourth" rule; effect-size metrics (Cohen's $d$, $\eta^2$);
  a priori power analysis with G\*Power.
- **Gerber & Green, *Field Experiments*** — design levers for power: larger samples, stronger
  treatments, and noise reduction via covariates, blocking, and matching.
- **Albers, *Quantitative Data Analysis*** — a study can only claim it *failed to find* a
  relationship, not that none exists.
:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Power as a tail probability.** For a one-sided test at level $\alpha$ with critical value
$z_\alpha$, write the true standardized effect as $\delta$ (the alternative mean in standard-error
units). The test statistic under the truth is $\mathcal N(\delta, 1)$, so

$$
\text{Power} = \Pr(Z > z_\alpha \mid Z \sim \mathcal N(\delta,1)) = 1 - \Phi(z_\alpha - \delta).
$$

Power rises with $\delta$, and $\delta$ itself grows with both the raw effect size and $\sqrt n$ —
which is why both levers work, and why power climbs only as fast as $\sqrt n$.

**Solving for sample size.** Inverting the two-error condition for a two-sample mean comparison with
effect size $d$ (Cohen's $d$) gives the familiar per-group formula

$$
n \;\approx\; \frac{2\,(z_{\alpha/2} + z_{\beta})^2}{d^{2}} .
$$

For $\alpha = 0.05$ (so $z_{\alpha/2}=1.96$) and 80% power ($z_\beta = 0.84$),
$(1.96+0.84)^2 \approx 7.85$, so $n \approx 15.7/d^2$ per group. A "small" effect $d=0.2$ needs
$\approx 393$ per group; a "large" $d=0.8$ needs only $\approx 25$ — the four-fold-effect,
sixteen-fold-sample relationship in one line.

**Why truth inflation is forced.** Conditioning on significance, the published estimate is drawn
from the *truncated* upper tail of the sampling distribution. When power is low the unconditional
mean lies below the threshold, so the conditional mean $\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta \mid |\hat\theta| >
c]$ can sit far above the true $\theta$ — the exaggeration (Type M) factor. The lower the power, the
heavier the truncation, the larger the inflation.

:::
