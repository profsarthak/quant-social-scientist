Sampling Distributions & the Central Limit Theorem

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Why a single number from your data is itself a roll of the dice — and why those rolls pile up into a bell.

Every statistic you compute is a random variable in disguise: run the study again and you’d get a slightly different number. The sampling distribution is the set of numbers you could have gotten, and the Central Limit Theorem tells you that — for averages — that set is almost always a bell curve.

Why it matters

You run a survey, compute an average, and report it. But you only ran the survey once. Had a different 1,200 people answered the phone, your average would have come out a little different. So the real question behind every empirical claim is: how much would my answer have bounced around if chance had dealt a different sample?

That bounce is not a nuisance to apologize for — it is the thing inference is about. Margins of error, confidence intervals, p-values, standard errors: every one of them is a statement about a sampling distribution. Get this idea and the rest of statistics stops being a bag of formulas and becomes one question asked many ways.

Intuition

There are two different distributions that beginners constantly confuse, and keeping them apart is most of the battle. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • A data distribution describes the individual values in one dataset — the heights of the people in your sample, say. Plot a histogram of your data and that’s what you see.
  • A sampling distribution describes how a summary of the data — the mean, a proportion, a treatment effect — would vary across many hypothetical datasets drawn the same way.

The leap is this: a statistic is a function of random data, so it is itself random. The sample mean \bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i is a sum of random variables, and a sum of random variables has its own probability distribution. (Ross 2018) Grinstead & Snell make this concrete by simulation: keep drawing samples, compute the mean each time, and watch those means scatter into a shape of their own. (Grinstead and Snell 1997) In an experiment, Gerber & Green give the cleanest gloss — the sampling distribution is “the collection of estimates that could have been generated by every possible random assignment.” That is exactly what lets you ask whether your result was a lucky draw. (Gerber and Green 2012)

Watch it happen

The most convincing argument here is not a proof — it’s a picture. Start with a population that is emphatically not bell-shaped (a heavily skewed one), draw samples, and plot the distribution of the sample mean as the sample size grows.

Show the simulation code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

# A strongly right-skewed "population": an exponential distribution.
# (Think: income, donation sizes, time-to-event — common in social data.)
pop_mean = 1.0
population = rng.exponential(scale=pop_mean, size=200_000)

sample_sizes = [2, 10, 30]
n_reps = 50_000

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 4, figsize=(11, 3.1))

# Panel 0: the raw population
axes[0].hist(population, bins=60, range=(0, 6), color="#9a6a2f", alpha=0.85)
axes[0].set_title("Population\n(raw data, skewed)", fontsize=10)
axes[0].set_yticks([])

# Panels 1-3: sampling distribution of the mean at increasing n
for ax, n in zip(axes[1:], sample_sizes):
    means = population[rng.integers(0, population.size, size=(n_reps, n))].mean(axis=1)
    ax.hist(means, bins=60, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.85, density=True)
    ax.set_title(f"Mean of n = {n}", fontsize=10)
    ax.set_yticks([])
    # overlay the Normal the CLT predicts: mean = pop_mean, sd = pop_sd / sqrt(n)
    pop_sd = population.std()
    xs = np.linspace(means.min(), means.max(), 200)
    se = pop_sd / np.sqrt(n)
    normal = np.exp(-0.5 * ((xs - pop_mean) / se) ** 2) / (se * np.sqrt(2 * np.pi))
    ax.plot(xs, normal, color="#b5446e", lw=1.8)

fig.suptitle("The sample mean forgets the shape it came from", fontsize=12)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The leftmost panel is the world as it actually is: lopsided. Yet by the time we average just 30 observations, the distribution of that average is almost perfectly Normal (the pink curve is the bell the theorem predicts). The sample mean forgets the shape it came from. That forgetting is the Central Limit Theorem.

The Central Limit Theorem

In plain language: if you add up or average enough independent random pieces, the result is approximately Normal — no matter what distribution the pieces came from. Francis Galton called it the “supreme law of unreason”: order emerging reliably out of individual chaos. (Grinstead and Snell 1997)

Stated carefully: let X_1, X_2, \dots be independent and identically distributed with finite mean \mu and finite variance \sigma^2. Then as n \to \infty, the standardized sample mean converges in distribution to a standard Normal:

Z_n \;=\; \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}(0, 1).

Two things deserve emphasis, because they are where the theorem is most often misremembered:

  • What it needs: independence, and a finite mean and variance. It does not need the data to be Normal — that’s the whole point. A version even allows the pieces to have different distributions, as long as no single one dominates the sum. (Ross 2018)
  • How fast: remarkably fast for symmetric data — a fair die’s average looks bell-shaped by n \approx 10. The famous “n \ge 30” is only a rule of thumb, and a bad one for heavily skewed data, which can need far more. (Bruce et al. 2020; Reinhart 2015)

Standard deviation vs standard error

This is the second great confusion, and it costs people dearly. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Standard deviation (\sigma) measures the spread of individual data points. How much do people differ from the average person? That’s the SD.
  • Standard error (SE) measures the spread of a statistic across hypothetical samples. It is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution. How much would my estimate bounce around if I redid the study? That’s the SE. (Bruce et al. 2020)

They are linked by the single most useful formula in applied statistics:

\text{SE}(\bar{X}) \;=\; \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}.

The \sqrt{n} is doing quiet, brutal work. Because uncertainty shrinks with the square root of the sample size, to halve your standard error you must quadruple your sample. This is why precision is expensive, and why a poll of 1,200 is “good enough” while a poll of 4,800 is only twice as good. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Worked examples

An astronomer measures a distance. (Ross 2018) Atmospheric distortion and human error make any single telescope reading noisy. So the astronomer takes n independent readings and averages them. By the CLT, that average is approximately Normal around the true distance — and its spread, the SE, shrinks like \sigma/\sqrt{n}. More readings, sharper answer.

A pollster samples a nation. (Grinstead and Snell 1997) The CLT is why a sample of roughly 1,200 people pins down a national preference to about a \pm 3\% margin — and, strikingly, why that barely depends on whether the country has 100,000 or 100 million people. It’s the sample size that drives precision, not the population size.

Female village councils in India. (Gerber and Green 2012) When researchers asked whether reserving council-head seats for women shifted budget priorities, they used the sampling distribution of the estimated effect to ask: is the spending difference we observed bigger than what random assignment alone could have produced? That comparison is the inference.

The RAND Health Insurance Experiment. (Angrist and Pischke 2009) Before trusting any treatment effect, the researchers used standard errors to check that groups assigned to different insurance plans were balanced at baseline — comparable starting points, within the bounds of chance.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Thinking your data should be Normal. The CLT is about the sampling distribution of a statistic, not your raw observations. Social-science data is routinely skewed and long-tailed (income, donations, durations). Forcing a Normal model onto raw data underestimates extreme “black swan” values. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Trusting n \ge 30 blindly. For strongly skewed data, t-tests and Normal approximations can be badly off even at moderate n — Reinhart’s example is charitable donations, where a few huge gifts wreck the approximation. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Eyeballing overlapping error bars. Two confidence intervals can overlap and the difference can still be significant. “Your eyeball is not a well-defined statistical procedure” — run the actual test. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Treating the bootstrap as a cure for small samples. Resampling your own data reveals the variability already present; it does not manufacture information you never collected. It frees you from assuming Normality, not from needing enough data. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • The gambler’s fallacy and optional stopping. A run of heads does not make tails “due” — the Law of Large Numbers governs the long run, not short streaks. And peeking at your data repeatedly and stopping the moment p < 0.05 massively inflates false positives. (Grinstead and Snell 1997; Reinhart 2015)

Appendix · the full mathematics

Mean and variance of the sample mean. Let X_1,\dots,X_n be i.i.d. with \mathbb{E}[X_i]=\mu and \operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2. Then by linearity of expectation,

\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] = \mu,

so the sample mean is unbiased. For the variance, independence makes the cross terms vanish:

\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}) = \operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \operatorname{Var}(X_i) = \frac{1}{n^2}\,(n\sigma^2) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}.

Taking the square root gives the standard error, \text{SE}(\bar X)=\sigma/\sqrt{n}, and with it the \sqrt{n} rule.

The Central Limit Theorem (Lindeberg–Lévy form). Define the standardized mean

Z_n = \frac{\bar X_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n} = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu).

Then for every real z,

\lim_{n\to\infty} \Pr(Z_n \le z) = \Phi(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{z} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-t^2/2}\,dt .

Sketch of why. Work with the characteristic function \varphi(t)=\mathbb{E}[e^{itZ_n}]. Writing Y_i=(X_i-\mu)/\sigma (mean 0, variance 1) and using independence,

\varphi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[\varphi_Y\!\left(\tfrac{t}{\sqrt n}\right)\right]^{n}.

A second-order Taylor expansion gives \varphi_Y(s) = 1 - \tfrac{1}{2}s^2 + o(s^2), so

\varphi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[1 - \frac{t^2}{2n} + o\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n}\right)\right]^{n} \;\longrightarrow\; e^{-t^2/2},

which is the characteristic function of the standard Normal. Lévy’s continuity theorem then delivers convergence in distribution. The finite-variance assumption is exactly what makes the quadratic term — and nothing higher — survive in the limit; when variance is infinite (heavy tails), the limit is a different, non-Normal stable law, which is the deep reason the CLT can fail.

Connections

  • Builds on: random variables, expectation and variance (the \sigma/\sqrt{n} standard error), the Normal distribution, and the Law of Large Numbers (which gives the destination the CLT then puts a shape around).
  • Leads to: confidence intervals and hypothesis testing, where the sampling distribution becomes the engine of inference.
  • Reappears in: the standard errors attached to every regression coefficient and every causal estimate.

::: {.math-appendix}

**Mean and variance of the sample mean.** Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ be i.i.d. with
$\mathbb{E}[X_i]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2$. Then by linearity of
expectation,

$$
\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] = \mu,
$$

so the sample mean is **unbiased**. For the variance, independence makes the cross terms
vanish:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X})
= \operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right)
= \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \operatorname{Var}(X_i)
= \frac{1}{n^2}\,(n\sigma^2)
= \frac{\sigma^2}{n}.
$$

Taking the square root gives the standard error, $\text{SE}(\bar X)=\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, and
with it the $\sqrt{n}$ rule.

**The Central Limit Theorem (Lindeberg–Lévy form).** Define the standardized mean

$$
Z_n = \frac{\bar X_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n} = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu).
$$

Then for every real $z$,

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty} \Pr(Z_n \le z) = \Phi(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{z} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-t^2/2}\,dt .
$$

*Sketch of why.* Work with the characteristic function $\varphi(t)=\mathbb{E}[e^{itZ_n}]$.
Writing $Y_i=(X_i-\mu)/\sigma$ (mean 0, variance 1) and using independence,

$$
\varphi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[\varphi_Y\!\left(\tfrac{t}{\sqrt n}\right)\right]^{n}.
$$

A second-order Taylor expansion gives
$\varphi_Y(s) = 1 - \tfrac{1}{2}s^2 + o(s^2)$, so

$$
\varphi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[1 - \frac{t^2}{2n} + o\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n}\right)\right]^{n}
\;\longrightarrow\; e^{-t^2/2},
$$

which is the characteristic function of the standard Normal. Lévy's continuity theorem then
delivers convergence in distribution. The finite-variance assumption is exactly what makes
the quadratic term — and nothing higher — survive in the limit; when variance is infinite
(heavy tails), the limit is a different, non-Normal stable law, which is the deep reason the
CLT can fail.

:::
