Credible Intervals, Computation & the Verdict

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Reading the posterior, computing one when no formula exists, and an honest reckoning of Bayesian against frequentist.

A posterior is the whole answer — but you still have to read it, and for any realistic model you can’t even write it down in closed form. This entry does both: it shows how to summarise a posterior into the interval people actually wanted, how a clever random walk (MCMC) conjures that posterior without the impossible integral, and where, in the end, Bayesian and frequentist thinking each earn their keep.

Why it matters

This is the last entry of Part II, and it closes two loops. First, it delivers the interval the confidence interval couldn’t — one you may legitimately read as “95% probability the parameter is in here.” Second, it confronts the wall raised in B2: the denominator is an intractable integral, so how does anyone do Bayesian inference on a real problem? The answer — simulation — is the engine behind the entire modern Bayesian toolkit, and once you grasp its core trick the rest is detail. Then we step back and weigh the two paradigms honestly, because a quantitative social scientist should be bilingual, not partisan.

The credible interval

A credible interval is a range read straight off the posterior that contains a stated share of its probability mass — a 95% credible interval holds 95% of the posterior. (Lambert 2018) And here is the pay-off the whole sub-chapter has been building toward:

ImportantWhat a Bayesian may say

A frequentist cannot say “there is a 95% probability the true value lies in 1%–5%,” because to them the parameter is a fixed constant — the interval either contains it or doesn’t. A Bayesian can say exactly that, because the parameter is a random variable carrying a distribution. (Lambert 2018)

The credible interval describes post-data uncertainty about the specific parameter — your actual state of knowledge now — rather than the long-run behaviour of a procedure. (Lambert 2018) Lambert’s running example: a 95% credible interval of 0\% \le \text{RT} \le 4\% for the proportion of red-tailed penguins is a direct statement that the true proportion is, with 95% probability, in that range. (Lambert 2018) It is the interval everyone always thought they were getting.

Equal-tailed vs highest-posterior-density

There are two ways to carve a 95% region out of a posterior. (Lambert 2018)

  • Equal-tailed (central) interval — trim the bottom 2.5% and top 2.5% of the mass; the interval runs between the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles and is centred on the median.
  • Highest posterior density (HPD) interval — the set of values such that every point inside has higher posterior density than every point outside. It is the shortest region holding 95%, and for a multi-peaked posterior it can be non-contiguous.
TipLambert’s treasure-hunting pirate

A pirate hunts buried gold. A central interval forces him to dig one long contiguous stretch — say 1.5 km to 8.5 km of beach — even where the gold is unlikely in the middle. An HPD interval lets him dig only the truly promising patches — 1–3 km and 7–9 km — skipping the barren middle and saving enormous excavation cost. (Lambert 2018) When the posterior is single-peaked and symmetric the two intervals coincide; when it is skewed or bimodal, HPD is sharper.

Credible vs confidence, on the same data

Here is the contrast made concrete. We flip a coin 20 times, see 14 heads, and form both 95% intervals. The Bayesian posterior (flat prior) is \text{Beta}(15,7); its credible interval is shaded. The frequentist Wald interval sits just below. The numbers nearly coincide — but what they claim could not be more different.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from math import lgamma

def beta_pdf(theta, a, b):
    logB = lgamma(a) + lgamma(b) - lgamma(a + b)
    return np.exp((a - 1) * np.log(theta) + (b - 1) * np.log(1 - theta) - logB)

k, n = 14, 20
a_post, b_post = 1 + k, 1 + (n - k)          # flat prior Beta(1,1) -> Beta(15,7)
theta = np.linspace(1e-4, 1 - 1e-4, 2000)
post = beta_pdf(theta, a_post, b_post)

# 95% equal-tailed credible interval by numerical CDF
cdf = np.cumsum(post) * (theta[1] - theta[0])
cdf /= cdf[-1]
lo_c = theta[np.searchsorted(cdf, 0.025)]
hi_c = theta[np.searchsorted(cdf, 0.975)]

# Frequentist Wald 95% CI
phat = k / n
se = np.sqrt(phat * (1 - phat) / n)
lo_f, hi_f = phat - 1.96 * se, phat + 1.96 * se

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.9))
ax.plot(theta, post, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.2)
ax.fill_between(theta, post, where=(theta >= lo_c) & (theta <= hi_c),
                color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.25)
ax.errorbar(phat, -0.25, xerr=[[phat - lo_f], [hi_f - phat]], fmt="o",
            color="#c0392b", capsize=4, lw=2)
ax.text(hi_c + 0.01, beta_pdf(0.7, a_post, b_post) * 0.6,
        f"credible: [{lo_c:.2f}, {hi_c:.2f}]\n95% prob. θ in here",
        color="#1f3a5f", fontsize=8.5)
ax.text(hi_f + 0.01, -0.25, f"confidence: [{lo_f:.2f}, {hi_f:.2f}]\nprocedure covers 95%",
        color="#c0392b", fontsize=8.5, va="center")
ax.set_xlim(0.3, 1.0)
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_xlabel("θ — the coin's bias")
ax.set_title("Same data, two intervals, two meanings")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The lesson of the picture: agreement on the numbers hides a chasm in meaning. The credible interval is a claim about the parameter; the confidence interval is a claim about the procedure. In this simple, flat-prior case they nearly match — but in small samples with real prior information, or in complex models, they diverge, and only one of them answers the question you actually asked.

Why we must compute

For all but textbook models, the posterior has no closed form, because the normalising denominator p(\text{data}) = \int p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,d\theta is an integral over every parameter value — and in a multi-parameter model that is high-dimensional. (Lambert 2018) Lambert’s recurring example: test scores across 100 schools can demand a 200-dimensional integral, which no computer can solve exactly or even approximate by brute force. (Lambert 2018) If we had to evaluate that denominator, applied Bayesian statistics would be dead in the water.

MCMC: sampling without the integral

The escape is Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) — instead of computing the posterior, draw samples from it, and let the cloud of samples stand in for the distribution. (Lambert 2018) The magic is that you can do this without ever computing the denominator.

MCMC is a guided random walk: each new sample depends on the current one, and the walk is rigged so it lingers in regions of high posterior density. Over time the collected points mirror the posterior’s shape. (Lambert 2018) The simplest version is the Metropolis algorithm:

  1. Propose a step to a nearby location.
  2. If the posterior density there is higher (uphill), accept — always move toward more probable values.
  3. If it is lower (downhill), accept only with probability r, the ratio of the new density to the old. So the walk usually climbs but occasionally wanders down, which is what lets it explore the whole distribution instead of getting stuck on the peak. (Lambert 2018)
TipLambert’s explorer in the fog

Imagine mapping a mountain range permanently shrouded in fog. You can’t photograph it from a satellite (you can’t see the whole posterior). But you can take local steps, recording your altitude and coordinates at each. Take enough steps — preferring uphill but sometimes slipping down — and your notebook reconstructs a full contour map of the range. That notebook is your set of MCMC samples. (Lambert 2018)

The crucial sleight of hand lives in step 3: because r is a ratio of two posterior densities, the intractable denominator p(\text{data}) — identical top and bottom — cancels exactly. MCMC needs only the unnormalised product likelihood × prior, which we can always evaluate. (The algebra is in the appendix.)

Watch a Metropolis sampler work

Below, a Metropolis sampler — knowing only the unnormalised target \theta^{14}(1-\theta)^6, never the normaliser — recovers the \text{Beta}(15,7) posterior from the figure above. The left panel is the trace (the walk’s path over time); the right is the histogram of its samples with the true posterior overlaid. The simulation never computed an integral.

Show the sampler code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from math import lgamma

rng = np.random.default_rng(20)

def log_target(t):                      # log of theta^14 (1-theta)^6, up to a constant
    if t <= 0 or t >= 1:
        return -np.inf
    return 14 * np.log(t) + 6 * np.log(1 - t)

n_steps, burn = 8000, 2000
samples = np.empty(n_steps)
t = 0.5
lt = log_target(t)
for i in range(n_steps):
    prop = t + rng.normal(0, 0.08)      # propose a nearby step
    lp = log_target(prop)
    if np.log(rng.random()) < lp - lt:  # accept uphill always, downhill with prob r
        t, lt = prop, lp
    samples[i] = t

post = samples[burn:]

def beta_pdf(theta, a, b):
    logB = lgamma(a) + lgamma(b) - lgamma(a + b)
    return np.exp((a - 1) * np.log(theta) + (b - 1) * np.log(1 - theta) - logB)

fig, (axT, axH) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 3.4),
                               gridspec_kw={"width_ratios": [1.5, 1]})
axT.plot(np.arange(burn), samples[:burn], color="#9aa0a6", lw=0.5)
axT.plot(np.arange(burn, n_steps), post, color="#1f3a5f", lw=0.5)
axT.axvline(burn, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.0)
axT.text(burn + 100, 0.92, "burn-in discarded", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axT.set_title("Trace: the 'fuzzy caterpillar'", fontsize=10)
axT.set_xlabel("step"); axT.set_ylabel("θ"); axT.set_ylim(0.3, 1.0)

xs = np.linspace(0.3, 1.0, 300)
axH.hist(post, bins=40, density=True, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.45, orientation="horizontal")
axH.plot(beta_pdf(xs, 15, 7), xs, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.0)
axH.set_title(f"Samples vs Beta(15,7)\nmean ≈ {post.mean():.3f}", fontsize=10)
axH.set_ylim(0.3, 1.0); axH.set_yticks([]); axH.set_xlabel("density")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The sampler’s mean \approx 0.68 recovers the analytic posterior mean 15/22 = 0.682 — and it did so by walking, never by integrating. Scale this idea up (with smarter proposals like Hamiltonian Monte Carlo) and it handles the 200-dimensional models that defeat pen and paper.

Summarising with samples

Once MCMC hands you thousands of draws, the Law of Large Numbers does the rest — sample summaries approximate posterior summaries. (Lambert 2018)

  • Posterior mean ≈ the average of the samples.
  • 95% credible interval ≈ the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the sorted samples — no formula, just counting. (Lambert 2018)
  • Posterior variance ≈ the variance of the samples.

The posterior you couldn’t write down becomes a list of numbers you can summarise any way you like.

Diagnostics: did the walk actually work?

Because MCMC starts somewhere arbitrary and explores blindly, you must check that it found the real posterior. (Lambert 2018)

  • Burn-in (warm-up). Early samples are contaminated by the random starting point, so discard them — often the first half of the chain — keeping only draws from the settled distribution. (Lambert 2018)
  • Trace plots. Plot the walk over time. A healthy chain is a “fuzzy caterpillar” — rapidly oscillating around a stable centre (the left panel above). A trace that drifts or sticks is a warning. (Lambert 2018)
  • Multiple chains — “Bob’s Bees.” Release one bee in a dark house and it may get stuck in a single room, fooling you into thinking it mapped the whole place. Release several bees from different starting points; if they all converge on the same regions, you can trust the map. (Lambert 2018)
  • \hat R (R-hat). The quantitative version of Bob’s Bees: it compares variation within chains to variation between chains. Convergence is signalled by \hat R \approx 1.0; values much above 1 mean the chains disagree and haven’t converged. (Lambert 2018)

The verdict: Bayesian vs frequentist

Lambert’s closing stance is refreshingly even-handed — the two schools are different paths to the same goal of learning from noisy data. (Lambert 2018)

Where Bayesian wins:

  • Natural uncertainty statements. “95% probability the effect is positive” is what policymakers actually want, and it is a direct Bayesian output. (Lambert 2018)
  • Prior information. Past studies and expert knowledge enter the model formally rather than informally. (Lambert 2018)
  • Small samples. Bayesian inference stays coherent when frequentist asymptotic (large-n) approximations break down. (Lambert 2018)
  • Flexibility. MCMC fits models of essentially arbitrary complexity — hierarchical, multilevel, custom — that have no closed-form frequentist solution. (Lambert 2018)

Where critics push back:

  • Subjectivity. Choosing a prior strikes some as unscientific — though Lambert’s retort stands: frequentist model and test choices are equally subjective, just less visible. (Lambert 2018)
  • Computational cost. MCMC can be slow and demanding for big models. (Lambert 2018)
TipLambert’s practical guidance: don’t be a zealot

Start simple — frequentist OLS or maximum likelihood to get an initial feel for the data — then build up to Bayesian models as complexity and the need for rigorous uncertainty quantification grow. And whatever you fit, treat posterior predictive checks (does data simulated from your fitted model resemble the real data?) as your “best friend.” (Lambert 2018)

That is the right note to end Part II on: the tools are not tribes to join but instruments to choose between, by the job in front of you.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Calling a confidence interval a credible interval. Only the Bayesian interval licenses “95% probability the parameter is in here.” Don’t smuggle that reading into a frequentist CI. (Lambert 2018)

  • Trusting un-diagnosed MCMC. A pretty number from a chain that never converged is fiction. Always check trace plots, run multiple chains, and confirm \hat R \approx 1. (Lambert 2018)

  • Forgetting burn-in. Including the arbitrary starting transient biases every summary. Discard the warm-up. (Lambert 2018)

  • Using a central interval on a skewed or bimodal posterior. It can include low-density regions and exclude high-density ones; reach for HPD when the shape isn’t symmetric. (Lambert 2018)

  • Becoming a zealot — either way. Bayesian isn’t automatically “better.” Match the method to the problem; start frequentist, escalate to Bayesian when the question demands it. (Lambert 2018)

Appendix · the full mathematics

Why the denominator cancels (the heart of MCMC). The Metropolis acceptance ratio for a proposed move \theta \to \theta' (with a symmetric proposal) is

r = \frac{p(\theta'\mid \text{data})}{p(\theta\mid \text{data})} = \frac{\,p(\text{data}\mid\theta')\,p(\theta')\,/\,p(\text{data})\,} {\,p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,/\,p(\text{data})\,} = \frac{p(\text{data}\mid\theta')\,p(\theta')}{p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)} .

The intractable p(\text{data}) appears identically in numerator and denominator and cancels — so the sampler needs only the unnormalised posterior (likelihood × prior). Accept if r \ge 1; otherwise accept with probability r (in practice compare \log r to \log u, u\sim\text{U}(0,1), for numerical stability — exactly the np.log(rng.random()) < lp - lt test in the code).

Why the chain targets the posterior. Metropolis satisfies detailed balance with respect to the posterior \pi: \pi(\theta)\,T(\theta\to\theta') = \pi(\theta')\,T(\theta'\to\theta), which makes \pi the chain’s stationary distribution. An ergodic chain then converges to \pi regardless of start, so its long-run samples are posterior draws.

Monte Carlo error. For M (roughly independent) posterior draws, a posterior mean estimate has standard error \approx \sigma_{\text{post}}/\sqrt{M} — the same 1/\sqrt{M} law as everywhere else, so more samples buy more precision (slowly). Autocorrelation in the chain inflates this via the effective sample size M_{\text{eff}} < M.

Equal-tailed vs HPD, formally. The equal-tailed interval is [\,q_{0.025},\,q_{0.975}\,] from the posterior quantile function. The 95% HPD is \{\theta : p(\theta\mid\text{data}) \ge c\} for the largest c with total mass 0.95 — the shortest such region, and the level-set that gives the pirate his disconnected digging zones when the posterior is multimodal.

```{python}
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from math import lgamma

def beta_pdf(theta, a, b):
    logB = lgamma(a) + lgamma(b) - lgamma(a + b)
    return np.exp((a - 1) * np.log(theta) + (b - 1) * np.log(1 - theta) - logB)

k, n = 14, 20
a_post, b_post = 1 + k, 1 + (n - k)          # flat prior Beta(1,1) -> Beta(15,7)
theta = np.linspace(1e-4, 1 - 1e-4, 2000)
post = beta_pdf(theta, a_post, b_post)

# 95% equal-tailed credible interval by numerical CDF
cdf = np.cumsum(post) * (theta[1] - theta[0])
cdf /= cdf[-1]
lo_c = theta[np.searchsorted(cdf, 0.025)]
hi_c = theta[np.searchsorted(cdf, 0.975)]

# Frequentist Wald 95% CI
phat = k / n
se = np.sqrt(phat * (1 - phat) / n)
lo_f, hi_f = phat - 1.96 * se, phat + 1.96 * se

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.9))
ax.plot(theta, post, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.2)
ax.fill_between(theta, post, where=(theta >= lo_c) & (theta <= hi_c),
                color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.25)
ax.errorbar(phat, -0.25, xerr=[[phat - lo_f], [hi_f - phat]], fmt="o",
            color="#c0392b", capsize=4, lw=2)
ax.text(hi_c + 0.01, beta_pdf(0.7, a_post, b_post) * 0.6,
        f"credible: [{lo_c:.2f}, {hi_c:.2f}]\n95% prob. θ in here",
        color="#1f3a5f", fontsize=8.5)
ax.text(hi_f + 0.01, -0.25, f"confidence: [{lo_f:.2f}, {hi_f:.2f}]\nprocedure covers 95%",
        color="#c0392b", fontsize=8.5, va="center")
ax.set_xlim(0.3, 1.0)
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_xlabel("θ — the coin's bias")
ax.set_title("Same data, two intervals, two meanings")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

The lesson of the picture: agreement on the *numbers* hides a chasm in *meaning*. The credible
interval is a claim about the parameter; the confidence interval is a claim about the procedure. In
this simple, flat-prior case they nearly match — but in small samples with real prior information,
or in complex models, they diverge, and only one of them answers the question you actually asked.

## Why we must compute

For all but textbook models, the posterior has **no closed form**, because the normalising
denominator $p(\text{data}) = \int p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,d\theta$ is an integral over
*every* parameter value — and in a multi-parameter model that is **high-dimensional**.
[@lambert_bayes] Lambert's recurring example: test scores across 100 schools can demand a
**200-dimensional integral**, which no computer can solve exactly or even approximate by brute force.
[@lambert_bayes] If we had to evaluate that denominator, applied Bayesian statistics would be dead in
the water.

## MCMC: sampling without the integral

The escape is **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)** — instead of *computing* the posterior, **draw
samples from it**, and let the cloud of samples stand in for the distribution. [@lambert_bayes] The
magic is that you can do this *without ever computing the denominator.*

MCMC is a **guided random walk**: each new sample depends on the current one, and the walk is rigged
so it lingers in regions of high posterior density. Over time the collected points *mirror the
posterior's shape.* [@lambert_bayes] The simplest version is the **Metropolis algorithm**:

1. **Propose** a step to a nearby location.
2. If the posterior density there is **higher** (uphill), **accept** — always move toward more
   probable values.
3. If it is **lower** (downhill), accept only **with probability $r$**, the ratio of the new density
   to the old. So the walk *usually* climbs but *occasionally* wanders down, which is what lets it
   explore the whole distribution instead of getting stuck on the peak. [@lambert_bayes]

::: {.callout-tip title="Lambert's explorer in the fog"}
Imagine mapping a mountain range permanently shrouded in fog. You can't photograph it from a
satellite (you can't see the whole posterior). But you *can* take local steps, recording your
altitude and coordinates at each. Take enough steps — preferring uphill but sometimes slipping down
— and your notebook reconstructs a full contour map of the range. That notebook is your set of MCMC
samples. [@lambert_bayes]
:::

The crucial sleight of hand lives in step 3: because $r$ is a **ratio** of two posterior densities,
the intractable denominator $p(\text{data})$ — identical top and bottom — **cancels exactly.** MCMC
needs only the *unnormalised* product likelihood × prior, which we can always evaluate. (The algebra
is in the appendix.)

### Watch a Metropolis sampler work

Below, a Metropolis sampler — knowing only the unnormalised target $\theta^{14}(1-\theta)^6$, never
the normaliser — recovers the $\text{Beta}(15,7)$ posterior from the figure above. The left panel is
the **trace** (the walk's path over time); the right is the **histogram** of its samples with the
true posterior overlaid. The simulation never computed an integral.

```{python}
#| label: fig-metropolis
#| fig-cap: "A Metropolis sampler recovering Beta(15,7) using only the unnormalised target. Left: the trace plot — a healthy 'fuzzy caterpillar' oscillating around a stable centre after a short burn-in (grey). Right: the histogram of post-burn-in samples matches the analytic posterior (blue curve) — the denominator was never computed."
#| code-fold: true
#| code-summary: "Show the sampler code"

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from math import lgamma

rng = np.random.default_rng(20)

def log_target(t):                      # log of theta^14 (1-theta)^6, up to a constant
    if t <= 0 or t >= 1:
        return -np.inf
    return 14 * np.log(t) + 6 * np.log(1 - t)

n_steps, burn = 8000, 2000
samples = np.empty(n_steps)
t = 0.5
lt = log_target(t)
for i in range(n_steps):
    prop = t + rng.normal(0, 0.08)      # propose a nearby step
    lp = log_target(prop)
    if np.log(rng.random()) < lp - lt:  # accept uphill always, downhill with prob r
        t, lt = prop, lp
    samples[i] = t

post = samples[burn:]

def beta_pdf(theta, a, b):
    logB = lgamma(a) + lgamma(b) - lgamma(a + b)
    return np.exp((a - 1) * np.log(theta) + (b - 1) * np.log(1 - theta) - logB)

fig, (axT, axH) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(9, 3.4),
                               gridspec_kw={"width_ratios": [1.5, 1]})
axT.plot(np.arange(burn), samples[:burn], color="#9aa0a6", lw=0.5)
axT.plot(np.arange(burn, n_steps), post, color="#1f3a5f", lw=0.5)
axT.axvline(burn, color="#c0392b", ls="--", lw=1.0)
axT.text(burn + 100, 0.92, "burn-in discarded", color="#c0392b", fontsize=8)
axT.set_title("Trace: the 'fuzzy caterpillar'", fontsize=10)
axT.set_xlabel("step"); axT.set_ylabel("θ"); axT.set_ylim(0.3, 1.0)

xs = np.linspace(0.3, 1.0, 300)
axH.hist(post, bins=40, density=True, color="#1f3a5f", alpha=0.45, orientation="horizontal")
axH.plot(beta_pdf(xs, 15, 7), xs, color="#1f3a5f", lw=2.0)
axH.set_title(f"Samples vs Beta(15,7)\nmean ≈ {post.mean():.3f}", fontsize=10)
axH.set_ylim(0.3, 1.0); axH.set_yticks([]); axH.set_xlabel("density")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

The sampler's mean $\approx 0.68$ recovers the analytic posterior mean $15/22 = 0.682$ — and it did
so by *walking*, never by integrating. Scale this idea up (with smarter proposals like Hamiltonian
Monte Carlo) and it handles the 200-dimensional models that defeat pen and paper.

## Summarising with samples

Once MCMC hands you thousands of draws, the [Law of Large Numbers](../01-foundations/law-of-large-numbers.qmd)
does the rest — sample summaries approximate posterior summaries. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Posterior mean** ≈ the average of the samples.
- **95% credible interval** ≈ the 2.5th and 97.5th **percentiles of the sorted samples** — no
  formula, just counting. [@lambert_bayes]
- **Posterior variance** ≈ the variance of the samples.

The posterior you couldn't write down becomes a list of numbers you can summarise any way you like.

## Diagnostics: did the walk actually work?

Because MCMC starts somewhere arbitrary and explores blindly, you must *check* that it found the real
posterior. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Burn-in (warm-up).** Early samples are contaminated by the random starting point, so discard them
  — often the first half of the chain — keeping only draws from the settled distribution.
  [@lambert_bayes]
- **Trace plots.** Plot the walk over time. A healthy chain is a **"fuzzy caterpillar"** — rapidly
  oscillating around a stable centre (the left panel above). A trace that drifts or sticks is a
  warning. [@lambert_bayes]
- **Multiple chains — "Bob's Bees."** Release *one* bee in a dark house and it may get stuck in a
  single room, fooling you into thinking it mapped the whole place. Release **several bees from
  different starting points**; if they all converge on the same regions, you can trust the map.
  [@lambert_bayes]
- **$\hat R$ (R-hat).** The quantitative version of Bob's Bees: it compares variation *within* chains
  to variation *between* chains. **Convergence is signalled by $\hat R \approx 1.0$;** values much
  above 1 mean the chains disagree and haven't converged. [@lambert_bayes]

## The verdict: Bayesian vs frequentist

Lambert's closing stance is refreshingly even-handed — the two schools are **different paths to the
same goal** of learning from noisy data. [@lambert_bayes]

**Where Bayesian wins:**

- **Natural uncertainty statements.** "95% probability the effect is positive" is what policymakers
  actually want, and it is a *direct* Bayesian output. [@lambert_bayes]
- **Prior information.** Past studies and expert knowledge enter the model *formally* rather than
  informally. [@lambert_bayes]
- **Small samples.** Bayesian inference stays coherent when frequentist *asymptotic* (large-$n$)
  approximations break down. [@lambert_bayes]
- **Flexibility.** MCMC fits models of essentially arbitrary complexity — hierarchical, multilevel,
  custom — that have no closed-form frequentist solution. [@lambert_bayes]

**Where critics push back:**

- **Subjectivity.** Choosing a prior strikes some as unscientific — though Lambert's retort stands:
  frequentist model and test choices are *equally* subjective, just less visible. [@lambert_bayes]
- **Computational cost.** MCMC can be slow and demanding for big models. [@lambert_bayes]

::: {.callout-tip title="Lambert's practical guidance: don't be a zealot"}
Start simple — frequentist **OLS or maximum likelihood** to get an initial feel for the data — then
**build up** to Bayesian models as complexity and the need for rigorous uncertainty quantification
grow. And whatever you fit, treat **posterior predictive checks** (does data simulated from your
fitted model resemble the real data?) as your **"best friend."** [@lambert_bayes]
:::

That is the right note to end Part II on: the tools are not tribes to join but instruments to choose
between, by the job in front of you.

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong in practice"}

- **Calling a confidence interval a credible interval.** Only the Bayesian interval licenses "95%
  probability the parameter is in here." Don't smuggle that reading into a frequentist CI.
  [@lambert_bayes]

- **Trusting un-diagnosed MCMC.** A pretty number from a chain that never converged is fiction.
  Always check trace plots, run multiple chains, and confirm $\hat R \approx 1$. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Forgetting burn-in.** Including the arbitrary starting transient biases every summary. Discard
  the warm-up. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Using a central interval on a skewed or bimodal posterior.** It can include low-density regions
  and exclude high-density ones; reach for HPD when the shape isn't symmetric. [@lambert_bayes]

- **Becoming a zealot — either way.** Bayesian isn't automatically "better." Match the method to the
  problem; start frequentist, escalate to Bayesian when the question demands it. [@lambert_bayes]

:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Why the denominator cancels (the heart of MCMC).** The Metropolis acceptance ratio for a proposed
move $\theta \to \theta'$ (with a symmetric proposal) is

$$
r = \frac{p(\theta'\mid \text{data})}{p(\theta\mid \text{data})}
= \frac{\,p(\text{data}\mid\theta')\,p(\theta')\,/\,p(\text{data})\,}
       {\,p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,/\,p(\text{data})\,}
= \frac{p(\text{data}\mid\theta')\,p(\theta')}{p(\text{data}\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)} .
$$

The intractable $p(\text{data})$ appears identically in numerator and denominator and **cancels** —
so the sampler needs only the *unnormalised* posterior (likelihood × prior). Accept if $r \ge 1$;
otherwise accept with probability $r$ (in practice compare $\log r$ to $\log u$, $u\sim\text{U}(0,1)$,
for numerical stability — exactly the `np.log(rng.random()) < lp - lt` test in the code).

**Why the chain targets the posterior.** Metropolis satisfies *detailed balance* with respect to the
posterior $\pi$: $\pi(\theta)\,T(\theta\to\theta') = \pi(\theta')\,T(\theta'\to\theta)$, which makes
$\pi$ the chain's **stationary distribution.** An ergodic chain then converges to $\pi$ regardless of
start, so its long-run samples *are* posterior draws.

**Monte Carlo error.** For $M$ (roughly independent) posterior draws, a posterior mean estimate has
standard error $\approx \sigma_{\text{post}}/\sqrt{M}$ — the same $1/\sqrt{M}$ law as everywhere
else, so more samples buy more precision (slowly). Autocorrelation in the chain inflates this via the
*effective sample size* $M_{\text{eff}} < M$.

**Equal-tailed vs HPD, formally.** The equal-tailed interval is $[\,q_{0.025},\,q_{0.975}\,]$ from
the posterior quantile function. The 95% HPD is $\{\theta : p(\theta\mid\text{data}) \ge c\}$ for the
largest $c$ with total mass $0.95$ — the shortest such region, and the level-set that gives the
pirate his disconnected digging zones when the posterior is multimodal.

:::
