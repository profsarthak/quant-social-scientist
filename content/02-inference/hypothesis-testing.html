Hypothesis Testing & p-values

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

The most used — and most misread — procedure in science: a careful proof by contradiction, and exactly what that little p means.

A hypothesis test asks one disciplined question: if nothing were going on, how surprising would my data be? The p-value measures that surprise. It is a genuinely useful number wrapped in a thick fog of misunderstanding — and clearing the fog is most of what it takes to use it well.

Why it matters

Null hypothesis significance testing (NHST) is the most widely used statistical procedure in all of science. (Bruce et al. 2020) It is also the most widely abused, and the gap between those two facts is the engine of the replication crisis. The trouble is rarely the arithmetic; it is that the p-value answers a narrow, conditional question while researchers crave a broad, unconditional one — and the slippage between the two has corrupted whole literatures.

So this entry does two things at once: it builds the testing machinery honestly, and it polices the single most consequential misreading in quantitative research. Get the definition of a p-value exactly right and most of the downstream sins become visible.

The logic: proof by contradiction

A hypothesis test is an argument by contradiction. (Bruce et al. 2020) You set up two rival claims:

  • The null hypothesis H_0 — the “nothing special is happening” baseline: no effect, no difference, no relationship. It exists to be falsified. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)
  • The alternative hypothesis H_1 — the research hypothesis, the effect you suspect is real.

You then assume the null is true and ask whether the data you actually collected is wildly inconsistent with that assumption. If the data would be bizarrely unlikely under “no effect,” you reject the null and take that as evidence for the alternative. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) The asymmetry is deliberate and important: you never prove the null, you only fail to reject it — exactly as a court returns “not guilty,” not “innocent.”

The test statistic: a surprise meter

How do you measure “inconsistent with the null”? You compress the data into a single test statistic — one number summarising how far your result sits from what the null predicts. (Bruce et al. 2020) Think of it as a surprise meter: the larger its magnitude, the more your data departs from the null’s expectation, and the more the null looks like the wrong model for your world. (Bruce et al. 2020) A test statistic of 0.3 says “business as usual”; a 4.0 says “something is off.”

The p-value, defined exactly

Here is the definition, and every word earns its place:

The p-value is the probability of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true. (Bruce et al. 2020)

It is the tail area — the probability that pure chance, with no real effect operating, would produce a result as striking as yours or more so. A small p-value means your data sits far out in the tail where the null rarely ventures, so the null becomes hard to believe.

The p-value as an area

That “tail area” is literal. Below is the sampling distribution of the test statistic under the null — the bell of results you’d see if nothing were going on. The observed statistic lands at \pm 2.1, and the p-value is simply the shaded area beyond it in both tails.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statistics import NormalDist

nd = NormalDist()
z_obs = 2.1
x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 600)
y = np.array([nd.pdf(v) for v in x])
p_two = 2 * (1 - nd.cdf(z_obs))   # two-tailed p-value ≈ 0.0357

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 3.6))
ax.plot(x, y, color="#1f3a5f", lw=1.8)
ax.fill_between(x, y, where=(x >=  z_obs), color="#c0392b", alpha=0.75)
ax.fill_between(x, y, where=(x <= -z_obs), color="#c0392b", alpha=0.75)
ax.axvline( z_obs, color="#c0392b", lw=1.0, ls="--")
ax.axvline(-z_obs, color="#c0392b", lw=1.0, ls="--")
ax.annotate(f"observed = +2.1", xy=(z_obs, nd.pdf(z_obs)), xytext=(2.4, 0.22),
            fontsize=9, color="#c0392b",
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#c0392b"))
ax.set_title(f"p-value = shaded tail area ≈ {p_two:.3f}")
ax.set_xlabel("Test statistic (standardized), under the null")
ax.set_yticks([])
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

If that shaded area is smaller than your pre-set threshold, you reject the null. The picture also makes the definition’s conditional vivid: the entire bell is drawn assuming the null is true. The p-value lives inside that assumption — which is exactly why it cannot, by itself, tell you whether the assumption holds.

What the p-value is not

This is the most important list in Part II. The p-value is: (Reinhart 2015)

  • NOT the probability that the null is true. It is computed assuming the null; it cannot turn around and report the null’s probability. (\Pr(\text{data}\mid H_0) is not \Pr(H_0\mid\text{data}) — flipping them is the transposed-conditional error, the same blunder as the base-rate fallacy.) (Reinhart 2015)
  • NOT the probability your result was a fluke. Same transposition: it gives the odds of the data if the null holds, not the odds favouring the null given the data. (Reinhart 2015)
  • NOT a measure of effect size. A microscopic, meaningless effect can yield p<0.001 if n is huge. Small p \ne big effect. (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • NOT the probability of replication, and a non-significant p>0.05 does NOT mean “no effect” — it may just mean the study was underpowered to detect one. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. (Reinhart 2015)

The decision rule: α and the two errors

To turn the continuous p-value into a decision, you fix a threshold in advance — the significance level \alpha, conventionally 0.05 in social science. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) \alpha is a pre-committed error budget: it is the rate at which you are willing to reject a true null and cry wolf. Choosing it before seeing the data is what keeps the procedure honest.

Because the verdict is probabilistic, two mistakes are possible: (Martin and Bridgmon 2012)

H_0 true H_0 false
Reject H_0 Type I error (false positive), rate \alpha Correct — power = 1-\beta
Fail to reject Correct Type II error (false negative), rate \beta

A Type I error cries wolf — declaring a drug works when it doesn’t. A Type II error misses a real wolf — failing to notice a drug that genuinely works. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) They trade off: tightening \alpha to 0.01 cuts false positives but raises the false-negative rate \beta, costing you power — the subject of the next entry. (Reinhart 2015)

One-tailed vs two-tailed

A two-tailed test asks “is there an effect in either direction?” and splits the rejection region across both tails (the case drawn above). A one-tailed test stakes everything on a direction chosen in advance (“B beats A”) and puts the whole rejection region in one tail, making it easier to reach significance there. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) The catch is non-negotiable: you must choose the direction before seeing the data. Switching to a one-tailed test after peeking, because it drags you under p<0.05, is a textbook form of p-hacking. (Reinhart 2015)

The workhorses: z- and t-tests

The z-test applies when the population standard deviation \sigma is known — rare outside standardized-test norming. (Bruce et al. 2020)

The t-test is what you actually use: \sigma is unknown, so you estimate it with the sample s, and the statistic follows Student’s t-distribution with its uncertainty-absorbing fat tails. (Bruce et al. 2020) The one-sample form is

t = \frac{\bar y - \mu_0}{\operatorname{se}(\bar y)} = \frac{\bar y - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt n},

the observed gap measured in standard errors. It comes in three flavours: (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • One-sample — compare a sample mean to a fixed benchmark. Does this class’s mean score differ from the district average of 105?
  • Two-sample (independent) — compare the means of two separate groups, with t = \frac{\bar X_1 - \bar X_2}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}}. Did the treatment group outscore the control group?
  • Paired (dependent) — compare the same units at two times, testing the mean of the differences. Did participants’ weight change from pre-test to post-test? Pairing strips out person-to-person variation and is far more powerful when it applies.

Statistical vs practical significance

The deepest trap in the whole apparatus: a result can be statistically significant yet practically trivial. (Bruce et al. 2020) Statistical significance is a binary yes/no about whether p<\alpha; practical (substantive) significance is about the magnitude and real-world importance of the effect. (Martin and Bridgmon 2012) Wooldridge’s example: a study of baseball salaries might find a statistically significant effect of “runs per year” (p<0.05), but if each extra run is worth only $10, the effect is economically meaningless. (Wooldridge 2019) With a large enough n, everything becomes significant; the question that matters is how big, not whether. Always report and interpret the effect size, not just the verdict.

NoteThe effect size is the scientific question (Kaplan)

Daniel Kaplan makes this the organizing principle of Lessons in Statistical Thinking: the job of statistics is to estimate how much — the effect size — because that magnitude is what actually informs a decision or a policy, whereas a significance verdict does not. (Kaplan 2024) He is blunt that the test was never the scientific question, quoting the biostatistician Frank Harrell: the “complacency … in statistics education has resulted in [the mis-]belief that null hypothesis significance testing ever answered the scientific question.” (Kaplan 2024) Kaplan’s reform is the one this entry keeps arriving at — report a magnitude with its uncertainty and ask what level of belief or plausibility it warrants, rather than collapsing the evidence into a binary “significant / not.” This reframes statistics as accounting for variation in service of judgement, not a verdict machine. (Kaplan 2024)

The duality with confidence intervals

Testing and interval estimation are the same logic in two costumes. (Wooldridge 2019) The bridge:

If the null value falls outside the 95% confidence interval, you reject H_0 at the 5% level.

So the scrap-rate interval [-2.28,\,-0.02] — which excludes zero — is a rejection of “no effect” at 5%. (Wooldridge 2019) The interval is strictly more informative than the test: it reports the whole range of effect sizes you can’t reject, not just a pass/fail. This is why a growing chorus urges reporting intervals and effect sizes over bare p-values — a reform we take up in the replication-crisis entry.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • Reading p as “the probability the null is true” (or “the probability it’s a fluke”). The cardinal error — a transposed conditional. The p-value lives entirely inside the assumption that the null holds. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Reading a big p-value as proof of no effect. p>0.05 can mean “no effect” or “too little data to see one.” Absence of evidence isn’t evidence of absence — check the power. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Confusing statistical with practical significance. A trivially small effect is “significant” at large n; a large, important effect can be “non-significant” at small n. The verdict is not the magnitude. (Bruce et al. 2020; Wooldridge 2019)

  • Choosing one- vs two-tailed after seeing the data, or nudging \alpha to fit the result. Both are p-hacking; both inflate false positives far above the nominal 5%. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Treating p = 0.049 and p = 0.051 as categorically different. The 0.05 line is a convention, not a law of nature. A p-value is continuous evidence, not a cliff edge. (Reinhart 2015)

Appendix · the full mathematics

The one-sample t-test. Under H_0: \mu = \mu_0, with X_i i.i.d. and \sigma estimated by s, the statistic

T = \frac{\bar X - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}

follows a t-distribution with n-1 degrees of freedom (exactly, if the X_i are Normal; approximately otherwise, by the CLT). The two-sided p-value is p = 2\,\Pr(t_{n-1} \ge |T_\text{obs}|).

Two-sample (Welch) form. For independent groups with possibly unequal variances,

T = \frac{\bar X_1 - \bar X_2}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}},

with Welch–Satterthwaite degrees of freedom. The denominator is the standard error of the difference — which is why two overlapping single-group intervals can still yield a significant T: the SE of a difference is not the sum of the two separate SEs (see the overlap fallacy).

Paired form. Reduce to one sample by differencing: let D_i = X_i^{\text{post}} - X_i^{\text{pre}} and run a one-sample test of \bar D against 0. Because \operatorname{Var}(D) = \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\operatorname{Cov}(X^{\text{pre}}, X^{\text{post}}) and the within-person correlation is usually positive, pairing shrinks the standard error — the source of its extra power.

Why the CI–test duality holds. The 95% interval is exactly the set of null values \mu_0 that would not be rejected at the 5% level: |{\bar X - \mu_0}|/(s/\sqrt n) \le t_{n-1,0.975} rearranges to \mu_0 \in [\bar X \pm t_{n-1,0.975}\, s/\sqrt n]. Reject \iff \mu_0 outside the interval.

```{python}
            arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", color="#c0392b"))
ax.set_title(f"p-value = shaded tail area ≈ {p_two:.3f}")
ax.set_xlabel("Test statistic (standardized), under the null")
ax.set_yticks([])
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

If that shaded area is smaller than your pre-set threshold, you reject the null. The picture also
makes the definition's conditional vivid: the *entire bell* is drawn **assuming the null is true**.
The p-value lives inside that assumption — which is exactly why it cannot, by itself, tell you
whether the assumption holds.

### What the p-value is **not**

This is the most important list in Part II. The p-value is: [@reinhart_swrong]

- **NOT the probability that the null is true.** It is computed *assuming* the null; it cannot turn
  around and report the null's probability. ($\Pr(\text{data}\mid H_0)$ is not
  $\Pr(H_0\mid\text{data})$ — flipping them is the **transposed-conditional** error, the same
  blunder as the [base-rate fallacy](../01-foundations/bayes-theorem.qmd).) [@reinhart_swrong]
- **NOT the probability your result was a fluke.** Same transposition: it gives the odds of the
  *data* if the null holds, not the odds *favouring* the null given the data. [@reinhart_swrong]
- **NOT a measure of effect size.** A microscopic, meaningless effect can yield $p<0.001$ if $n$ is
  huge. Small p $\ne$ big effect. [@bruce_practical]
- **NOT the probability of replication**, and a non-significant $p>0.05$ does **NOT** mean "no
  effect" — it may just mean the study was [underpowered](statistical-power.qmd) to detect one.
  *Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.* [@reinhart_swrong]

## The decision rule: α and the two errors

To turn the continuous p-value into a decision, you fix a threshold in advance — the **significance
level $\alpha$**, conventionally $0.05$ in social science. [@martin_bridgmon] $\alpha$ is a
**pre-committed error budget**: it is the rate at which you are willing to reject a *true* null and
cry wolf. Choosing it *before* seeing the data is what keeps the procedure honest.

Because the verdict is probabilistic, two mistakes are possible: [@martin_bridgmon]

|  | $H_0$ true | $H_0$ false |
|---|---|---|
| **Reject $H_0$** | **Type I error** (false positive), rate $\alpha$ | Correct — **power** $= 1-\beta$ |
| **Fail to reject** | Correct | **Type II error** (false negative), rate $\beta$ |

A **Type I error** cries wolf — declaring a drug works when it doesn't. A **Type II error** misses a
real wolf — failing to notice a drug that genuinely works. [@martin_bridgmon] They trade off:
tightening $\alpha$ to $0.01$ cuts false positives but *raises* the false-negative rate $\beta$,
costing you [power](statistical-power.qmd) — the subject of the next entry. [@reinhart_swrong]

## One-tailed vs two-tailed

A **two-tailed** test asks "is there an effect *in either direction*?" and splits the rejection
region across both tails (the case drawn above). A **one-tailed** test stakes everything on a
*direction* chosen in advance ("B beats A") and puts the whole rejection region in one tail, making
it easier to reach significance there. [@martin_bridgmon] The catch is non-negotiable: **you must
choose the direction before seeing the data.** Switching to a one-tailed test *after* peeking,
because it drags you under $p<0.05$, is a textbook form of [p-hacking](replication-crisis.qmd).
[@reinhart_swrong]

## The workhorses: z- and t-tests

**The z-test** applies when the population standard deviation $\sigma$ is *known* — rare outside
standardized-test norming. [@bruce_practical]

**The t-test** is what you actually use: $\sigma$ is unknown, so you estimate it with the sample
$s$, and the statistic follows Student's [t-distribution](confidence-intervals.qmd) with its
uncertainty-absorbing fat tails. [@bruce_practical] The one-sample form is

$$
t = \frac{\bar y - \mu_0}{\operatorname{se}(\bar y)} = \frac{\bar y - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt n},
$$

the observed gap measured in standard errors. It comes in three flavours: [@bruce_practical]

- **One-sample** — compare a sample mean to a fixed benchmark. *Does this class's mean score differ
  from the district average of 105?*
- **Two-sample (independent)** — compare the means of two separate groups, with
  $$ t = \frac{\bar X_1 - \bar X_2}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}}. $$
  *Did the treatment group outscore the control group?*
- **Paired (dependent)** — compare the *same* units at two times, testing the mean of the
  differences. *Did participants' weight change from pre-test to post-test?* Pairing strips out
  person-to-person variation and is far more powerful when it applies.

## Statistical vs practical significance

The deepest trap in the whole apparatus: a result can be **statistically** significant yet
**practically** trivial. [@bruce_practical] Statistical significance is a binary yes/no about
whether $p<\alpha$; **practical (substantive) significance** is about the *magnitude* and *real-world
importance* of the effect. [@martin_bridgmon] Wooldridge's example: a study of baseball salaries
might find a statistically significant effect of "runs per year" ($p<0.05$), but if each extra run
is worth only **\$10**, the effect is economically meaningless. [@wooldridge_intro] With a large
enough $n$, *everything* becomes significant; the question that matters is *how big*, not *whether*.
Always report and interpret the effect size, not just the verdict.

::: {.callout-note title="The effect size *is* the scientific question (Kaplan)"}
Daniel Kaplan makes this the organizing principle of *Lessons in Statistical Thinking*: the job of
statistics is to estimate **how much** — the **effect size** — because that magnitude is what actually
informs a decision or a policy, whereas a significance verdict does not. [@kaplan_lessons] He is blunt
that the test was never the scientific question, quoting the biostatistician **Frank Harrell**: the
*"complacency … in statistics education has resulted in [the mis-]belief that null hypothesis significance
testing ever answered the scientific question."* [@kaplan_lessons] Kaplan's reform is the one this entry
keeps arriving at — report a **magnitude with its uncertainty** and ask what *level of belief or
plausibility* it warrants, rather than collapsing the evidence into a binary "significant / not." This
reframes statistics as **accounting for variation** in service of judgement, not a verdict machine.
[@kaplan_lessons]
:::

## The duality with confidence intervals

Testing and [interval estimation](confidence-intervals.qmd) are the same logic in two costumes.
[@wooldridge_intro] The bridge:

> If the null value falls **outside** the 95% confidence interval, you reject $H_0$ at the 5% level.

So the scrap-rate interval $[-2.28,\,-0.02]$ — which excludes zero — *is* a rejection of "no effect"
at 5%. [@wooldridge_intro] The interval is strictly more informative than the test: it reports the
*whole range* of effect sizes you can't reject, not just a pass/fail. This is why a growing chorus
urges **reporting intervals and effect sizes over bare p-values** — a reform we take up in the
[replication-crisis entry](replication-crisis.qmd).

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong in practice"}

- **Reading $p$ as "the probability the null is true" (or "the probability it's a fluke").** The
  cardinal error — a transposed conditional. The p-value lives entirely *inside* the assumption that
  the null holds. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Reading a big p-value as proof of no effect.** $p>0.05$ can mean "no effect" *or* "too little
  data to see one." Absence of evidence isn't evidence of absence — check the
  [power](statistical-power.qmd). [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Confusing statistical with practical significance.** A trivially small effect is "significant"
  at large $n$; a large, important effect can be "non-significant" at small $n$. The verdict is not
  the magnitude. [@bruce_practical; @wooldridge_intro]

- **Choosing one- vs two-tailed *after* seeing the data**, or nudging $\alpha$ to fit the result.
  Both are p-hacking; both inflate false positives far above the nominal 5%. [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Treating $p = 0.049$ and $p = 0.051$ as categorically different.** The 0.05 line is a
  convention, not a law of nature. A p-value is continuous evidence, not a cliff edge. [@reinhart_swrong]

:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**The one-sample t-test.** Under $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$, with $X_i$ i.i.d. and $\sigma$ estimated by
$s$, the statistic

$$
T = \frac{\bar X - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}
$$

follows a $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom (exactly, if the $X_i$ are Normal;
approximately otherwise, by the [CLT](sampling-distributions-clt.qmd)). The two-sided p-value is
$p = 2\,\Pr(t_{n-1} \ge |T_\text{obs}|)$.

**Two-sample (Welch) form.** For independent groups with possibly unequal variances,

$$
T = \frac{\bar X_1 - \bar X_2}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}},
$$

with Welch–Satterthwaite degrees of freedom. The denominator is the standard error *of the
difference* — which is why two overlapping single-group intervals can still yield a significant
$T$: the SE of a difference is **not** the sum of the two separate SEs (see the
[overlap fallacy](confidence-intervals.qmd)).

**Paired form.** Reduce to one sample by differencing: let $D_i = X_i^{\text{post}} -
X_i^{\text{pre}}$ and run a one-sample test of $\bar D$ against 0. Because $\operatorname{Var}(D) =
\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2\operatorname{Cov}(X^{\text{pre}}, X^{\text{post}})$ and the within-person
correlation is usually positive, pairing *shrinks* the standard error — the source of its extra
power.

**Why the CI–test duality holds.** The 95% interval is exactly the set of null values $\mu_0$ that
would *not* be rejected at the 5% level: $|{\bar X - \mu_0}|/(s/\sqrt n) \le t_{n-1,0.975}$
rearranges to $\mu_0 \in [\bar X \pm t_{n-1,0.975}\, s/\sqrt n]$. Reject $\iff$ $\mu_0$ outside the
interval.

:::
