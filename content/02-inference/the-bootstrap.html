The Bootstrap

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Pull yourself up by your bootstraps: resample your own data to get a standard error or confidence interval for any statistic — no formula required.

Classical inference needs a formula for the sampling distribution — and for anything past a mean, those formulas get ugly or don’t exist. The bootstrap sidesteps the algebra entirely: treat your sample as a stand-in for the population, resample it with replacement thousands of times, recompute your statistic each time, and read the standard error and confidence interval straight off the spread of those replicates. It works for the median, a correlation, a complicated machine-learning estimator — any statistic — which is why it’s an indispensable, one-size-fits-all tool.

Why it matters

For a mean, the CLT hands you a standard error. For a median, a correlation, a ratio, or a tangled multi-step estimator, the analytic formula is hard or nonexistent. (Bruce et al. 2020) The bootstrap is the “one size fits all” answer: a computer-intensive method that replaces a derivation with brute-force resampling, giving honest standard errors and confidence intervals for statistics you could never tackle by hand. (Bruce et al. 2020)

The core idea: resample with replacement

You only have one sample — so make it work harder. Treat the observed sample of size n as a “hypothetical population,” and draw a new sample of size n from it, with replacement — a bootstrap sample. (Bruce et al. 2020) Recompute your statistic on that bootstrap sample. Repeat B times (typically 1,000 or 10,000), and the collection of bootstrap replicates approximates the statistic’s sampling distribution. (Bruce et al. 2020) Bruce & Bruce’s image: “imagine replicating the original sample thousands or millions of times so that you have a hypothetical population that embodies all the knowledge from your original sample.” (Bruce et al. 2020)

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
sample = rng.exponential(scale=4.0, size=80)        # one skewed sample
B = 5000
boot_medians = np.array([np.median(rng.choice(sample, size=len(sample), replace=True))
                         for _ in range(B)])

se_boot = boot_medians.std()
lo, hi = np.percentile(boot_medians, [2.5, 97.5])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.2, 4.0))
ax.hist(boot_medians, bins=40, color="#161613", alpha=0.85, edgecolor="white")
ax.axvline(np.median(sample), color="#5e5e54", lw=2.2, label=f"sample median = {np.median(sample):.2f}")
ax.axvline(lo, color="#e8531d", ls="--", lw=1.8)
ax.axvline(hi, color="#e8531d", ls="--", lw=1.8, label=f"95% percentile CI [{lo:.2f}, {hi:.2f}]")
ax.text(0.02, 0.9, f"bootstrap SE = {se_boot:.2f}", transform=ax.transAxes, fontsize=10,
        color="#161613", fontweight="bold")
ax.set_xlabel("median of a bootstrap resample"); ax.set_ylabel("frequency")
ax.set_title("5,000 bootstrap medians ≈ the sampling distribution")
ax.legend(fontsize=8.5, frameon=False)
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The plug-in principle

Why is resampling your data legitimate? The plug-in principle: in the absence of the true population distribution, use the empirical distribution of the sample — which puts probability 1/n on each observed value — as a stand-in. (Bruce et al. 2020) For large n this empirical distribution is “an arbitrarily good approximation to the actual distribution,” (Ashley 2012) and the intuition is Dayal’s: “if the sample is like the population, then many samples from the sample are representative of many samples from the population.” (Bruce et al. 2020) Resampling the sample mimics resampling the population.

Standard errors and confidence intervals

Two outputs fall straight out of the replicates: (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Bootstrap standard error — just the standard deviation of the bootstrap replicates. It “sums up the variability in the sampling distribution,” and shines for complex estimators with no analytic SE. (Bruce et al. 2020)
  • Percentile confidence interval — the most intuitive CI: sort the B replicates and trim 2.5% from each end; the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles are your 95% interval. (Bruce et al. 2020) No Normality assumed — if the bootstrap distribution is skewed, the interval is too.
  • Bias-corrected (BCa) — because the bootstrap can reveal asymmetry and bias the percentile method ignores, the BCa interval adjusts for both, at the cost of more replicates ($$10,000) to stabilise. (Wooldridge 2019; James et al. 2021)

When it works — and when it fails

The bootstrap is powerful but “not a panacea.” (Ashley 2012) Know its boundaries: (Ashley 2012)

  • Works for “standard” statistics — means, variances, regression coefficients, correlations.
  • Small samples. It “does not compensate for a small sample size.” If n is tiny (say 10), the empirical distribution is a poor stand-in for the population and the bootstrap fails. (Ashley 2012)
  • Sample extremes. It is “unlikely to be satisfactory” for maxima, minima, or the range — statistics driven by the tails, which a resample can’t reproduce. (Ashley 2012)
  • Dependent data. A naive bootstrap “fails to replicate the dependence” in time-series or clustered data. The fix is the block bootstrap — resample whole blocks (or clusters) to preserve the internal structure. (Ashley 2012)

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong

  • Bootstrapping a tiny sample. Garbage in, garbage out: the method can’t manufacture information the sample doesn’t contain. (Ashley 2012)

  • Bootstrapping the max/min/range. Tail-driven statistics break the plug-in approximation; use extreme-value theory instead. (Ashley 2012)

  • Ignoring dependence. Time-series or clustered data need a block bootstrap; the naive version understates uncertainty. (Ashley 2012)

  • Too few replicates for a CI. A stable percentile interval (and especially BCa) needs thousands of resamples, not a few hundred. (Wooldridge 2019)
