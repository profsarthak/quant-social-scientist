Confidence Intervals

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

A point estimate says where; a confidence interval says how sure — and almost everyone reads it wrong.

A single estimate is a best guess with no error bars. A confidence interval wraps that guess in a range of plausible values — and carries a guarantee, though a subtler one than nearly everyone believes. The “95%” is a property of the procedure, not of the interval sitting in front of you.

Why it matters

“Support is 52%.” “The effect was 0.3 points.” A point estimate delivered alone is quietly dishonest: it tells you the best guess but says nothing about how close that guess is likely to be to the truth. (Bruce et al. 2020) Two studies can both report “52%” — one a careful national survey, one a classroom poll of thirty — and the bare number hides the chasm between them.

The confidence interval closes that gap. It is the natural sequel to estimation: take the point estimate and attach to it the standard error the sampling distribution gives you, and you get not a guess but a range of defensible guesses. Confidence intervals are also, not coincidentally, the single most misinterpreted object in quantitative research — so getting their logic exactly right is worth real effort.

What a confidence interval is

The recipe is short:

\text{point estimate} \;\pm\; \text{margin of error}, \qquad \text{margin of error} = (\text{critical value}) \times (\text{standard error}).

The standard error measures how much the estimate bounces from sample to sample; the critical value says how many standard errors you must reach out to capture your chosen share of that bounce. For a 95% interval the critical value is about 2 (more precisely 1.96 for the Normal). (Bruce et al. 2020) So a 95% interval reaches out roughly two standard errors on each side of the estimate — wide enough that, across repeated studies, the net it casts lands over the true value 95% of the time.

The interpretation almost everyone gets wrong

This is the crux, and it is worth slowing down. (Reinhart 2015)

ImportantThe two readings

Correct (frequentist). “If I repeated this whole sampling procedure many times, computing a fresh 95% interval each time, about 95% of those intervals would contain the true parameter.” The procedure has a 95% hit rate. (Bruce et al. 2020; Grinstead and Snell 1997)

Wrong (and seductive). “There is a 95% probability that the true value lies in this interval, [5.6\%,\, 9.4\%].” (Reinhart 2015)

Why is the second wrong? Because in the frequentist world the parameter is a fixed constant, not a random variable. (Reinhart 2015) Once you have computed your interval, [5.6\%, 9.4\%] are just two numbers, and the truth is a third fixed number. The truth is either between them or it isn’t — there is no probability left to assign to this particular, already-decided case. The randomness lived in the sampling, which is now over. The 95% describes the long-run reliability of the net, not the odds for the one cast you happen to be holding.

(This is precisely the door through which Bayesian inference will later walk: if you want to say “95% probability the parameter is in here,” you need a credible interval, which requires treating the parameter as random and supplying a prior. The two intervals answer different questions — a theme we develop fully in the Bayesian sub-chapter.)

Watch the procedure work

The cleanest way to feel the correct interpretation is to do the repeating that the definition imagines. Below, the true population proportion is fixed at 0.54 (an election too close to call). We run 100 independent polls of n = 1{,}000, build a 95% interval from each, and stack them up. The truth doesn’t move — the intervals do, and about five of them miss.

Show the simulation code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(3)
p_true, n, n_polls = 0.54, 1000, 100

phat = rng.binomial(n, p_true, size=n_polls) / n
se   = np.sqrt(phat * (1 - phat) / n)
lo, hi = phat - 1.96 * se, phat + 1.96 * se
covers = (lo <= p_true) & (hi >= p_true)
n_miss = int((~covers).sum())

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.4))
for i in range(n_polls):
    color = "#1f3a5f" if covers[i] else "#c0392b"
    ax.plot([lo[i], hi[i]], [i, i], color=color, lw=1.3, alpha=0.9)
ax.axvline(p_true, color="#b5446e", ls="--", lw=1.8)
ax.set_yticks([])
ax.set_xlabel("Estimated support")
ax.set_title(f"{n_miss} of 100 intervals miss the truth — that is what '95% confidence' means")
ax.text(p_true + 0.004, 2, "true value 0.54", color="#b5446e", fontsize=9, rotation=90, va="bottom")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Stare at the red intervals. Nothing went wrong in those polls — they followed the identical, correct procedure. They simply drew an unlucky sample, as roughly 5% of honest studies always will. That is the entire content of “95% confidence”: not a probability about your one interval, but a batting average of the method.

Building one: means and proportions

For a mean, use the sample mean and its standard error s/\sqrt n:

\bar y \;\pm\; c\cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt n},

where c is the critical value. (Wooldridge 2019) Wooldridge’s worked case: across 20 manufacturing firms, a 95% interval for the change in scrap rates came out as [-2.28,\,-0.02] — the whole interval sits below zero, so the data point to a genuine decrease in waste (barely). (Wooldridge 2019)

For a proportion, the standard error is \sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)/n}:

\hat p \;\pm\; z\cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat p(1-\hat p)}{n}}.

This is the machinery behind every poll’s “margin of error.” (Grinstead and Snell 1997) To pin a proportion to a \pm 3\% margin (a 6-point-wide interval) you need about n = 1{,}111 respondents — which is why national polls cluster around a thousand. (Grinstead and Snell 1997) And the long-run promise checks out: in a simulation of 10,000 polls drawn from a true support of 0.54, about 9,648 (≈96%) of the computed intervals landed within 3% of the truth. (Grinstead and Snell 1997)

When \sigma is unknown: the t-distribution

The formulas above quietly assumed we knew the population spread \sigma. We almost never do — we estimate it with the sample standard deviation s, and that estimate is itself uncertain, especially in small samples. (Bruce et al. 2020) Plugging a noisy s into the formula and using the Normal critical value would understate our uncertainty.

The fix is the t-distribution, worked out by W. S. Gosset writing as “Student” while a brewery forbade him publishing under his own name. (Bruce et al. 2020) The t looks like the Normal but with fatter tails: to capture 95% of its area you must reach further out than 1.96, and that extra reach is precisely the penalty for not knowing \sigma. Its exact shape is set by the degrees of freedom, usually n-1 for a single mean — the same degree of freedom spent estimating the mean in the variance formula. As n grows, s pins down \sigma ever more tightly, the tails thin, and the t-distribution melts into the Normal: for n past ~30 the two are practically identical, which is why “t vs z” only matters for small samples. (Bruce et al. 2020)

The bootstrap: a confidence interval with no formula

What if there is no clean algebra for your statistic’s standard error — a median, a trimmed mean, a ratio? The bootstrap sidesteps the math entirely. (Bruce et al. 2020) Treat your sample as a mini-population and draw new samples from it, with replacement, each the same size n. Compute your statistic on each resample; after, say, 1,000 repetitions you have an empirical distribution of the statistic. For a 95% interval, simply trim the lowest and highest 2.5% — the remaining range is your interval. (Bruce et al. 2020)

TipWhat the bootstrap can and cannot do

It is superb for statistics with no tidy formula (medians, trimmed means) and frees you from assuming Normality. But it does not manufacture information: it reveals how much your existing sample would vary if redrawn from a similar population. It cannot rescue a small or biased sample — resampling 30 unrepresentative people 1,000 times just gives you 1,000 views of the same 30 people. (Bruce et al. 2020)

Width: the anatomy of precision

The width of an interval is your precision, and three things drive it. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Sample size. Width shrinks like 1/\sqrt n, so — the recurring tax of the \sqrt n — to halve an interval you must quadruple the sample. (Grinstead and Snell 1997)
  • Confidence level. Demanding more confidence (99% vs 95%) forces a wider interval; to be surer of catching the truth you must cast a bigger net.
  • Variability. A noisier population (larger \sigma) yields wider, less precise intervals.

You can buy precision, but the exchange rate is brutal and fixed.

The duality with hypothesis testing

Confidence intervals and significance tests are two faces of one coin. (Wooldridge 2019) The rule:

If a hypothesised value falls outside the 95% confidence interval, you would reject that hypothesis at the 5% level — and vice versa.

The most-used special case: if a treatment effect’s 95% interval excludes zero, the effect is “statistically significant” at 5%; if it includes zero, it isn’t. (Wooldridge 2019) Wooldridge’s scrap-rate interval [-2.28, -0.02] excludes zero (just), so the decrease is significant at 5%. The interval does everything a test does and more — it shows the whole range of values you can’t reject, not merely a yes/no verdict — which is why many statisticians now urge reporting intervals over bare p-values. This duality is the bridge to the next entry.

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong in practice

  • “95% probability the truth is in this interval.” The single most common error. The procedure has a 95% hit rate; the fixed truth is either in your one interval or it isn’t. Want the probabilistic reading? That requires a Bayesian credible interval. (Reinhart 2015)

  • Eyeballing overlap between two intervals. It is not true that overlapping 95% intervals mean “no significant difference.” Two intervals can overlap and the difference still be significant — comparing two groups needs a test built for the difference, not a glance at their separate bars. “Your eyeball is not a well-defined statistical procedure.” (Reinhart 2015)

  • Using z when you should use t. With small n and an estimated \sigma, the Normal critical value gives intervals that are too narrow — falsely precise. Use t until n is comfortably large. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Treating the bootstrap as a small-sample cure. It quantifies the variability already in your data; it cannot create data you never collected or undo selection bias. (Bruce et al. 2020)

  • Confusing interval width with bias. A tight interval around a biased estimate is confidently wrong. Width measures precision (variance), not accuracy (bias) — both must be right.

Appendix · the full mathematics

Where the mean interval comes from (the pivot). By the CLT, \bar X \approx \mathcal N(\mu, \sigma^2/n), so the standardised quantity Z = (\bar X - \mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt n) is approximately standard Normal. Since \Pr(-1.96 \le Z \le 1.96) = 0.95, substitute and rearrange the inequality to isolate \mu:

\Pr\!\left(\bar X - 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \;\le\; \mu \;\le\; \bar X + 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\right) = 0.95 .

The randomness is in the endpoints \bar X \pm 1.96\,\sigma/\sqrt n, not in \mu — which is exactly why the probability statement is about the interval, not the fixed \mu.

The t statistic. When \sigma is replaced by the sample s, the standardised quantity is no longer Normal but follows Student’s t with n-1 degrees of freedom:

T = \frac{\bar X - \mu}{s/\sqrt n} \sim t_{\,n-1}.

The interval becomes \bar X \pm t_{n-1,\,0.975}\, s/\sqrt n, with the t critical value exceeding 1.96 (e.g., 2.26 at n=10, df =9) and shrinking toward 1.96 as n\to\infty.

The proportion sample size. The margin is z\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}, worst-case at p = 0.5 where \sqrt{p(1-p)} = 0.5. Using the round critical value z \approx 2 gives the handy conservative rule \text{margin} \approx 2 \cdot 0.5/\sqrt n = 1/\sqrt n. Setting 1/\sqrt n = 0.03 yields

n = \frac{1}{(0.03)^2} \approx 1{,}111,

the figure the source quotes. (The slightly sharper z = 1.96 gives n \approx 1{,}068.) Either way: a \pm 3\% poll needs roughly a thousand respondents, and N never appears.

Bootstrap percentile interval. Draw B resamples of size n with replacement; compute \hat\theta^{*}_1,\dots,\hat\theta^{*}_B. The percentile 95% interval is \big[\hat\theta^{*}_{(0.025)},\, \hat\theta^{*}_{(0.975)}\big] — the empirical 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the resampled estimates. No Normality assumed; the data’s own shape drives the interval.

distribution of the statistic. For a 95% interval, simply **trim the lowest and highest 2.5%** —
the remaining range is your interval. [@bruce_practical]

::: {.callout-tip title="What the bootstrap can and cannot do"}
It is superb for statistics with no tidy formula (medians, trimmed means) and frees you from
assuming Normality. But it does **not** manufacture information: it reveals how much your *existing*
sample would vary if redrawn from a similar population. It cannot rescue a small or biased sample —
resampling 30 unrepresentative people 1,000 times just gives you 1,000 views of the same 30 people.
[@bruce_practical]
:::

## Width: the anatomy of precision

The **width** of an interval *is* your precision, and three things drive it. [@bruce_practical]

- **Sample size.** Width shrinks like $1/\sqrt n$, so — the recurring tax of the $\sqrt n$ — **to
  halve an interval you must quadruple the sample.** [@grinstead_snell]
- **Confidence level.** Demanding more confidence (99% vs 95%) forces a *wider* interval; to be
  surer of catching the truth you must cast a bigger net.
- **Variability.** A noisier population (larger $\sigma$) yields wider, less precise intervals.

You can buy precision, but the exchange rate is brutal and fixed.

## The duality with hypothesis testing

Confidence intervals and significance tests are two faces of one coin. [@wooldridge_intro] The rule:

> If a hypothesised value falls **outside** the 95% confidence interval, you would **reject** that
> hypothesis at the 5% level — and vice versa.

The most-used special case: **if a treatment effect's 95% interval excludes zero, the effect is
"statistically significant" at 5%; if it includes zero, it isn't.** [@wooldridge_intro] Wooldridge's
scrap-rate interval $[-2.28, -0.02]$ excludes zero (just), so the decrease is significant at 5%. The
interval does everything a test does *and more* — it shows the whole range of values you can't
reject, not merely a yes/no verdict — which is why many statisticians now urge **reporting intervals
over bare p-values**. This duality is the bridge to the [next entry](hypothesis-testing.qmd).

## Common pitfalls

::: {.callout-warning title="Where this goes wrong in practice"}

- **"95% probability the truth is in this interval."** The single most common error. The procedure
  has a 95% hit rate; the fixed truth is either in your one interval or it isn't. Want the
  probabilistic reading? That requires a Bayesian
  [credible interval](bayesian-worldview.qmd). [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Eyeballing overlap between two intervals.** It is *not* true that overlapping 95% intervals mean
  "no significant difference." Two intervals can overlap and the difference still be significant —
  comparing two groups needs a test built for the *difference*, not a glance at their separate bars.
  "Your eyeball is not a well-defined statistical procedure." [@reinhart_swrong]

- **Using $z$ when you should use $t$.** With small $n$ and an estimated $\sigma$, the Normal
  critical value gives intervals that are too narrow — falsely precise. Use $t$ until $n$ is
  comfortably large. [@bruce_practical]

- **Treating the bootstrap as a small-sample cure.** It quantifies the variability already in your
  data; it cannot create data you never collected or undo selection bias. [@bruce_practical]

- **Confusing interval width with bias.** A tight interval around a biased estimate is *confidently
  wrong*. Width measures precision (variance), not accuracy (bias) — both must be right.

:::

## Appendix · the full mathematics {.unnumbered}

::: {.math-appendix}

**Where the mean interval comes from (the pivot).** By the [CLT](sampling-distributions-clt.qmd),
$\bar X \approx \mathcal N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$, so the standardised quantity
$Z = (\bar X - \mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt n)$ is approximately standard Normal. Since
$\Pr(-1.96 \le Z \le 1.96) = 0.95$, substitute and rearrange the inequality to *isolate* $\mu$:

$$
\Pr\!\left(\bar X - 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n} \;\le\; \mu \;\le\; \bar X + 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\right) = 0.95 .
$$

The randomness is in the *endpoints* $\bar X \pm 1.96\,\sigma/\sqrt n$, not in $\mu$ — which is
exactly why the probability statement is about the *interval*, not the fixed $\mu$.

**The $t$ statistic.** When $\sigma$ is replaced by the sample $s$, the standardised quantity is no
longer Normal but follows Student's $t$ with $n-1$ degrees of freedom:

$$
T = \frac{\bar X - \mu}{s/\sqrt n} \sim t_{\,n-1}.
$$

The interval becomes $\bar X \pm t_{n-1,\,0.975}\, s/\sqrt n$, with the $t$ critical value exceeding
1.96 (e.g., 2.26 at $n=10$, df $=9$) and shrinking toward 1.96 as $n\to\infty$.

**The proportion sample size.** The margin is $z\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}$, worst-case at $p = 0.5$ where
$\sqrt{p(1-p)} = 0.5$. Using the round critical value $z \approx 2$ gives the handy conservative
rule $\text{margin} \approx 2 \cdot 0.5/\sqrt n = 1/\sqrt n$. Setting $1/\sqrt n = 0.03$ yields

$$
n = \frac{1}{(0.03)^2} \approx 1{,}111,
$$

the figure the source quotes. (The slightly sharper $z = 1.96$ gives $n \approx 1{,}068$.) Either
way: a $\pm 3\%$ poll needs roughly a thousand respondents, and $N$ never appears.

**Bootstrap percentile interval.** Draw $B$ resamples of size $n$ with replacement; compute
$\hat\theta^{*}_1,\dots,\hat\theta^{*}_B$. The percentile 95% interval is
$\big[\hat\theta^{*}_{(0.025)},\, \hat\theta^{*}_{(0.975)}\big]$ — the empirical 2.5th and 97.5th
percentiles of the resampled estimates. No Normality assumed; the data's own shape drives the
interval.

:::
