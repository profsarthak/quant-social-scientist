Hierarchical & Multilevel Bayesian Models

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

Clustered data forces a choice — one estimate for everyone, or a separate one per group? Hierarchical models refuse the false choice and let the data decide how much to pool.

Data come in groups: students in schools, patients in hospitals, repeated measures on people. You can pool everything (one estimate for all — blind to real group differences) or pool nothing (a separate estimate per group — hopeless for small groups). Hierarchical models take the middle path: group parameters are drawn from a shared population distribution, so each group borrows strength from the rest and is shrunk toward the overall mean — by an amount the data itself decides. This is the Bayesian engine behind mixed-effects models.

Why it matters

The single most common structure in social data is groups within a population, and the naïve analyses both fail. Hierarchical modelling is the principled fix — and it is everywhere modern Bayesian work lives: multilevel regression, meta-analysis, small-area estimation, MRP for polling. It also unifies three ideas you’ve met separately: random effects, regularization, and the Bayesian treatment of priors.

The spectrum of pooling

Facing grouped data, you stand between two extremes (Lambert 2018):

  • Complete pooling — ignore the groups, fit one global parameter for all observations. It uses the full sample but produces too little uncertainty and often misrepresents the data-generating process by erasing real group variation (Lambert 2018).
  • No pooling (heterogeneous) — fit a separate, independent model per group. It respects group differences but overfits, and is “terrible” for small groups, whose scant data give wild, noisy estimates (Lambert 2018).

The hierarchical model bridges the gap: group-specific parameters that are nonetheless tied together through a common population distribution (Lambert 2018).

The hierarchical solution

The trick is to extend the idea of a prior to multiple levels (Lambert 2018):

\begin{aligned} \text{Likelihood:} \quad & x_{ij} \sim \text{Normal}(\mu_j,\ \sigma) && \text{(data within group } j)\\ \text{Group level:} \quad & \mu_j \sim \text{Normal}(\bar\mu,\ \bar\sigma) && \text{(groups drawn from a population)}\\ \text{Hyperprior:} \quad & \bar\mu \sim \text{Normal}(50,10),\ \ \bar\sigma \sim \text{Cauchy}^{+}(0,2.5) && \text{(population params inferred from data)} \end{aligned}

The hyperparameters \bar\mu,\bar\sigma describe the population of groups and are learned from the data, not fixed (Lambert 2018). The structure assumes exchangeability: knowing nothing about the groups but their data, relabelling them wouldn’t change our beliefs — the justification for giving them a common prior (Lambert 2018).

Partial pooling and shrinkage

Because the groups share a population distribution, their estimates are partially pooled — each borrows statistical strength from the others and is pulled toward the grand mean \bar\mu (Lambert 2018). The shrinkage rule is the beautiful part (Lambert 2018):

Groups with small samples or noisy data are shrunk more toward the population average; large, data-rich groups stay close to their raw empirical means.

And the model learns how much to pool from the between-group variance \bar\sigma (Lambert 2018): if \bar\sigma is estimated small, it moves toward complete pooling; if large, toward no pooling. The data picks the point on the spectrum.

Show the figure code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(11)
K = 12
mu_pop, tau = 50.0, 6.0           # population mean and between-group SD
sigma = 12.0                      # within-group SD
true_mu = rng.normal(mu_pop, tau, K)
n_j = rng.choice([3, 5, 8, 15, 40, 120], size=K)     # very unequal group sizes

ybar = np.array([rng.normal(true_mu[j], sigma/np.sqrt(n_j[j])) for j in range(K)])  # no-pool est
se2 = sigma**2 / n_j              # sampling variance of each group mean
grand = np.average(ybar, weights=1/se2)
# partial-pooling (Normal-Normal) estimate: precision-weighted blend of ybar_j and grand mean
shrink = se2 / (se2 + tau**2)     # 0 = trust data, 1 = full shrink to grand mean
partial = (1 - shrink) * ybar + shrink * grand

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7.4, 4.4))
for j in range(K):
    lw = 0.5 + 2.5 * (n_j[j] / n_j.max())
    ax.plot([0, 1], [ybar[j], partial[j]], "-", color="#1f4e79", lw=lw, alpha=0.7)
    ax.plot(0, ybar[j], "o", color="#c0392b", ms=4)
    ax.plot(1, partial[j], "o", color="#1f8a4c", ms=4)
    ax.text(-0.04, ybar[j], f"n={n_j[j]}", ha="right", va="center", fontsize=7, color="#555")
ax.axhline(grand, color="#444", ls="--", lw=1.2)
ax.text(1.02, grand, "grand mean", va="center", fontsize=8.5, color="#444")
ax.set_xticks([0, 1]); ax.set_xticklabels(["no pooling\n(raw group mean)", "partial pooling\n(hierarchical)"])
ax.set_xlim(-0.25, 1.35); ax.set_ylabel("group estimate")
ax.set_title("Shrinkage: small, noisy groups are pulled hardest toward the mean")
fig.tight_layout()
plt.show()

A worked example: English school scores

Lambert’s case is GCSE scores across 3,458 English schools (Lambert 2018). A pooled model gives a single effect for, say, poverty (free school meals) — but that’s “highly suspect” because regions differ in quality. A heterogeneous model gives a separate effect for each of the 152 Local Education Authorities (LEAs) — but the estimates are “extreme” for LEAs containing only a few schools. The hierarchical model draws each LEA’s parameter from an England-level distribution, yielding LEA estimates shifted toward the grand mean with lower uncertainty, because each LEA now uses information from all the others (Lambert 2018).

Connections to mixed models and regularization

Two bridges make this less exotic than it looks (Lambert 2018):

  • Frequentist mixed/random-effects models are the direct counterpart. The difference is philosophical: frequentists treat group effects as random and population effects as fixed constants; Bayesians treat all parameters as random variables with distributions (Lambert 2018). (See mixed-effects models.)
  • Regularization. Shrinkage is regularization: like Lasso/Ridge, the hierarchical prior prevents overfitting by reining in extreme parameter values unsupported by the population-level data (Lambert 2018). A multilevel model is a self-tuning regulariser.

The funnel and non-centered parameterization

Hierarchical models bite back when sampled. The posterior has a “funnel” — high curvature where the between-group SD \bar\sigma is small (Lambert 2018):

  • When \bar\sigma is small, the \mu_j are squeezed tight, demanding tiny leapfrog steps; when it is large, the posterior is flat, demanding long steps. A single HMC step size can’t serve both, producing divergences (Lambert 2018).
  • Non-centered parameterization fixes it: introduce a standardized helper \mu_{\text{raw},j}\sim \text{Normal}(0,1) and set \mu_j = \bar\mu + \mu_{\text{raw},j}\cdot\bar\sigma. This decouples the group parameters from the population parameters, flattening the geometry so the sampler explores it without divergences (Lambert 2018).

Common pitfalls

WarningWhere this goes wrong
  • Defaulting to complete or no pooling. Both are usually wrong; the hierarchical middle is the honest model for grouped data. (Lambert 2018)
  • Fitting the centered parameterization with little data per group. You’ll hit the funnel and divergences — switch to the non-centered form. (Lambert 2018)
  • Over-tight hyperpriors on \bar\sigma. They pre-decide the amount of pooling; use a weakly-informative half-Cauchy/half-Normal and let the data speak. (Lambert 2018)
  • Forgetting that shrinkage is intentional. A small group’s estimate should be pulled toward the mean — that’s the model protecting you from noise, not a bug. (Lambert 2018)
  • Ignoring exchangeability. If groups aren’t exchangeable (a known covariate orders them), model that covariate rather than assuming a common prior. (Lambert 2018)

Appendix · the mathematics

The shrinkage estimate, derived. With group means \bar x_j of sampling variance \sigma_j^2 = \sigma^2/n_j and a group-level prior \mu_j\sim\mathcal N(\bar\mu,\tau^2), the Normal–Normal posterior mean is the precision-weighted blend

\hat\mu_j = (1-B_j)\,\bar x_j + B_j\,\bar\mu, \qquad B_j = \frac{\sigma_j^2}{\sigma_j^2 + \tau^2}.

B_j is the shrinkage factor: as n_j\to\infty, \sigma_j^2\to 0, B_j\to 0 — no shrinkage (trust the data); as n_j\to 0 or \tau^2\to 0, B_j\to 1 — full shrinkage to the grand mean. The code computes exactly this B_j. The hierarchical model estimates \tau^2 jointly, which is how it learns the pooling.

Why non-centering flattens the funnel. In the centered form, \mu_j\mid\bar\sigma has SD \bar\sigma, so the joint density of (\mu_j,\log\bar\sigma) narrows to a cusp as \bar\sigma\to0 — Neal’s funnel. Writing \mu_j=\bar\mu+\bar\sigma\,z_j with z_j\sim\mathcal N(0,1) makes z_j a priori independent of \bar\sigma, so the sampled geometry is a product of well-scaled standard Normals — no cusp, constant curvature, one step size suffices.

Stein’s insight. That a shrunk estimate beats the raw per-group means in total expected error is the Bayesian face of the James–Stein phenomenon: pooling across \ge 3 groups dominates the obvious estimator. Hierarchical shrinkage is Stein estimation with the pooling strength learned from data.

::: {.math-appendix}

**The shrinkage estimate, derived.** With group means $\bar x_j$ of sampling variance
$\sigma_j^2 = \sigma^2/n_j$ and a group-level prior $\mu_j\sim\mathcal N(\bar\mu,\tau^2)$, the
[Normal–Normal](bayesian-machinery.qmd) posterior mean is the precision-weighted blend

$$
\hat\mu_j = (1-B_j)\,\bar x_j + B_j\,\bar\mu,
\qquad B_j = \frac{\sigma_j^2}{\sigma_j^2 + \tau^2}.
$$

$B_j$ is the **shrinkage factor**: as $n_j\to\infty$, $\sigma_j^2\to 0$, $B_j\to 0$ — no shrinkage (trust the
data); as $n_j\to 0$ or $\tau^2\to 0$, $B_j\to 1$ — full shrinkage to the grand mean. The code computes
exactly this $B_j$. The hierarchical model estimates $\tau^2$ jointly, which is how it *learns* the pooling.

**Why non-centering flattens the funnel.** In the centered form, $\mu_j\mid\bar\sigma$ has SD $\bar\sigma$,
so the joint density of $(\mu_j,\log\bar\sigma)$ narrows to a cusp as $\bar\sigma\to0$ — Neal's funnel.
Writing $\mu_j=\bar\mu+\bar\sigma\,z_j$ with $z_j\sim\mathcal N(0,1)$ makes $z_j$ **a priori independent** of
$\bar\sigma$, so the sampled geometry is a product of well-scaled standard Normals — no cusp, constant
curvature, one step size suffices.

**Stein's insight.** That a shrunk estimate beats the raw per-group means in total expected error is the
Bayesian face of the **James–Stein** phenomenon: pooling across $\ge 3$ groups dominates the obvious
estimator. Hierarchical shrinkage is Stein estimation with the pooling strength learned from data.

:::
